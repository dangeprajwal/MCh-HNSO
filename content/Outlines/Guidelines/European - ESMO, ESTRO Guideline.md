### Laryngeal Cancer — Quick overview


- Early (T1–2 N0): single-modality organ-preserving therapy — **transoral surgery (TLM/TORS)** _or_ **definitive RT (66–70 Gy)**.
- Locally advanced with organ-preservation intent: **concurrent cisplatin-based CRT (cisplatin 100 mg/m² q3w)** or **induction TPF → RT** in selected patients.
- Advanced/unresectable/non-functional larynx or extensive cartilage invasion: **total laryngectomy ± reconstruction** followed by pathology-driven adjuvant RT/CRT.


- Definitive RT: **66–70 Gy** (early).
- Altered/hyperfractionation example: **80.5 Gy in 70 × 1.15 Gy BID** (historical schedule for accelerated control).
- Prophylactic nodal RT: ≈ **50 Gy**; involved nodes up to **70 Gy**.
- Concurrent cisplatin: **100 mg/m²** on days **1, 22, 43** (aim cumulative ≥**200 mg/m²**).
- Postop CRT for R1/ECS: **RT 66 Gy + cisplatin 100 mg/m² q3w**.

### Full Guideline Details 

#### 1. Multidisciplinary approach & general principles

- All patients should be managed by a multidisciplinary team (MDT): head & neck surgery, radiation oncology, medical oncology, radiology, pathology, nuclear medicine, speech & swallow therapy, nutrition, dental, nursing, and psycho-social support.
- Use UICC/AJCC 8th edition TNM for staging and clearly document tumor subsite (glottic, supraglottic, subglottic).
- Individualise treatment using tumour stage, expected function (voice/swallow), comorbidities, and patient preference.

#### 2. Diagnostic work-up 

- Biopsy with histopathology.
- Endoscopic assessment: flexible nasoendoscopy and, when needed, direct laryngoscopy under GA with rigid endoscopy for mapping.
- Imaging: contrast-enhanced CT and/or MRI of neck (evaluate cartilage invasion, soft-tissue extent); chest CT for distant staging.
- FDG-PET/CT: recommended in selected high-risk patients, for unknown primary, and for response assessment post non-surgical therapy.
- Baseline evaluations: nutrition, swallowing assessment, dental clearance (if RT planned), labs (CBC, renal, liver), and performance status.

#### 3. Early-stage disease (cT1–2 N0)

- Single modality recommended: either
    - **Transoral laser microsurgery (TLM) / Transoral robotic surgery (TORS)** where feasible for resectable lesions with good exposure — or
    - **Definitive radiotherapy** (IMRT/VMAT preferred) — typical dose **66–70 Gy** in standard fractionation.
- For T1 glottic tumours, elective neck treatment is usually **not** required.
- Choice between RT and TLM depends on voice outcome, anatomy, surgeon expertise, patient preference and resource availability.

#### 4. Locally advanced disease and organ-preservation strategies

- Organ-preservation valid for many T2–T3 and selected T4a tumours if functional larynx can be maintained. Two main validated approaches:
    1. **Concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CRT)** with high-dose cisplatin (preferred in fit patients).
    2. **Induction chemotherapy (TPF)** followed by RT for responders (an alternative when CRT is less desirable).
- Exclude patients with massive cartilage invasion, airway compromise, or non-functional larynx from preservation attempts — offer upfront surgery.
#### 5. Radiotherapy technical guidance

- Use **IMRT/VMAT** technique for improved normal tissue sparing and conformality.
- Dose schedules:
    - Early disease: **66–70 Gy** to primary (standard).
    - Stage II/selected early: consider **altered fractionation** (e.g., hyperfractionation or accelerated regimens) where appropriate.
    - Prophylactic node levels (when indicated): ~**50 Gy** (to elective volumes).
    - Macroscopic nodal disease: boost to **~66–70 Gy**.

#### 6. Concurrent chemotherapy: indication & dosing

- Standard radiosensitiser for definitive CRT: **cisplatin 100 mg/m²** on days **1, 22 and 43** administered with definitive RT (aim cumulative ≥200 mg/m²).
- Weekly low-dose cisplatin schedules (e.g., 30 mg/m²) have data suggesting possible inferiority; weekly 40 mg/m² equivalence is not definitively proven.
- Patients unfit for high-dose cisplatin: consider carboplatin-based regimens, cetuximab (if platinum contraindicated) with RT, or RT alone/altered fractionation depending on patient factors.
#### 7. Induction chemotherapy (larynx preservation)

- **TPF (docetaxel + cisplatin + 5-FU)** for three cycles is the standard induction regimen when induction is chosen for organ preservation.
- After induction: good responders proceed to RT (with or without concurrent radiosensitiser per institutional protocols); poor responders go to surgery.
- Sequential induction → CRT (adding full concurrent cisplatin after induction) has not shown OS superiority and increases toxicity; avoid unless within a defined protocol or clear institutional rationale.

#### 8. Surgery (when indicated)

- **Transoral approaches** (TLM/TORS) for early tumours with suitable exposure.
- **Open partial laryngectomy**: selected cases when transoral approach not feasible and function-preserving resection possible.
- **Total laryngectomy**: indicated for non-functional larynx, extensive cartilage invasion, T4b disease, or failed organ-preservation with persistent disease.
- Neck dissection: selective neck dissection for clinically/radiologically node-positive neck and for primary surgical cases (bilateral when tumour crosses midline or supraglottic involvement).

#### 9. Postoperative (adjuvant) RT / CRT

- Indications for adjuvant RT: adverse pathological features such as T3–4, close/positive margins (R1), perineural invasion (PNI), lymphovascular invasion (LVI), multiple positive nodes, extracapsular spread (ECS).
- Dose guidance (pathology-based):
    - Single risk factor: **~58 Gy**.
    - Multiple adverse features: **~63–64 Gy**.
    - Positive margins (R1) and ECS: **postop CRT** (recommended dose example **66 Gy** with concurrent cisplatin 100 mg/m² q3w).
- Start adjuvant RT ideally within **6–7 weeks** following surgery.
#### 10. Neck management after definitive nonsurgical therapy

- Routine planned neck dissection is **not** mandatory when PET/CT at ~12 weeks is negative and nodes have regressed — adopt PET-directed surveillance.
- Persistently PET-positive or morphologically suspicious nodes should trigger neck dissection.

#### 11. Recurrent / metastatic disease

- First-line systemic options (for R/M HNSCC including larynx):
    - **Pembrolizumab + platinum + 5-FU** (preferred for many when systemic therapy needed and PD-L1 status considered).
    - **Pembrolizumab monotherapy** for PD-L1 CPS ≥1 (less toxic, slower onset of response).
    - EXTREME regimen (platinum + 5-FU + cetuximab) remains an option in PD-L1 negative patients or when immunotherapy contraindicated.
- On progression after PD-1 inhibitors: no universal standard — consider cetuximab, taxane, methotrexate, clinical trials, or best supportive care depending on performance status.

#### 12. Biomarkers

- **PD-L1 (CPS)** testing recommended for R/M setting to guide pembrolizumab use.
- **p16 IHC**: not routinely required for laryngeal cancers (p16 prognostic value is oropharynx-specific).

#### 13. Follow-up and survivorship

- Follow-up exam schedule: every **2–3 months** for first **2 years**, then every **4–6 months** until year **5**, then annually.
- Use flexible endoscopy for local surveillance; perform imaging when clinical suspicion of recurrence exists or per protocol (FDG-PET/CT at ~3 months following CRT for nodal assessment when indicated).
- Supportive care: speech/swallow therapy, nutritional support, dental care, TSH monitoring if neck RT given, smoking and alcohol cessation interventions, psychosocial support.

#### 14. Special situations & practical considerations

- **Unknown primary with neck metastasis**: full workup (PET/CT, panendoscopy, tonsillectomy and base-of-tongue mucosectomy if indicated). Treatment often follows mucosal RT ± neck treatment or neck dissection with adjuvant therapy based on pathology.
- **Oligometastatic disease**: consider metastasis-directed therapy in select patients after multidisciplinary discussion.

#### 15. Limitations and caveats

- Many recommendations are driven by historic trials (some predating contemporary IMRT techniques); always adapt to modern RT techniques and institutional experience.
    
- Some organ-preservation data are from heterogeneous trials; tailor decisions to individual patient anatomy/function and local expertise.
