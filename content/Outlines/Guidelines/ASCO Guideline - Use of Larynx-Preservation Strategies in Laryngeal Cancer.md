### Overarching Principles

*   **Goal:** To preserve the larynx (either the organ itself or its function) without compromising survival compared to total laryngectomy.
*   **Voluntary Guideline:** Adherence is voluntary. The ultimate treatment decision is made by the physician in light of each patient's individual circumstances.
*   **Multidisciplinary Team is Essential:** Optimal outcomes require a team including head and neck surgery, radiation oncology, medical oncology, pathology, nursing, speech/swallowing therapy, audiology, social services, nutrition, and tobacco cessation support.
*   **No Survival Advantage:** No larynx-preservation approach offers a survival advantage over total laryngectomy and adjuvant therapy. The benefit is functional and quality-of-life.
*   **Patient Counseling is Crucial:** Patients must be fully apprised of the advantages, disadvantages, risks, toxicities, and anticipated functional outcomes of all options, including total laryngectomy.

### 1. Management by Disease Stage

#### A. Limited-Stage Disease (T1, T2)
**General Recommendation:** All patients with T1 or T2 laryngeal cancer, with rare exception, should be treated initially with intent to preserve the larynx.

| Factor | Details & Considerations |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Treatment Options** | • **Radiation Therapy (RT)** <br> • **Larynx-Preservation Surgery** (Endoscopic laser resection or open partial laryngectomy) |
| **Comparative Outcomes** | Survival outcomes are similar between radiation and surgery. The choice depends on factors beyond survival. |
| **Selection Considerations** | The decision is complex and must be individualized based on:<br>• **Tumor Factors:** Extent, volume, location (e.g., involvement of anterior commissure), and depth of invasion.<br>• **Patient Factors:** Age, occupation, personal preferences, compliance with treatment, and medical comorbidities.<br>• **Institutional Factors:** Local expertise and availability of support/rehabilitative services. |
| **Key Specifics by Subsite:** <br> **T1 Glottic Cancer** | • **Preferred Options:** Endoscopic resection (for selected patients) **OR** Radiation Therapy.<br>• **Rationale:** Both offer high local control. Endoscopic resection may be more efficient, preserve RT for second primaries, and offer cost savings. Not suitable for all lesions (e.g., indistinct lesions in abnormal mucosa are better for RT). |
| **T2 Glottic Cancer (Favorable\*)** | • **Preferred Options:** Open organ-preservation surgery **OR** Radiation Therapy.<br>• **Endoscopic resection** is an option for selected patients.<br>• **Rationale:** Open surgery offers the highest local control rates but causes permanent hoarseness. RT also has high control rates and may offer better functional voice outcomes. |
| **T2 Glottic Cancer (Unfavorable\*)** | • **Preferred Options:** Open organ-preservation surgery **OR** Concurrent Chemoradiation (for selected node-positive patients).<br>• **Radiation therapy** or **endoscopic resection** are options for selected patients.<br>• **Rationale:** Surgery offers higher local control than RT alone. Voice quality is of less concern if the tumor has already irreversibly compromised vocal cord function. |
| **T1-T2 Supraglottic Cancer (Favorable\*)** | • **Preferred Options:** Open organ-preservation surgery **OR** Radiation Therapy.<br>• **Endoscopic resection** is an option for selected patients.<br>• **Rationale:** Open surgery has high local control but requires a temporary tracheostomy and carries a risk of aspiration. RT also has high control rates and may offer better functional outcomes. |
| **T2 Supraglottic Cancer (Unfavorable\*)** | • **Preferred Options:** Open organ-preservation surgery **OR** Concurrent Chemoradiation (for selected node-positive patients).<br>• **Radiation therapy** or **endoscopic resection** are options for selected patients.<br>• **Rationale:** Surgery is more likely to yield higher local control. For T2N+ disease, randomized trial evidence supports chemoradiation as an organ-preservation option. |
| **Critical Rules** | • **Avoid Combined Modality (Surgery + RT)** if possible, as functional outcomes are worse. Single-modality treatment is effective.<br>• **Surgical Margins:** Surgery must aim for tumor-free margins. "Narrow-margin excision" followed by postoperative RT is not acceptable.<br>• **Salvage Therapy:** Local recurrence after RT may be salvaged with organ-preservation surgery, but a substantial proportion (especially T2) will require total laryngectomy.<br>• **Role of Chemotherapy:** Induction chemotherapy is **not** recommended outside of a clinical trial. Concurrent chemoradiation is reserved for selected Stage III, T2N+ patients where total laryngectomy is the only surgical option or if functional outcomes from conservation surgery are expected to be poor. |

*\*Definitions:*
*   *Favorable T2 Glottic:* Superficial tumor on imaging with normal cord mobility.
*   *Unfavorable T2 Glottic:* Deeply invasive tumor on imaging, with or without subglottic extension, with impaired cord mobility.
*   *Favorable Supraglottic:* T1/T2 tumor, superficial invasion on imaging, preserved cord mobility, or tumor of the aryepiglottic fold with minimal pyriform sinus involvement.
*   *Unfavorable Supraglottic:* More locally advanced and invasive T2 lesions.

#### B. Advanced-Stage Disease (T3, T4)
**General Recommendation:** For most patients with T3 or T4 disease **without tumor invasion through cartilage into soft tissues**, a larynx-preservation approach is a standard option.

| Factor | Details & Considerations |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Treatment Options** | 1. **Concurrent Chemoradiation Therapy**<br>2. **Organ-Preservation Surgery** (e.g., supracricoid partial laryngectomy - in highly selected patients)<br>3. **Radiation Therapy Alone**<br>*All approaches reserve total laryngectomy for salvage of recurrence.* |
| **No Survival Difference** | All options, including initial total laryngectomy, have similar overall survival when salvage surgery is incorporated. |
| **Selection of Patients for Surgery** | Only a **minority** of T3-T4 patients are suitable for specialized partial laryngectomy procedures. Adding postoperative RT compromises functional outcomes. |
| **Role of Concurrent Chemoradiation** | • **Offers the highest rate of larynx preservation** compared to RT alone or induction chemotherapy followed by RT.<br>• **Cost:** Higher acute "in-field" toxicities (e.g., mucositis, dysphagia) but no more long-term speech/swallowing difficulties than other approaches.<br>• **Drug of Choice:** The best evidence supports the use of **cisplatin**. |
| **Role of Radiation Alone** | An appropriate option for patients who desire larynx preservation but are not candidates for organ-preservation surgery or chemoradiation. Survival is similar, but the likelihood of larynx preservation is **lower**. |
| **Not Recommended** | • **Induction chemotherapy before organ-preservation surgery** (outside a trial).<br>• **Adding induction chemo before concurrent treatment** or using **concurrent chemo with altered fractionation RT** – insufficient evidence that this improves survival or larynx preservation. |
| **Contraindication for Larynx Preservation** | Patients with **tumor penetration through cartilage into soft tissues** are considered poor candidates. Primary surgery (usually total laryngectomy) is commonly recommended. |

### 2. Management of the Neck (Cervical Lymph Nodes)

| Nodal Status | Recommended Management |
| :--- | :--- |
| **T1-T2 Glottic, Clinically N0** | • **Most do NOT require** routine elective treatment of the neck. |
| **Advanced Glottic (T3-T4) or Any Supraglottic, Clinically N0** | • **SHOULD have elective treatment** of the neck (either elective neck dissection or elective nodal irradiation). |
| **Clinically N1 Disease, treated with RT/ChemoRT** | • If they achieve a **complete clinical response**, they do **NOT** require an elective neck dissection.<br>• Neck dissection **SHOULD** be performed for patients who do **not** have a complete clinical response. |
| **Clinically N2 or N3 Disease, treated with RT/ChemoRT** | • **Surgical treatment of the neck (neck dissection) is recommended, regardless of clinical response.**<br>• Although many patients will have a negative pathology (pCR), salvage surgery for subsequent neck recurrence is rarely successful. This risk must be weighed against the morbidity of dissection and discussed with the patient. |
| **Neck with Clinically Involved Nodes, Primary treated with Surgery** | • **Should have a therapeutic neck dissection.**<br>• If the pathology from the dissection shows poor-risk features (e.g., extracapsular extension), adjuvant concurrent chemoradiation is indicated. |

### 3. Patient Selection & Predictive Factors

*   **No Validated Biomarkers:** There are no consistent molecular or biological markers (e.g., p53) to predict success of larynx preservation.
*   **Key Negative Predictor:** **Tumor penetration through cartilage into soft tissues** is a primary negative factor, making patients poor candidates.
*   **Multidisciplinary Assessment:** Selection requires a team to assess tumor factors, patient comorbidities, psychosocial situation, and preferences.
*   **Impact of Smoking:** Continued cigarette smoking is associated with worse outcomes after radiation therapy. Patients should be strongly encouraged to abstain from smoking after diagnosis and throughout treatment.

---

### 4. Functional Outcomes & Quality of Life (QoL)

*   **Beyond Organ Preservation:** Preservation of the laryngeal structure is not a success if it results in severe chronic dysphagia, aspiration, or a need for a permanent tracheostomy or feeding tube.
*   **Varied Functional Impact:** Different organ-preservation strategies (surgery vs. chemoradiation) have different impacts on function and QoL. These outcomes are site-dependent.
*   **Patient Perception is Key:** The perceived severity of side effects varies. A permanent gastrostomy tube or stoma can be a greater concern to patients than loss of speech.
*   **Broad QoL Impact:** Emotional, social, and financial domains are significantly affected, not just speech and swallowing.
*   **Long-Term Follow-Up Needed:** Substantial functional morbidity and toxicities like dysphagia may be underestimated without long-term follow-up.
*   **Need for Assessment:** The guideline stresses the need to incorporate validated pretreatment and longitudinal QoL and functional assessments in future studies to better guide therapy and minimize morbidity.

### 5. Gaps in Evidence & Future Research Priorities

The guideline highlights several areas lacking evidence and in need of further study:
*   **Biomarkers and Imaging:** Reliable markers and advanced imaging techniques to predict prognosis and response to therapy.
*   **Optimal Treatment Combinations:** Defining the roles of induction, concurrent, and adjuvant therapy, as well as newer targeted therapies.
*   **Radiation Techniques:** The role of more targeted radiation (e.g., IMRT) and newer fractionation schedules.
*   **Timing of Surgery:** Determining the optimal timing for salvage surgery in cases of persistent disease.
*   **Surveillance:** Optimal strategies for detecting recurrence and preventing second primary cancers.
*   **Functional Management:** Practical interventions for nutrition, speech, swallowing, rehabilitation, and risk-factor modification.
*   **Patient-Reported Outcomes (PROs):** More emphasis on PROs in randomized trials to inform clinical decision-making.
