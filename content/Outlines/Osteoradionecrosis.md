### Introduction 
- Role of radiotherapy in head and neck,
- The history of ORN dates back 100 years when it was first noted by Regaud in 1922
- Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) is a late complication of radiation exposure, occurring when irradiated bone becomes devitalized.
### Aetiology
- Primary tumor site: The posterior mandible is more commonly affected due to its dense, compact nature.
- Proximity of tumor to bone
- Extent of mandible included in the primary radiation field
- State of dentition: Odontogenic and periodontal disease
- Poor oral hygiene
- Radiation dose >60 Gy
- Use of brachytherapy
- Nutritional status
- Concomitant chemo-radiation
- Ill-fitting prosthesis causing chronic trauma
- Acute trauma from surgical procedures
- Advanced stage tumors
- Proton beam therapy
### Risk Prediction and prevention strategies
- **Categorization of Factors:**
  - **Tumor-related Factors:**
    - Primary tumor site, size, stage, and proximity of tumor to bone.
  - **Treatment-related Factors:**
    - Total RT dose, RT technique, volume of irradiated mandible, dose fractionation, concurrent chemotherapy, and reirradiation.
  - **Patient-related Factors:**
    - Tobacco and alcohol use, oral hygiene, dental caries, periodontal disease, and dental extractions before or after RT.
- **Spontaneous ORN:**
  - Can occur at radiation doses above 70 Gy without preceding dental trauma.
- **Salivary Gland Exposure to RT:**
  - Leads to decreased salivary flow, increasing risk of radiation caries, pulpal disease, infection, and need for dental extraction, which can trigger ORN.
- **Dosimetric Factors:**
  - **Gomez et al.'s Findings:**
    - Maximum mandibular dose of >70 Gy and mean mandibular dose of >40 Gy are associated with increased subsequent dental events and extractions after IMRT.
    - A mean parotid dose of >26 Gy is predictive of subsequent dental caries.

### Diagnosis

**Definition:**
  - Initially described as "radiation osteitis."

**Clinical Diagnostic Criteria (Consensus-based):**
  - The affected site is within the head and neck radiation field.
  - Mucosal breakdown or failure to heal, resulting in bone exposure.
  - The overlying bone is necrotic.
  - Bone exposure persists for at least 3 months.
  - There are no recurrent tumors/metastases at the affected site.

**Note:**
  - These criteria do not include radiographic evidence of osteoradionecrosis with intact mucosa.

### Staging

**Marx’s Classification System for ORN Treatment**

**Stage I**
- **Treatment**: Primary Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBO) therapy.
- **Process**: 
  - 30 HBO dives.
  - Re-evaluation: If clinical improvement (granulation tissue, re-mucosalization) is observed, complete a full course of 60 dives to achieve full mucosal cover.
  - If no improvement, categorize as non-responder and advance to Stage II.

**Stage II**
- **Treatment**: Combination of trans-oral debridement or sequestrectomy with primary mucosal repair, followed by additional HBO therapy.
- **Process**:
  - If healing progresses, complete a total of 60 dives.
  - If wound breaks down with recurrent bone exposure, categorize it as a non-responder and advance to Stage III.

**Stage III**
- **Treatment**: Definitive surgical extirpation of all diseased bone, primary wound closure, and external fixation, followed by HBO therapy.
- **Process**:
  - 20 HBO dives post-surgery.
  - Ten weeks post-resection: Staged reconstruction with autogenous cancellous bone in a freeze-dried allogenic bone carrier.
  - Additional 10 dives post-operative HBO therapy.
  - Maxillo-mandibular fixation for eight weeks.
- **Immediate Stage III Classification**: Patients with pathologic fracture, oro-cutaneous fistula, or radiographic evidence of bony resorption of the mandible are directly classified as Stage III.

**Kagan and Schwartz’s Clinical Staging System**

**Stage I**
- **Definition**: Minimal soft tissue ulceration and limited exposed cortical bone.
- **Treatment**: Conservative management.

**Stage II**
- **Definition**: Localized involvement of mandibular cortex and underlying medullary bone.
- **Subdivisions**:
  - **Stage IIa**: Minimal soft tissue ulceration.
  - **Stage IIb**: Presence of oro-cutaneous fistula and mild soft tissue necrosis.
- **Treatment**: Conservative management or minor surgical procedures.

**Stage III**
- **Definition**: Full-thickness involvement of the bone, including the inferior border. Pathological fracture may be present.
- **Treatment**: Surgical intervention, including bone and/or soft-tissue replacement.

**Notani's Classification**

- **Stage I ORN:**
  - Confined to alveolar bone.
- **Stage II ORN:**
  - Limited to alveolar bone and/or above the level of the inferior alveolar canal.
- **Stage III ORN:**
  - Involves the area below the inferior alveolar canal, with fistula or bone fracture.

**Lyons classification**

- **Stage I** < 2.5 cm length of bone affected (damaged or exposed); asymptomatic. Medical Treatment only 
- **Stage II** > 2.5 cm length of bone; asymptomatic, including pathological fracture or involvement of inferior dental nerve or both. Medical treatment only unless there is dental sepsis or obviously loose, necrotic bone 
- **Stage III** > 2.5 cm length of bone; symptomatic, but with no other features despite medical treatment. Consider debridement of loose or necrotic bone, and local pedicled flap 
- **Stage IV** > 2.5 cm length of bone; pathological fracture, involvement of inferior dental nerve, or orocutaneous fistula, or a combination. Reconstruction with free flap if patient’s overall condition allows

### Signs and Symptoms

- **Exposed Bone Area:**
  - Area of exposed bone.

- **Tooth-Related Symptoms:**
  - Tooth mobility.
  - Spontaneous tooth exfoliation.

- **Symptoms:**
  - Early stages can be asymptomatic.
  - Pain (with or without swelling) is common.
  - Poor oral hygiene and food impaction within the exposed bone area.

- **Neurological Symptoms:**
  - Sensory neurological symptoms (e.g., dysesthesia or anesthesia in the distribution of the inferior alveolar nerve in the mandible) in late-stage ORN.
  - Trismus.
  - Neuropathic pain.
  - Secondary infection leading to:
    - Chronic pus drainage.
    - Draining extra-oral fistulae.
    - Pathological jaw fracture.

- **Radiographic Signs:**
- **Orthopantomogram (OPG)**
	- **Characteristics**: Undefined radiolucency without sclerotic demarcation surrounding the necrotic zone.
	- **Additional Findings**: Radiopaque areas may be seen when bone sequestra form.
	- **Requirements for Visibility**: Substantial alteration in mineral content and extensive bone involvement, visible only in later stages.
	- **Bone Mineral Loss**: A 30% loss of bone mineral content is necessary before any radiographic change can be seen.
- **Computed Tomography (CT) Findings:**
	  - Osteolytic lesions.
	  - Cortical erosions (buccal or lingual surface).
	  - Bone fragmentation. 
	  - Loss of spongiosa trabeculation.
- **Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) with Gadolinium**
	- **Findings**: Abnormal marrow signal, cortical destruction, and slight-to-mild irregular enhancement.
	- **Advantages**: Excellent tissue contrast and high spatial resolution.

### Pathophysiology
#### Factors Contributing to Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) and Theories of Pathogenesis

- **Radiation Osteitis**
	- **Ewing (1926)**: First reported bone changes due to radiation therapy, termed "radiation osteitis".
	- **Watson and Scarborough (1938)**: Proposed that "radiation osteitis" was caused by radiation, trauma, and infection. They believed trauma to soft tissue allowed bacteria to infect underlying bone, leading to osteomyelitis.
	-  **Meyer's Classic Triad:** Pathogenesis involves a sequence of "radiation, trauma, and infection."
- **Microorganisms Role**
	- **Daly (1972)**: Challenged the role of trauma in ORN.
	- **Modern Understanding**: Microorganisms are surface contaminants, not the true cause of ORN.
- **Radiation-Induced Endarteritis**
	- **Marx (1980s)**: Proposed that radiation induces endarteritis, causing hypoxia, hypocellularity, and hypovascularity, leading to tissue breakdown and chronic wounds.
- **Vascular Obliteration**
	- **Bras et al. (1990)**: Compared irradiated and non-irradiated mandible specimens. Found that radiation-induced obliteration of the inferior alveolar artery leads to ischemic necrosis.
- **Osteoclast Suppression Theory**
	- **Recent Theory**: Suggests osteoclast damage precedes vascular changes. Suppression of osteoclast-mediated bone turnover initiates ORN development.
	- **Bisphosphonate-Induced Osteonecrosis**: Supports this theory as bisphosphonates disrupt osteoclast function, leading to osteonecrosis.
- **Fibro-Atrophic Theory**
	- **Recent Theory**: Proposes radiation causes fibroblast depletion and dysregulation leading to reduced collagen production, contributing to ORN development.
	- **Initiation**: - **Endothelial Cell Injury**: Damage from radiotherapy due to direct radiation and/or radiation-generated reactive oxygen species (ROS) or free radicals.
	- **Chemotactic Cytokines Production**: Radiation-induced ROS/free radicals produce cytokines such as:
		  - Tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α)
		  - Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF)
		  - Fibroblast growth factor-beta (FGF-β)
		  - Interleukins 1, 4, and 6
		  - Transforming growth factor-beta1 (TGF-β1)
		  - Connective tissue growth factor
	  - **Acute Inflammatory Response**:
	  - **TNF Activation**: Triggers acute inflammation, releasing more ROS from polymorphs and phagocytes.
	  - **Endothelial Cell Destruction**: Leads to vascular thrombosis, microvascular necrosis, and local ischemia.
	  - **Barrier Loss**: Endothelial cell barrier destruction allows passage of cytokines, causing fibroblast transformation into myofibroblasts.
	  - **Myofibroblast Activity**:
	- **Proliferation**: High proliferation rates.
	- **Extracellular Matrix Production**: Secretes abnormal matrix products.
	- **Degradation Ability**: Reduced ability to degrade abnormal cellular components.
	- **Imbalance**: Imbalance between synthesis and degradation in irradiated tissues, including bone.
	- **Consequences**:
		- **Osteoblast Destruction**: Reduced bony matrix.
		- **Fibrous Tissue Replacement**: Excessive proliferation of myofibroblasts replaces bony matrix with fibrous tissues.

- **Therapeutic Approaches:**
  - **New Therapeutic Regimens:**
    - Pentoxifylline and tocopherol (vitamin E) act synergistically as potent antifibrotic agents to reverse changes caused by ORN.
### Prevalence 

#### Reduction in Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) Rates and Advances in Radiation Therapy

The incidence of ORN in the mandible is between 2 and 22% of cases, and it most often affects the body

- **Reduction in ORN Rates:**
  - **Past vs. Present:**
    - There has been a 20% decrease in the rate of ORN from earlier decades.
    - Modern-era rates are 4-8%.

- **Contributing Factors:**
  - **Advances in Radiation Modalities:**
    - The transition from conventional/2D radiation therapy (RT) to 3-D conformal RT.
    - Further advancement to intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT).

- **Proton Radiation Therapy (PRT):**
  - **Advantages:**
    - Provides more conformal treatment volumes.
    - Greater tissue-sparing capability due to its Bragg Peak property.
  - **Comparative Outcomes:**
    - **Zhang et al.'s Study:**
      - Reported reduced incidence of ORN in oropharyngeal cancer patients.
      - 2% incidence with PRT compared to 7.7% with IMRT.

### Prophylactic Oral Care
All patients should undergo prophylactic oral care before, during, and after radiation therapy.

**Tooth Extractions**
	- **Indication**: Extraction indicated advanced caries with poor restorative prognosis, periodontal disease, and nonfunctional teeth within the radiation field. 
	- **Timing**: Removal of dental foci of infection within the RT field before RT is generally recommended. All diseased teeth should be extracted 21 days before starting radiation therapy (minimum two weeks before). Alternatively, extractions can be performed within four months after completing therapy.
	-  **Post-Radiation Extractions**: Teeth extracted more than four months post-therapy should be managed with Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBO) using the Marx protocol: 20 dives at 2.4 atmospheres for 90 minutes each before extraction, and 10 dives after extraction.
	- Prescription of neutral sodium fluoride 1.1% with 5,000 ppm toothpaste for dentate patients undergoing or with a history of head and neck RT.

**Treatment of Cervical Root Caries**
	- Cervical root caries, common in xerostomic patients, must be treated promptly to prevent pulp chamber involvement or undermining of the clinical crown structure.

**Advances in Radiation Therapy**
	- **Intensity Modulated Radiation Therapy (IMRT)**: IMRT aims to decrease the incidence of ORN by improving dose conformality, sparing larger mandible volumes, and enhancing dose homogeneity.
	- **3D vs. IMRT**: A study showed that appropriately constrained IMRT can reduce the volume of mandible receiving more than 50, 55, and 60 Gy in oral cancer patients.
	- **Dosimetric Contouring:** Provides an estimate of prescribed radiation dose to specified jaw regions, allowing clinicians to make dental treatment recommendations based on predicted ORN risk.

### HOPON Trial on Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBOT) for ORN Prevention

**Purpose**: The HOPON (Hyperbaric Oxygen for the Prevention of Osteoradionecrosis) trial aimed to assess the benefit of hyperbaric oxygen in preventing osteoradionecrosis (ORN) during surgery on the irradiated mandible due to poor existing evidence.

**Design**:
- **Type**: Phase III, multi-centre, randomised controlled trial
- **Design**: Unblinded; diagnosis assessed on anonymised clinical photographs and radiographs by a blinded expert panel
- **Participants**: 144 patients, with data from 100 patients analyzed
- **Randomisation**: 1:1 to hyperbaric oxygen arm (Marx protocol) and control arm; both groups received antibiotics and chlorhexidine mouthwash

**Objectives**:
- **Primary**: Occurrence of ORN at 6 months post-surgery
- **Secondary**: Time of onset of ORN, acute symptoms, pain, quality of life, implant position, and successful retention

**Results**:
- **ORN Incidence at 6 Months**: 6.4% in the HBOT group, 5.7% in the control group
- **Acute Symptoms**: Fewer in the hyperbaric arm
- **Late Pain and Quality of Life**: No significant differences between groups

**Conclusion**: The low incidence of ORN led to the recommendation against the routine use of HBOT for dental extractions or implant placements in irradiated mandibles, contrasting with prior studies and reviews, suggesting unnecessary indication of HBOT in such scenarios.

### Time-lapse to ORN

#### Development and Onset of Osteoradionecrosis (ORN)

- **Median Time Interval:**
	- **Retrospective Study:**
	    - The median time between radiation therapy (RT) and ORN development is 13 months.
	    - Range: 2–122 months.
- **Frequency of Onset:**
	- **First Few Years Post-RT:**
	    - ORN is most frequently observed (70-94%) within the first few years after RT completion.
- **Latency Period:**
	- **Reported Median Latency:**
	    - Generally reported as 12–24 months.

- **Onset Types:**
  - **Early Onset ORN:**
    - Occurs within 24 months post-RT.
    - Associated with radiation doses higher than 60 Gy.
    - Can develop spontaneously or following dentoalveolar trauma.
  - **Late Onset ORN:**
    - Arises from trauma in a chronically hypoxic tissue environment.

- **Increased Risk Factors:**
  - **Post-Radiation Extraction:**
    - Systematic review indicates an increased risk of ORN within 2–5 years after RT.

- **Comparative Onset by Cancer Type:**
  - **Institutional Retrospective Study (IMRT):**
    - ORN developed earlier in oropharyngeal cancer patients (median: 14.6 months) than in oral cavity cancer patients (median: 36.1 months).

### Management Strategies

- **Conservative Management:**
  - **Components:**
    - Close observation.
    - Strict oral hygiene maintenance.
    - Saline irrigation and chlorhexidine mouth rinses.
    - Systemic antibiotic therapy for acute infections.
    - Anti-inflammatory and analgesics as needed.
    - Avoidance of local irritants like tobacco and alcohol.
    - Discontinuation of ill-fitting dentures.

 **Medical Management of Osteoradionecrosis (ORN)** - **PENTOCLO**

- **Pentoxifylline**:
	  - Methylxanthine derivative
	  - Actions: Counteracts inflammatory mediators (including TNF-alpha), increases erythrocyte flexibility, dilates blood vessels, inhibits inflammatory reactions in vivo, and increases collagenase activity in vitro.
- **Tocopherol**:
	  - Vitamin E analogue
	  - Actions: Scavenges reactive oxygen species generated during oxidative stress, protects cell membranes against lipid peroxidation, partially inhibits TGF-1, and reduces fibrosis by inhibiting procollagen gene expression.
- **Clodronate**, 
	- a first-generation non-nitrogenous bisphosphonate, is added to promote osteogenesis and osteoblast differentiation.
	- Used in non-responsive and advanced cases.

- **Simple Surgical Intervention:**
  - **Procedures:**
    - Smoothening of sharp bony edges to prevent traumatic ulcerations.
    - Gentle debridement of mobile bony sequestrum.
    - Removal of fixation plates and screws if contributing factors.

- **Early Intervention:**
  - Studies suggest that early intervention with minor surgical procedures combined with pharmacological methods may improve ORN prognosis.

- **Surgical Management:**
  - Employed when conservative management fails and there is progressive ORN (Notani III).
  - **Procedures:**
    - Extensive surgical resections (e.g., segmental mandibulectomy).
    - Osteo cutaneous free-flap reconstruction - standard of care
    - Fibula remains a common choice for mandibular reconstruction.

- **Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBO) Therapy:**
	  - Controversial literature on its use.
	  - **Considerations:**
	    - HBO may be considered on an individual basis for patients who fail conservative management and subsequent surgical resection.
	    - Insufficient evidence to support its use prior to dentoalveolar procedures for ORN prevention.
	- **Initial Success of Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBO) Therapy for ORN**
		- **Mainous and Hart (1975, 1976)**
			- **1975**: Reported successful outcomes using HBO as adjunctive therapy to surgery in 14 patients with refractory ORN.
			- **1976**: Expanded the series to 69 patients, again reporting successful results with HBO as an adjunctive treatment for ORN.
		- **Davis et al. (1979)**
			- Treated 23 patients with adjunctive HBO; 19 remained in remission for 2 years.
		- **Mansfield et al. (1981)**
			- 11 out of 12 patients with refractory ORN responded favorably to HBO.
		- **Marx (1983)**
			- Successfully treated 58 patients with refractory ORN using his published HBO protocol.
		- **Kraut (1985)**
			- Successfully used HBO prophylactically before and after dental extractions in three cases to prevent ORN development.
	- Reevaluation of HBO Utility for ORN
		- **Mounsey et al. (1993)**
			- Reviewed 41 ORN patients treated with HBO.
			- Found HBO beneficial for mild ORN, but surgery plus HBO needed for advanced ORN.
		- **Maier et al. (2000)**
			- Analyzed 41 advanced ORN patients.
			- There was no significant difference in recovery between patients treated with debridement and antibiotics alone versus those treated with debridement, antibiotics, and post-operative HBO.
		- **Gal et al. (2003)**
			- Treated 30 Stage III ORN patients with radical resection and osteocutaneous free flap reconstruction without peri-operative HBO.
			- Reported a 97% success rate, suggesting HBO delays definitive therapy in advanced disease.
		- **Annane et al. (2004)**
		- Conducted a prospective, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study with 68 patients.
		- Study terminated early as it showed no significant benefit of HBO over placebo in patients with overt ORN.
	- **Morbidity and Contraindications of HBO**
		- **Minimal Morbidity**: Includes transient myopia, middle ear barotrauma, and seizures.
		- **Absolute Contraindications**: Optic neuritis, history of bullous pulmonary disease (COPD), or congenital pulmonary blebs.

**Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) Gel in Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) Treatment**

**Description**: PRP gel is created by centrifuging whole blood to isolate a platelet-rich gel, which contains growth factors like PDGF, VEGF, and TGF-β. It is used to improve the survival of bone grafts in various medical fields, including dental implantology, cosmetic surgery, and orthopedics. PRP gel has shown potential in promoting bone healing and regeneration in ORN, but its effectiveness is still debated due to varying outcomes in studies.
### Recent Treatment based on staging 

#### Stage I Disease
- **Characteristics**: Small, superficial, localized bone resorption with cutaneous or mucosal dehiscence.
- **Treatment**:
  - Local wound care (oral rinses)
  - Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBO) therapy (20 dives)
  - Antibiotic therapy to address super-infection
  - Additional 10 dives of HBO if there is definitive improvement
  - Trans-oral debridement and further HBO therapy for non-responders

#### Stage II Disease
- **Characteristics**: Larger, deeper bone resorption involving cortical and medullary bone, moderate mucosal or cutaneous breakdown.
- **Treatment**:
  - Antibiotics
  - Trans-oral debridement or sequestrectomy
  - HBO therapy (20 dives preoperatively, 10 post-operatively)
  - Debridement of necrotic bone to a base of bleeding bone, primary mucosal closure, or soft tissue flap if needed
  - Additional 10 dives of HBO post-operatively
  - Aggressive surgical extirpation and immediate reconstruction with a well-vascularized free tissue transfer for wound problems or repeat bone exposure

#### Stage III Disease
- **Characteristics**: Full thickness devitalization of bone, resorption of the inferior border of the mandible, fistula, or pathological fracture.
- **Treatment**:
  - Aggressive surgical extirpation of all diseased hard and soft tissue
  - Immediate reconstruction using a free tissue transfer
  - Restoration of bony continuity, allowing for timely functional dental rehabilitation
  - Often performed without HBO therapy or debridement pre- or post-operatively
