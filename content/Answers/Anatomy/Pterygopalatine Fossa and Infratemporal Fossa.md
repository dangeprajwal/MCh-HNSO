## Part A: Pterygopalatine Fossa (PPF)

### Boundaries

The pterygopalatine fossa is a small, pyramidal, fat-filled space located deep to the posterior maxillary wall, medial to the infratemporal fossa.

| Wall | Boundary |
|------|----------|
| **Anterior** | Posterior wall of maxillary sinus (infratemporal surface of maxilla) |
| **Posterior** | Root of pterygoid process and greater wing of sphenoid |
| **Medial** | Perpendicular plate of palatine bone |
| **Lateral** | Open — communicates with infratemporal fossa via pterygomaxillary fissure |
| **Superior** | Orbital process of palatine bone and orbital surface of sphenoid |
| **Inferior** | Palatine canal narrows to palatovaginal and vomeronasal canals |

### Contents of the PPF

| Structure | Component |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Maxillary artery** (terminal segment — 3rd part) | Main blood supply to PPF; branches: descending palatine, infraorbital, sphenopalatine, posterior superior alveolar |
| **Maxillary nerve (V2)** | Enters via foramen rotundum; gives infraorbital, posterior superior alveolar, palatine, and nasopalatine branches |
| **Pterygopalatine ganglion (PPG)** | Parasympathetic ganglion suspended from V2; carries secretomotor fibres to lacrimal gland, nasal/palatal glands; sympathetic fibres from deep petrosal nerve |
| **Fatty areolar tissue** | Fills remaining space |

### Communications of the PPF

The PPF is a major crossroads of the skull base:

| Communication | Route | Structures Transmitted |
|---------------|-------|------------------------|
| **Middle cranial fossa** | Foramen rotundum | V2 (maxillary nerve) |
| **Middle cranial fossa** | Pterygoid canal (Vidian canal) | Vidian nerve (greater + deep petrosal) |
| **Orbit** | Inferior orbital fissure | Infraorbital nerve and artery, zygomatic nerve |
| **Nasal cavity** | Sphenopalatine foramen | Sphenopalatine artery, nasopalatine nerve |
| **Oral cavity/hard palate** | Greater and lesser palatine canals | Descending palatine vessels and nerves |
| **Nasopharynx** | Palatovaginal canal | Pharyngeal branch of V2 |
| **Infratemporal fossa** | Pterygomaxillary fissure | Maxillary artery, posterior superior alveolar nerve |

### Surgical Relevance of the PPF

1. **Tumour spread corridor:** PPF acts as a highway for perineural spread along V2 (maxillary nerve) from oral cavity, maxillary sinus, and nasopharyngeal tumours to the middle cranial fossa
2. **Maxillary sinus cancer** — T4 classification includes PPF involvement; indicates advanced disease
3. **Oral cavity cancers** — posterior extension via pterygomaxillary fissure into PPF; a route to skull base involvement
4. **Nasopharyngeal cancer** — inferior extension through the sphenopalatine foramen and PPF
5. **Vidian nerve sectioning** — access via PPF for vasomotor rhinitis (trans-nasal approach)
6. **Sphenopalatine ganglion block** — transnasal or trans-oral approach through PPF for pain management

## Part B: Infratemporal Fossa (ITF)

### Definition

The infratemporal fossa is an irregular space located below and medial to the zygomatic arch, behind the maxilla, lateral to the lateral pterygoid plate.

### Boundaries

| Wall | Boundary |
|------|----------|
| **Anterior** | Posterior wall of maxillary sinus and tuberosity of maxilla |
| **Posterior** | Styloid process, carotid sheath, and stylomandibular ligament |
| **Medial** | Lateral pterygoid plate; communicates with PTF via pterygomaxillary fissure |
| **Lateral** | Ramus of mandible |
| **Superior** | Greater wing of sphenoid (infratemporal surface), medial part of temporal bone |
| **Inferior** | Open — communicates with parapharyngeal space |

### Contents of the ITF

| Structure | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Masticator muscles** | Lateral pterygoid (2 heads), medial pterygoid (upper fibres), temporalis (insertion) |
| **Mandibular nerve (V3)** | Enters via foramen ovale; divides into anterior (motor) and posterior (sensory) divisions; branches: buccal, lingual, inferior alveolar, auriculotemporal, chorda tympani joins here |
| **Maxillary artery** (2nd/pterygoid segment) | With branches: anterior tympanic, deep temporal, pterygoid, masseterine, buccal |
| **Pterygoid venous plexus** | Rich venous network; communicates with cavernous sinus via emissary veins (route for spread of infection/tumour) |
| **Chorda tympani** | Branch of VII; enters ITF through petrotympanic fissure; joins lingual nerve |
| **Otic ganglion** | Below foramen ovale; parasympathetic to parotid gland |

### Masticator Space

The **masticator space** is a fascial compartment within the ITF defined by the superficial layer of deep cervical fascia as it splits to enclose the masseter, pterygoid muscles, lower ramus and body of mandible, and inferior alveolar nerve/artery.

**Boundaries of masticator space:**
- Anterolateral: Masseter muscle and its fascia
- Medial: Medial pterygoid and its fascia
- Contains: Mandibular ramus, masseter, medial/lateral pterygoid muscles, inferior alveolar nerve and vessels

**Surgical relevance:**
1. **Oral cavity cancer:** Mandibular involvement — the masticator space acts as a barrier initially, but once breached, cancer spreads rapidly into the ITF
2. **Composite resection planning:** Identifies whether the inferior alveolar nerve is invaded, dictating mandibulectomy approach
3. **CT/MRI staging:** Masticator space involvement by oral SCC upstages to T4a (AJCC 8th edition)
4. **Trismus:** Tumour infiltration of masticator muscles causes trismus — a sign of advanced disease (T4) in oral cavity and PNS cancers
5. **Deep lobe parotid tumours:** Herniate through the stylomandibular tunnel into the masticator/parapharyngeal space

## Part C: Routes of Tumour Spread into the ITF

### From Oral Cavity (Posterior Triangle/Mandible/Retromolar)
- Retromolar trigone tumours → directly into pterygomandibular raphe → medial pterygoid → ITF
- Lower alveolar tumours → mandibular canal → inferior alveolar nerve → foramen ovale → middle cranial fossa (perineural spread)
- Buccal mucosa tumours → through buccal fat pad → lateral pterygoid muscle → ITF

### From Maxillary Sinus / PNS
- Through posterior wall of maxillary sinus → pterygomaxillary fissure → PTF → ITF
- T4b maxillary sinus cancer: pterygoid plates, skull base, or nasopharynx involvement

### From Nasopharynx
- Lateral spread → eustachian tube → lateral pharyngeal recess → parapharyngeal space → ITF
- Superior spread → foramen lacerum → middle cranial fossa (perineural along V2/V3)

### From Parotid/Parapharyngeal Space
- Deep lobe parotid malignancy → stylomandibular tunnel → ITF
- PPS tumours → superior extension → ITF

### Clinical Implications of ITF Involvement
- Trismus (pterygoid muscle invasion)
- Hearing loss (eustachian tube obstruction)
- Facial numbness (V2/V3 involvement)
- Pain (inferior alveolar nerve)
- Skull base erosion with intracranial extension

## Part D: Approaches to the Infratemporal Fossa

### 1. Preauricular (Subtemporal) Approach
**Also called:** Temporal or zygomatic approach

**Steps:**
1. Preauricular incision extending into temporal region (question mark incision)
2. Temporalis muscle elevation from temporal squama
3. Zygomatic arch osteotomy (two cuts, arch reflected inferiorly with masseter)
4. Coronoid process osteotomy → displacement of temporalis
5. Removal of posterior maxillary wall
6. Lateral pterygoid plate removal if needed
7. Access to ITF contents

**Indications:** Lateral skull base tumours, zygomatic arch tumours, temporomandibular joint pathology, anterolateral approach to ITF

### 2. Transparotid Approach
- Direct access through parotid gland to deep lobe and ITF
- Used for deep lobe parotid tumours with ITF extension
- Risk: facial nerve injury

### 3. Transmandibular/Mandibular Swing Approach
- Midline/paramedian mandibulotomy → swing open mandible → access to parapharyngeal space and ITF
- Used for oropharyngeal tumours with lateral extension and PPS tumours
- Combined with lip-split incision

### 4. Fisch Type A–D Infratemporal Fossa Approach

Ugo Fisch (1978) described a systematic classification of lateral skull base approaches:

#### Fisch Type A (Infratemporal Fossa Approach Type A)

**Target:** Jugular foramen, petrous apex, lower clivus

**Indications:**
- Glomus jugulare tumours (primary indication)
- Petroclival meningiomas
- Carcinoma of external auditory canal (temporal bone resection)
- Extensive cholesteatoma with jugular foramen involvement
- Facial nerve schwannoma

**Key Steps:**

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| 1. Incision | C-shaped postauricular incision extending into neck |
| 2. Neck dissection | Exposure of ICA, IJV, CN IX–XII in neck |
| 3. Canal closure | External auditory canal closed as a cul-de-sac (skin removed, meatus oversewn) — EAC is obliterated |
| 4. Mastoidectomy | Complete canal wall down mastoidectomy |
| 5. Facial nerve | Entire facial nerve from stylomastoid foramen to geniculate ganglion identified; anterior transposition of the facial nerve (moved from posterior to anterior to styloid process) — KEY STEP that distinguishes Type A |
| 6. Jugular bulb exposure | Sigmoid sinus and jugular bulb skeletonized |
| 7. ICA exposure | Petrous ICA (horizontal segment) exposed by removing bone of carotid canal |
| 8. Tumour removal | Jugular foramen tumour removed with control of ICA and sigmoid sinus |
| 9. Obliteration | Dead space obliterated with abdominal fat |

**Consequences:**
- Conductive hearing loss (inevitable — canal closed)
- Facial paresis/palsy (temporary — nerve transposed; permanent in malignant cases requiring resection)
- Lower cranial nerve deficits (CN IX–XII) — depend on extent of tumour involvement

#### Fisch Type B

**Target:** Clivus, petrous apex (anterior to cochlea), sphenoidal sinus

**Additional steps vs Type A:**
- Mandibular condyle resection
- Section of pterygoid muscles
- Access to petrous apex anterior to cochlea

**Indications:** Chordomas, chondrosarcomas, petroclival lesions

#### Fisch Type C

**Target:** Sphenoid, clivus, petrous apex with extension into sphenoidal sinus

**Additional steps vs Type B:**
- Transection of V3 at foramen ovale
- Exposure of cavernous sinus
- Access to nasopharynx

**Indications:** Extensive tumours of clivus/sphenoid, extensive juvenile angiofibroma

#### Fisch Type D

**Target:** Orbit and adjacent structures

**Modifications:** Orbitozygomatic craniotomy variants

### 5. Endoscopic Endonasal Approach to PPF/ITF

**Snyderman (2010) described endoscopic approaches:**
- Trans-nasal approach through sphenopalatine foramen → PPF → ITF
- Medially-based access to PPF via middle meatus
- Avoids external scarring
- Limited by inability to achieve proximal/distal vascular control

**Indications:** PPF tumours (schwannomas, JNA with PTF extension), biopsy of ITF lesions, juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma (class IIIA–B)

## Part E: Management of ITF Involvement in Cancer

### Oral Cavity Cancer with ITF Extension
- **T-staging:** T4b when pterygoid muscles, skull base, or ICA involved; T4a when through cortical bone only
- **Resectability assessment:** ICA encasement (>270° involvement) = generally unresectable
- **Surgical options:** En bloc resection with medial/total maxillectomy + mandibulectomy; preauricular approach for pterygoid/ITF clearance
- **Reconstruction:** Typically requires free flap — fibula osteocutaneous, ALT, or RFFF
- **Adjuvant therapy:** Mandatory for T4; CRT if margins positive or perineural invasion present

### Maxillary Sinus Cancer with PTF/ITF Involvement
- T4a: through anterior orbital floor, skin of cheek, pterygoid plates, infratemporal fossa, cribriform plate, sphenoid, frontal sinus
- T4b: orbital apex, dura, brain, middle cranial fossa, cranial nerves other than maxillary V2, nasopharynx, clivus
- **Surgery:** Total maxillectomy ± orbital exenteration; anterolateral approach to PTF

### Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma with ITF Invasion
- T2 stage (AJCC 8th): parapharyngeal extension
- T3: skull base, cervical vertebrae, pterygoid structures, or paranasal sinuses
- **Treatment:** Primarily chemoradiotherapy (surgery not standard for primary NPC)

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The pterygopalatine fossa (PPF) is a small pyramidal space behind the posterior maxillary wall — a major anatomical crossroads with 6+ communications
> 2. The PPF contains V2 (maxillary nerve), maxillary artery (3rd part), and pterygopalatine ganglion
> 3. PPF involvement in maxillary sinus/oral cavity cancer = advanced disease; the pterygomaxillary fissure is the lateral communication to ITF
> 4. The infratemporal fossa (ITF) lies below the middle cranial fossa, behind the maxilla, lateral to lateral pterygoid plate
> 5. ITF contains V3, maxillary artery (2nd segment), pterygoid venous plexus, and masticator muscles
> 6. Masticator space = fascial compartment containing mandibular ramus + pterygoid + masseter muscles; ITF invasion through this space causes trismus
> 7. Fisch Type A approach is designed for glomus jugulare tumours: EAC closed, canal wall down mastoidectomy, anterior transposition of facial nerve, exposure of petrous ICA and jugular bulb
> 8. Perineural spread along V3 through foramen ovale to middle cranial fossa is a critical route in oral cavity and ITF cancers
> 9. ICA encasement >270° in ITF = unresectable; preoperative balloon occlusion test essential

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The pterygoid venous plexus communicates with the cavernous sinus via emissary veins — this is why cavernous sinus thrombosis can complicate masticator space infections
> - Trismus in a patient with oral cavity cancer = masticator space involvement = T4a or T4b staging
> - The "pterygomaxillary fissure" on CT is the key landmark — if it is obliterated by tumour, PPF is involved
> - Vidian nerve (in pterygoid canal) is involved early in nasopharyngeal carcinoma spread — Vidian neuralgia = early symptom of NPC
> - Fisch Type A ALWAYS closes the ear canal — inform patients of permanent hearing loss before surgery
> - MRI is superior to CT for assessing PPF/ITF soft tissue involvement; fat obliteration of PPF on MRI is the key sign of perineural spread
