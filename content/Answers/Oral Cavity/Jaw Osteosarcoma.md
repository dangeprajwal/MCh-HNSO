## Overview

Osteosarcoma of the jaw (mandible/maxilla) accounts for **5–10% of all osteosarcomas** and is the most common primary malignant bone tumour of the head and neck. Unlike appendicular osteosarcoma, jaw osteosarcoma has **distinct clinical and biological behaviour** — later age of onset, lower metastatic rate, and better prognosis when adequately resected.

## Epidemiology

| Feature | Jaw Osteosarcoma | Long Bone Osteosarcoma |
|---|---|---|
| Peak age | 3rd–4th decade (30–40 yr) | 2nd decade (10–20 yr) |
| M:F ratio | 1.2:1 | 1.5:1 |
| Most common site | Mandible (40%), maxilla | Distal femur, proximal tibia |
| Metastasis at diagnosis | 5–10% | 15–20% |
| 5-year survival | 30–70% (varies by margins) | 60–70% (with chemotherapy) |

**Mandibular distribution:** Body (60%) > symphysis (20%) > ramus (15%) > condyle (5%)

## Aetiology and Pathogenesis

### Risk Factors

| Factor | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Prior radiation exposure | Radiation-induced sarcoma; latency 5–20 years |
| Paget's disease of bone | 1% sarcomatous transformation |
| Fibrous dysplasia | Rare malignant transformation |
| Li-Fraumeni syndrome | Germline p53 mutations |
| Retinoblastoma survivors | RB1 gene mutation; radiation field |
| Chronic osteomyelitis | Chronic inflammation → transformation (rare) |

### Molecular Pathogenesis

| Pathway | Role |
|---|---|
| p53 mutations | Found in 20–40% of osteosarcomas; loss of tumour suppression |
| RB1 inactivation | [[Answers/General/Cell Cycle and Cancer|cell cycle]] dysregulation |
| MDM2 amplification | p53 inhibition |
| c-MYC amplification | Oncogenic signalling |
| CDKN2A deletion | Cell cycle deregulation |
| Wnt/β-catenin pathway | Aberrant osteoblast differentiation |

Unlike long bone osteosarcoma, jaw osteosarcoma has **fewer consistent genetic alterations** — the molecular profile is less well characterised.

## Pathology

### Histological Classification

The WHO classification recognises the same subtypes in jaw as in long bones:

| Subtype | Frequency (Jaw) | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Osteoblastic | 40–50% | Osteoid production by malignant cells |
| Chondroblastic | 25–40% | Cartilaginous matrix; most common in maxilla |
| Fibroblastic | 10–15% | Spindle cell pattern with minimal osteoid |
| Telangiectatic | Rare | Blood-filled spaces mimicking ABC |
| Small cell | Rare | Mimics Ewing sarcoma; IHC essential |
| Low-grade central | 5–10% | Parosteal-like; better prognosis |

**Diagnostic criterion:** Production of **osteoid or immature bone** by malignant mesenchymal cells — the hallmark of osteosarcoma.

### Grading

| Grade | Characteristics | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| Low-grade | Bland spindle cells, minimal atypia | Less aggressive; mainly surgical |
| High-grade | Marked pleomorphism, mitoses, necrosis | Aggressive; may need chemotherapy |

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms
- **Jaw swelling** — most common presenting complaint (70–80%)
- Pain — 50–60% (more frequent than in [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Ameloblastoma of Mandible|ameloblastoma]])
- Tooth loosening or displacement
- Paraesthesia/anaesthesia of inferior alveolar nerve (numb chin)
- Pathological fracture (advanced cases)
- Nasal obstruction, epistaxis (maxillary osteosarcoma)

### Imaging

| Modality | Findings |
|---|---|
| OPG/plain radiograph | "Sun-ray" periosteal spiculation; "moth-eaten" osteolysis; Codman's triangle |
| CT with contrast | Extent of bone destruction, soft tissue mass, cortical breach |
| MRI | Marrow extent, soft tissue invasion, neurovascular involvement |
| Chest CT | Pulmonary metastasis screening (mandatory) |
| [[Answers/General/PET-CT in Head and Neck Cancer|PET-CT]] | Staging, response assessment |
| Bone scan | Polyostotic disease exclusion |

**Classic radiographic triad:** Osteolysis + periosteal reaction (sun-ray/Codman's triangle) + soft tissue mass.

## Management

### Principles

**Surgery is the mainstay** of treatment for jaw osteosarcoma. The role of chemotherapy is debated and less established compared to extremity osteosarcoma.

### Surgery

- **Wide resection with clear margins** — the single most important prognostic factor
- Minimum **1–2 cm bone margin** beyond radiographic extent
- **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Types of Mandibulectomy|segmental mandibulectomy]]** for mandibular lesions
- **Partial/total maxillectomy** (± orbital exenteration) for maxillary lesions
- Neck dissection only for clinically positive nodes (nodal metastasis is rare: <5%)

### Margin Status and Outcomes

| Margin Status | 5-Year Survival | Local Recurrence |
|---|---|---|
| R0 (clear) | 60–70% | 10–15% |
| R1 (microscopic positive) | 30–40% | 40–60% |
| R2 (gross residual) | <10% | >80% |

Clear surgical margins are the **strongest predictor of survival** — more important than chemotherapy effect.

### Reconstruction

| Defect | Reconstruction |
|---|---|
| Mandibular segmental | Fibula free flap (gold standard) |
| Anterior mandible | Double-barrel fibula |
| Hemimandible with condyle | Fibula + condylar prosthesis or iliac crest |
| Infrastructure maxillectomy | [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Obturator vs Flap Reconstruction|obturator reconstruction]] prosthesis or scapula/fibula free flap |
| Total maxillectomy | Free flap + prosthetic rehabilitation |

## Role of Chemotherapy

### The Controversy

| Argument FOR Chemotherapy | Argument AGAINST Chemotherapy |
|---|---|
| Extrapolation from limb OS data | No prospective HN-specific trials |
| Meta-analysis (Smeele 1997) suggested benefit | Meta-analysis (Kassir 1997) found no proven benefit |
| High-grade tumours with systemic risk | NCDB: surgery alone (74.7% OS) vs surgery + chemo (71.3% OS) — no difference |
| Neoadjuvant may facilitate resection | Jaw OS has lower metastatic rate than limb OS |

### Current Practice

- **High-grade osteosarcoma:** Many centres offer [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy in Oral Cancer|neoadjuvant chemotherapy]] (MAP regimen: methotrexate, doxorubicin, cisplatin) extrapolated from extremity protocols
- **Low-grade osteosarcoma:** Surgery alone — chemotherapy NOT indicated
- **Neoadjuvant chemotherapy** may facilitate surgical resection of large tumours and allows assessment of chemosensitivity on pathological response
- **No level I evidence** for chemotherapy specifically in jaw osteosarcoma

## Role of Radiotherapy

- Osteosarcoma is considered **relatively radioresistant**
- **Not a primary treatment modality**
- Indications:
  - Unresectable tumours
  - Positive margins where re-excision is not feasible
  - Palliation of inoperable disease
- **Proton beam therapy** — offers theoretical advantage with Bragg peak; studied in skull base osteosarcoma
- Doses: 60–70 Gy if used

## Differences from Appendicular Osteosarcoma

| Feature | Jaw Osteosarcoma | Long Bone Osteosarcoma |
|---|---|---|
| Age at onset | 30–40 years | 10–20 years |
| Chondroblastic subtype | More common | Less common |
| Metastatic rate | Lower (10–20%) | Higher (15–25%) |
| Role of chemotherapy | Controversial | Established (improves survival) |
| Surgery emphasis | Margins paramount | Margins + chemo response |
| Survival determinant | Surgical margins | Chemo response + margins |
| Radiation role | Limited | Limited |

## Outcomes

| Study | N | 5-Year OS | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kassir et al. (1997) | Meta-analysis | 37% | No chemo benefit proven |
| Smeele et al. (1997) | Meta-analysis | 38% | Chemo may improve survival |
| Guadagnolo et al. (2009) | 119 | 77% | R0 margins critical; chemo benefit unclear |
| Smith et al. (2003) NCDB | 587 | 55% | Surgery alone adequate for most |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Jaw osteosarcoma accounts for 5–10% of all osteosarcomas; mandible (40%) > maxilla; peak age 30–40 years
> 2. Key difference from long bone OS: later onset, lower metastatic rate, and survival determined primarily by surgical margins
> 3. Prior radiation, Paget's disease, fibrous dysplasia, and Li-Fraumeni syndrome are recognised risk factors
> 4. Chondroblastic subtype is more common in jaw OS than appendicular OS, especially in maxilla
> 5. Classic radiographic triad: osteolysis + sun-ray periosteal spiculation + soft tissue mass; Codman's triangle
> 6. Diagnostic hallmark: osteoid production by malignant mesenchymal cells
> 7. Wide surgical resection with clear margins (R0) is the most important treatment — 5-year survival 60–70% with clear margins vs <10% with R2
> 8. Role of chemotherapy is controversial — two competing meta-analyses reached opposite conclusions; no prospective HN-specific trials exist
> 9. Radiotherapy has limited role — used for positive margins or unresectable disease; proton beam therapy under investigation
> 10. Fibula free flap is the gold standard reconstruction for mandibular defects after segmental mandibulectomy

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Jaw osteosarcoma in a young adult should NOT be managed identically to extremity OS** — surgery with clear margins is paramount; chemotherapy benefit is unproven
> 2. **Sun-ray spiculation on OPG** with jaw swelling and pain in a 30–40 year old is jaw osteosarcoma until proven otherwise — urgent CT and biopsy needed
> 3. **Chondroblastic variant** can be confused with chondrosarcoma on biopsy — look for osteoid at the periphery of cartilaginous nodules to confirm osteosarcoma
> 4. **Maxillary osteosarcoma** is more difficult to resect with clear margins due to proximity to orbit, skull base, and pterygoid region — has worse prognosis than mandibular
> 5. Always obtain **chest CT before and after resection** — lung is the primary site of distant failure; pulmonary metastasectomy may be beneficial in selected patients
