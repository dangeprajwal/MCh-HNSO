## Overview

Complete rehabilitation after mandibular reconstruction requires restoration of **occlusion and masticatory function** through dental prostheses. [[Answers/General/Osseointegrated Implants|osseointegrated implants]] implants with fixed dentures represent the gold standard for dental rehabilitation, but the timing of implant placement — immediate (at the time of flap reconstruction) versus delayed (secondary procedure) — remains debated.

## Timing of Implant Placement

### Immediate (Primary) Implant Placement

Implants are placed into the fibula or other bone graft at the time of initial reconstruction surgery.

**Advantages:**
- Single operation — avoids second surgical procedure
- Implants guided by pre-operative [[Answers/Oral Cavity/3D Models in Mandibular Reconstruction|3D virtual surgical planning]]
- Bone stock is maximal (no resorption yet)
- Prosthodontic rehabilitation begins earlier (4–6 months post-op)
- Patient satisfaction from faster return to function

**Disadvantages:**
- Implant position may be compromised by oncological needs
- If [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Oral Cancer|adjuvant RT]] is required → increased implant failure risk (20–40%)
- Longer operative time at initial surgery
- No histopathological confirmation of margins before implant commitment
- If flap fails, implants are lost

### Delayed (Secondary) Implant Placement

Implants are placed as a separate procedure after reconstruction has healed, typically ≥12 months post-surgery.

**Advantages:**
- Confirmed disease-free status before implant investment
- Bone healing and soft tissue maturation are complete
- Adjuvant therapy (if needed) has been completed
- Implant position optimised for prosthetic outcome
- Lower implant failure rate in non-irradiated bone

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires second surgical procedure under general anaesthesia
- Delayed rehabilitation (total time to dental function: 18–24 months)
- Hardware from reconstruction plates may need removal first
- Bone resorption may reduce available stock
- Patient may decline second surgery

### Comparison

| Parameter | Immediate | Delayed |
|---|---|---|
| Timing | At reconstruction | ≥12 months post-surgery |
| Number of surgeries | 1 | 2 |
| Time to dental function | 4–6 months | 18–24 months |
| Implant survival (no RT) | 85–95% | 90–97% |
| Implant survival (with RT) | 60–75% | 75–85% |
| Oncological safety | Unknown margins at time of placement | Confirmed disease-free |
| 3D planning benefit | Maximum | Moderate |
| Current preference | Increasing (with VSP) | Traditional standard |

## Impact of Radiotherapy on Implants

Adjuvant radiotherapy significantly affects implant osseointegration:

| Factor | Non-Irradiated | Irradiated (≥50 Gy) |
|---|---|---|
| Implant survival (5-year) | 90–97% | 70–85% |
| ORN risk | Negligible | 5–15% |
| Healing time | 3–4 months | 6–12 months |
| HBO adjunct | Not needed | Controversial (see [[Answers/General/Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy|HBO file]]) |

**Key considerations:**
- Implants placed **before RT** have better survival than those placed after RT
- If adjuvant RT is anticipated, immediate placement has the theoretical advantage of placing implants in non-irradiated bone
- However, irradiation of implants already in situ also carries failure risk
- **Minimum 12 months** between completion of RT and delayed implant placement

## Jatin Shah's Recommendation

Shah recommends **delayed implant placement** as the preferred approach:
- Minimum **12 months** should elapse after reconstruction before considering implants
- Panoramic radiograph must confirm satisfactory bony union at all osteotomy sites
- Metallic plates/screws at the implant site should be removed first
- Oral-maxillofacial surgeon should assess implant positioning
- Implants are exposed 4–6 months after placement for prosthetic loading

## Technical Considerations

### Implant Placement in Fibula Flap

| Challenge | Solution |
|---|---|
| Fibula height discrepancy (2 cm vs 4 cm mandible) | Double-barrel fibula; vertical distraction osteogenesis |
| Limited bone width | Wider implants (4–5 mm); bone grafting |
| Soft tissue bulk over fibula | Debulking of skin paddle before implant exposure |
| Plate interference | Remove plates before implant placement |
| Vertical bone deficiency | Onlay bone grafting; zygomatic implants for maxilla |

### Virtual Surgical Planning (VSP)

- **3D-printed cutting guides** allow precise fibula osteotomies matching the mandibular defect
- Implant positions can be **pre-planned** in the virtual model
- Guide-compatible implant sleeves allow immediate placement with prosthetically driven positioning
- VSP reduces operative time and improves implant angulation accuracy

### Number of Implants Required

| Defect | Minimum Implants | Prosthesis Type |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral mandible (hemimandible) | 3–4 | Fixed partial denture |
| Anterior mandible | 4–6 | Fixed full-arch |
| Segmental (body) | 2–3 | Fixed or removable |

## Implant Survival Data

| Study Type | Implants in Native Bone | Implants in Fibula | Implants in Irradiated Fibula |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5-year survival | 95–97% | 85–95% | 70–85% |
| 10-year survival | 90–95% | 75–90% | 60–75% |

Mean implant survival in fibula flaps (meta-analysis): **83%** with minimum 5-year follow-up. Modern improvements in implant design, surface coating, and 3D planning have progressively improved outcomes.

## Prosthetic Options

| Option | Indication | Advantages |
|---|---|---|
| Implant-supported fixed denture | Sufficient implants + bone | Best function; permanent |
| Implant-retained overdenture | Limited implants (2–3) | Removable; easier hygiene |
| Conventional removable denture | Unable to place implants | No surgery; immediate |
| Bar-and-clip attachment | Moderate implant number | Good retention; removable |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Dental rehabilitation after mandibular reconstruction requires osseointegrated implants for optimal masticatory function
> 2. Delayed placement (≥12 months) is the traditional standard — allows confirmed disease-free status and completion of adjuvant therapy
> 3. Immediate placement is gaining favour with VSP — single surgery, faster rehabilitation, but risk if adjuvant RT needed
> 4. Adjuvant RT reduces implant survival from 90–97% to 70–85% at 5 years
> 5. Shah recommends delayed placement with minimum 12 months after reconstruction
> 6. Double-barrel fibula addresses the height discrepancy between fibula (2 cm) and native mandible (4 cm)
> 7. Metallic reconstruction plates should be removed before implant placement if they interfere with implant sites
> 8. VSP with 3D-printed guides enables prosthetically driven implant positioning even at primary surgery
> 9. Minimum 3–4 implants for hemimandibular reconstruction; 4–6 for anterior arch
> 10. Mean implant survival in fibula flaps is 83% at 5 years — comparable to native bone outcomes in modern series

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **If adjuvant RT is NOT anticipated** (e.g., pT2N0 with clear margins), immediate implant placement with VSP is increasingly the preferred approach — saves the patient a second surgery
> 2. **If adjuvant RT is likely** (pT3–T4, close margins, N+), defer implants — wait minimum 12 months after RT completion
> 3. The **skin paddle** of the fibula often needs debulking before implant exposure — plan a minor revision surgery at the time of implant uncovering
> 4. For anterior mandibular defects, the **double-barrel technique** is essential to provide adequate bone height for implant placement
> 5. Always involve the **prosthodontist from the outset** — implant positioning must be driven by the planned prosthesis, not just available bone
