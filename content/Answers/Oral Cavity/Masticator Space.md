## Introduction

The masticator space is a fascially defined compartment in the suprahyoid neck that contains the muscles of mastication, the ramus and posterior body of the mandible, and the mandibular division of the trigeminal nerve (V3). It is one of the most clinically relevant deep spaces in head and neck oncology because its involvement by oral cavity cancers defines T4b disease (very advanced, generally unresectable), and it serves as a conduit for perineural spread to the skull base. Understanding its fascial boundaries, contents, and communications is essential for staging, surgical planning, and radiation field design.

## Anatomy

### Fascial Boundaries (Curtin 1987)

The masticator space is defined by the **superficial layer of the deep cervical fascia (investing fascia)** as it splits to enclose the muscles of mastication:

| Boundary | Structure |
|---|---|
| **Lateral** | Superficial layer of deep cervical fascia over masseter and temporalis |
| **Medial** | Medial pterygoid fascia (separates from parapharyngeal space) |
| **Superior** | Temporal fossa (continuous superiorly along temporalis muscle) |
| **Inferior** | Inferior border of mandible |
| **Anterior** | Masseteric fascia over anterior border of masseter |
| **Posterior** | Parotid fascia (separating from parotid space) |

### Contents

**Muscles:**
- Masseter (superficial and deep heads)
- Temporalis
- Medial pterygoid
- Lateral pterygoid

**Bones:**
- Ramus of mandible
- Posterior body of mandible
- Coronoid process
- Condylar process and TMJ

**Nerves:**
- Mandibular nerve (V3) — enters through foramen ovale
- Inferior alveolar nerve
- Lingual nerve
- Buccal nerve
- Auriculotemporal nerve
- Nerve to mylohyoid

**Vessels:**
- Internal maxillary artery (second and third parts)
- Pterygoid venous plexus
- Middle meningeal artery

### Key Communications

| Communication | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| **Foramen ovale → middle cranial fossa** | Perineural spread of V3 to Meckel's cave and cavernous sinus |
| **Pterygomaxillary fissure → pterygopalatine fossa** | Tumour spread to V2, orbit |
| **Mandibular notch → deep temporal space** | Contiguous tumour extension |
| **Medially → parapharyngeal space** | Separated by thin fascial plane; easily breached |
| **Inferiorly → submandibular space** | Along mandibular body |
| **Posteriorly → parotid space** | Through stylomandibular tunnel |

## Relationship to the Infratemporal Fossa

The masticator space and infratemporal fossa (ITF) overlap significantly but are defined differently:

| Feature | Masticator Space | Infratemporal Fossa |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Fascial space (radiological) | Anatomical region (surgical) |
| Boundaries | Defined by fascial layers | Defined by bony landmarks |
| Overlap | Contains most ITF contents | Partially within masticator space |
| Usage | Imaging/staging | Surgical planning |

In practice, these terms are often used interchangeably in clinical settings, although the masticator space is the preferred term in cross-sectional imaging.

## Pathology of the Masticator Space

### Malignant Tumours

**Direct extension from adjacent sites:**
- Oral cavity SCC (retromolar trigone, posterior alveolus, buccal mucosa)
- Maxillary sinus carcinoma (through posterior wall)
- Nasopharyngeal carcinoma (lateral extension)
- Oropharyngeal SCC (through pterygomandibular raphe)

**Perineural tumour spread:**
- Along V3 (inferior alveolar nerve) → masticator space → foramen ovale → middle cranial fossa
- Along V3 motor branches → masticator space infiltration manifesting as **denervation atrophy** of muscles of mastication

**Primary masticator space tumours:**
- Osteosarcoma/chondrosarcoma of mandibular ramus
- Rhabdomyosarcoma (in children)
- Lymphoma

### Benign Tumours

- Schwannoma of V3
- Meningioma extending through foramen ovale
- Ameloblastoma of mandibular ramus

### Inflammatory/Infectious

- Masticator space abscess (odontogenic origin — most common non-neoplastic cause)
- Pericoronitis extending to masticator space
- Osteomyelitis of mandible

## Clinical Features of Masticator Space Involvement

- **Trismus** — the cardinal sign; caused by infiltration/spasm of pterygoid muscles and/or masseter
- **Pain** — deep facial pain, pain on chewing
- **V3 sensory deficit** — numbness of lower lip, chin, lower teeth (numb chin syndrome)
- **V3 motor deficit** — weakness/wasting of masseter and temporalis (deviation of jaw to affected side)
- **Swelling** — temporal fossa fullness, infrazygomatic mass
- **Referred otalgia** — via auriculotemporal nerve

### Numb Chin Syndrome

Numbness in the mental nerve distribution (lower lip and chin) in a patient with known malignancy should raise suspicion for:
1. Perineural spread along the inferior alveolar nerve into the masticator space
2. Mandibular metastasis
3. Leptomeningeal carcinomatosis

This is an **ominous sign** requiring urgent MRI evaluation.

## Imaging

### MRI (Preferred Modality)

**T1-weighted images:**
- Normal masticator space shows **high-signal fat** surrounding muscles and mandibular ramus
- Tumour infiltration replaces fat → **loss of normal T1 hyperintense fat signal** (the key finding)

**T1 post-gadolinium with fat saturation:**
- Enhancing tumour mass within the masticator space
- Enhancing and thickened V3 nerve (perineural spread)
- Enhancement extending through foramen ovale to Meckel's cave

**T2-weighted images:**
- Tumour typically hyperintense
- Denervation oedema of muscles of mastication (early sign of perineural involvement)

**Key MRI findings of perineural spread:**
1. Enhancing and enlarged nerve
2. Widened foramen ovale
3. Obliteration of perineural fat pad at foramen ovale
4. Enhancement in Meckel's cave
5. Denervation changes in muscles of mastication (oedema early, atrophy late)

### CT

- Best for bony changes: erosion of mandibular ramus, widening of foramen ovale
- Cortical bone destruction distinguishes malignant from benign causes
- Helpful for dental assessment (odontogenic abscess vs tumour)

## Staging Significance

Masticator space involvement in oral cavity cancer = **T4b** (AJCC 8th Edition):
- "Tumour invades masticator space, pterygoid plates, or skull base and/or encases internal carotid artery"
- This classifies the tumour as **Stage IVB** regardless of nodal status
- Traditionally considered **unresectable** — though selected cases may be offered surgery at high-volume centres

## Surgical Relevance

### When Is Surgery Considered?

- **Limited masticator space involvement** — tumour confined to medial pterygoid without skull base extension
- Patient is fit for extended surgery
- Multidisciplinary team consensus at a high-volume centre
- Clear plan for achieving negative margins
- Adjuvant chemoradiation will follow

### Surgical Approaches

1. **Mandibulotomy with mandibular swing** — for lateral masticator space access
2. **Maxillary swing** — for medial masticator space/pterygomaxillary region
3. **Cranio-orbito-zygomatic approach** — when skull base involvement requires combined intracranial access
4. **Transcervical approach with parotidectomy** — for posterior masticator space lesions

### Structures at Risk During Surgery

- Internal maxillary artery and pterygoid venous plexus (haemorrhage)
- Mandibular nerve (V3) — sacrifice if involved by tumour
- Facial nerve (marginal mandibular branch) — at risk during mandibulotomy
- Middle meningeal artery — at risk during superior dissection near foramen spinosum

## Radiation Therapy Implications

When the masticator space is involved or at risk:
- The **CTV must include foramen ovale** and the entire course of V3 from the mental foramen to Meckel's cave
- If perineural spread is documented, the radiation field must be extended to the cavernous sinus
- IMRT planning is essential to minimise dose to temporal lobes and optic apparatus
- Dose: typically 60–66 Gy to the masticator space (with boost to gross disease up to 70 Gy)

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The masticator space is defined by the investing fascia splitting around the muscles of mastication
> 2. Contains: masseter, temporalis, pterygoids, mandibular ramus, V3, internal maxillary artery, pterygoid venous plexus
> 3. Communicates with middle cranial fossa via foramen ovale — a critical pathway for perineural spread
> 4. Trismus is the cardinal clinical sign of masticator space involvement
> 5. MRI with gadolinium is the imaging modality of choice — look for loss of T1 fat signal and enhancing V3
> 6. Masticator space involvement = T4b oral cavity cancer = Stage IVB (generally unresectable)
> 7. Numb chin syndrome in a cancer patient is an ominous sign requiring urgent MRI
> 8. Perineural spread along V3 can reach Meckel's cave and the cavernous sinus
> 9. Radiation fields must include foramen ovale to Meckel's cave when masticator space is involved
> 10. Curtin (1987) defined the radiological anatomy that separated masticator space from parapharyngeal space

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Trismus + V3 numbness in a patient with oral cancer = masticator space invasion until proven otherwise
> - On MRI, compare both sides — unilateral loss of fat signal in the masticator space confirms tumour infiltration
> - Denervation oedema (T2 hyperintensity) of masseter/temporalis on the affected side may be the earliest sign of perineural V3 involvement
> - The masticator space abscess is the most common non-neoplastic masticator space pathology — usually odontogenic; look for dental caries or periapical abscess on CT
> - When planning radiation for masticator space involvement, always trace the V3 pathway from the mental foramen through the mandibular canal, foramen ovale, and Meckel's cave — the entire pathway must be covered
