## Introduction

The infratemporal fossa (ITF) is a deep, irregularly shaped space situated between the lateral wall of the pharynx medially and the ramus of the mandible laterally, inferior to the greater wing of the sphenoid and the temporal bone. It serves as a critical crossroads for tumour spread between the oral cavity, paranasal sinuses, nasopharynx, parapharyngeal space, and middle cranial fossa. Involvement of the ITF by oral cavity or sinonasal tumours upstages the disease to T4b (very advanced local disease) in most [[Answers/Oral Cavity/AJCC 8th Edition Oral Cavity Staging|AJCC staging]] systems, profoundly affecting treatment planning and prognosis.

## Anatomy of the Infratemporal Fossa

### Boundaries

| Boundary | Structure |
|---|---|
| **Superior** | Greater wing of sphenoid, infratemporal surface of temporal bone |
| **Inferior** | Open (continuous with submandibular space) |
| **Medial** | Lateral pterygoid plate, pharyngeal wall |
| **Lateral** | Ramus of mandible, zygomatic arch |
| **Anterior** | Posterior wall of maxilla (infratemporal surface) |
| **Posterior** | Tympanic plate, styloid process, carotid sheath |

### Contents

- **Muscles:** Lateral and medial pterygoid muscles, lower part of temporalis
- **Nerves:** Mandibular division of trigeminal nerve (V3), inferior alveolar nerve, lingual nerve, buccal nerve, auriculotemporal nerve, chorda tympani
- **Vessels:** Internal maxillary artery and its branches, pterygoid venous plexus
- **Fat:** Buccal fat pad (extends into the ITF)

### Key Communications

| Connection | Pathway |
|---|---|
| Middle cranial fossa | Foramen ovale (V3), foramen spinosum (middle meningeal artery) |
| [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Pterygopalatine Fossa|pterygopalatine fossa]] | Pterygomaxillary fissure |
| Orbit | Inferior orbital fissure |
| Oral cavity | Gap between lateral pterygoid plate and mandible |
| Parapharyngeal space | Medial to medial pterygoid |
| Nasopharynx | Posterior to pterygoid plates |

## Routes of Tumour Spread into the ITF

### From Oral Cavity

- **Retromolar trigone tumours** → posteriorly through the pterygomandibular raphe → between medial pterygoid and ramus → ITF
- **Posterior alveolar/buccal tumours** → via buccal fat pad → ITF
- **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]]/tongue tumours** → posterolateral along the lingual nerve → ITF
- **Perineural spread** along the inferior alveolar nerve (V3) → mandibular foramen → ITF → foramen ovale → middle cranial fossa

### From Paranasal Sinuses

- **Maxillary sinus tumours** → through the posterior wall of the maxilla → ITF
- **Maxillary sinus T4a:** invades anterior orbital contents, skin of cheek, pterygoid plates, ITF, cribriform plate, sphenoid or frontal sinuses
- Extension from the pterygopalatine fossa through the pterygomaxillary fissure

### From Nasopharynx

- Lateral extension through the pharyngobasilar fascia → parapharyngeal space → ITF

## Clinical Features of ITF Involvement

- **[[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]]** — the hallmark clinical sign; caused by infiltration of pterygoid muscles (medial and lateral pterygoid)
- Pain — deep facial pain, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Referred Otalgia in Oral Cancer|referred otalgia]] (V3 involvement)
- Numbness — hypoaesthesia in V3 distribution (lower lip, chin)
- Cranial nerve deficits — V3 motor (masseter weakness), or extension to middle cranial fossa causing other CN palsies
- Swelling — fullness in the temporal or infratemporal region
- [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|dysphagia]] — if parapharyngeal space involved

## Imaging

### CT

- Best for bone involvement — erosion of pterygoid plates, posterior maxillary wall, skull base foramina
- Widening of foramen ovale or pterygomaxillary fissure suggests perineural spread

### MRI

- Superior for soft tissue delineation within the ITF
- **T1-weighted with gadolinium** — enhancing tumour vs fat (loss of normal fat signal in the ITF indicates tumour infiltration)
- **Perineural spread** — enhancing and thickened nerve, widened foramen, obliteration of Meckel's cave
- Can distinguish tumour from inflammatory change in pterygoid muscles

### [[Answers/General/PET-CT in Head and Neck Cancer|PET-CT]]

- Useful for detecting occult distant metastases in T4b disease
- Helps assess treatment response

## Staging Implications

ITF involvement has different staging significance depending on the primary site:

| Primary Site | ITF Involvement = | AJCC 8th Edition |
|---|---|---|
| Oral cavity | T4b | Masticator space, pterygoid plates, skull base, ICA encasement |
| Maxillary sinus | T4a | Through posterior wall |
| Nasopharynx | T4 | With intracranial extension |

T4b oral cavity cancers are generally considered **unresectable** by many guidelines, though selected centres offer surgery for carefully chosen patients.

## Surgical Approaches to the ITF

### 1. Maxillary Swing (Weber-Ferguson Approach)

**Best for:** Tumours on the **medial** aspect of the ITF, pterygomaxillary region, nasopharynx

**Principle:** The maxilla is osteotomised and swung laterally on its soft-tissue pedicle (preserving anterior and lateral attachments and blood supply), exposing the medial ITF.

**Technique (Jatin Shah):**
- Weber-Ferguson incision + sublabial extension
- Osteotomies of the palate, lateral nasal wall, and orbital floor
- Maxilla swung laterally, preserving alveolar process and blood supply
- Wide exposure of medial ITF, pterygomaxillary region, lateral nasopharynx
- After tumour resection, maxilla replaced and fixed

**Advantages:** Excellent medial ITF exposure; preserves alveolar process and facial contour
**Limitations:** Posterior maxillary wall may fracture; not suitable for laterally placed tumours

### 2. [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Access Mandibulotomy|access mandibulotomy]] (Lip-Split) Approach

**Best for:** Tumours in the **lateral** ITF between the mandibular ramus and lateral pterygoid plate

**Technique:**
- Midline lip-splitting incision + mandibulotomy between lateral incisor and canine
- Lateral swing of the hemi-mandible → wide exposure of lateral ITF
- Incision extends along floor of mouth to retromolar trigone, then into upper gingivobuccal sulcus

**Advantages:** Excellent lateral ITF access
**Limitations:** Mandibulotomy morbidity; risk to mental nerve, tooth roots

### 3. Cranio-Orbito-Zygomatic (COZ) Approach

**Best for:** Tumours involving the **floor of the middle cranial fossa** with ITF extension

**Technique:**
- Combined pterional craniotomy + zygomatic osteotomy
- Provides both intracranial (extradural) and ITF exposure
- Zygomatic arch disassembly gives access to deep temporal space

### 4. Fisch Infratemporal Fossa Approach (Types A–D)

Originally designed for temporal bone tumours but applicable to ITF:
- **Type A:** Glomus tumours of temporal bone
- **Type B:** Tumours of the petrous apex
- **Type C:** Tumours of the clivus and petrous apex (via ITF)
- **Type D:** Lateral extension of Type C

### 5. Endoscopic-Assisted Approaches

**Emerging role** in selected ITF pathology:
- Endoscopic medial maxillectomy + Denker's approach → access to anterior ITF
- Combined endoscopic + open approaches for improved visualisation
- Best suited for benign tumours (juvenile angiofibroma, schwannoma) or limited malignant extension

## Treatment Planning Considerations

### Resectable ITF Involvement

- Selected T4b oral cancers with limited ITF extension may be resectable with curative intent at high-volume centres
- Requires multidisciplinary planning (head and neck surgeon, neurosurgeon, reconstructive surgeon)
- Consider [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy in Oral Cancer|neoadjuvant chemotherapy]] to downstage before surgery
- Adjuvant chemoradiation is mandatory

### Unresectable ITF Involvement

- Extensive ITF involvement with ICA encasement, cavernous sinus invasion, or extensive middle cranial fossa involvement
- Treat with **definitive chemoradiation** (cisplatin-based)
- Consider induction chemotherapy (TPF regimen) followed by radiation ± [[Answers/General/Monoclonal Antibodies in Head and Neck Cancer|cetuximab]]

### Reconstruction After ITF Resection

- Large composite defects require free flap reconstruction
- **Anterolateral thigh (ALT) flap** — most commonly used for ITF dead-space obliteration
- **Rectus abdominis flap** — bulky flap for large defects
- **Temporalis muscle rotation** — for smaller defects, temporal bone coverage

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The ITF is a crossroads connecting oral cavity, sinuses, nasopharynx, parapharyngeal space, and middle cranial fossa
> 2. Trismus is the hallmark clinical sign of ITF involvement (pterygoid muscle infiltration)
> 3. ITF involvement upstages oral cancers to T4b (generally considered unresectable)
> 4. Main routes: retromolar trigone → pterygomandibular raphe, posterior maxillary wall (sinuses), perineural spread along V3
> 5. MRI with gadolinium is the imaging modality of choice for soft tissue and perineural assessment
> 6. Maxillary swing approach — best for medial ITF tumours (pterygomaxillary region)
> 7. Mandibulotomy approach — best for lateral ITF tumours
> 8. COZ approach — for combined intracranial and ITF lesions
> 9. Perineural spread along V3 can reach the middle cranial fossa through foramen ovale
> 10. Selected T4b cases may be offered surgery at high-volume centres with multidisciplinary expertise

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Always assess trismus preoperatively — limited mouth opening >grade 2 trismus strongly suggests pterygoid muscle invasion and ITF involvement
> - Numbness in the V3 distribution (lower lip, chin) may indicate perineural spread along the inferior alveolar nerve into the ITF
> - On MRI, loss of the normal high-signal fat in the ITF on T1-weighted images is the key sign of tumour infiltration
> - The pterygoid venous plexus in the ITF is a source of severe haemorrhage during surgery — have bipolar cautery and Surgicel readily available
> - The buccal fat pad serves as a conduit for tumour spread from the oral cavity into the ITF — assess it on imaging and remove it en bloc during surgery
