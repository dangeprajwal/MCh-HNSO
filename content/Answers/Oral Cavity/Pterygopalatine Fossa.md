## Introduction

The pterygopalatine fossa (PPF, also known as the pterygomaxillary fossa or sphenomaxillary fossa) is a small, fat-filled pyramidal space located deep in the face, posterior to the maxilla. It has been described as the "Piccadilly Circus" or "crossroads" of the deep face due to its extraordinary number of communications with surrounding anatomical compartments — the orbit, nasal cavity, oral cavity, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Infratemporal Fossa Spread|infratemporal fossa]], middle cranial fossa, and nasopharynx. In head and neck oncology, the PPF is critically important as a conduit for tumour spread (both direct invasion and perineural spread) from the oral cavity, paranasal sinuses, and nasopharynx to the skull base and intracranial cavity. Understanding its anatomy is essential for surgical planning, imaging interpretation, and radiation field design.

## Boundaries

| Wall | Bony Structure |
|---|---|
| Anterior | Posterior wall of maxilla (superomedial surface) |
| Posterior | Pterygoid process of sphenoid (root of medial pterygoid plate) |
| Medial | Perpendicular plate of palatine bone |
| Lateral | Open — communicates with infratemporal fossa via pterygomaxillary fissure |
| Superior | Inferior surface of body of sphenoid (greater wing) |
| Inferior | Tapers to the greater palatine canal |

The PPF is a narrow, inverted pyramidal space approximately 2 cm in height. Its lateral wall is the only boundary that is not bony — instead, it opens into the infratemporal fossa through the pterygomaxillary fissure. This open lateral wall is the route through which tumours and infections pass between the PPF and the infratemporal fossa.

## Communications

The PPF communicates with **eight** surrounding anatomical regions through specific foramina and fissures:

### Communications Table

| Communication | Foramen/Fissure | Structures Transmitted |
|---|---|---|
| Orbit | Inferior orbital fissure (IOF) | Infraorbital nerve (V2), infraorbital artery, inferior ophthalmic vein |
| Nasal cavity | Sphenopalatine foramen | Sphenopalatine artery, nasopalatine nerve, posterior superior lateral nasal nerves |
| Oral cavity (palate) | Greater and lesser palatine canals | Greater and lesser palatine nerves and vessels |
| Infratemporal fossa | Pterygomaxillary fissure | Internal maxillary artery (terminal part), posterior superior alveolar nerve |
| Middle cranial fossa | Foramen rotundum | Maxillary nerve (V2) |
| Middle cranial fossa | Vidian (pterygoid) canal | Vidian nerve (nerve of pterygoid canal), vidian artery |
| Pharynx | Pharyngeal (palatovaginal) canal | Pharyngeal nerve, pharyngeal artery |
| Cheek/face | Infraorbital canal → infraorbital foramen | Infraorbital nerve and vessels |

## Contents

### Nerves

1. **Maxillary nerve (V2)** — enters through foramen rotundum from the middle cranial fossa; the principal nerve of the PPF
2. **Pterygopalatine (sphenopalatine) ganglion** — a parasympathetic ganglion suspended from V2 by two ganglionic branches; receives:
   - Parasympathetic fibres via the vidian nerve (from greater superficial petrosal nerve → facial nerve)
   - Sympathetic fibres via the vidian nerve (from deep petrosal nerve → superior cervical ganglion)
   - Sensory fibres from V2

   Postganglionic parasympathetic fibres from the ganglion supply the lacrimal gland, nasal glands, and palatal glands

3. **Branches arising in the PPF:**
   - Zygomatic nerve → orbit via IOF
   - Posterior superior alveolar nerve → posterior teeth/maxillary sinus
   - Infraorbital nerve → orbit → face
   - Greater palatine nerve → hard palate
   - Lesser palatine nerve → soft palate
   - Nasopalatine nerve → nasal septum → incisive canal → anterior palate
   - Pharyngeal nerve → nasopharynx

### Arteries

- **Internal maxillary artery (third/pterygopalatine segment)** — the terminal portion enters the PPF through the pterygomaxillary fissure and gives off:
  - Posterior superior alveolar artery
  - Infraorbital artery
  - Descending palatine artery (→ greater and lesser palatine arteries)
  - Sphenopalatine artery (terminal branch — principal blood supply to the nasal cavity)
  - Pharyngeal artery
  - Artery of the vidian canal

### Veins

- Pterygoid venous plexus drains the PPF
- Inferior ophthalmic vein communicates through the IOF

### Fat

- The PPF is filled with fat, which serves as a natural contrast agent on both CT and MRI
- **Fat obliteration is the key imaging sign** of pathological PPF involvement — replacement of normal bright fat signal by tumour on T1-weighted MRI is the most reliable indicator of PPF invasion (Curtin 1985)

## Oncological Significance

### Routes of Tumour Spread Through the PPF

The PPF is a critical junction through which tumours from multiple primary sites can access the skull base:

**1. Direct Invasion**
- Maxillary sinus carcinoma → destroys posterior wall of maxilla → invades PPF
- Nasopharyngeal carcinoma → spreads laterally into PPF
- Oral cavity tumours (hard palate, upper alveolus) → ascend through greater palatine canal into PPF
- Infratemporal fossa tumours → enter PPF through pterygomaxillary fissure

**2. Perineural Spread (PNS)**
- The most clinically significant route of PPF involvement
- Tumours of the palate, maxillary sinus, and face spread along branches of V2 to the PPF
- From the PPF, perineural spread continues retrograde along V2 through foramen rotundum to Meckel's cave and the cavernous sinus → intracranial spread
- **Pathway:** Palatal nerve or infraorbital nerve → PPF → V2 → foramen rotundum → Meckel's cave → cavernous sinus

### Primary Sites That Spread Through the PPF

- **Maxillary sinus** — the most common source of PPF invasion (direct + PNS)
- **Hard palate** — via greater palatine canal (PNS along greater palatine nerve)
- **Upper alveolus/gingiva** — via posterior superior alveolar nerve to PPF
- **Nasopharynx** — direct lateral extension
- **Cutaneous malignancies of the midface** — PNS along infraorbital nerve retrograde to PPF
- **Adenoid cystic carcinoma** — highest propensity for PNS among all head and neck malignancies

### Staging Implications

- PPF invasion by maxillary sinus carcinoma → **T4a** ([[Answers/Oral Cavity/AJCC 8th Edition Oral Cavity Staging|AJCC 8th edition staging]] edition)
- PPF involvement represents a critical threshold — tumours reaching the PPF can access the middle cranial fossa via foramen rotundum
- Intracranial extension via the PPF → **T4b** (unresectable by conventional criteria)

## Imaging of the PPF

### CT Features

- PPF appears as a fat-filled space posterior to the maxilla on axial images
- **Widening of the PPF** and **loss of normal fat density** indicate tumour invasion
- Widening of the foramen rotundum or vidian canal suggests perineural spread
- **Bone algorithm** — assess integrity of the bony boundaries, widening of foramina

### MRI Features (Preferred for PPF Assessment)

- **T1-weighted (pre-contrast):** Normal PPF appears bright (high signal) due to fat content; tumour appears as loss of this bright fat signal
- **T1-weighted (post-contrast with fat saturation):** Enhancing tumour within the PPF is clearly delineated against suppressed fat
- **Key signs of PNS:** Enlargement and enhancement of the nerve, widening of the foramen, denervation changes in muscles supplied by the affected nerve
- Compare with the contralateral normal PPF — asymmetry of fat content is an important clue

### Imaging Checklist for PPF Assessment

1. Is the normal fat preserved? (T1-weighted)
2. Is there abnormal enhancement? (post-contrast T1 with fat saturation)
3. Is the foramen rotundum widened? (coronal CT bone algorithm)
4. Is the vidian canal widened?
5. Is there extension into Meckel's cave? (contrast-enhanced T1, coronal)
6. Compare with contralateral PPF

## Surgical Approaches to the PPF

### 1. Maxillary Swing Approach (Maxillotomy)

- The workhorse open approach for PPF and infratemporal fossa tumours
- Weber-Fergusson incision → osteotomy of the maxilla → swing of maxilla laterally → direct access to PPF
- Provides excellent exposure of the PPF, infratemporal fossa, and nasopharynx

### 2. Endoscopic Endonasal Transpterygoid Approach

- Increasingly used for PPF lesions
- Access through the middle meatus → sphenopalatine foramen → PPF
- Posterior wall of the maxillary sinus removed to widen access
- Can be extended to the infratemporal fossa, Meckel's cave, and petrous apex
- Advantages: no external incision, lower morbidity, excellent visualisation
- Limitation: reduced ability to manage large lateral or inferior extensions

### 3. Sublabial Transmaxillary Approach (Caldwell-Luc)

- Approach through the maxillary sinus via sublabial incision
- Posterior wall of maxillary sinus removed to access PPF
- Limited exposure; largely replaced by endoscopic techniques

### 4. Combined Open + Endoscopic Approach

- For extensive tumours involving both the PPF and adjacent compartments
- Combines the wide exposure of open surgery with the detailed visualisation of endoscopy

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The PPF communicates with 8 anatomical regions — orbit (IOF), nasal cavity (sphenopalatine foramen), palate (greater palatine canal), ITF (pterygomaxillary fissure), middle cranial fossa (foramen rotundum, vidian canal), pharynx (pharyngeal canal), face (infraorbital canal)
> 2. Key contents: V2 (maxillary nerve), pterygopalatine ganglion, internal maxillary artery (third part), fat
> 3. Perineural spread along V2 is the most clinically significant route of PPF involvement
> 4. Pathway: palatal/infraorbital nerve → PPF → V2 → foramen rotundum → Meckel's cave → cavernous sinus
> 5. Fat obliteration on T1-weighted MRI is the key imaging sign of PPF invasion (Curtin 1985)
> 6. Maxillary sinus carcinoma is the most common tumour to invade the PPF
> 7. Adenoid cystic carcinoma has the highest propensity for perineural spread to the PPF
> 8. PPF invasion by maxillary sinus tumour = T4a; intracranial extension = T4b
> 9. MRI with contrast and fat saturation is the gold standard for PPF assessment
> 10. Always compare with the contralateral normal PPF on imaging — asymmetry is a crucial diagnostic clue

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The PPF is the "Grand Central Station" of the deep face — understand its communications and you understand how disease spreads from the face to the brain
> - On MRI, a normal PPF is bright on T1 (fat). If one side is dark and the other is bright, suspect tumour — this is the single most important PPF imaging sign
> - Adenoid cystic carcinoma of the palate is the classic tumour that spreads perineurally through the greater palatine canal to the PPF and then to the skull base — always image the PPF in palatal ACC
> - Widening of the foramen rotundum on CT bone algorithm is a subtle but critical sign of perineural spread — compare both sides
> - The endoscopic endonasal transpterygoid approach has revolutionised access to the PPF — but for large tumours with lateral extension into the infratemporal fossa, a combined open-endoscopic approach may still be needed
