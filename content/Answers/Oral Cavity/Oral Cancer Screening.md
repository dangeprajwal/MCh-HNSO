## Introduction

Oral cancer is one of the few cancers where the primary site is directly accessible to visual examination, making it theoretically amenable to screening. Despite this, approximately 60% of oral cancers in developed countries and >70% in developing countries are diagnosed at an advanced stage (Stage III/IV), largely because early lesions are painless and patients delay seeking care. Oral cancer screening aims to detect cancers and potentially malignant disorders at an early, curable stage. The rationale is strongest in populations with high prevalence of tobacco and areca nut use (South and Southeast Asia), where oral cancer is among the most common malignancies.

## Types of Screening

### Population-Based (Mass) Screening

- Screening of an entire population or large demographic group regardless of risk
- **Not recommended** for oral cancer by any guideline body due to low prevalence in most Western populations
- The low positive predictive value in low-risk populations leads to many false positives

### Targeted (High-Risk) Screening

- Screening focused on individuals with known risk factors
- **Recommended approach** for oral cancer
- Target groups: tobacco users, areca/betel quid chewers, heavy alcohol users, patients with known OPMDs, prior oral cancer
- The Kerala trial demonstrated benefit specifically in tobacco/alcohol users

### Opportunistic Screening

- Examination performed during routine dental or medical visits
- Recommended by ACS and ADA as part of routine oral examination
- No evidence of mortality reduction from opportunistic screening alone, but low cost and potential for early detection

## The Kerala Trial (Sankaranarayanan et al. 2005)

This is the **only Level I evidence** for oral cancer screening:

- **Design:** Cluster-randomised controlled trial in Trivandrum, Kerala, India
- **Population:** 191,873 individuals (96,517 intervention, 95,356 control)
- **Intervention:** Three rounds of visual oral examination by trained health workers over 9 years
- **Compliance:** 53% of intervention group screened at least once
- **Results:**
  - **No significant mortality reduction** in the overall intention-to-treat analysis (HR 0.79, 95% CI 0.51–1.22)
  - **Significant 24% mortality reduction** among tobacco/alcohol users in the intervention arm (HR 0.66, 95% CI 0.45–0.95)
  - Stage shift: more early-stage cancers detected in the screening arm
  - Sensitivity of visual examination: 67.4%; specificity: 99.3%
- **Conclusion:** Visual screening reduces oral cancer mortality in **high-risk populations** (tobacco/alcohol users) but not in the general population

## Visual Oral Examination (VOE)

### Technique

- Systematic examination of all oral mucosal surfaces under good illumination
- Patient seated, dentures removed
- Bimanual palpation of floor of mouth and tongue
- Assessment of lips, buccal mucosa, gingivae, hard and soft palate, tongue (dorsal, ventral, lateral borders), floor of mouth, retromolar trigone, tonsillar pillars

### Performance

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Sensitivity | 60–97% (varies with training) |
| Specificity | 75–99% |
| PPV in high-risk population | 4–40% |
| PPV in low-risk population | <5% |

### Oral Potentially Malignant Disorders (OPMDs)

The WHO (Warnakulasuriya 2007) recommends the term OPMD for conditions carrying increased risk of malignant transformation:

| OPMD | Transformation Rate |
|---|---|
| Leukoplakia | 1–5% (varies with type) |
| Erythroplakia | 14–50% (highest risk) |
| [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Lichen Planus|Oral lichen planus]] | ~1% |
| Oral submucous fibrosis | 3–8% |
| Actinic cheilitis | 10–20% |
| Dyskeratosis congenita | Variable |

## Adjunctive Screening Technologies

### 1. Vital Staining — Toluidine Blue (Tolonium Chloride)

- **Principle:** Metachromatic dye preferentially stains DNA of dysplastic/malignant cells (which have increased nuclear:cytoplasmic ratio)
- **Technique:** 1% toluidine blue solution applied to mucosa → rinse → retained blue staining marks suspicious areas
- **Sensitivity:** 72–100%; **Specificity:** 45–67%
- **Limitations:** False positives with inflammation, ulceration, infection
- **Role:** Useful as an adjunct to guide biopsy site selection, not as a standalone screening tool

### 2. Autofluorescence (Tissue Fluorescence Imaging)

- **VELscope** (Visually Enhanced Lesion Scope) — the most studied device
- **Principle:** Normal mucosa emits green autofluorescence when illuminated by blue-violet light (400–460 nm); dysplastic/malignant tissue shows **loss of fluorescence** (appears dark)
- **Mechanism:** Disruption of flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) and collagen cross-links in dysplastic tissue
- **Sensitivity:** 84–100%; **Specificity:** 15–83% (high false positive rate)
- **Limitations:** Inflammation, pigmented lesions, vascular lesions all cause loss of fluorescence → poor specificity
- **Role:** May help delineate extent of field change; useful for margin assessment intraoperatively

### 3. Narrow Band Imaging (NBI)

- **Principle:** Filters endoscopic light to specific wavelengths (415 nm blue, 540 nm green) that are absorbed by haemoglobin → enhances visualisation of superficial mucosal vasculature
- **Application:** Identifies irregular vascular patterns (intrapapillary capillary loops — IPCLs) associated with dysplasia/early cancer
- **Primarily used** in oropharynx, hypopharynx, and larynx; emerging role in oral cavity
- **Sensitivity:** 95%; **Specificity:** 97% (in oropharyngeal/laryngeal screening)

### 4. Brush Biopsy (OralCDx)

- **Technique:** Transepithelial brush sampling of suspicious lesion → computer-assisted cytological analysis
- **Principle:** Full-thickness epithelial cells collected by rotating a stiff brush → analysed for atypical cells
- **Sensitivity:** 71–100%; **Specificity:** 32–93%
- **Role:** Useful for lesions in low-risk patients where the clinical threshold for incisional biopsy is not met
- **Limitation:** A positive brush biopsy still requires confirmatory incisional biopsy

### 5. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

- Non-invasive cross-sectional imaging of mucosal microstructure
- Can identify epithelial thickening, basement membrane disruption
- Resolution: 10–20 μm (comparable to low-power histology)
- **Emerging technology** — not yet validated for screening

### 6. Salivary Biomarkers

- Analysis of saliva for tumour-associated markers
- Markers studied: IL-8, IL-6, TNF-α, VEGF, p53 antibodies, CD44, exosomal miRNA
- **Emerging field** — no validated salivary diagnostic test exists yet for routine screening
- Potential for a non-invasive, low-cost screening tool in resource-limited settings

## Oral Cancer Screening in India

India has one of the highest burdens of oral cancer worldwide:
- Oral cavity is the most common cancer in Indian men (~30% of all cancers)
- Strong association with tobacco chewing, areca nut, and betel quid
- Most patients present at advanced stages

### Government Initiatives

- **National Oral Health Programme** — includes oral cancer screening component
- Community health workers trained in visual oral examination
- Integration with primary health care centres
- Focus on tobacco cessation counselling alongside screening

### Challenges

- Low awareness of early signs among both public and primary care providers
- Limited access to specialist centres for biopsy and treatment
- Sociocultural acceptance of tobacco/betel chewing
- Need for infrastructure to support follow-up of screen-positive individuals

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The Kerala trial (Sankaranarayanan 2005) is the only RCT showing oral cancer screening reduces mortality — specifically in tobacco/alcohol users (24% reduction)
> 2. Visual oral examination is the primary screening method — sensitivity 60–97%, specificity 75–99%
> 3. Targeted screening of high-risk groups is recommended; mass population screening is not
> 4. OPMDs (Warnakulasuriya 2007): leukoplakia, erythroplakia, OLP, OSMF, actinic cheilitis
> 5. Erythroplakia has the highest malignant transformation rate (14–50%)
> 6. Toluidine blue improves biopsy site selection but has poor specificity as a standalone tool
> 7. VELscope autofluorescence: high sensitivity but poor specificity (many false positives)
> 8. Narrow band imaging enhances mucosal vascular patterns — high accuracy in pharynx/larynx
> 9. Brush biopsy is useful when the threshold for incisional biopsy is not met — but positive results still need biopsy confirmation
> 10. Salivary biomarkers and OCT are emerging technologies not yet validated for routine use

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - In India, all patients with tobacco/areca nut habits should receive a systematic visual oral examination at every healthcare contact — this is the single most impactful screening intervention
> - Erythroplakia (bright red velvety patch) has a much higher transformation rate than leukoplakia — biopsy immediately, do not observe
> - Toluidine blue is most useful for guiding biopsy in a patient with widespread mucosal change — it highlights the most dysplastic areas
> - VELscope is better for delineating the extent of field change than for initial screening — high false positive rate limits its standalone screening value
> - A negative brush biopsy does NOT exclude malignancy — incisional biopsy remains the gold standard for any clinically suspicious lesion
