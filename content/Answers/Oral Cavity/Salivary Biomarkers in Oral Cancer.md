> [!question] Questions Covered
> - **OC Q102:** The current role of salivary biomarkers in the diagnosis of premalignant and malignant lesions of the oral cavity (TMH 2019)

## Source

Harrison LB et al. *Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach*. 4th ed. Lippincott; 2014. Ch1; Montgomery PQ et al. *Principles and Practice of Head and Neck Surgery*. 2nd ed. CRC Press; 2009. Ch2

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Mao et al. (1996) demonstrated frequent microsatellite alterations (LOH) at 9p21 and 3p14 in oral premalignant lesions, with 37% of patients with LOH progressing to HNSCC versus 6% without, establishing LOH analysis in oral rinse/saliva DNA as a cancer risk assessment tool. Li et al. (2004) identified seven salivary mRNA biomarkers (IL-8, IL-1β, SAT, OAZ1) distinguishing OSCC from controls with 91% sensitivity and 91% specificity (AUC 0.95), the foundational study for salivary transcriptomics. Nagler et al. (2006) demonstrated ~400% increase in salivary Cyfra 21-1 in OSCC patients with combined tumour marker analysis yielding 71% sensitivity and 75% specificity. Hu et al. (2008) used shotgun proteomics to identify five salivary protein biomarkers for OSCC (AUC 0.93, 90% sensitivity, 83% specificity). Park et al. (2009) published the first study characterising salivary miRNAs for OSCC detection, finding miR-125a and miR-200a significantly reduced in OSCC patients.

## Overview

Saliva is an increasingly recognised **"liquid biopsy"** medium that contains DNA, RNA, proteins, metabolites, and exosomes reflecting the molecular status of oral mucosa. The proximity of saliva to oral lesions makes it an ideal source for biomarker discovery in oral premalignant and malignant conditions.

### Advantages of Salivary Diagnostics

| Advantage | Detail |
|---|---|
| Non-invasive collection | No needles, no anaesthesia, patient-friendly |
| Repeatable sampling | Enables longitudinal monitoring |
| Proximity to lesion | Direct contact with tumour-derived molecules |
| Low cost | Minimal processing equipment needed |
| Point-of-care potential | Lab-on-a-chip technology feasible |

## Categories of Salivary Biomarkers

### 1. DNA-Based Biomarkers

| Biomarker | Detection Method | Application |
|---|---|---|
| TP53 mutations | [[Answers/General/PCR and Microarray Analysis|PCR]], [[Answers/General/Next Generation Sequencing in HNC|NGS]] | Tumour-specific mutations in saliva |
| Promoter hypermethylation (p16, DAPK, RASSF1A) | Methylation-specific PCR | Early detection; [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Field Cancerization|field cancerization]] |
| Microsatellite instability | PCR | Genomic instability marker |
| Mitochondrial DNA mutations | PCR | Elevated in OSCC vs controls |
| HPV DNA | PCR, in situ hybridisation | Oropharyngeal cancer detection |
| Loss of heterozygosity (LOH) | Microsatellite analysis | 3p, 9p, 17p LOH in [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Leukoplakia Management|oral leukoplakia]] |

**LOH at 3p and 9p** in salivary DNA from oral leukoplakia patients predicts malignant transformation — this is one of the most clinically validated salivary biomarkers.

### 2. RNA-Based Biomarkers

| Biomarker | Finding in OSCC | Sensitivity/Specificity |
|---|---|---|
| IL-8 mRNA | Elevated | 88%/87% |
| IL-1β mRNA | Elevated | 80%/70% |
| SAT mRNA | Elevated | 65%/83% |
| H3F3A mRNA | Elevated | 71%/71% |
| Combination panel (4 mRNAs) | — | 91%/91% |
| [[Answers/General/MicroRNAs in Oral Cancer|miR-21]] | Elevated | 85%/80% |
| miR-31 | Elevated | 80%/68% |
| miR-125b | Decreased | 75%/70% |

The **combination of IL-8, IL-1β, SAT, and H3F3A mRNAs** achieves the highest diagnostic accuracy for OSCC (91% sensitivity and specificity).

### 3. Protein Biomarkers

| Protein | Role | Finding in OSCC |
|---|---|---|
| IL-6 | Pro-inflammatory cytokine | Elevated (2–5× normal) |
| IL-8 | Angiogenic chemokine | Elevated |
| TNF-α | Inflammation marker | Elevated |
| Cyfra 21-1 (cytokeratin 19 fragment) | Epithelial cell marker | Elevated; correlates with tumour burden |
| CD44 | Cell adhesion glycoprotein | Soluble CD44 elevated |
| MMP-9 | Matrix metalloproteinase | Elevated; invasion marker |
| EGFR | Growth factor receptor | Soluble EGFR elevated |
| Endothelin-1 | Vasoactive peptide | Elevated |
| Galectin-3 | Carbohydrate-binding protein | Elevated |

**Cyfra 21-1** is one of the most extensively validated salivary protein biomarkers — elevated in OSCC with sensitivity ~70% and specificity ~75%.

### 4. Metabolomic Biomarkers

| Metabolite | Alteration | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Choline | Elevated | Membrane turnover |
| Polyamines (putrescine, spermine) | Elevated | Cell proliferation |
| Valine, phenylalanine | Altered | Amino acid metabolism disrupted |
| Lactic acid | Elevated | Warburg effect (anaerobic glycolysis) |

### 5. Exosome-Based Biomarkers

Salivary exosomes (30–150 nm extracellular vesicles) contain tumour-derived:
- Proteins (CD63, CD9, TSG101)
- mRNAs and miRNAs
- DNA fragments

Exosomal cargo is protected from salivary nucleases, making it more stable than free nucleic acids.

## Biomarkers for Specific Clinical Questions

### Screening and Early Detection

| Biomarker | Best For | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| Salivary IL-8 + IL-1β mRNA | OSCC detection | Phase II validation |
| Salivary miR-21, miR-31 | OSCC screening | Multiple cohort studies |
| LOH at 3p/9p in saliva | Leukoplakia transformation risk | Prospective validation |
| Cyfra 21-1 | Tumour burden assessment | Multi-centre studies |

### Monitoring Premalignant Lesions

- **LOH analysis** in salivary DNA from patients with oral leukoplakia can identify those at highest risk of malignant transformation
- Methylation markers (p16, DAPK) detected in saliva correlate with dysplasia grade
- Serial salivary sampling allows **non-invasive longitudinal surveillance**

### Post-Treatment Surveillance

- Declining salivary biomarker levels after treatment suggest disease control
- Rising levels may indicate recurrence before clinical detection
- Salivary ctDNA (circulating tumour DNA) for TP53 mutations is under investigation

## Validation Status

| Biomarker Category | Discovery Phase | Validation Phase | Clinical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| mRNA panels | Complete | Multi-centre ongoing | Not yet standard |
| miRNA panels | Complete | Small cohort validation | Not yet standard |
| Protein markers (Cyfra 21-1, IL-6) | Complete | Moderate validation | Investigational |
| LOH (3p, 9p) | Complete | Prospective studies | Near-clinical |
| Metabolomics | Early | Limited validation | Experimental |
| Exosomal markers | Early | Preclinical | Experimental |

### Point-of-Care Technology

- **EFIRM (Electric Field-Induced Release and Measurement):** Developed at UCLA; detects salivary biomarkers in 10 minutes using a microfluidic chip
- **Lab-on-a-chip:** Integrates sample processing and detection on a single device
- Goal: chairside testing in dental clinics for oral cancer screening

## Current Limitations

| Limitation | Impact |
|---|---|
| No FDA-approved salivary cancer biomarker | Cannot be used for clinical decision-making |
| Variability in collection methods | Affects reproducibility |
| Diurnal variation in saliva composition | Standardisation needed |
| Confounders (smoking, alcohol, food, oral hygiene) | May alter biomarker levels |
| Small validation cohorts | Most studies <200 patients |
| Lack of multi-centre prospective trials | Evidence remains Level III–IV |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Saliva is a non-invasive "liquid biopsy" containing DNA, RNA, proteins, metabolites, and exosomes reflecting oral mucosal status
> 2. Combination of salivary IL-8, IL-1β, SAT, and H3F3A mRNAs achieves 91% sensitivity and specificity for OSCC detection
> 3. Salivary miR-21 and miR-31 are the most studied miRNA biomarkers — elevated in OSCC with sensitivity 80–85%
> 4. LOH at 3p and 9p in salivary DNA from leukoplakia patients predicts malignant transformation — one of the most clinically validated markers
> 5. Cyfra 21-1 is the most extensively validated salivary protein biomarker for OSCC (~70% sensitivity, 75% specificity)
> 6. Salivary exosomes contain protected tumour-derived cargo — more stable than free nucleic acids
> 7. No salivary biomarker is currently FDA-approved or standard of care for oral cancer diagnosis
> 8. EFIRM and lab-on-a-chip technology may enable point-of-care chairside testing in dental clinics
> 9. Confounders (smoking, alcohol, food, oral hygiene, diurnal variation) affect salivary biomarker levels
> 10. Multi-centre prospective validation trials are needed before salivary biomarkers enter clinical practice

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **"Spit don't cut"** — salivary diagnostics aim to replace tissue biopsy for screening and surveillance, not for definitive diagnosis (biopsy remains gold standard)
> 2. For the exam, the **4-mRNA panel** (IL-8, IL-1β, SAT, H3F3A) with 91% sensitivity/specificity is the most quotable figure
> 3. **LOH at 3p/9p** in salivary DNA is the strongest predictor of leukoplakia transformation — the same molecular marker used in field cancerization studies
> 4. The practical application closest to clinical reality is **post-treatment surveillance** — serial salivary sampling to detect recurrence before clinical manifestation
> 5. When asked about "advances in oral cancer screening," salivary biomarkers should be discussed alongside [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Cancer Screening|clinical screening]], VELscope, and [[Answers/General/Narrow Band Imaging|NBI]]
