## Introduction

Surgery is the primary treatment for oral cavity squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC). Adjuvant (postoperative) radiotherapy (RT) ± concurrent chemotherapy is added when pathological features indicate a significant risk of locoregional recurrence. The decision to recommend adjuvant therapy is one of the most consequential in oral cancer management — it can halve recurrence rates but carries significant morbidity. This answer covers the indications, evidence, technique, and the emerging concept of DARS-sparing RT.

## Indications for Adjuvant Therapy

### Adjuvant RT Alone — Intermediate Risk

Postoperative RT alone (60–66 Gy) is indicated when one or more of the following **intermediate-risk features** are present:

- pT3 or pT4a tumour
- [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Depth of Invasion in Oral Cancer|Depth of invasion]] >5 mm
- Close margins (1–5 mm)
- [[Answers/General/Perineural Invasion|perineural invasion]] (PNI)
- Lymphovascular invasion (LVI)
- pN1 disease (single positive node without ENE)
- Multiple positive nodes without ENE

### Adjuvant Concurrent Chemoradiation — High Risk

Postoperative concurrent chemoradiation (RT + cisplatin 100 mg/m² days 1, 22, 43) is indicated for **high-risk features**:

- **Positive (involved) [[Answers/General/Surgical Margins in Head and Neck Cancer|surgical margins]]**
- **Extranodal extension (ENE)**

These are the only two features for which the RTOG 9501/EORTC 22931 combined analysis (Bernier 2005) demonstrated a consistent benefit of adding chemotherapy to radiation.

### The Bernier 2005 Combined Analysis — Critical Evidence

| Feature | Benefit of Adding Chemo to RT |
|---|---|
| Positive margins | **Yes** — significant improvement |
| Extranodal extension | **Yes** — significant improvement |
| ≥2 positive nodes (without ENE) | Trend only — not statistically significant |
| PNI alone | No significant benefit from adding chemo |
| pT3–T4 alone | No significant benefit from adding chemo |

**Exam pearl:** The Bernier 2005 combined analysis is the most important paper for this topic. It established that **only positive margins and ENE** are indications for concurrent chemoRT (not PNI, LVI, or node positivity alone).

## Evidence Base

### RTOG 9501 (Cooper 2004)

- 459 patients with high-risk resected HNSCC
- Randomised: RT alone (60–66 Gy) vs RT + cisplatin (100 mg/m² × 3 cycles)
- **Results:** ChemoRT improved locoregional control (HR 0.61) and DFS (HR 0.78); no OS benefit
- High-risk defined as: ≥2 positive nodes, ENE, or positive margins

### EORTC 22931 (Bernier 2004)

- 334 patients with high-risk resected HNSCC
- Same design: RT alone vs RT + cisplatin
- **Results:** ChemoRT improved PFS (HR 0.75), locoregional control (HR 0.69), and **OS** (HR 0.70)
- Broader high-risk criteria: T3–T4, pN2–N3, PNI, LVI, ENE, positive margins

### Combined Analysis (Bernier 2005)

- Pooled data identified that only **positive margins and ENE** consistently benefited
- Other features showed trends but did not reach significance for the added benefit of chemotherapy
- This paper fundamentally changed practice — chemoRT is now reserved for these two high-risk features

## Technical Aspects

### Dose and Fractionation

| Target | Dose | Fractionation |
|---|---|---|
| Tumour bed (high risk) | 60–66 Gy | 2 Gy/fraction, 5 days/week |
| Involved nodal regions | 60–66 Gy | Same |
| Elective nodal regions | 50–54 Gy | Same or simultaneous integrated boost |

Peters 1993 established that **minimum 57.6 Gy** (1.8 Gy fractions) is needed for the tumour bed, with a boost to 63 Gy for high-risk regions (positive margins, ENE).

### Timing

- Adjuvant RT should commence **within 6 weeks of surgery** — delays beyond 6 weeks are associated with significantly worse outcomes
- The total "package time" (surgery to completion of RT) should ideally be **<11 weeks**
- Prolonged wound healing, complications, and delayed referral are the main reasons for exceeding this window

### IMRT vs Conventional RT

- **IMRT is the standard of care** for adjuvant RT in oral cavity cancer
- Benefits: better dose conformality, reduced dose to mandible (lower [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Osteoradionecrosis of the Mandible|ORN]] risk), reduced dose to contralateral parotid (preserves salivary function), ability to spare DARS
- Volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT) is a further refinement with faster delivery

## DARS-Sparing Radiotherapy

### Concept

Dysphagia/aspiration-related structures (DARS) are the anatomical structures whose radiation damage causes [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]] dysfunction. Eisbruch et al. (2004) identified these structures and demonstrated that IMRT could reduce dose to them without compromising tumour coverage.

### Key DARS Structures

| Structure | Function | Dose Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Pharyngeal constrictors (superior, middle, inferior) | Pharyngeal propulsion | Mean dose <50 Gy |
| Supraglottic larynx | Airway protection, epiglottic tilt | Mean dose <50 Gy |
| Glottic larynx | Vocal cord adduction for airway protection | Mean dose <45 Gy |
| Cricopharyngeus (UES) | Upper oesophageal sphincter relaxation | Mean dose <50 Gy |
| Base of tongue | Bolus propulsion | Mean dose <50 Gy |

### Rationale

- Radiation-induced dysphagia is one of the most debilitating late effects of head and neck RT
- Late dysphagia occurs in 30–50% of patients after definitive or adjuvant RT
- Mechanisms: fibrosis of constrictors, reduced laryngeal elevation, impaired epiglottic tilt, oedema
- See [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Swallowing Assessment and Rehabilitation|Swallowing Assessment and Rehabilitation]] for detailed management

### Application in Oral Cavity Cancer

- In adjuvant RT for oral cavity cancer, DARS-sparing is particularly relevant when the **pharyngeal constrictors and supraglottic larynx** are not within the primary tumour bed
- For anterior oral cavity tumours (lip, anterior tongue, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]]), DARS can often be spared effectively
- For posterior oral cavity/oropharyngeal tumours, DARS are often within or adjacent to the CTV, limiting sparing ability
- Prophylactic swallowing exercises during RT further reduce dysphagia risk (see Carnaby-Mann 2012)

### Current Status

DARS-sparing RT is increasingly standard practice where technically feasible. Several prospective trials have demonstrated that:
- DARS-optimised IMRT reduces mean pharyngeal constrictor doses by 5–10 Gy
- This translates to clinically meaningful improvements in swallowing function at 6–12 months
- There is no compromise in tumour control when DARS constraints are applied judiciously

## Specific Considerations for Oral Cavity

### Why Oral Cavity Is Different From Other HN Subsites

1. **Surgery first** — unlike oropharynx or larynx, the primary treatment for oral cavity SCC is surgery, not RT. RT is adjuvant
2. **No role for definitive RT** as primary treatment for resectable oral cavity SCC (inferior outcomes compared to surgery)
3. **Mandible dose** is a critical concern — IMRT helps reduce dose to the contralateral mandible and minimise ORN risk
4. **Dental planning** — pre-RT dental assessment is essential (see [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Osteoradionecrosis of the Mandible|ORN]])
5. **[[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]] risk** — dose to the pterygoid muscles and TMJ should be minimised

### When NOT to Give Adjuvant RT

- pT1–T2 N0 with clear margins (≥5 mm), no PNI, no LVI, and DOI <5 mm
- These low-risk patients have excellent outcomes with surgery alone
- Adding RT in low-risk patients adds morbidity without proven benefit

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Adjuvant RT (60–66 Gy) is indicated for intermediate-risk features: pT3–T4, DOI >5 mm, close margins, PNI, LVI, pN+
> 2. Adjuvant concurrent chemoRT (cisplatin 100 mg/m² × 3) is reserved for high-risk features: positive margins and ENE only
> 3. RTOG 9501 (Cooper 2004) and EORTC 22931 (Bernier 2004) are the two landmark trials establishing level I evidence for postoperative chemoRT
> 4. Bernier 2005 combined analysis: only positive margins and ENE consistently benefit from adding cisplatin — this is the key exam evidence
> 5. Peters 1993: minimum dose 57.6 Gy; boost to 63 Gy for high-risk areas; start within 6 weeks of surgery
> 6. Total package time (surgery → completion of RT) should be <11 weeks for optimal outcomes
> 7. IMRT is standard of care — reduces ORN risk, preserves salivary function, enables DARS sparing
> 8. DARS include pharyngeal constrictors, supraglottic larynx, glottic larynx, cricopharyngeus, and base of tongue
> 9. DARS-sparing is most feasible for anterior oral cavity tumours; posterior tumours limit sparing ability
> 10. No role for definitive (primary) RT in resectable oral cavity SCC — surgery is always first-line treatment

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The single most testable fact: positive margins and ENE → chemoRT; everything else → RT alone. This comes from Bernier 2005 combined analysis
> - Timing matters: adjuvant RT should start within 6 weeks. If your patient has a complicated wound or fistula, involve radiation oncology early to plan around healing delays
> - For oral cavity cancer specifically, always mention that surgery is first-line — unlike oropharynx or larynx where primary RT/CRT is an option. Examiners test this distinction
> - DARS-sparing RT is increasingly expected in answers about functional outcomes and [[Answers/General/Quality of Life in Head and Neck Cancer|quality of life]] — mention pharyngeal constrictor dose <50 Gy as the key constraint
> - Pre-RT dental assessment to prevent ORN is a mandatory step that should appear in any complete answer about adjuvant RT in oral cancer
