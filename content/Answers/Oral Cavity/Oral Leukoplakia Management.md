## Introduction

Oral leukoplakia is defined as a **white plaque of questionable risk having excluded other known diseases or disorders that carry no increased risk for cancer** (WHO 2005). It is the most common oral potentially malignant disorder (OPMD), with a prevalence of 2–4% in the adult population and a pooled malignant transformation rate of approximately **3.5%** (range 0.13–34% depending on study design and follow-up). The TMH 2019 question specifically asks for a management schema based on location, morphology, dysplasia grade, and other factors — this answer provides a risk-stratified management algorithm.

## Clinical Classification

### By Morphology (Clinical Appearance)

| Type | Description | Transformation Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Homogeneous | Uniformly white, flat, thin | Low (~1–5%) |
| Non-homogeneous: speckled | Mixed white and red (erythroleukoplakia) | High (~20–25%) |
| Non-homogeneous: nodular | Raised, granular surface | High |
| Non-homogeneous: verrucous | Warty, corrugated surface | Moderate–High |
| Proliferative verrucous leukoplakia (PVL) | Multifocal, progressive, resistant to treatment | Very high (~70%) |

**Non-homogeneous leukoplakia has a 4–5× higher transformation risk than homogeneous type** (RR 4.23, Warnakulasuriya 2016).

### By Location

| Site | Transformation Risk |
|---|---|
| Lateral tongue | Highest (~12.7%) |
| [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]] | High |
| Ventral tongue | High |
| Soft palate | Moderate |
| Buccal mucosa | Low–Moderate |
| Hard palate/gingiva | Low |

Tongue and floor of mouth ("high-risk sites") together account for the majority of malignant transformations.

## [[Answers/General/Histological Grading of SCC|histological grading]] of Dysplasia

### WHO 2017 Classification

| Grade | Features |
|---|---|
| Mild dysplasia | Architectural disturbance in lower one-third of epithelium; minimal cytological atypia |
| Moderate dysplasia | Architectural disturbance extending to middle one-third; moderate cytological atypia |
| Severe dysplasia | Architectural disturbance in more than two-thirds; marked cytological atypia |
| Carcinoma in situ | Full-thickness architectural and cytological changes without stromal invasion |

### Binary Grading System

Increasingly preferred for clinical decision-making:
- **Low-grade dysplasia** = mild dysplasia
- **High-grade dysplasia** = moderate dysplasia, severe dysplasia, and carcinoma in situ

The binary system has **better interobserver reproducibility** and correlates more directly with management decisions.

## Risk Stratification

### Factors Predicting Malignant Transformation

| Factor | Relative Risk | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Non-homogeneous morphology | RR 4.23 | Strongest clinical predictor |
| Dysplasia (any grade) | RR 2.75 | Strongest histological predictor |
| Tongue/floor of mouth | RR 2.09 | High-risk sites |
| Large size (>200 mm²) | RR 2.08 | Reflects field extent |
| Female sex | Variable | Some studies show higher risk |
| Non-smoker leukoplakia | Higher per lesion | Paradox: may represent intrinsically more aggressive biology |
| LOH at 3p/9p | 3–33× increased risk | Best molecular predictor |

### The Non-Smoker Paradox

Leukoplakia in non-smokers has a **higher per-lesion transformation rate** than in smokers. This is counterintuitive but may be because:
- Smoking-related leukoplakia is a reactive keratosis that often regresses with cessation
- Non-smoker leukoplakia may represent a more genetically driven process with higher intrinsic malignant potential

## Management Schema

### Step 1: Initial Assessment

1. **History:** tobacco/alcohol/betel nut habits, duration, symptoms
2. **Clinical examination:** location, size, morphology (homogeneous vs non-homogeneous), multifocality
3. **Photography:** baseline documentation for comparison
4. **Incisional biopsy:** mandatory for all leukoplakia — from the most clinically suspicious area (erythematous/non-homogeneous zones)
5. **Multiple biopsies** if the lesion is large or heterogeneous (sampling error is a significant problem)

### Step 2: Risk-Stratified Management

**Low-Risk Leukoplakia:**
*Homogeneous, small (<200 mm²), non-tongue/FOM, no dysplasia or mild dysplasia*

- Habit cessation (tobacco, alcohol, betel)
- Close observation: 6-monthly clinical review with photography
- Re-biopsy if any clinical change (increase in size, change in texture, new erythroplakia, ulceration)
- Many homogeneous leukoplakias in smokers regress with cessation

**Moderate-Risk Leukoplakia:**
*Homogeneous on tongue/FOM, OR non-homogeneous at any site, OR moderate dysplasia*

- Habit cessation
- **Excisional biopsy/surgical excision** recommended
- Laser ablation (CO₂ laser) is an alternative for superficial lesions
- Follow-up: 3–6 monthly for minimum 5 years
- Re-biopsy any recurrence

**High-Risk Leukoplakia:**
*Severe dysplasia/CIS at any site, OR non-homogeneous on tongue/FOM, OR PVL*

- **Surgical excision with clear margins** is mandatory
- Specimen orientation for complete histological assessment
- Consider wider margins (≥5 mm) given field cancerization
- Close follow-up: 3-monthly for 2 years, then 6-monthly lifelong
- Re-excise recurrences promptly
- Consider molecular risk profiling (LOH at 3p/9p) where available

### Step 3: Treatment Modalities

| Modality | Indication | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cold knife excision | Gold standard; all grades | Complete histology; margin assessment | Scarring; functional impairment |
| CO₂ laser excision | Superficial lesions | Precise; minimal bleeding; good healing | Margins may be thermally damaged |
| CO₂ laser ablation | Large homogeneous leukoplakia | Treats wide areas; repeatable | No histology; recurrence common |
| [[Answers/General/Photodynamic Therapy|photodynamic therapy]] | Widespread/multifocal lesions | Non-destructive; repeatable | Variable response; cost |
| Cryotherapy | Small, accessible lesions | Simple; outpatient | No histology; unpredictable depth |

### Step 4: Post-Treatment Surveillance

- **Recurrence rate after excision: 10–35%** — lifelong follow-up is essential
- Surgical excision **reduces but does not eliminate** transformation risk (Mehanna 2009: surgery reduces MTR by ~50%)
- Recurrence or new lesions may develop due to [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Field Cancerization|field cancerization]]
- Molecular markers (LOH, p53 immunostaining) may refine risk assessment in the future

## Special Entities

### Proliferative Verrucous Leukoplakia (PVL)

- Distinct clinicopathological entity with **very high transformation rate (~70%)**
- Multifocal, progressive, resistant to all forms of treatment
- Often starts as simple homogeneous leukoplakia, evolves through verrucous leukoplakia to verrucous carcinoma or conventional SCC
- More common in women, non-smokers
- Management: repeated excision of evolving lesions + lifelong intensive surveillance
- No effective prevention strategy exists

### Erythroplakia

- Red velvety patch with highest transformation rate of all OPMDs (>50%)
- Histologically shows severe dysplasia or CIS in >90% of cases
- Management: treat as high-risk — surgical excision with clear margins is mandatory

## Role of Chemoprevention

See [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Chemoprevention in Oral Cancer|Chemoprevention in Oral Cancer]] for detailed discussion. In summary, no chemoprevention agent is currently recommended for routine use. Retinoids produce short-term responses but relapse on withdrawal. Tobacco cessation remains the single most effective intervention.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Oral leukoplakia has a pooled malignant transformation rate of ~3.5%; non-homogeneous types transform at 4–5× the rate of homogeneous types
> 2. Non-homogeneous morphology (RR 4.23), dysplasia (RR 2.75), and tongue/FOM location (RR 2.09) are the three strongest predictors of transformation
> 3. Incisional biopsy is mandatory for all leukoplakia — multiple biopsies for large/heterogeneous lesions to reduce sampling error
> 4. Binary dysplasia grading (low-grade vs high-grade) has better interobserver reproducibility and correlates with management decisions
> 5. Mehanna 2009: surgical excision reduces malignant transformation risk by ~50% but does not eliminate it — lifelong follow-up is essential
> 6. Recurrence rate after excision is 10–35% due to field cancerization — the entire mucosal field remains at risk
> 7. PVL is the most dangerous OPMD with ~70% transformation rate; multifocal, progressive, treatment-resistant
> 8. Erythroplakia shows severe dysplasia/CIS in >90% of biopsies — treat as high-risk surgical excision
> 9. The non-smoker paradox: leukoplakia in non-smokers may have higher intrinsic transformation risk than smoking-related leukoplakia
> 10. LOH at 3p and 9p is the most promising molecular marker for risk stratification of oral leukoplakia

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The exam question asks for a "schema" — structure your answer as a risk-stratified algorithm (low/moderate/high risk) based on the four factors: location, morphology, dysplasia, and size
> - Always biopsy the worst-looking area — erythematous zones within a leukoplakia are more likely to harbour dysplasia than white areas
> - The take-home message from Mehanna 2009: surgery halves the risk but doesn't eliminate it. This is why follow-up is lifelong, not just 5 years
> - PVL is the most important special entity to mention — examiners expect you to know it. Remember: multifocal, progressive, women > men, non-smokers, ~70% transformation, treatment-resistant
> - For a non-homogeneous leukoplakia on the lateral tongue with moderate-severe dysplasia, the answer is always excision with clear margins + close surveillance — this is the exam's prototypical high-risk OPMD
