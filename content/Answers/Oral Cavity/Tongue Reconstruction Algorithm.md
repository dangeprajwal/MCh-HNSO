## Introduction

Tongue reconstruction after glossectomy is one of the most challenging problems in head and neck reconstructive surgery. The tongue is a uniquely dynamic organ essential for swallowing, speech articulation, airway protection, and mastication. The goals of reconstruction are to restore the patient's ability to swallow without aspiration and to form words with the greatest possible clarity. The approach to reconstruction is fundamentally defect-driven — determined by the volume of tongue resected, whether the floor of mouth is involved, the status of the hypoglossal and lingual nerves, and the integrity of the mandible. The reconstructive algorithm has evolved from primary closure and skin grafts to an era dominated by microvascular free tissue transfer, with the radial forearm free flap (RFFF) and anterolateral thigh (ALT) flap as the two workhorses.

## Principles of Tongue Reconstruction

### Functional Goals (in Order of Priority)

1. **Swallowing without aspiration** — the foremost priority; the tongue propels the food bolus posteriorly to initiate the pharyngeal phase of deglutition
2. **Speech articulation** — near-normal tongue mobility is vital for intelligible speech
3. **Airway protection** — tongue base contributes to the hyomandibular muscle complex; loss increases aspiration risk
4. **Oral competence** — maintenance of lip seal and containment of food/saliva
5. **Sensation** — sensory feedback assists in locating food particles and directing them to the occlusal plane

### Key Reconstructive Principles

- **Preserve tongue mobility** — when residual native tongue retains motor supply (intact hypoglossal nerve), the reconstruction must provide redundant tissue that does not tether the remnant
- **Restore volume** — as defect size increases, matching the neotongue volume to the oral cavity and pharyngeal chamber becomes critical; the neotongue must maintain contact with the palate
- **Avoid tethering** — any reconstruction that restricts residual tongue movement worsens both speech and swallowing outcomes
- **Thin pliable tissue for small defects** — prevents bulking that restricts movement
- **Bulky tissue for large defects** — restores volume to maintain palatal contact; flaps should be 20–30% larger than the defect to account for volume loss over time
- **Sensory reinnervation** — neurorrhaphy of donor nerve to lingual nerve improves functional outcomes

## Glossectomy Classification

### Ansarin Classification (2019)

| Type | Glossectomy | Description |
|---|---|---|
| I | Mucosectomy | Mucosal resection only |
| II | Partial | <50% of oral tongue |
| IIIa | Hemiglossectomy | ~50% of tongue |
| IIIb | Compartmental hemi | Hemiglossectomy + floor of mouth |
| IVa | Subtotal | >50% but not total |
| IVb | Near-total | Minimal residual tongue |
| V | Total glossectomy | Complete tongue resection |

### Defect-Oriented Classification (Urken 1994)

Urken classified tongue defects based on:
1. Mobile tongue vs base of tongue involvement
2. Amount of tongue resected (minimal, subtotal, total)
3. Floor of mouth involvement
4. Mandibular integrity (intact vs segmental mandibulectomy)

This classification directly guides the choice of reconstructive flap.

## Reconstruction Algorithm by Defect Size

### 1. Minor Glossectomy (<25% of tongue, floor of mouth intact)

**Reconstruction:** Primary closure

- Simple approximation of remaining tongue mucosa
- Provides excellent functional outcomes — preserves bulk, mobility, and sensation
- Applicable only when closure does not restrict tongue mobility
- No flap needed

### 2. Minor Glossectomy with Floor of Mouth Involvement

**Reconstruction:** Radial forearm free flap (RFFF)

- Floor of mouth involvement necessitates introduction of tissue to prevent tethering
- RFFF is the workhorse — provides the thinnest available tissue with excellent oral lining
- Contains well-defined sensory nerves (medial and lateral antebrachial cutaneous) for sensory reinnervation
- The flap is folded along the lateral border of the tongue — the portion covering the tongue is functionally separate from that covering the floor of mouth

### 3. Hemiglossectomy (~50% of tongue)

**Reconstruction:** RFFF (thin patients) or ALT flap (standard)

- RFFF with de-epithelialised segments provides added bulk when needed
- ALT flap preferred when more tissue volume is required — provides more subcutaneous tissue than RFFF with lower donor site morbidity
- **Critical:** ensure the remaining tongue with intact hypoglossal nerve is not tethered by the flap inset
- Sensory reinnervation to the lingual nerve improves functional outcomes

### 4. Subtotal/Near-Total Glossectomy (larynx preserved)

**Reconstruction:** ALT flap (preferred) or rectus abdominis flap

- These defects are associated with a high incidence of significant permanent swallowing problems
- With preservation of a segment of tongue base and at least one hypoglossal nerve, rehabilitation of functional swallowing is achievable
- ALT provides considerably more tissue bulk than RFFF
- Rectus abdominis flap is an alternative when large volume is needed
- Laryngeal suspension should be considered to position the larynx more cephalad and anterior, reducing aspiration

### 5. Total Mobile Tongue Resection (mandible intact)

**Reconstruction:** Rectus abdominis flap or large ALT flap

- Goal: place neotongue soft tissue above the occlusal plane and maintain palatal contact
- Rectus abdominis flap — anterior rectus sheath sutured to the lower border of the mandible creates a fascial platform supporting the neotongue
- De-epithelialised subcutaneous tissue placed under the skin paddle provides vascularised bulk
- Gravity will cause the neotongue to sag — intraoperative manoeuvres to maintain cephalad position are critical
- **Laryngeal suspension** is an essential adjunct

### 6. Total Mobile Tongue Resection + Segmental Mandibulectomy

**Reconstruction:** Iliac crest osteocutaneous free flap (composite reconstruction)

- Requires two integrated strategies: bone replacement + soft tissue volume
- Iliac crest free flap placed in horizontal orientation — bone serves as a rigid platform supporting the soft tissue neotongue, preventing caudal migration
- The associated skin paddle provides mucosal lining
- Alternative: double free flap (fibula for mandible + ALT or rectus for tongue)

### 7. Total Glossectomy (oral tongue + tongue base)

**Reconstruction:** Rectus abdominis flap or double flap

- The most challenging reconstruction scenario
- Volume restoration is the critical factor — neotongue must fill the oral and pharyngeal cavity to maintain palatal and pharyngeal wall contact
- Dynamic reconstruction (reinnervated muscle flaps — latissimus dorsi via hypoglossal nerve) has been attempted but functional superiority over static reconstruction has not been proven
- Laryngeal suspension is mandatory
- High rates of long-term gastrostomy dependence

## Flap Options: Comparison

### Radial Forearm Free Flap (RFFF)

- **Advantages:** Thin and pliable; consistent anatomy; long pedicle; large-calibre vessels; allows sensory reinnervation
- **Disadvantages:** Limited bulk; donor site morbidity (skin graft on forearm); sacrifice of radial artery
- **Best for:** Minor–moderate glossectomy defects, floor of mouth reconstruction

### Anterolateral Thigh (ALT) Flap

- **Advantages:** Large skin paddle available; good subcutaneous bulk; long pedicle; low donor site morbidity (primary closure often possible); can be thinned
- **Disadvantages:** Variable perforator anatomy; may be too bulky in obese patients
- **Best for:** Hemiglossectomy and subtotal glossectomy defects

### Rectus Abdominis Free Flap

- **Advantages:** Large volume of tissue; reliable pedicle (deep inferior epigastric); fascial component for structural support
- **Disadvantages:** Abdominal wall morbidity (hernia risk); bulky; non-sensate
- **Best for:** Total mobile tongue and total glossectomy defects

### Regional Flaps (Pectoralis Major)

- Not recommended as primary choice — provides good initial results but does not stand the test of time due to muscle atrophy and loss of bulk
- Role limited to salvage situations where microvascular surgery is not feasible

## Functional Outcomes

### Factors Affecting Outcome

- **Volume of tongue resected** — single most important determinant
- **Hypoglossal nerve preservation** — critical for residual tongue mobility
- **Adjuvant radiotherapy** — significantly reduces tongue strength scores
- **Defect location** — tongue base resection has greater impact on swallowing than mobile tongue resection
- **Reconstruction type** — tongue strength is higher with primary closure than free flap (reflects smaller defects rather than inherent flap superiority)

### Outcome Data

- Lateral tongue resection patients retain the highest tongue strength
- Tongue ROM after reconstruction: mean 82% of normal
- Total glossectomy patients can achieve 75% understandable speech — better than expected
- No significant differences in speech intelligibility between RFFF and ALT flap reconstruction

## Adjuncts to Tongue Reconstruction

### Laryngeal Suspension

- Statically positions the larynx more cephalad and anterior
- Essential adjunct after total mobile tongue or total glossectomy
- Reduces aspiration by favouring airway protection during swallowing

### Sensory Reinnervation

- Neurorrhaphy of flap sensory nerve (antebrachial cutaneous nerve from RFFF) to lingual nerve
- Provides protective sensation and proprioceptive feedback for food bolus management
- Most beneficial in partial glossectomy defects where functional recovery is expected

### Palatal Augmentation Prosthesis

- Lowers the palatal vault to facilitate tongue–palate contact
- Useful when neotongue bulk is insufficient to reach the palate
- Alternative to increasing flap volume; avoids coverage of sensate palatal mucosa

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Goals of tongue reconstruction in priority order: swallowing, speech, airway protection, oral competence, sensation
> 2. RFFF is the workhorse for minor–moderate glossectomy defects — thin, pliable, sensate
> 3. ALT flap is preferred for hemiglossectomy and subtotal glossectomy — provides more bulk with less donor morbidity
> 4. Rectus abdominis or iliac crest composite for total mobile tongue defects
> 5. Volume restoration is critical in large defects — neotongue must maintain palatal contact
> 6. Avoid tethering of residual native tongue — preservation of mobility is paramount
> 7. Laryngeal suspension is mandatory after total glossectomy to reduce aspiration
> 8. Dynamic reconstruction with reinnervated muscle has not proven superior to static reconstruction
> 9. Flap volume should be 20–30% larger than the defect to account for postoperative atrophy
> 10. Pectoralis major does not stand the test of time for tongue reconstruction — free flaps preferred

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The single most important factor determining functional outcome after glossectomy is the volume of native tongue preserved and whether the hypoglossal nerve is intact
> - For hemiglossectomy defects, insetting the RFFF folded along the lateral tongue border — with separate segments for tongue surface and floor of mouth — prevents tethering and optimises mobility
> - After total glossectomy, placing the rectus abdominis fascial layer sutured to drill holes in the mandible creates a hammock that prevents the neotongue from sagging under gravity
> - Adjuvant radiotherapy significantly reduces tongue strength — counsel patients preoperatively about expected functional decline
> - Total glossectomy patients can still achieve surprisingly intelligible speech (75% understandability) — aggressive speech therapy rehabilitation is worthwhile
