## Introduction

The lips are critical functional and aesthetic structures. The lower lip is the most common site for lip cancer (90% of lip cancers), and reconstruction after ablative surgery must address three essential goals: (1) oral competence (prevention of drooling), (2) adequate oral aperture (for eating, speech, and dental care), and (3) aesthetic restoration. The reconstruction algorithm is primarily dictated by the **size of the defect** as a proportion of total lip width and whether the defect involves the commissure.

## Surgical Anatomy

### Key Structures

- **Orbicularis oris muscle** — the sphincteric muscle of the lip; its integrity is essential for oral competence
- **Labial arteries** — superior and inferior labial arteries (branches of the facial artery) run within the orbicularis oris muscle, 2–3 mm deep to the mucosal surface
- **Vermilion border** — the mucocutaneous junction; accurate realignment is critical for aesthetic outcome
- **White roll** — the fine white line at the mucocutaneous junction; even 1 mm mismatch is noticeable
- **Modiolus** — the convergence point of facial muscles at the oral commissure
- **Mental nerve** — provides sensation to the lower lip; exits through the mental foramen below the second premolar

### Blood Supply

The lip has a **rich dual blood supply** from the labial arteries (from the facial artery) and branches from the mental/infraorbital arteries. This rich vascularity allows for robust flap survival and permits neurovascular-preserving techniques like the Karapandzic flap.

## Reconstruction Algorithm by Defect Size

| Defect Size | Technique |
|---|---|
| **<30% of lip width** | Primary closure (wedge excision/V-closure) |
| **30–60%** | Abbe-Estlander flap (cross-lip) |
| **60–80%** | Karapandzic flap |
| **80–100% (total)** | Bernard-Webster or bilateral Karapandzic |
| **Commissure involvement** | Estlander flap or Gillies fan flap |

## Techniques for Total Lower Lip Reconstruction

### 1. Bernard-Webster Flap (Cheek Advancement)

This is the **workhorse technique for total or near-total lower lip reconstruction**.

**Principle:** Bilateral full-thickness cheek advancement flaps are mobilised medially to reconstruct the lip, with perialar crescentic (Webster) or triangular (Bernard) excisions on the upper lip to set back the commissures and prevent fish-mouth deformity.

**Technique:**
1. Total lower lip excision including chin skin if involved
2. Rectangular-shaped excision facilitates closure
3. Bilateral cheek flaps elevated in full-thickness fashion (skin + muscle + mucosa)
4. Perialar crescentic excisions or triangular (Burow's) excisions on the upper lip — these allow the commissures to be repositioned medially
5. **Mucosal preservation** — the mucosa underlying the excised perialar skin triangles is preserved to create the new vermilion lining
6. Lateral cheek flaps advanced medially and closed in layers (mucosa, muscle, skin)

**Advantages:**
- Single-stage procedure
- Can reconstruct nearly the entire lower lip
- Achieves oral competence in most patients

**Disadvantages:**
- Reduced oral aperture (microstomia) — the most significant limitation
- "Permanent smile" deformity, especially in edentulous patients
- Loss of orbicularis oris function (muscle is not preserved in the flap) → no dynamic sphincteric control
- Lip sensation may be reduced
- The reconstructed lip lacks a true vermilion — the "neo-vermilion" is advanced buccal mucosa

### 2. Karapandzic Flap (Neurovascular Rotation-Advancement)

The Karapandzic flap is ideal for **subtotal lower lip defects (60–80%)** where the lateral commissural remnants are preserved. It can also be used bilaterally for total defects.

**Principle:** Full-thickness rotation-advancement of the perioral tissues, preserving the neurovascular supply to the orbicularis oris muscle. This maintains oral competence through functional sphincteric reconstruction.

**Technique (Jatin Shah):**
1. Skin incisions placed ~2 cm inferior to vermilion border, following the nasolabial crease
2. **Skin and subcutaneous tissue** elevated superficial to the orbicularis oris muscle
3. **Mucosal incisions** placed at the gingivolabial sulcus — deep to the orbicularis oris
4. The orbicularis oris muscle is kept intact between the two planes of dissection — its **nerve and blood supply are preserved**
5. Bilateral flaps rotated medially; closure begins with accurate vermilion border alignment (5-0 nylon)
6. Muscular, then cutaneous and mucosal closure in layers

**Advantages:**
- **Preserves orbicularis oris innervation and blood supply** — dynamic oral competence maintained
- Functional sphincter reconstruction — superior to Bernard flap for oral continence
- Natural vermilion border preserved
- Sensation maintained
- Single-stage procedure

**Disadvantages:**
- **Microstomia** — significant reduction in oral aperture (the main limitation)
- Blunting of the commissures
- Round mouth appearance
- Limited to defects where at least some commissural tissue remains laterally

### 3. Abbe-Estlander Flap (Cross-Lip Flap)

**Principle:** A full-thickness flap of the upper lip, pedicled on the superior labial artery, is rotated into the lower lip defect. Used for defects of **30–60% of the lip**.

**Technique:**
- Full-thickness wedge-shaped flap designed from the upper lip, centred on the labial artery
- Flap rotated 180° into the lower lip defect
- Bridge pedicle maintained for 2–3 weeks → divided in a second stage
- **Estlander modification** — when the defect involves the commissure, the flap is rotated around the commissure without a bridge (single-stage)

**Advantages:**
- Provides "like-for-like" tissue (vermilion, muscle, mucosa)
- Maintains sensate lip tissue
- Good colour and texture match

**Disadvantages:**
- Two-stage procedure (except Estlander)
- Reduces upper lip tissue
- Creates an immobile segment in the lower lip (no motor innervation of the transferred segment)
- Maximum one-third of the upper lip can be borrowed

### 4. Gillies Fan Flap

**Principle:** Bilateral fan-shaped cheek rotation flaps pivoted at the commissures. Used for defects of 60–80%.

**Technique:**
- Full-thickness cheek flaps designed as fan shapes radiating from the commissures
- Flaps rotated medially to fill the lower lip defect
- Preserves some neurovascular supply

### 5. Free Flap Reconstruction

**Indications:** When local tissue is insufficient — massive through-and-through defects involving lip + chin + mandible, or after previous surgery/radiation that has exhausted local options.

**Options:**
- **Radial forearm free flap (RFFF)** — thin, pliable; most commonly used for lip soft tissue
- **Anterolateral thigh (ALT) flap** — for larger composite defects
- **Gracilis free functional muscle transfer** — for dynamic reconstruction with facial nerve reinnervation (provides sphincteric function)

**Disadvantages:**
- No vermilion, no orbicularis oris function (except gracilis)
- Insensate lip (requires separate nerve coaptation)
- Colour and texture mismatch
- Complex surgery

## Commissure Reconstruction

When the commissure is involved:
- **Estlander flap** — cross-lip flap rotated around the commissure (single-stage)
- **Converse modification** — a variation where the flap is inset into an excised area of cheek to create a neo-commissure
- **Gillies fan flap** — rotational advancement at the commissure

## Key Principles of Lip Reconstruction

1. **Accurate vermilion alignment** — even 1 mm mismatch at the white roll is cosmetically unacceptable
2. **Preserve orbicularis oris function** whenever possible — oral competence is the most important functional goal
3. **Reconstruct in layers** — mucosa, muscle, skin
4. **Use like tissue** — lip tissue for lip (Abbe, Karapandzic) is superior to non-lip tissue (cheek, free flap)
5. **Plan for adequate oral aperture** — microstomia after reconstruction may require commissuroplasty
6. **Subunit principle** — if >50% of a lip subunit is resected, replace the entire subunit for best aesthetic result

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Lower lip reconstruction is guided by defect size: <30% primary closure, 30–60% Abbe, 60–80% Karapandzic, >80% Bernard-Webster
> 2. Karapandzic flap preserves orbicularis oris nerve and blood supply — best for maintaining oral competence
> 3. Bernard-Webster is the workhorse for total lower lip reconstruction — single-stage cheek advancement with perialar crescentic excisions
> 4. Microstomia is the most common complication of both Karapandzic and Bernard-Webster techniques
> 5. Abbe-Estlander flap provides "like-for-like" tissue but is a two-stage procedure and limited to one-third of upper lip
> 6. The labial artery runs within the orbicularis oris muscle, 2–3 mm from the mucosal surface — it is the pedicle for both Abbe and Karapandzic flaps
> 7. Accurate white roll/vermilion border alignment is the most critical step for aesthetic outcome
> 8. Free flap reconstruction (RFFF, gracilis) is reserved for massive defects or exhausted local options

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - In Karapandzic flap, the key technical step is keeping the dissection planes superficial (skin) and deep (mucosa) to the orbicularis oris — the muscle itself is never cut
> - For Bernard-Webster, always preserve the mucosa under the perialar skin excision — it forms the neo-vermilion
> - The "permanent smile" deformity of Bernard-Webster is worse in edentulous patients — consider dental prosthetics to support the reconstructed lip
> - When borrowing tissue from the upper lip (Abbe), never take more than one-third — the upper lip is cosmetically more important and less forgiving
> - After Karapandzic reconstruction, stretching exercises and commissuroplasty may be needed if microstomia limits oral function
