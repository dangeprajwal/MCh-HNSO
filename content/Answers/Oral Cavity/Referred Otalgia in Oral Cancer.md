## Definition

Referred otalgia (secondary otalgia) is **ear pain originating from a non-otologic source** mediated through shared sensory nerve pathways between the ear and the head and neck structures. In head and neck oncology, referred otalgia is a critical symptom that may be the **first or only manifestation** of an occult primary malignancy.

## Neuroanatomical Basis

The ear receives sensory innervation from **five cranial nerves and two cervical nerves**, creating an extensive referral network. Any pathology along the distribution of these shared nerves can produce otalgia.

### Sensory Innervation of the Ear

| Nerve | Branch | Ear Region Supplied | Source of Referred Pain |
|---|---|---|---|
| CN V₃ (mandibular) | Auriculotemporal | Tragus, anterior EAC, anterior TM | Oral cavity, TMJ, mandible |
| CN VII (facial) | Nervus intermedius | Concha, posterior EAC | Tongue base (via chorda tympani) |
| CN IX (glossopharyngeal) | Jacobson's nerve (tympanic) | Middle ear, inner TM | Oropharynx, tongue base, tonsil |
| CN X (vagus) | Arnold's nerve (auricular) | Posterior EAC, posterior TM, pinna | Larynx, hypopharynx, pyriform sinus |
| C2–C3 (cervical plexus) | Great auricular nerve | Lower pinna, mastoid area | Neck, cervical spine |

*EAC: external auditory canal; TM: tympanic membrane*

## Mechanism of Referred Otalgia in Oral Cancer

### CN V₃ Pathway (Most Common in Oral Cancer)

The **auriculotemporal nerve** (branch of V₃) supplies the tragus and anterior EAC. It communicates with the **lingual nerve** (also V₃), which innervates the anterior two-thirds of the tongue and floor of mouth.

**Mechanism:** Tumour invasion or perineural spread along the lingual nerve → afferent pain signals travel to the trigeminal nucleus → convergence with auriculotemporal afferents → perceived as ear pain.

### CN IX Pathway (Oropharyngeal Cancer)

The **tympanic nerve (Jacobson's nerve)** — a branch of CN IX — innervates the middle ear mucosa and inner surface of the tympanic membrane. CN IX also supplies the:
- Posterior one-third of tongue
- Tonsillar fossa
- Soft palate
- Upper pharynx

**Mechanism:** Oropharyngeal tumour → glossopharyngeal afferents → converge with tympanic nerve signals in nucleus tractus solitarius → deep ear pain.

### CN X Pathway (Laryngeal/Hypopharyngeal Cancer)

**Arnold's nerve** (auricular branch of vagus) supplies the posterior EAC and posterior TM surface. CN X also innervates:
- Larynx (via superior and recurrent laryngeal nerves)
- Hypopharynx
- Pyriform sinus

**Mechanism:** Laryngeal/hypopharyngeal tumour → vagal afferents → convergence with Arnold's nerve in spinal trigeminal nucleus → ear pain at posterior wall of canal.

## Clinical Significance

### As a Presenting Symptom

| Primary Site | Otalgia Frequency | Nerve Pathway |
|---|---|---|
| Tongue (posterior/lateral) | 15–25% | CN V₃ (lingual → auriculotemporal) |
| Floor of mouth | 10–15% | CN V₃ |
| Oropharynx/tonsil | 25–50% | CN IX (most common cause of HN cancer otalgia) |
| Tongue base | 30–40% | CN IX, CN X |
| Hypopharynx/larynx | 20–35% | CN X (Arnold's nerve) |
| Nasopharynx | 10–20% | CN V₂/V₃, eustachian tube |

### Red Flags for Occult Malignancy

Referred otalgia should raise suspicion for head and neck cancer when:
- **Unilateral** and persistent (>2–3 weeks)
- Normal otoscopic examination
- Associated with **odynophagia, dysphagia, or dysphonia**
- Risk factors present: tobacco, alcohol, betel quid use
- Age >40 years with unexplained ear pain
- Weight loss or neck mass

### Clinical Axiom

> **"Any patient over 40 with unexplained unilateral otalgia and a normal ear examination should be considered to have a head and neck malignancy until proven otherwise."**

## Evaluation

### Systematic Assessment Algorithm

**Step 1: Exclude Primary Ear Pathology**
- Otoscopy — TM integrity, canal pathology, middle ear effusion
- Audiometry if hearing loss present

**Step 2: Head and Neck Examination**
- Complete oral cavity examination — floor of mouth, tongue, alveolus
- Oropharyngeal assessment — tonsillar fossa, tongue base, soft palate
- Flexible nasopharyngolaryngoscopy — tongue base, vallecula, pyriform sinus, larynx, nasopharynx
- Neck palpation — cervical lymphadenopathy

**Step 3: Focused Neurological Examination**
- CN V₃ — sensation anterior tongue, mandibular area
- CN VII — facial weakness, taste
- CN IX — gag reflex, posterior tongue sensation
- CN X — vocal fold mobility, cough reflex
- CN XII — tongue protrusion and deviation

**Step 4: Imaging**
- **MRI with gadolinium** — tongue base, oropharynx, perineural spread
- **CT neck with contrast** — if mass or nodal disease suspected
- **PET-CT** — for unknown primary evaluation

### Location of Otalgia as Diagnostic Clue

| Otalgia Location | Predominant Nerve | Likely Primary Site |
|---|---|---|
| Tragus, anterior ear | CN V₃ | Oral cavity, TMJ |
| Deep ear pain | CN IX | Oropharynx, tongue base |
| Posterior ear canal | CN X | Larynx, hypopharynx |
| Lower pinna, mastoid | C2–C3 | Cervical pathology |

## Referred Otalgia in Oral Cancer Specifically

### Oral Cavity Sites Most Commonly Causing Otalgia

1. **Posterior oral tongue** — lingual nerve involvement → V₃ pathway
2. **Floor of mouth** — deep invasion near lingual nerve
3. **Retromolar trigone** — proximity to V₃ main trunk at mandibular foramen
4. **Lower alveolus** — inferior alveolar nerve (V₃) involvement

### Perineural Spread and Otalgia

Perineural invasion (PNI) along the **inferior alveolar nerve or lingual nerve** can produce:
- Numbness of the lip/chin (numb chin syndrome) + referred otalgia
- Pain radiating from the oral lesion to the ear
- Formication (crawling sensation) along the nerve distribution

PNI along V₃ represents **advanced disease** and is an indication for [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Oral Cancer|adjuvant radiotherapy]].

## Differential Diagnosis of Otalgia

### Non-Malignant Causes

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Dental | Impacted third molars, dental abscess, TMJ dysfunction |
| Inflammatory | Tonsillitis, pharyngitis, sinusitis, parotitis |
| Neurological | Trigeminal neuralgia, glossopharyngeal neuralgia, Eagle syndrome |
| Musculoskeletal | Cervical spondylosis, myofascial pain |
| GERD | Laryngopharyngeal reflux causing vagal irritation |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Referred otalgia is mediated through five cranial nerves (V₃, VII, IX, X) and cervical plexus (C2–C3) sharing sensory innervation of the ear and head/neck structures
> 2. CN IX (glossopharyngeal) pathway is the most common cause of cancer-related referred otalgia — oropharyngeal tumours in 25–50% cause otalgia
> 3. CN V₃ pathway produces otalgia in oral cavity cancers — lingual nerve signals converge with auriculotemporal afferents
> 4. Unilateral otalgia with a normal ear in a patient >40 years with risk factors is head and neck cancer until proven otherwise
> 5. Location of ear pain is a diagnostic clue: tragus/anterior ear = V₃ (oral cavity); deep ear = CN IX (oropharynx); posterior canal = CN X (larynx)
> 6. Complete head and neck examination including flexible nasopharyngolaryngoscopy is mandatory in unexplained referred otalgia
> 7. Posterior oral tongue and retromolar trigone cancers are the oral cavity subsites most commonly causing referred otalgia
> 8. Perineural spread along the inferior alveolar or lingual nerve produces otalgia + numbness — indicates advanced disease needing adjuvant RT
> 9. TMJ dysfunction, dental pathology, and GERD are the commonest non-malignant causes of referred otalgia
> 10. Systematic evaluation: exclude primary ear disease → complete head and neck exam → neurological assessment → targeted imaging (MRI/PET-CT)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **"Normal ear + ear pain = look at the throat"** — this clinical maxim should trigger a thorough head and neck examination
> 2. In oral cancer patients, **new-onset otalgia during follow-up** may herald recurrence or second primary — always re-examine the oral cavity and oropharynx
> 3. The **Jacobson's nerve (CN IX) pathway** explains why early tonsillar and tongue base cancers present with deep otalgia before the primary becomes clinically obvious
> 4. **Arnold's nerve reflex** (cough triggered by ear canal stimulation) confirms the vagus pathway — patients with this reflex are susceptible to referred otalgia from laryngeal/hypopharyngeal cancers
> 5. When a patient presents with unexplained otalgia and a negative primary examination, **PET-CT** may be the best investigation to detect an occult primary cancer
