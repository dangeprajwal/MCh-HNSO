## Introduction

Oral submucous fibrosis (OSMF) is a chronic, progressive, fibrotic condition of the oral mucosa caused primarily by the habitual chewing of areca nut (betel nut). It is classified by the WHO as an oral potentially malignant disorder (OPMD) with one of the highest malignant transformation rates among all OPMDs. The disease is virtually confined to South and Southeast Asia, the Pacific Islands, and diaspora communities where areca nut chewing is practised. It is characterised by progressive fibrosis of the oral submucosa, leading to mucosal rigidity, restricted mouth opening ([[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]]), and epithelial atrophy that predisposes to squamous cell carcinoma.

## Aetiology

### Areca Nut (Betel Nut) — The Primary Cause

- **Arecoline** — the principal alkaloid in areca nut; responsible for fibroblast stimulation
- Areca nut contains four alkaloids: arecoline, arecaidine, guvacine, and guvacoline
- Chewed as:
  - **Paan/betel quid** — betel leaf (Piper betle) + areca nut + slaked lime ± tobacco ± spices
  - **Paan masala/gutka** — commercially prepared mixture of areca nut, slaked lime, catechu, tobacco
  - **Plain areca nut** (supari) — even without tobacco, areca nut is independently carcinogenic

### Other Contributing Factors

- **Slaked lime (calcium hydroxide)** — increases pH in the oral cavity, facilitating absorption of arecoline and hydrolysis of arecoline to arecaidine
- **Tobacco** — synergistic carcinogenic effect; tobacco + areca nut users have higher OSMF and oral cancer rates
- **Capsaicin** (from chillies) — may contribute to mucosal irritation
- **Iron deficiency** — anaemia is commonly associated; iron-deficient mucosa may be more susceptible
- **Genetic predisposition** — HLA associations reported (HLA-A10, HLA-B7, HLA-DR3)
- **Immunological factors** — elevated levels of CD4+ T cells and cytokines in affected tissue

## Pathogenesis

### Molecular Mechanism

1. **Arecoline exposure** → stimulates fibroblasts in the lamina propria via upregulation of TGF-β and PDGF
2. **Increased collagen synthesis** — particularly collagen types I and III; arecoline directly stimulates procollagen gene transcription
3. **Decreased collagen degradation** — arecoline inhibits collagenase activity and upregulates tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs)
4. **Cross-linking of collagen** — copper in areca nut activates lysyl oxidase → increased collagen cross-linking → insoluble, resistant to degradation
5. **Epithelial atrophy** — progressive fibrosis of lamina propria → reduced vascularity → epithelial thinning
6. **Muscle degeneration** — fibrosis extends to involve the musculature (buccinator, pterygoids) → trismus

### Immune-Mediated Component

- Arecoline-modified collagen acts as a neoantigen → T-cell mediated immune response
- Mast cell degranulation → histamine release → increased vascular permeability → inflammatory cell infiltration
- Chronic inflammation → perpetuates the fibrotic cycle

### Pathway to Malignant Transformation

- Epithelial atrophy → reduced epithelial barrier → increased carcinogen penetration
- Loss of normal epithelial architecture → dysplasia
- Concurrent carcinogen exposure (tobacco, arecoline-derived nitrosamines)
- p53 mutations, LOH at multiple chromosomal loci
- **Malignant transformation rate: 3–8%** (Murti et al. reported 7.6% over 17 years; annual rate ~0.5%)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms (Progressive)

1. **Burning sensation** — earliest symptom; aggravated by spicy foods
2. **Vesiculation and ulceration** — intermittent blistering of oral mucosa
3. **Mucosal rigidity** — blanching and loss of normal elasticity
4. **Trismus** — progressive reduction in mouth opening (the hallmark feature)
5. **Difficulty in eating** — inability to tolerate spicy foods, difficulty chewing
6. **[[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|dysphagia]]** — if fibrosis extends to the oropharynx/soft palate
7. **Hearing loss** — Eustachian tube involvement (rare)

### Clinical Staging (Khanna & Andrade)

| Stage | Mouth Opening | Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|
| Group I (Early) | >35 mm | Burning sensation, blanching |
| Group II (Moderate) | 20–35 mm | Palpable fibrous bands, restricted tongue protrusion |
| Group III (Severe) | <20 mm | Thick fibrous bands, severe trismus, restricted mouth opening |
| Group IVa | Trismus + OPMD | Leukoplakia/[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Leukoplakia Management|erythroplakia]] superimposed |
| Group IVb | Trismus + SCC | Malignant transformation |

### Sites Involved

- Buccal mucosa (most common — corresponds to site of quid placement)
- Soft palate and uvula (uvula may shrink and become fibrotic)
- Retromolar trigone
- Lip mucosa
- Tongue (restricted mobility)
- [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]] (rare)

## Histopathology

### Early Stage

- Subepithelial inflammation with oedema
- Dilated blood vessels
- Fibroblast proliferation in lamina propria

### Intermediate Stage

- Dense collagen deposition in lamina propria
- Decreased vascularity
- Epithelial atrophy with loss of rete ridges
- Muscle degeneration begins

### Advanced Stage

- Dense avascular fibrosis extending from submucosa to muscle
- Complete loss of rete ridges
- Severe epithelial atrophy
- Hyalinisation of collagen bundles
- Muscle replaced by fibrous tissue

## Management

### Prevention

- **Cessation of areca nut/betel quid chewing** — the single most important intervention
- Public health campaigns targeting areca nut use in endemic regions
- Regulation of gutka/paan masala products (banned in several Indian states)

### Medical Management (Stage I–II)

**Anti-fibrotic agents:**
- **Intralesional corticosteroids** — dexamethasone or triamcinolone acetonide (1–2 mL, weekly injections into fibrous bands)
- **Intralesional hyaluronidase** (1500 IU) — breaks down ground substance, facilitates collagen remodelling; often combined with dexamethasone
- **Intralesional collagenase** — enzymatic degradation of fibrous tissue

**Systemic agents:**
- **Lycopene** (16 mg/day) — antioxidant; modest improvement in mouth opening
- **Pentoxifylline** (400 mg TDS) — improves microcirculation, inhibits TGF-β
- **Curcumin** — anti-inflammatory, anti-fibrotic properties
- **Iron and multivitamin supplementation** — correct associated deficiencies

**Physiotherapy:**
- Mouth-opening exercises (critical adjunct to all treatments)
- Heat therapy
- Forceful mouth opening devices

### Surgical Management (Stage III–IV)

Surgery is indicated for severe trismus (mouth opening <20 mm) refractory to medical management:

**1. Fibrous Band Release**
- Excision or release of fibrous bands
- **Must be followed by interpositional grafting** — otherwise, fibrosis recurs

**2. Coronoidectomy**
- Bilateral coronoidectomy when trismus is refractory to band release alone
- Addresses fibrotic contracture of temporalis muscle insertion

**3. Interpositional Grafts/Flaps**
- **Buccal fat pad (BFP) flap** — the most commonly used local flap; pedicled on buccal and superficial temporal branches of internal maxillary artery
- **Nasolabial flap** — for moderate defects
- **Palatal island flap** — for palatal OSMF
- **Split-thickness skin graft** — older technique; higher contracture rate
- **Radial forearm free flap** — for extensive bilateral disease
- **Collagen membrane** — emerging option for interpositional grafting

**4. Tongue Flap**
- Dorsal tongue flap used to cover buccal defect after fibrous band excision
- Limited donor site morbidity

### Post-Surgical Rehabilitation

- **Aggressive mouth-opening exercises** — begin 1 week post-surgery; lifelong
- **Dynamic mouth-opening devices** — Therabite, Dynasplint
- Without physiotherapy, surgical gains are lost to recurrent fibrosis
- **Lifelong areca nut cessation** is mandatory — continued use guarantees recurrence

## Malignant Transformation Surveillance

- All OSMF patients require lifelong follow-up
- Biopsy any:
  - Non-healing ulcer
  - Leukoplakia or erythroplakia superimposed on OSMF
  - Rapidly increasing trismus
  - New mass or induration
- Maintain a high index of suspicion — SCC developing in OSMF background is often detected late due to trismus limiting examination

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. OSMF is caused primarily by arecoline from areca nut (betel nut) chewing
> 2. Arecoline stimulates fibroblast collagen synthesis, inhibits collagenase, and promotes cross-linking via lysyl oxidase
> 3. Progressive fibrosis of lamina propria → epithelial atrophy → trismus → predisposition to SCC
> 4. Malignant transformation rate: 3–8% (highest among OPMDs after erythroplakia)
> 5. Trismus is the hallmark clinical feature; staging based on mouth opening (Khanna & Andrade)
> 6. Medical management: intralesional steroids + hyaluronidase, lycopene, pentoxifylline
> 7. Surgery (for severe trismus): fibrous band release + interpositional graft (buccal fat pad most popular)
> 8. Coronoidectomy may be needed when trismus is refractory to band release alone
> 9. Post-surgical mouth-opening exercises are essential — without them, gains are lost
> 10. Areca nut cessation is mandatory; continued use guarantees disease progression and recurrence

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Even areca nut without tobacco causes OSMF — this is a common misconception among patients
> - The buccal fat pad flap is the workhorse for interpositional grafting after fibrous band release — readily available, well-vascularised, and easy to harvest
> - Always screen OSMF patients for iron deficiency anaemia — correction improves mucosal health
> - Severe trismus in an OSMF patient with rapid worsening should raise suspicion for malignant transformation — biopsy urgently (may need an awake fibreoptic intubation for examination under anaesthesia)
> - The combination of intralesional dexamethasone + hyaluronidase (1500 IU) injected weekly for 6–8 weeks, combined with mouth-opening exercises, is the standard first-line medical regimen
