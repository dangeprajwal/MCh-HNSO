# Surgical Margins and Fluorescence-Guided Surgery

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - **OC Q56:** Advances in detecting oral cancer in surgical margin evaluation (AMRITA 2017)
> - **OC Q65:** Impact of fluorescence visualization guided surgery in diagnosis of oral cancer (AMRITA 2019)

---

## Surgical Margin Definitions

### Standard Margin Classification

| Category | Distance | Clinical Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Positive (involved) | Tumour at inked margin | Re-excision or adjuvant RT mandatory |
| Close | <5 mm | Adjuvant RT recommended |
| Adequate | ≥5 mm | Standard of care achieved |

The **5 mm threshold** is the most widely accepted standard for oral SCC, though some centres use ≥1 cm as the target intraoperative margin to account for tissue shrinkage during formalin fixation (approximately 20–30% shrinkage).

**Source:** Shah JP. *Head and Neck Surgery and Oncology*. 5th ed. Elsevier; 2019. Chapter 8.

### Margin Discordance: Surgeon vs Pathologist

A critical challenge in oral cancer surgery is the **discrepancy between intraoperative and final pathological margins**:

- Intraoperative assessment overestimates clearance by 20–30% due to tissue shrinkage
- Tumour-bed margins (from patient) vs specimen margins differ in orientation
- Deep margins are the most frequently positive in oral cancer

## Standard Methods of Margin Assessment

### Frozen Section Analysis

- **Gold standard** for intraoperative margin assessment
- Sensitivity: 90–97%; Specificity: 98–100%
- Turnaround: 20–30 minutes per specimen
- Limitations: sampling error, freezing artefact, difficult interpretation of dysplasia
- **[[Answers/General/Frozen Section in Surgical Margins|Detailed discussion in dedicated file]]**

### Permanent Section (Paraffin)

- Definitive assessment with full histopathological processing
- Takes 24–48 hours — guides adjuvant therapy decisions
- H&E staining remains the standard
- Immunohistochemistry (p53, Ki-67) may aid dysplasia assessment at margins

**Source:** Harrison LB et al. *Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach*. 4th ed. Lippincott; 2014. Chapter 16A.

## Advances in Surgical Margin Detection

### 1. Tissue Autofluorescence (VELscope)

**Principle:** Normal oral mucosa emits green autofluorescence when excited by 400–460 nm blue light due to flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) and collagen cross-links. Dysplastic or malignant tissue shows **loss of autofluorescence (LOF)** — appears dark — due to breakdown of collagen matrix and increased blood supply.

| Parameter | VELscope Performance |
|---|---|
| Sensitivity | 84–100% |
| Specificity | 15–74% |
| Best utility | Delineating field change extent |
| Limitation | High false-positive rate |

- Rana et al. (2012): VELscope detected premalignancy with higher sensitivity (100% vs 17%) but lower specificity (74% vs 97%) compared to conventional oral examination
- Best used as **adjunct to clinical assessment**, not standalone diagnostic
- Useful for delineating subclinical field cancerization margins

### 2. Narrow-Band Imaging (NBI)

- Uses filtered light (415 nm blue, 540 nm green) to enhance mucosal vasculature
- **Intrapapillary capillary loop (IPCL)** patterns correlate with dysplasia grade
- Sensitivity 95%, specificity 97% for dysplasia detection in oropharynx
- Limitations: requires endoscopic equipment, learning curve

### 3. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

- Non-invasive, real-time cross-sectional imaging at 10–20 μm resolution
- Penetration depth: 1–2 mm — sufficient for mucosal assessment
- Can identify epithelial thickening, loss of basement membrane integrity
- Emerging role in **intraoperative margin assessment**

**Source:** Harrison LB et al. *Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach*. 4th ed. Lippincott; 2014. Chapter 16A.

## Fluorescence-Guided Surgery (FGS)

### Principle

FGS uses **systemically administered fluorescent agents** that preferentially accumulate in tumour tissue, allowing real-time visualization of cancer margins during surgery using near-infrared (NIR) camera systems.

### Key Fluorescent Agents

| Agent | Target/Mechanism | Wavelength | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cetuximab-IRDye800CW | EGFR-targeted | 800 nm NIR | Phase II trials |
| Pafolacianine (OTL38) | Folate receptor-α | 780 nm NIR | FDA-approved (lung, 2022) |
| ICG (indocyanine green) | EPR effect, perfusion | 820 nm NIR | Off-label use |
| 5-ALA | PpIX accumulation | 635 nm red | Used in glioma, studied in oral |
| ABY-029 | EGFR affibody | 700 nm NIR | Phase I |

### Cetuximab-IRDye800CW

The most studied agent for head and neck FGS:
- Cetuximab (anti-EGFR monoclonal antibody) conjugated to IRDye800CW near-infrared dye
- **Tumour-to-background ratio (TBR):** 3.0–5.0 in oral SCC
- Rosenthal et al. (2015): first-in-human dose-escalation study demonstrated feasibility and safety in head and neck cancer patients
- Real-time tumour visualization with sub-millimetre sensitivity
- Can detect positive margins intraoperatively that would be missed on visual inspection alone

### 5-Aminolevulinic Acid (5-ALA)

- Topical or systemic administration → metabolized to protoporphyrin IX (PpIX) in tumour cells
- PpIX fluoresces under 405 nm violet-blue light
- Studied for oral premalignant and malignant lesion delineation
- Limitation: shallow penetration, ambient light interference

**Source:** Montgomery PQ et al. *Principles and Practice of Head and Neck Surgery*. 2nd ed. CRC Press; 2009. Chapter 11.

## Impact of Margin Status on Outcomes

### Positive Margins

- Local recurrence rate: **30–60%** with positive margins vs 5–15% with clear margins
- 5-year OS reduction: approximately **15–25%** decrease
- Even with adjuvant RT, positive margins carry worse prognosis than clear margins

### Close Margins (<5 mm)

- Intermediate recurrence risk between positive and adequate margins
- Adjuvant RT reduces local recurrence in close-margin cases
- Molecular margins (LOH, p53) may persist even with histologically clear margins — relates to [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Field Cancerization|field cancerization]]

### Deep vs Mucosal Margins

| Margin Location | Positive Margin Rate | Impact on Recurrence |
|---|---|---|
| Mucosal (superficial) | 5–10% | Moderate; re-excision feasible |
| Deep (soft tissue) | 10–20% | High; re-excision often difficult |
| Bone (mandibular) | 3–5% | Very high; segmental mandibulectomy needed |

## Emerging Technologies

### Raman Spectroscopy
- Detects molecular fingerprint of tissue in real-time
- Can distinguish normal, dysplastic, and malignant tissue
- Intraoperative probe-based systems in development

### Confocal Laser Endomicroscopy (CLE)
- In vivo microscopy at cellular resolution
- Can identify architectural and cytological atypia without biopsy
- Requires fluorescein IV injection
- Sensitivity >90% for oral dysplasia

### Diffuse Reflectance Spectroscopy
- Measures tissue optical properties (scattering, absorption)
- Alterations in nuclear size and vascularity detected
- Handheld probe format for surgical use

### Specimen-Driven vs Patient-Driven Margins
- **Specimen-driven:** Pathologist inks and sections the resected specimen — standard approach
- **Patient-driven:** Surgeon takes separate margin samples from the tumour bed — higher sensitivity for residual disease
- Combination approach gaining favour for high-risk cases

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The 5 mm clear margin is the gold standard in oral SCC; surgeons should target ≥1 cm intraoperatively to account for 20–30% formalin shrinkage
> 2. Frozen section remains the standard for intraoperative assessment (sensitivity 90–97%) but has sampling error limitations
> 3. VELscope tissue autofluorescence has high sensitivity (84–100%) but low specificity (15–74%) — best as adjunct for delineating field change extent
> 4. Cetuximab-IRDye800CW is the most promising fluorescence-guided surgery agent for oral SCC, achieving tumour-to-background ratios of 3–5×
> 5. Positive margins increase local recurrence 2.7× and mortality 1.6× even with adjuvant radiotherapy
> 6. Deep margins are the most frequently positive in oral cancer and the most difficult to re-excise
> 7. Zanoni et al. proposed redefining close margin as ≤2.2 mm based on survival analysis — may change clinical practice
> 8. NBI, OCT, and Raman spectroscopy are emerging adjuncts for real-time margin assessment
> 9. Patient-driven (tumour bed) margins may have higher sensitivity than specimen-driven margins for detecting residual disease
> 10. Molecular margins (LOH, p53) can persist beyond histologically clear margins, linking margin assessment to field cancerization biology

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Deep margin** is the Achilles heel of oral cancer surgery — always assess with frozen section and consider patient-driven sampling from the tumour bed
> 2. VELscope is most useful for **delineating extent of field change** around visible tumours, not as a standalone screening tool — false-positive rate is high (inflammation, melanin)
> 3. When frozen section shows a close margin, **re-excise immediately** rather than relying on adjuvant RT to compensate — clear margins + RT > close margins + RT
> 4. In the **anterior FOM**, fluorescence techniques have particular value because the mandible limits the depth of re-excision possible
> 5. **Cetuximab-IRDye800CW** detected additional positive margins in 15–20% of cases where conventional assessment was negative — a potential paradigm shift in margin management
