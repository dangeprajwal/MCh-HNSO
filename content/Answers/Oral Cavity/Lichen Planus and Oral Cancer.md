## Introduction

Oral lichen planus (OLP) is a chronic T cell-mediated inflammatory mucosal disease affecting 1–2% of the adult population. The WHO classifies OLP as an **oral potentially malignant disorder (OPMD)**, recognising that patients with OLP have a statistically increased risk of developing oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC). However, the association between OLP and oral cancer remains one of the most debated topics in oral medicine — the magnitude of the risk, the mechanisms involved, and whether OLP genuinely undergoes "malignant transformation" or simply co-exists with cancer continue to be questioned. For a detailed review of OLP clinical features, classification, and management, see [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Lichen Planus|Oral Lichen Planus]].

## The Diagnostic Problem: OLP vs OLL

A fundamental challenge in studying malignant transformation is the diagnostic distinction between **true OLP** and **oral lichenoid lesions (OLL)**:

| Feature | Oral Lichen Planus (OLP) | Oral Lichenoid Lesion (OLL) |
|---|---|---|
| Aetiology | Idiopathic autoimmune | Identifiable cause (drug, contact, GVHD) |
| Distribution | Bilateral, symmetric | Often unilateral |
| Clinical pattern | Reticular, papular, plaque | Mixed, atypical |
| Histology | Band-like lymphocytic infiltrate, basal cell degeneration | Similar but with eosinophils/plasma cells, deeper infiltrate |
| Transformation risk | ~1.1% | ~2.5% (higher) |

The **modified WHO criteria (van der Meij 2003)** require both clinical AND histopathological criteria to be met for an OLP diagnosis. Many older studies reporting high transformation rates did not apply strict diagnostic criteria, likely including OLL cases in their OLP cohorts — this inflates the reported transformation rate.

## Evidence for Malignant Transformation

### Meta-Analytic Evidence

| Study | Patients | OLP → OSCC Rate | OLL → OSCC Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aghbari 2017 | 20,095 | 1.1% | 2.5% |
| Gonzalez-Moles 2008 | Review | 0.5–2.0% | Higher |
| Fitzpatrick 2014 | 7,806 | 1.09% | — |

**Key numbers for exam:**
- **Overall OLP malignant transformation rate: ~1.1%** (pooled estimate)
- **Annual transformation rate: ~0.2% per year**
- **OLL has a higher transformation rate (~2.5%)** than true OLP
- The risk, while statistically significant, is **low** — most OLP patients will never develop OSCC

### Clinical Features Associated With Higher Transformation Risk

| Risk Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| Erosive/atrophic type | Higher risk than reticular/plaque types |
| Tongue location | Lateral tongue OLP has highest risk |
| Tobacco/alcohol use | Synergistic risk with OLP |
| Female sex | Some studies show higher risk in women |
| Long disease duration | Cumulative risk increases with time |
| Hepatitis C association | Geographic variation; stronger in Mediterranean populations |
| Dysplasia on biopsy | Controversial — "OLP with dysplasia" may actually be OLL |

## Proposed Mechanisms of Malignant Transformation

### 1. Chronic Inflammation Model

OLP is characterised by persistent T cell-mediated inflammation with:
- Sustained release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-8)
- Chronic oxidative stress → DNA damage via reactive oxygen species (ROS)
- NF-κB pathway activation → promotes cell survival and proliferation
- Chronic inflammation mirrors the "tumour-promoting" phase of carcinogenesis

This is analogous to other inflammation-driven cancers (e.g., Barrett's oesophagus → oesophageal adenocarcinoma, ulcerative colitis → colorectal cancer).

### 2. Genetic and [[Answers/General/Epigenetics in Oncogenesis|epigenetics]] Alterations

Studies have demonstrated:
- **p53 overexpression** in OLP epithelium (10–60% of cases, vs <5% in normal mucosa)
- **Loss of heterozygosity (LOH)** at chromosomes 3p, 9p, 17p in OLP — same loci as early OSCC
- **Microsatellite instability** in a subset of OLP
- **Promoter hypermethylation** of [[Answers/General/Tumour Suppressor Genes|tumour suppressor genes]] (p16, p14)
- **[[Answers/General/Telomerase and Cancer|telomerase]] activation** in OLP epithelium

These molecular findings support the concept that OLP epithelium harbours pre-neoplastic genetic changes even before histological dysplasia is apparent.

### 3. Immunosurveillance Failure

- OLP involves breakdown of immunological tolerance to basal keratinocytes
- Chronic immunosuppressive therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors) may impair anti-tumour immunosurveillance
- Paradox: the treatment that controls OLP symptoms may theoretically increase transformation risk
- However, no clinical study has demonstrated that immunosuppressive treatment increases cancer incidence

### 4. Field Cancerization

OLP patients may have widespread mucosal field changes rendering the entire oral mucosa susceptible. This overlaps with the concept of [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Field Cancerization|field cancerization]] from tobacco/alcohol exposure, and the two may be synergistic.

## Controversies

### Arguments Against True Transformation

1. **Diagnostic misclassification** — many reported cases of "OLP → OSCC" may be OLL or lichenoid dysplasia misdiagnosed as OLP
2. **Recall bias** — patients with OLP are under regular surveillance, increasing cancer detection (surveillance bias)
3. **Co-existing risk factors** — many patients have concurrent tobacco/alcohol use
4. **Absence of dysplasia** — true OLP by definition lacks epithelial dysplasia; if dysplasia is present, it may be a different entity (lichenoid dysplasia)

### Arguments For True Transformation

1. **Consistent meta-analytic evidence** — even with strict criteria, transformation rate is ~1.1%
2. **Molecular evidence** — p53 overexpression, LOH, MSI in OLP epithelium
3. **Biological plausibility** — chronic inflammation is an established cancer promoter
4. **WHO classification** — OLP is officially listed as an OPMD

### Current Consensus

OLP is an OPMD with a **low but real risk** of malignant transformation (~1.1%). The risk is sufficient to warrant **long-term clinical surveillance** but not aggressive enough to justify prophylactic treatment or surgery in the absence of dysplasia or suspicious change.

## Management Implications

### Surveillance Protocol

- **All OLP patients** should be on lifelong follow-up (minimum 6-monthly)
- Clinical examination with photographic documentation at each visit
- **Biopsy indications:**
  - Any new or changing erosive/ulcerative lesion
  - Non-healing ulcer within OLP >3 weeks
  - New induration or mass within OLP
  - Persistent unilateral lesion (may be OLL rather than OLP)
  - Any clinical suspicion of malignancy
- Incisional biopsy from the most suspicious area (erythematous/erosive zones, not reticular striae)

### Risk Factor Modification

- **Tobacco and alcohol cessation** — mandatory; synergistic risk
- **Treat Hepatitis C** if present
- **Optimise oral hygiene** — reduce chronic mucosal irritation
- **Minimise immunosuppressive therapy** duration — use lowest effective dose

### When Cancer Develops in OLP

- Managed as standard OSCC — staging, surgical excision ± neck dissection ± adjuvant therapy
- The surrounding OLP mucosa remains at risk → close surveillance of remaining mucosa
- Field cancerization concept applies — second primaries are possible
- Consider wider excision margins if feasible due to the field effect

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. OLP is classified by WHO as an oral potentially malignant disorder (OPMD) with a malignant transformation rate of ~1.1% (Aghbari 2017 meta-analysis, 20,095 patients)
> 2. Oral lichenoid lesions (OLL) have a higher transformation rate (~2.5%) than true OLP — diagnostic distinction is critical
> 3. Modified WHO criteria (van der Meij 2003) require both clinical AND histopathological criteria for OLP diagnosis
> 4. Erosive/atrophic type OLP carries higher transformation risk than reticular type
> 5. Proposed mechanisms: chronic inflammation → oxidative DNA damage → NF-κB activation; p53 overexpression; LOH at 3p, 9p, 17p
> 6. The chronic inflammation model parallels Barrett's oesophagus and ulcerative colitis as inflammation-driven precancerous conditions
> 7. Controversy persists: diagnostic misclassification, surveillance bias, and co-existing risk factors may inflate reported transformation rates
> 8. Immunosuppressive therapy for OLP has not been shown to increase cancer risk clinically, despite theoretical concerns
> 9. All OLP patients require lifelong follow-up (minimum 6-monthly) with biopsy of any suspicious change
> 10. When cancer develops in OLP, it is managed as standard OSCC, but the remaining mucosa is at continued risk (field effect)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The 1.1% transformation rate is the most commonly cited figure — remember Aghbari 2017 (20,095 patients) for the exam
> - The OLP vs OLL distinction is not academic — it has real clinical implications. OLL has double the transformation rate, and many "OLP → cancer" cases may actually be misclassified OLL
> - Erosive OLP on the lateral tongue is your highest-risk scenario — this is the subtype that deserves the most vigilant surveillance
> - The biological parallel to draw in exams: OLP → OSCC is analogous to Barrett's → oesophageal adenocarcinoma (chronic inflammation → cancer, ~1% annual transformation rate in both)
> - Never reassure an OLP patient that their condition is benign — it is a lifelong OPMD requiring surveillance. But equally, don't cause alarm — 99% of OLP patients will not develop cancer
