## Introduction

The role of neoadjuvant (induction) chemotherapy in oral cavity squamous cell carcinoma remains one of the most debated topics in head and neck oncology. Unlike oropharyngeal and laryngeal cancers where chemotherapy plays an integral role in organ preservation strategies, the standard treatment for oral cavity cancer is upfront surgery followed by adjuvant therapy as indicated. Neoadjuvant chemotherapy has been investigated for potential benefits including tumour downsizing, mandibular preservation, reduction of distant metastasis, and selection of chemosensitive patients. However, multiple RCTs have consistently failed to demonstrate an overall survival benefit. Despite this, emerging evidence supports its use in specific clinical scenarios — particularly mandibular preservation and converting borderline resectable or unresectable tumours to resectable ones.

## Rationale for Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy

### Theoretical Advantages

1. **Tumour downsizing** — reduction in tumour volume may allow less extensive surgery, preserving form and function
2. **Mandibular preservation** — if tumour abutting the mandible responds to chemotherapy, marginal mandibulectomy may replace segmental resection
3. **In vivo chemosensitivity testing** — clinical response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy may predict response to adjuvant treatment and guide post-surgical management
4. **Treatment of micrometastases** — early systemic therapy may eradicate occult distant disease
5. **Improved surgical margins** — tumour shrinkage may facilitate R0 resection
6. **Better drug delivery** — intact tumour vasculature (pre-surgery) allows optimal drug delivery

### Theoretical Disadvantages

1. **Delay to definitive surgery** — the most important concern; 6–9 weeks of chemotherapy delays curative resection
2. **Non-responders progress** — tumours that do not respond may become unresectable during the delay
3. **No proven survival benefit** — multiple RCTs have failed to demonstrate overall survival advantage
4. **Treatment-related toxicity** — mucositis, myelosuppression, nephrotoxicity may compromise subsequent surgery
5. **Altered surgical planes** — post-chemotherapy fibrosis may make surgical dissection more difficult
6. **False sense of margin adequacy** — clinical tumour shrinkage may not reflect the true extent of microscopic disease

## Key Randomised Controlled Trials

### Licitra Trial (2003) — The First RCT

- **Population:** 195 patients with resectable T2 (>3 cm) – T4, N0-N2 oral cavity SCC
- **Arms:** 3 cycles neoadjuvant cisplatin + 5-FU → surgery vs upfront surgery alone
- **Results:**
  - Clinical response rate: 80% (33% complete + 49% partial)
  - Pathological complete response (pCR): 8%
  - Mandible resection: NACT 31% vs surgery 52% (**p = 0.01**)
  - **No difference in overall survival** (median follow-up 11.5 years — Bossi 2014)
  - Patients with pCR had significantly better 10-year OS (76.2% vs 41.3%)

### Zhong Trial (2013) — Phase III TPF Trial

- **Population:** 256 patients with stage III–IVA locally advanced resectable oral SCC
- **Arms:** 2 cycles neoadjuvant TPF → surgery + PORT vs upfront surgery + PORT
- **Results:**
  - Clinical response rate: 80.6%
  - pCR: 13.4%
  - **No difference in DFS or OS** (median follow-up 30 months)
  - Trend towards fewer distant metastases in NACT arm (5.5% vs 8.7%, NS)
  - Responders had superior OS and locoregional/distant control

### Chaukar Trial (TMH, 2022) — Mandibular Preservation

- **Population:** Treatment-naive oral cavity SCC, cT2-T4, N0/N+, M0
- **Arms:** Upfront segmental mandibulectomy vs 2 cycles NACT (TPF) → surgery guided by response
- **Primary endpoint:** Mandibular preservation
- **Results:**
  - **47% mandibular preservation rate** in the NACT arm
  - No compromise in oncological outcomes
  - Supports NACT specifically for the goal of mandibular preservation

## MACH-NC Meta-Analysis (Pignon 2009)

The Meta-Analysis of Chemotherapy in Head and Neck Cancer (MACH-NC) is the most comprehensive analysis of chemotherapy timing in HNSCC:

- **93 RCTs, 17,346 patients** (all HNSCC sites, not oral cavity-specific)
- **Induction chemotherapy:** 5% absolute survival benefit at 5 years (HR 0.96, p = 0.10 — not statistically significant when restricted to induction)
- **Concurrent chemoradiotherapy:** 6.5% absolute benefit (HR 0.81, p < 0.0001 — clearly significant)
- **Adjuvant chemotherapy:** No benefit
- The benefit of induction chemotherapy was most pronounced with taxane-containing regimens (TPF > PF)
- **Key limitation:** The meta-analysis includes all HNSCC sites; oral cavity-specific data does not support the same conclusions

## Current Evidence Summary

| Parameter | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Overall survival benefit | Not demonstrated (multiple RCTs) |
| Clinical response rate | 80% (consistent across trials) |
| Pathological CR rate | 8–13% |
| Mandibular preservation | Supported (Chaukar 2022) |
| Distant metastasis reduction | Trend but not significant |
| pCR patients | Significantly better survival |

## Current Indications

### Accepted/Emerging Indications

1. **Mandibular preservation** — when tumour abuts but does not invade the mandible, NACT may downstage sufficiently to allow marginal mandibulectomy instead of segmental resection (Chaukar 2022)
2. **Borderline resectable/unresectable tumours** — NACT may convert technically unresectable T4b tumours to resectable ones
3. **Organ preservation intent** — reducing extent of resection to preserve critical structures (tongue, mandible)
4. **In vivo chemosensitivity testing** — response to NACT may guide decisions about adjuvant treatment

### Not Recommended As Standard

- **Routine use in all resectable oral cancer** — Level I evidence does not support NACT as standard for all operable oral cavity SCC
- Surgery remains the primary treatment modality

## Chemotherapy Regimens

### TPF (Docetaxel, Cisplatin, 5-Fluorouracil)

- **Standard NACT regimen** (superior to PF based on TAX 323 and TAX 324 trials in HNSCC)
- Docetaxel 75 mg/m² day 1 + cisplatin 75 mg/m² day 1 + 5-FU 750 mg/m²/day continuous infusion days 1–5
- Repeat every 3 weeks × 2–3 cycles
- Requires G-CSF prophylaxis

### PF (Cisplatin, 5-Fluorouracil)

- Cisplatin 100 mg/m² day 1 + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/day continuous infusion days 1–5
- Older regimen; inferior to TPF in HNSCC trials
- Less frequently used currently

## Response Assessment

- **Clinical examination** after each cycle
- **Imaging (CT/MRI)** after completion of NACT (before surgery)
- **RECIST criteria** for objective response assessment
- Surgery should proceed **within 3–4 weeks** of completing NACT
- **Non-responders must proceed to surgery immediately** — further chemotherapy is futile and delays curative treatment

## Patient Selection

The key to successful neoadjuvant therapy in oral cancer is **careful patient selection**:

- Good performance status (ECOG 0–1)
- Adequate organ function (renal, hepatic, haematological)
- Tumour abutting but not frankly invading the mandible (mandibular preservation candidate)
- Locally advanced but borderline resectable tumour where downsizing may reduce surgical morbidity
- Patient counselled that NACT delays surgery by 6–9 weeks and carries no proven survival benefit
- Multidisciplinary tumour board decision

## Future Directions

- **Neoadjuvant immunotherapy** — anti-PD-1 agents (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) showing promising pathological response rates in early-phase trials
- **Combination chemo-immunotherapy** — TPF + anti-PD-1 being studied in ongoing trials
- **Biomarker-guided selection** — identifying molecular predictors of chemotherapy response to select patients most likely to benefit
- **Window-of-opportunity trials** — short courses of targeted therapy before surgery to study tumour biology

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Neoadjuvant chemotherapy in oral cavity cancer does NOT improve overall survival (Level I evidence — Licitra 2003, Zhong 2013)
> 2. Clinical response rate to NACT is consistently ~80%, but pCR is only 8–13%
> 3. Patients achieving pathological complete response have significantly better survival
> 4. TPF is superior to PF as the NACT regimen (based on TAX 323/324 in HNSCC)
> 5. Mandibular preservation is the strongest evidence-based indication (Chaukar 2022 — 47% preservation rate)
> 6. NACT may convert borderline unresectable tumours to resectable ones
> 7. The main risk of NACT is delaying curative surgery by 6–9 weeks
> 8. Non-responders must proceed immediately to surgery — further chemotherapy is futile
> 9. MACH-NC meta-analysis: concurrent chemoRT (6.5% benefit) is superior to induction chemo (5% benefit) across HNSCC
> 10. Upfront surgery remains the standard of care for resectable oral cavity cancer

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The most compelling argument for NACT in oral cancer today is mandibular preservation — when a tumour is abutting but not invading the mandible, NACT response may allow marginal instead of segmental mandibulectomy, dramatically improving quality of life
> - Always counsel patients that NACT delays definitive surgery and has no proven survival benefit — informed consent is critical
> - Response to NACT is an excellent prognostic biomarker — pathological responders do well regardless of treatment sequencing
> - If a patient does not respond clinically after cycle 1 of NACT, proceed directly to surgery — there is no value in completing all planned cycles in a non-responder
> - Neoadjuvant immunotherapy is the most exciting emerging frontier in oral cancer — early trials show pCR rates of 15–20% with anti-PD-1 monotherapy, potentially higher with combination regimens
