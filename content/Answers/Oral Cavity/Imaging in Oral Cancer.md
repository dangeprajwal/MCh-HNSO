> Mukherji et al. (2001) published the landmark AJR study establishing CT diagnostic accuracy for mandibular invasion (sensitivity 96%, specificity 87%), providing the reference standard for bone assessment. Curtin et al. (1998) demonstrated that MRI was superior to CT for assessing perineural spread and soft tissue extent in oral cancer, establishing MRI as the preferred modality for primary site evaluation. Nörenberg et al. (2011) published the seminal review on oral cavity imaging establishing the complementary roles of CT and MRI. Baek et al. (2012) demonstrated that contrast-enhanced MRI had strong correlation with pathological DOI (r = 0.91) in oral tongue cancer. Shintani et al. (2010) evaluated the diagnostic value of OPG, CT, and MRI for mandibular invasion, showing CT was superior for cortical bone assessment and MRI for marrow invasion.

## Preoperative Imaging — Systematic Approach

### Goals of Imaging in Oral Cancer

1. **Define primary tumour extent** — size, depth of invasion (DOI), involvement of adjacent structures
2. **Assess mandibular invasion** — critical for surgical planning (marginal vs [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Types of Mandibulectomy|segmental mandibulectomy]])
3. **Evaluate cervical lymph nodes** — identify metastatic nodes, [[Answers/General/Extranodal Extension|extranodal extension]]
4. **Identify distant metastases** — lung, liver, bone
5. **Detect synchronous primaries** — second primary tumours in the upper aerodigestive tract
6. **Guide reconstruction planning** — vascularity, donor site assessment

### Standard Preoperative Workup (cT3N0 Lateral Tongue SCC)

| Investigation | Timing | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| MRI face and neck with contrast | Pre-treatment | Primary site extent, DOI, soft tissue, bone marrow |
| CT neck with contrast | Pre-treatment | Nodal assessment, mandible cortex |
| Chest CT | Pre-treatment | Lung metastases, second primary |
| OPG (orthopantomogram) | Pre-treatment | Dental status, mandible overview |
| [[Answers/General/PET-CT in Head and Neck Cancer|PET-CT]] | Selected cases | Distant metastases, equivocal nodes |
| Ultrasound-guided FNAC | When nodes equivocal | Cytological confirmation of nodal disease |

## MRI — Primary Tumour Assessment

### Role

MRI is the **modality of choice for primary tumour evaluation** in oral cancer because of superior soft tissue contrast.

### Technique

- T1-weighted (anatomy), T2-weighted (tumour bright against muscle), post-contrast T1 with fat saturation (enhancement)
- Axial and coronal planes; sagittal for tongue tumours
- 3 mm slice thickness for small tumours
- Diffusion-weighted imaging ([[Answers/General/Diffusion Weighted MRI|DWI]]) adds functional information

### What MRI Shows Best

| Feature | MRI Capability |
|---|---|
| Tumour extent in soft tissues | Excellent — best modality |
| [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Depth of Invasion in Oral Cancer|Depth of invasion]] | Correlation r = 0.87–0.95 with pathology |
| Bone marrow invasion | Superior to CT |
| Perineural spread | Best modality (fat-suppressed T1 post-contrast) |
| Tongue intrinsic/extrinsic muscles | Excellent |
| [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]] involvement | Good |
| Pre-epiglottic/paraglottic space | Good |

### DOI Measurement on MRI

- Measured on contrast-enhanced T1 or T2-weighted images
- Draw a line at the mucosal surface level → measure perpendicular depth to deepest tumour extent
- MRI **overestimates DOI by 1–3 mm** compared to pathology (due to peri-tumoural oedema)
- Baek et al. (2012): correlation r = 0.91 between MRI DOI and pathological DOI

### Limitations

- Motion artefact from [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]]/breathing
- Dental amalgam artefacts degrade images
- Overestimates tumour extent (peri-tumoural oedema)
- Limited for cortical bone assessment
- Longer acquisition time, claustrophobia, cost

## CT — Mandible and Nodal Assessment

### Role

CT is the **preferred modality for assessing mandibular cortical invasion** and provides excellent nodal assessment.

### Technique

- Contrast-enhanced MDCT with bone algorithm reconstruction
- Axial 1–2 mm slices through the oral cavity; coronal and sagittal reformats
- Bone window for cortical assessment; soft tissue window for nodal evaluation

### What CT Shows Best

| Feature | CT Capability |
|---|---|
| Mandible cortical erosion | Excellent — best modality |
| Mandible medullary invasion | Good (sclerosis, lysis) |
| Cervical lymph nodes | Excellent (size, morphology, necrosis) |
| Extranodal extension (ENE) | Good (irregular margin, fat stranding) |
| Distant metastases (chest CT) | Standard for staging |
| Dental status | Good |

### Mandible Assessment on CT

**Signs of mandibular invasion on CT:**
- Cortical erosion or destruction
- Widening of the inferior alveolar canal
- Replacement of marrow fat by soft tissue density
- Periosteal reaction
- Pathological fracture

**Mukherji 2001 diagnostic accuracy:**
- Sensitivity: 96%
- Specificity: 87%
- PPV: 89%
- NPV: 95%

### CT Criteria for Mandible Management

| CT Finding | Surgical Implication |
|---|---|
| No cortical contact | No mandibulectomy needed |
| Tumour abutting periosteum | Consider [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Marginal Mandibulectomy|marginal mandibulectomy]] |
| Cortical erosion without marrow | Marginal mandibulectomy |
| Marrow invasion | Segmental mandibulectomy |
| Inferior alveolar canal involvement | Segmental mandibulectomy |

## Orthopantomogram (OPG)

- **Screening tool** for dental status and gross mandibular pathology
- Low sensitivity for early cortical invasion (~50–60%)
- Useful for:
  - Pre-RT dental assessment (identifies teeth needing extraction)
  - Overall mandible survey
  - [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Dental Implants after Mandibular Reconstruction|dental implants]] planning for reconstruction
- **Not sufficient as sole imaging** for mandibular invasion — always supplement with CT ± MRI

## Intraoral Ultrasound

### Role

Emerging modality for **measuring tumour thickness/DOI in anterior oral tongue cancers**.

### Advantages

- Real-time imaging, no radiation, low cost
- Excellent resolution for superficial structures
- Correlation with pathological thickness r = 0.90
- Performed in outpatient clinic

### Limitations

- Operator dependent
- Limited to accessible anterior sites (tongue, floor of mouth)
- Cannot assess posterior tongue, retromolar, or deep structures
- Cannot evaluate bone or lymph nodes

## PET-CT

### Role

- Not routinely required for all oral cancers
- **Indications:**
  - Advanced stage (III–IV) — distant metastasis screening
  - Equivocal conventional imaging findings
  - Post-treatment surveillance (detecting recurrence)
  - Unknown primary with cervical metastasis

### Limitations in Oral Cancer

- False positives from post-surgical inflammation, dental infection
- Limited spatial resolution for small primary tumours
- FDG uptake in normal muscles of mastication
- Not useful for mandibular invasion assessment

## Advances in Imaging

### Dual-Energy CT (DECT)

- Improved bone marrow assessment using material decomposition algorithms
- Can differentiate tumour invasion from inflammatory changes in mandible
- Sensitivity and specificity comparable to MRI for marrow invasion
- Emerging technology, not yet standard

### Diffusion-Weighted MRI (DWI)

- ADC (apparent diffusion coefficient) values can differentiate malignant from benign tissue
- Helps distinguish recurrence from post-treatment changes
- Improves assessment of cervical lymph node metastasis
- Complementary to conventional MRI sequences

### Cone-Beam CT (CBCT)

- Higher spatial resolution than conventional CT for dental and mandibular assessment
- Lower radiation dose
- Useful for implant planning in reconstructed mandible
- Limited soft tissue contrast

### Narrow-Band Imaging ([[Answers/General/Narrow Band Imaging|NBI]])

- Endoscopic technique enhancing mucosal vasculature visualisation
- Improves detection of superficial oral mucosal lesions and margins
- Used intraoperatively to guide excision margins
- See also [[Answers/General/Surgical Margins in Head and Neck Cancer|surgical margins]] and fluorescence-guided surgery (autofluorescence, VELscope)

## Postoperative Imaging

### Timing and Indications

| Investigation | Timing | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Baseline CT/MRI | 3–6 months post-treatment | New baseline for surveillance |
| Chest CT | Annually for 5 years | Lung metastases/second primary |
| PET-CT | 3–4 months post-CRT | Assess treatment response |
| MRI | If recurrence suspected | Distinguish recurrence from fibrosis |

### Post-Treatment Challenges

- Post-surgical/RT changes make interpretation difficult
- Fibrosis, oedema, and flap tissue alter anatomy
- Contrast enhancement may persist for months
- DWI can help distinguish recurrence from fibrosis

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. MRI is the modality of choice for primary tumour assessment: soft tissue extent, DOI, perineural spread, bone marrow invasion
> 2. CT is preferred for mandibular cortical erosion assessment (Mukherji 2001: sensitivity 96%, specificity 87%) and cervical nodal evaluation
> 3. MRI DOI correlates with pathological DOI (r = 0.87–0.95) but overestimates by 1–3 mm due to peri-tumoural oedema
> 4. OPG is a screening tool for dental status and gross mandibular pathology but has low sensitivity for early cortical invasion (~50–60%)
> 5. CT signs of mandible invasion: cortical erosion, widening of IA canal, marrow replacement, periosteal reaction
> 6. PET-CT is reserved for advanced stage, equivocal findings, and post-treatment surveillance — not routine for all oral cancers
> 7. Intraoral ultrasound is an emerging tool for DOI measurement in anterior tongue (r = 0.90) — real-time, low cost, but operator dependent
> 8. Dual-energy CT and diffusion-weighted MRI are the most promising emerging advances
> 9. Post-treatment imaging should begin at 3–6 months to establish a new baseline after surgical and anatomical changes settle
> 10. Standard preoperative workup for cT3N0 oral SCC: MRI face/neck + CT neck + chest CT + OPG

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The AMRITA 2008 question asks for a complete imaging protocol for cT3N0 lateral tongue SCC — structure your answer as: preoperative (MRI + CT + chest CT + OPG), intraoperative ([[Answers/General/Frozen Section in Surgical Margins|frozen section]] margins), and postoperative (baseline scan at 3–6 months + annual chest CT)
> - For mandibular invasion: CT for cortical bone, MRI for marrow invasion — they are complementary, not interchangeable. In exam answers, always state both
> - MRI overestimating DOI by 1–3 mm is a commonly tested fact — mention it alongside the strong correlation coefficient to show you understand the practical limitation
> - When discussing "advances," focus on dual-energy CT, DWI-MRI, intraoral ultrasound, and NBI — these are the four most exam-relevant emerging technologies
> - For mandible management decisions, always correlate imaging with clinical assessment (mobility, anaesthesia of IAN) — imaging alone can be misleading in previously irradiated or infected bone
