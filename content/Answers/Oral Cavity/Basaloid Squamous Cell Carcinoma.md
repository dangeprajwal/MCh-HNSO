## Introduction

Basaloid squamous cell carcinoma (BSCC) is a high-grade histological variant of squamous cell carcinoma with distinctive morphologic features — basaloid cell nests with peripheral palisading, comedonecrosis, and an intimately associated squamous component. It has a predilection for the upper aerodigestive tract, particularly the hypopharynx (pyriform sinus), supraglottic larynx, base of tongue, and palatine tonsil. Once considered universally aggressive, the recognition that oropharyngeal BSCC is frequently HPV-associated has fundamentally altered the understanding of its prognosis.

## Epidemiology

- Accounts for approximately **1–6%** of all head and neck SCCs
- Male predominance (M:F = 3:1 to 10:1)
- Peak incidence in the 6th–7th decade of life
- Associated with heavy tobacco and alcohol use (non-oropharyngeal sites)
- HPV16-associated BSCC tends to occur in younger patients with less tobacco exposure

### Site Distribution

| Site | Frequency | HPV Association |
|---|---|---|
| Base of tongue | Most common oropharyngeal site | Strong (HPV16) |
| Palatine tonsil | Common | Strong (HPV16) |
| Pyriform sinus | Most common non-OP site | Rare |
| Supraglottic larynx | Common | Rare |
| Oral cavity | Uncommon | Variable |
| Sinonasal | Rare | Rare |

## Pathology

### Gross Features

- Firm to hard, tan-white mass with central necrosis
- May be exophytic but usually deeply invasive
- Often larger than suspected clinically — shallow biopsies may underestimate depth

### Histological Features

The hallmark is a **biphasic pattern** with basaloid and squamous components:

**Basaloid component** (predominant):
- Small, crowded cells with hyperchromatic nuclei, scant cytoplasm, and high mitotic activity
- Growth patterns: solid, lobular, cribriform, cords, trabeculae, and gland-like or cystic
- **Peripheral nuclear palisading** — similar to basal cell carcinoma
- **Comedonecrosis** — central necrosis within tumour lobules
- Intercellular deposition of **hyaline/mucohyaline material** — resembling basement membrane material of salivary gland tumours
- Rarely, spindle cell component or true neural-type rosettes

**Squamous component** (usually minor):
- Dysplastic squamous epithelium, foci of abrupt keratinization
- In situ SCC or invasive SCC
- May be focal and absent in small biopsies
- Continuity with surface epithelium may be present

### Immunohistochemistry

| Marker | Status in BSCC |
|---|---|
| Cytokeratins (AE1/3, 34βE12, CAM5.2) | Positive |
| EMA, CEA | Positive |
| p63 | Positive |
| p16 (HPV surrogate) | Positive in oropharyngeal BSCC |
| Chromogranin, synaptophysin | Usually negative |
| S-100 | Negative |

### Cell of Origin

The cell of origin is believed to be a **single totipotential cell** in the basal layer of surface epithelium or within seromucous glands, capable of divergent differentiation.

## Differential Diagnosis

The critical differential diagnoses include:

1. **Adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC)** — also shows basaloid cells with cribriform pattern, but ACC has myoepithelial differentiation (positive for S-100, SMA, calponin), lacks squamous component, and typically arises from [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Minor Salivary Gland Tumours of the Oral Cavity|minor salivary gland tumours]]
2. **Small cell neuroendocrine carcinoma** — positive for neuroendocrine markers (synaptophysin, chromogranin), negative for p63
3. **Adenosquamous carcinoma** — has true glandular differentiation (mucin-producing glands)
4. **Poorly differentiated SCC** — lacks the organised basaloid lobular pattern, comedonecrosis, and hyaline material

## HPV-Related vs Non-HPV-Related BSCC

This is the most clinically important distinction in BSCC:

| Feature | HPV-Positive BSCC | HPV-Negative BSCC |
|---|---|---|
| Site | Oropharynx (tonsil, BOT) | Larynx, hypopharynx |
| Age | Younger (50s) | Older (60–70s) |
| Risk factors | HPV16, sexual behaviour | Tobacco, alcohol |
| p16 IHC | Strongly positive | Negative |
| [[Answers/General/EBV Testing in Head and Neck Cancer|EBV]] | Negative | Negative |
| Prognosis | Favourable (similar to HPV+ SCC) | Poor |
| Treatment response | Good response to CRT | Aggressive behaviour |

Chernock et al. (2010) showed that HPV-positive BSCC is a molecularly distinct subtype with a transcriptionally active HPV and overexpression of p16. These tumours behave similarly to HPV-positive conventional oropharyngeal SCC and should not be classified as "high-grade" in the traditional sense.

## Clinical Behaviour and Prognosis

- Non-oropharyngeal BSCC is genuinely aggressive — high rates of nodal and distant metastasis, 5-year survival 30–50%
- Oropharyngeal BSCC (HPV-associated) has 5-year survival >70%, comparable to conventional HPV-positive SCC
- Fritsch and Lentsch (2014) analysis of 541 BSCC cases from SEER showed that **location is the strongest prognostic factor** — oropharyngeal BSCC had significantly better survival than hypopharyngeal, laryngeal, or oral cavity BSCC
- Lymph node metastasis at presentation is common (60–70%)
- Distant metastases occur in 20–30% (lung, bone, liver, brain)

## Management

Treatment follows the same principles as conventional SCC of the respective site:

- **Early stage (T1-T2)** — Surgery or radiation therapy
- **Locally advanced** — Combined modality (surgery + [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Oral Cancer|adjuvant RT]]/CRT, or definitive CRT)
- **Oropharyngeal BSCC** — Treated as per HPV-positive oropharyngeal SCC protocols if p16-positive (consideration of de-escalation in clinical trials)
- **Non-oropharyngeal BSCC** — Aggressive multimodality treatment warranted given poor prognosis; no evidence that treatment should differ from conventional SCC of the same site

HPV testing (p16 IHC ± HPV ISH) is mandatory in all oropharyngeal BSCC for prognostic stratification and treatment planning.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. BSCC is a high-grade variant of SCC with basaloid lobular growth, comedonecrosis, hyaline material, and a minor squamous component
> 2. Predilection for hypopharynx, supraglottic larynx, base of tongue, and tonsil
> 3. Cell of origin is a totipotential cell in the basal layer of surface epithelium
> 4. HPV-positive oropharyngeal BSCC has favourable prognosis — similar to conventional HPV+ SCC
> 5. Non-oropharyngeal BSCC is genuinely aggressive with 5-year survival 30–50%
> 6. Location is the strongest prognostic factor (Fritsch & Lentsch 2014)
> 7. IHC: positive for CK, EMA, p63; negative for neuroendocrine markers — distinguishes from small cell carcinoma
> 8. Must be distinguished from adenoid cystic carcinoma (S-100+, myoepithelial markers+)
> 9. HPV testing is mandatory in all oropharyngeal BSCC

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - A shallow biopsy may miss the squamous component — request deeper biopsy if basaloid pattern is seen without squamous differentiation
> - Cystic neck metastases are common in HPV-positive BSCC — do not mistake for branchial cleft cyst
> - The hyaline basement membrane material can mimic ACC — check for S-100 and myoepithelial markers
> - BSCC of the oral cavity is uncommon and does not benefit from the favourable HPV-associated prognosis seen in oropharyngeal cases
> - When BSCC arises in the oropharynx, treat and prognosticate based on HPV status, not the basaloid morphology
