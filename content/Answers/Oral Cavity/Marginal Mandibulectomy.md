## Introduction

Marginal mandibulectomy (rim resection) involves removal of the alveolar portion of the mandible while preserving the inferior border and its continuity. This technique is central to oral cancer surgery, allowing oncologically adequate resection of tumours abutting the mandible without the functional and aesthetic morbidity of segmental mandibulectomy. The key to appropriate patient selection lies in understanding the pathways of mandibular invasion and the biomechanical limits of the residual bone.

## Pathways of Mandibular Invasion

### Entry Points

- Tumour reaches the mandible at the **point of abutment** — the junction of attached and reflected mucosa (gingivobuccal/gingivolingual sulcus)
- **No lymphatic channels** pass through the mandible — the historical rationale for routine segmental resection ("commando operation") is obsolete

### Dentate Patients

- Tumour extends from sulcus to alveolar process → enters **cancellous bone through dental pores** in the alveolar ridge
- **Cortical bone inferior to tooth roots remains uninvolved** — this is the biological basis for marginal mandibulectomy
- The intact inferior cortex and mandibular canal provide a natural barrier

### Edentulous Patients

- Alveolar ridge atrophy reduces vertical height → tumour reaches cortical bone more easily
- **Pipestem mandible** (severely atrophic): marginal mandibulectomy almost impossible — iatrogenic fracture risk
- Feasibility depends on residual vertical height of mandible body

### Patterns of Bone Invasion (Wong et al. 2000)

| Pattern | Histology | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Erosive** | Broad advancing front, osteoclast-mediated resorption | Better prognosis |
| **Infiltrative** | Finger-like projections/islands of tumour into bone | 4× increased risk of death |

- Large, deep tumours more likely to demonstrate infiltrative pattern
- Infiltrative pattern associated with greater width and [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Depth of Invasion in Oral Cancer|depth of invasion]], higher positive margin rate

## Indications and Contraindications

### Indications for Marginal Mandibulectomy

- Tumour abutting or adherent to mandibular periosteum
- Superficial cortical bone involvement (erosive pattern)
- Tumour in gingivobuccal/gingivolingual sulcus with adequate mandibular height
- Retromolar trigone tumours with no cortical invasion on imaging (coronoid process + adjacent alveolar process)

### Contraindications (Proceed to Segmental Mandibulectomy)

| Contraindication | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Gross cancellous bone invasion | Tumour extends beyond alveolar bone |
| Invasion of inferior alveolar nerve/canal | Deep bone involvement |
| Massive soft tissue disease abutting mandible | Inadequate soft tissue clearance |
| Previously irradiated mandible | Multifocal cortical invasion pattern; high fracture/[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Osteoradionecrosis of the Mandible|ORN]] risk |
| Edentulous pipestem mandible | Insufficient residual bone height |
| Primary bone tumour or metastasis | Requires wide bone resection |
| Residual mandibular height <10 mm after resection | Fracture risk unacceptable |

### Imaging for Decision-Making

- **Coronal CT** views essential to assess craniocaudal relationship of tumour to mandible height
- **OPG (Orthopantomogram)**: screening for gross bone invasion
- **MRI**: superior for medullary invasion, bone marrow oedema
- Even without demonstrable bone invasion, marginal resection may NOT be safe if tumour extends along the entire height of the mandible

## Surgical Technique

### Types of Marginal Mandibulectomy

| Type | Description | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **Superior (horizontal)** | Resection of upper alveolar portion | FOM/alveolar tumours |
| **Sagittal (lingual plate)** | Resection of inner cortical table only | Lingual sulcus tumours |
| **Combined** | Both lingual plate and alveolar crest | Extensive sulcus involvement |
| **Posterior marginal** | Coronoid process + posterior alveolar ridge | Retromolar trigone tumours |
| **Reverse marginal** | Resection of lower cortex | Prevascular facial nodes fixed to lower border |

### Technical Principles (Jatin Shah)

1. **Avoid right-angled cuts** — lead to stress concentration and spontaneous fracture; all cuts should follow a **smooth curve**
2. Mark osteotomy with pencil/burr before cutting
3. Oscillating saw preferred for precision; avoid Gigli saw (irregular cuts)
4. Maintain **at least one-third** of vertical mandibular height (minimum 10 mm residual)
5. **Do not extract teeth** near the tumour preoperatively — opens dental sockets, risks tumour implantation into mandibular canal, compromises feasibility
6. Exposed bone covered by: primary mucosal closure, split-thickness skin graft, or radial forearm free flap

### Approach Options

- **Peroral**: small alveolar carcinomas, adequate exposure
- **Visor flap** (lower lip-sparing): avoids lip split
- **Lip-split/[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Access Mandibulotomy|access mandibulotomy]]**: for posterior tumours requiring wider access
- **Note**: combination of mandibulotomy + posterior marginal mandibulectomy should be avoided (increased ORN risk)

## Oncologic Outcomes

### Marginal vs Segmental Mandibulectomy

| Study | Marginal | Segmental | Conclusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Barttelbort 1987 | Local control 75% | Local control 64% | Rim resection oncologically equivalent |
| Munoz Guerra 2003 | No survival difference | — | Tumour stage more important than resection type |
| Dubner & Heller | Local recurrence 19% | Local recurrence 6% | Higher recurrence in marginal, but not size/stage dependent |
| Chen 2011 | Local control 85.7% (with bone invasion) | — | Superficial bone invasion not a contraindication |

### Key Oncologic Points

- Marginal mandibulectomy is **oncologically safe** when appropriately selected
- Recurrence after marginal resection is in soft tissue, not bone — reflects inadequate soft tissue margins rather than inadequate bone resection
- Recurrence after segmental resection is also in extraosseous soft tissue (not residual bone)
- **Bone margin of 1 cm** in soft tissue and bone recommended for alveolar carcinomas

## Functional Outcomes

### Advantages Over Segmental Mandibulectomy

- Preserves mandibular continuity → maintains facial contour and symmetry
- Maintains lower lip sensation (mental nerve preserved if anterior to resection)
- Preserves muscular attachments (mylohyoid, geniohyoid, genioglossus)
- Allows dental rehabilitation (implant placement on residual bone)
- No need for bone graft or free flap reconstruction in most cases
- Superior cosmetic outcome and patient satisfaction

### Complications

- **Pathological fracture**: main risk — minimised by smooth curved cuts, adequate residual height (>10 mm), reconstruction plate if thin bone bridge remains
- **Osteomyelitis/ORN**: if postoperative RT planned, risk is lower than after segmental resection but still present
- **Malocclusion**: if insufficient bone remains or cuts extend into body

> [!abstract] Key Points
> - Marginal mandibulectomy preserves mandibular continuity by resecting the alveolar portion while maintaining the inferior border
> - Tumour enters the mandible through dental pores in the alveolar crest — cortical bone inferior to tooth roots remains uninvolved (basis for rim resection)
> - Infiltrative pattern of invasion (vs erosive) carries 4× worse prognosis and higher positive margin rates
> - Contraindicated in: gross bone invasion, irradiated mandible, pipestem mandible, IAN canal involvement, residual height <10 mm
> - Oncologic outcomes are equivalent to segmental mandibulectomy when properly selected

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Never extract teeth** adjacent to an oral cancer preoperatively — this opens a pathway for tumour seeding into the mandibular canal and compromises the option for marginal mandibulectomy
> - **Coronal CT** is the key imaging for surgical planning — assess the craniocaudal relationship of tumour to mandible height
> - All osteotomy cuts must follow a **smooth curve** — right-angled cuts create stress risers leading to pathological fracture
> - If residual mandibular height is <10 mm after rim resection, **convert to segmental mandibulectomy** with reconstruction rather than risk fracture
> - In irradiated patients, mandibular invasion follows a **multifocal cortical pattern** rather than the predictable alveolar pattern — marginal mandibulectomy is unreliable in this setting
