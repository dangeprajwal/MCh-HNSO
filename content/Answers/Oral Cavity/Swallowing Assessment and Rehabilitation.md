## Introduction

Dysphagia is one of the most significant functional consequences of head and neck cancer treatment, affecting up to 60% of patients after surgery, radiotherapy, or chemoradiotherapy. Aspiration occurs in up to 36% of unselected HNC patients and up to 82% of those who complain of dysphagia — and critically, 36–80% of those who aspirate do so silently, without cough or clinical signs. This makes instrumental assessment essential rather than optional. Swallowing rehabilitation in HNC encompasses assessment tools (clinical and instrumental), compensatory strategies, rehabilitative exercises, and prosthetic interventions. Understanding the normal physiology of swallowing is prerequisite to managing its disorders.

## Normal Swallowing Physiology

### Phases of Swallowing

| Phase | Structures | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Oral preparatory | Lips, teeth, tongue, palate | Mastication, bolus formation |
| Oral transit | Tongue, floor of mouth | Tongue propels bolus posteriorly |
| Pharyngeal | Tongue base, pharynx, larynx | Pharyngeal contraction, airway closure, UES relaxation |
| Oesophageal | Oesophagus | Peristalsis to stomach |

### Key Structures for Safe Swallowing

- **Tongue base** — the most essential lingual region for swallowing; even after total oral tongue resection, swallowing is possible if the tongue base is preserved
- **Suprahyoid muscles** — elevate and anteriorly tilt the larynx during swallowing; disruption leads to aspiration
- **Epiglottic inversion** — deflects bolus away from airway
- **Vocal fold closure** — three-tier airway protection (true folds, false folds, epiglottis)

## Instrumental Swallowing Assessment

### Modified Barium Swallow (MBS) Study

Also known as the videofluoroscopic swallow study (VFSS), the MBS is the most widely used and generally preferred instrumental evaluation for oropharyngeal dysphagia.

**Technique:**
- Performed jointly by a speech pathologist and radiologist
- Patient swallows radiopaque liquids, pastes, and solids of varying consistencies under fluoroscopic guidance
- Lateral and anteroposterior views obtained
- Assessed in real-time and on slow-motion playback

**What MBS Assesses:**
- All three stages of the oropharyngeal swallow (oral preparatory, oral transit, pharyngeal)
- Bolus formation and oral transit time
- Tongue base retraction
- Pharyngeal contraction and residue
- Hyolaryngeal excursion
- Epiglottic inversion
- Upper oesophageal sphincter (UES) opening
- Aspiration (before, during, or after the swallow) and penetration
- Effectiveness of compensatory strategies in real-time

**Advantages:**
- Visualises all phases of swallowing including oral phase
- Allows real-time assessment of compensatory strategies
- Differentially diagnoses the mechanism of aspiration
- Gold standard for oropharyngeal dysphagia assessment

**Limitations:**
- Requires fluoroscopy suite (radiation exposure)
- Patient must be able to sit upright and cooperate
- Not portable — cannot be performed at bedside
- Provides limited view of mucosal detail/structural pathology

**MBS vs Traditional Barium Esophagram:**
- The traditional barium esophagram evaluates oesophageal structural integrity with a large continuous liquid bolus — it is NOT the evaluation of choice for oropharyngeal swallowing disorders
- The MBS uses small calibrated boluses of varying consistencies specifically to evaluate oropharyngeal physiology and guide rehabilitation

### Fiberoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing (FEES)

Introduced by Langmore et al. (1988), FEES is a complementary instrumental evaluation performed at the bedside.

**Technique:**
- Flexible nasopharyngoscope passed transnasally
- Positioned above the laryngeal inlet
- Patient swallows food and liquids coloured with food dye
- Direct visualisation of pharyngeal and laryngeal anatomy during swallowing

**What FEES Assesses:**
- Pharyngeal residue (before and after swallowing)
- Laryngeal penetration and aspiration (before and after the swallow)
- Glottic competency and vocal fold mobility
- Secretion management
- Effectiveness of compensatory strategies
- Can be paired with sensory testing (FEESST — air pulse to aryepiglottic fold)

**Advantages:**
- No radiation exposure
- Portable — performed at bedside, ICU, or in clinic
- Excellent view of laryngeal anatomy and vocal fold mobility
- Can be repeated frequently for monitoring
- Provides immediate biofeedback during therapy sessions

**Limitations:**
- Cannot visualise oral preparatory or oral transit phases
- "White-out" period at the onset of pharyngeal swallow obscures peak deglutition
- Less useful in oral cavity and oropharyngeal cancer patients (where oral phase is critical)
- Requires endoscopy expertise

### MBS vs FEES: Head-to-Head Comparison

| Parameter | MBS | FEES |
|---|---|---|
| Oral phase | Visualised | Not visualised |
| Pharyngeal phase | Visualised (including white-out) | White-out obscures peak |
| Aspiration detection | Equivalent | Equivalent |
| Radiation | Yes | No |
| Portability | No (fluoroscopy suite) | Yes (bedside) |
| Biofeedback | Limited | Excellent |
| Mucosal detail | No | Yes |
| Sensory testing | No | Yes (FEESST) |
| Best for | Oral/oropharyngeal tumours | Laryngeal/pharyngeal tumours |
| Repeatability | Limited (radiation) | Easily repeatable |

FEES has been shown to be equivalent to MBS in its ability to detect aspiration. The choice depends on the clinical scenario: MBS is preferred for oral cavity and oropharyngeal tumour patients; FEES is preferred for laryngeal tumour patients and for bedside/ICU assessment.

## Other Assessment Tools

### Clinical Bedside Swallow Evaluation (CBE)

- Cranial nerve examination, oral motor assessment, trial swallows
- Screens for dysphagia but cannot detect silent aspiration
- Should always be followed by instrumental assessment in HNC patients

### Penetration-Aspiration Scale (PAS)

- 8-point ordinal scale (Rosenbek et al. 1996) scored from MBS or FEES
- 1 = no penetration; 8 = silent aspiration (material enters airway, no cough)
- Standardised outcome measure for swallowing studies

### Performance Status Scale (PSS)

- 3-domain scale: Normalcy of Diet, Eating in Public, Understandability of Speech
- Clinician-rated functional outcome measure for HNC patients
- Correlates with tongue strength, jaw ROM, and saliva production

### MD Anderson Dysphagia Inventory (MDADI)

- Patient-reported outcome measure for swallowing-related quality of life
- 20-item questionnaire covering global, emotional, functional, and physical domains

## Swallowing Disorders After Oral/Oropharyngeal Surgery

### Mechanisms of Post-Surgical Dysphagia

| Resection | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Oral tongue | Loss of bolus control and propulsion | Poor oral transit, premature spillage |
| Tongue base | Loss of tongue base retraction | Pharyngeal residue, aspiration |
| Floor of mouth | Tethering, loss of hyoid mobility | Reduced hyolaryngeal excursion |
| Suprahyoid muscles | Loss of laryngeal elevation | Aspiration during swallowing |
| Mandible | Altered occlusion, loss of support | Oral phase dysfunction |
| Soft palate | Velopharyngeal incompetence | Nasal regurgitation |

### Swallowing Restoration After Base of Tongue Resection

The tongue base is the most critical structure for safe swallowing — its retraction generates the driving force that propels the bolus through the pharynx and protects the airway:

1. **Reconstruction:** Volume restoration with bulky flaps (ALT, rectus abdominis) to maintain contact with the posterior pharyngeal wall
2. **Laryngeal suspension** — statically positions the larynx cephalad and anterior, reducing aspiration risk; mandatory after total tongue base or total glossectomy
3. **Compensatory strategies:** Chin tuck, effortful swallow, supraglottic swallow
4. **Rehabilitative exercises:** Tongue base retraction exercises, Masako manoeuvre
5. **Palatal augmentation prosthesis** — lowers the palatal vault to facilitate neotongue–palate contact

## Radiation-Induced Dysphagia

### Acute Effects (during and immediately post-RT)

- Mucositis → odynophagia → reduced oral intake
- Oedema of pharyngeal/laryngeal structures
- Xerostomia → difficulty forming and propelling bolus
- Most patients recover oral function within 3–6 months of RT completion

### Late Effects (>3 months post-RT)

- **Fibrosis** of pharyngeal constrictors → reduced pharyngeal contraction → residue
- **Reduced hyolaryngeal excursion** → impaired UES opening → aspiration
- **Tongue base fibrosis** → reduced retraction → pharyngeal residue
- **Laryngeal oedema** → impaired airway closure
- **Trismus** → restricted oral access → difficulty eating
- **Xerostomia** — persistent in most patients after bilateral parotid irradiation

### Late Radiation-Associated Dysphagia (Late RAD)

Hutcheson et al. (2012) defined late RAD as progressive dysphagia developing ≥5 years after RT/CRT:

- Distinct clinical entity — not simply progressive worsening of acute RT effects
- Characterised by cranial neuropathy (lower cranial nerves X, XI, XII), progressive fibrosis, and pharyngeal stricture
- May develop insidiously in long-term survivors previously swallowing well
- Associated with aspiration pneumonia and gastrostomy dependence
- IMRT techniques that spare swallowing structures (DARS) may reduce this complication

## Swallowing Rehabilitation Strategies

### Compensatory Strategies (Immediate Effect)

These strategies do not change swallowing physiology but redirect the bolus to reduce aspiration:

| Strategy | Mechanism | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Chin tuck (chin down) | Widens valleculae, narrows airway entrance | Delayed pharyngeal swallow |
| Head rotation (to weak side) | Directs bolus through stronger side | Unilateral pharyngeal weakness |
| Head tilt (to strong side) | Gravity directs bolus to stronger side | Unilateral oral dysfunction |
| Supraglottic swallow | Voluntary breath hold → swallow → cough | Reduced airway closure |
| Super-supraglottic swallow | Effortful breath hold → swallow → cough | Impaired glottic/supraglottic closure |
| Effortful swallow | Increased tongue base effort | Pharyngeal residue |
| Multiple swallows | Clears residue with repeated swallows | Pharyngeal residue |

### Rehabilitative Exercises (Long-Term Effect)

These exercises aim to strengthen and restore the neuromuscular function of swallowing:

- **Tongue strengthening exercises** — resistance exercises against palate using Iowa Oral Performance Instrument (IOPI)
- **Masako manoeuvre** (tongue hold) — protrude tongue between teeth and swallow; strengthens pharyngeal constrictors
- **Mendelsohn manoeuvre** — voluntarily prolong laryngeal elevation during swallowing; improves UES opening
- **Shaker exercise** (head lift) — lying supine, lift head to look at toes for 1 minute, repeat; strengthens suprahyoid muscles, improves UES opening
- **Pharyngocise protocol** (Carnaby-Mann 2012) — structured exercise programme during CRT shown to maintain muscle structure and swallowing function in an RCT
- **Effortful pitch glide** — sequential pitch change with maximal effort; exercises laryngeal elevators
- **Lee Silverman Voice Treatment (LSVT)** — effortful phonation exercises; transfers to improved swallowing effort

### Prophylactic Swallowing Exercises During CRT

There is growing evidence that swallowing exercises initiated **before and during** chemoradiotherapy reduce the severity of post-treatment dysphagia:

- The Pharyngocise RCT demonstrated superior muscle maintenance and functional swallowing in the exercise group
- "Use it or lose it" principle — maintaining swallowing muscle activity during treatment prevents disuse atrophy compounding radiation fibrosis
- Compliance is the major challenge — patients are often too symptomatic to exercise during CRT

### Prosthetic Interventions

- **Palatal augmentation prosthesis** — lowers the palatal vault for patients with reduced tongue mobility; more useful for speech than swallowing (except when it fills a pooling defect)
- **Palatal obturator** — for oronasal fistulae after palatal resection
- **Palatal drop prosthesis** — for soft palate deficiency/velopharyngeal incompetence

### Dietary Modifications

- Texture-modified diets based on instrumental assessment findings
- International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative (IDDSI) framework — standardised scale from Level 0 (thin liquids) to Level 7 (regular foods)
- Thickened liquids reduce aspiration risk in patients with thin-liquid aspiration

## Enteral Nutrition

- **Nasogastric tube** — short-term (days to weeks); uncomfortable, risk of reflux aspiration
- **Percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG)** — for patients requiring >4–6 weeks of non-oral feeding
- **Prophylactic PEG** — placed before CRT when prolonged dysphagia is anticipated (controversial — may reduce motivation for oral intake rehabilitation)
- Goal is always to return to oral feeding when safe

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. MBS (videofluoroscopic swallow study) is the gold standard for oropharyngeal dysphagia — visualises all phases including oral transit
> 2. FEES is equivalent to MBS for aspiration detection but cannot visualise the oral phase — best for laryngeal/pharyngeal pathology and bedside assessment
> 3. 36–80% of HNC patients who aspirate do so silently — instrumental assessment is mandatory, not optional
> 4. Tongue base is the most critical structure for safe swallowing — even total oral tongue loss is compatible with oral feeding if tongue base is preserved
> 5. Late RAD (Hutcheson 2012) is a distinct entity: progressive dysphagia ≥5 years post-RT with cranial neuropathy and fibrosis
> 6. Compensatory strategies (chin tuck, head rotation, supraglottic swallow) provide immediate benefit during meals
> 7. Rehabilitative exercises (Shaker, Mendelsohn, Masako, tongue strengthening) aim for long-term neuromuscular improvement
> 8. Pharyngocise RCT: preventive exercises during CRT maintain muscle structure and swallowing function
> 9. Laryngeal suspension is mandatory after total tongue base or total glossectomy to reduce aspiration
> 10. MBS is preferred for oral cavity tumours; FEES is preferred for laryngeal tumours — choose based on clinical scenario

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Never rely on a clinical bedside assessment alone to clear an HNC patient for oral feeding — silent aspiration occurs in up to 80% of aspirators; always get MBS or FEES
> - For oral cavity and oropharyngeal cancer patients, MBS is superior to FEES because the oral phase (which is most affected by surgery) is only visible on MBS
> - The Shaker exercise (supine head lift) is the simplest evidence-based exercise for improving UES opening — teach it to every patient with pharyngeal residue or reduced hyolaryngeal excursion
> - Start prophylactic swallowing exercises BEFORE chemoradiotherapy begins — waiting until after treatment results in worse outcomes due to fibrosis and disuse atrophy
> - The quality of reconstruction matters more than the extent of resection — a total glossectomy with a well-inset flap can swallow better than a partial glossectomy with a tethered, bulky reconstruction
