## Introduction

Lichen planus (LP) is a chronic T-cell mediated inflammatory disease affecting the skin and mucous membranes. Oral lichen planus is one of the most common oral mucosal diseases, affecting approximately 1–2% of the general population. It predominantly affects middle-aged women (F:M = 1.4:1) and is characterised by a spectrum of clinical presentations from asymptomatic white reticular patterns to painful erosive and ulcerative lesions. Its classification as a potentially malignant disorder remains contentious, but the WHO has included it in its list of oral potentially malignant disorders since 2005.

## Aetiopathogenesis

### Immunopathology

OLP is a **T-cell mediated autoimmune disease** targeting the basal cell layer of the oral epithelium:

1. **Antigen presentation** — An unknown antigen (possibly a self-antigen modified by drugs, dental materials, or infection) is presented by basal keratinocytes or Langerhans cells via MHC class I molecules
2. **CD8+ cytotoxic T-cell activation** — Antigen-specific and non-specific mechanisms trigger CD8+ T-cell attack on basal keratinocytes
3. **Basal cell [[Answers/General/Apoptosis|apoptosis]]** — TNF-α, granzyme B, and Fas-FasL interactions cause basal cell death
4. **Basement membrane disruption** — T-cell attack disrupts the epithelial-connective tissue interface → basement membrane degeneration
5. **Chronic inflammation** — CD4+ helper T cells (Th1 predominant) and mast cells perpetuate the inflammatory cycle through cytokine release (IL-2, IFN-γ, TNF-α)

### Associated Factors

- **Hepatitis C virus** — Strong association in Mediterranean and Japanese populations; screen all OLP patients
- **Dental materials** — Contact hypersensitivity to amalgam, gold, or other restorations → lichenoid contact reaction
- **Drugs** — NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, antimalarials, thiazides → oral lichenoid drug reaction
- **Stress** — Psychological stress exacerbates OLP; cortisol pathway implicated
- **Graft-versus-host disease** — Chronic oral GVHD closely mimics OLP clinically and histologically

## Clinical Features

### Classification (WHO Modified Criteria — van der Meij & van der Waal 2003)

| Type | Appearance | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Reticular** | White lace-like striae (Wickham's striae) | Usually asymptomatic |
| **Papular** | Small white raised dots | Mild or none |
| **Plaque-like** | White patches (mimics [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Leukoplakia Management|leukoplakia]]) | Mild or none |
| **Erosive** | Central red atrophic areas with surrounding striae | Painful |
| **Ulcerative** | Yellowish ulcers with surrounding erythema and striae | Most painful |
| **Bullous** | Fluid-filled vesicles/bullae | Painful when ruptured |

### Distribution

- **Bilateral** and **symmetrical** involvement is characteristic (helps distinguish from lichenoid reactions which are typically unilateral)
- Most common sites: buccal mucosa (90%), tongue, gingivae
- Gingival involvement may present as **desquamative gingivitis** (also seen in pemphigus and pemphigoid)
- About 30% of patients have concurrent cutaneous LP (purple pruritic papules on wrists, legs, trunk)

### Oral Lichenoid Reaction vs OLP

| Feature | OLP | Lichenoid Reaction |
|---|---|---|
| Distribution | Bilateral, symmetrical | Unilateral, localised |
| Temporal relation | No identifiable trigger | Drug/material related |
| Histology | Band-like T-cell infiltrate | Mixed infiltrate, eosinophils |
| Resolution | Chronic, waxes and wanes | Resolves with removal of cause |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Criteria (van der Meij & van der Waal 2003)

- Bilateral, more-or-less symmetrical lesions
- White lace-like network of slightly raised grey-white lines (reticular pattern)
- Erosive, atrophic, bullous, and plaque-type lesions accepted only in the presence of reticular lesions elsewhere in the oral mucosa

### Histological Criteria

1. Well-defined **band-like lymphocytic infiltrate** confined to the superficial lamina propria (interface mucositis)
2. **Degeneration of the basal cell layer** — liquefactive degeneration, "saw-tooth" rete ridge pattern
3. **Civatte bodies** (colloid/apoptotic bodies) — eosinophilic globules representing apoptotic keratinocytes
4. Absence of epithelial dysplasia

### Direct Immunofluorescence

- Shows **fibrinogen deposition** in a shaggy pattern along the basement membrane zone
- Helps exclude pemphigus (IgG intercellular) and pemphigoid (linear IgG/C3 at BMZ)
- **Perilesional biopsy** is essential — biopsy from the lesional area may lack intact epithelium

### Definitive Diagnosis

Requires **both** clinical and histological criteria to be met. van der Meij and van der Waal (2003) showed that only 47% of clinically diagnosed OLP cases met both clinical and histological criteria — highlighting the poor clinicopathologic correlation and the need for biopsy.

## Management

### Stepwise Algorithm (Scott-Brown's)

Treatment follows the same algorithmic approach used for aphthous ulcers:

**Step 1 — Identify and eliminate triggers:**
- Patch testing for dental material hypersensitivity
- Drug review — substitute suspected medications
- Hepatitis C screening
- Haematinic screen (ferritin, B12, folate)

**Step 2 — Topical therapy (first-line):**
- **Topical corticosteroids** — the mainstay of treatment (Cochrane 2020)
  - Betamethasone mouthwash (0.5 mg dissolved in 10 mL water, rinse 3 min)
  - Clobetasol propionate 0.05% ointment for localised lesions
  - Fluticasone spray for accessible areas
- **Topical tacrolimus** (0.1% ointment) — useful for refractory localised lesions; calcineurin inhibitor that suppresses T-cell activation

**Step 3 — Systemic therapy (for refractory/widespread disease):**
- **Systemic corticosteroids** — prednisolone 40–60 mg tapering; short course for acute flares
- **Azathioprine** or **mycophenolate mofetil** — steroid-sparing agents for chronic disease
- **Tacrolimus mouthwash** — bridge between topical and systemic therapy

**Step 4 — Refractory cases:**
- Systemic retinoids (acitretin)
- Hydroxychloroquine
- Phototherapy (PUVA)

> Note: Colchicine, useful in aphthous ulcers, has **no role** in OLP management.

## Malignant Transformation

This is the most examined and debated aspect of OLP:

- WHO classifies OLP as an **oral potentially malignant disorder (OPMD)**
- Overall malignant transformation rate: **1.14%** (Gonzalez-Moles et al. 2019, meta-analysis of 12,838 patients)
- Annual transformation rate: approximately **0.5%** or less
- Montgomery states transformation rate is "extremely low and certainly less than 1%"
- Higher risk in:
  - **Erosive/ulcerative forms** (vs reticular)
  - Tongue and lateral border involvement
  - Female patients
  - Tobacco users with OLP
  - Long disease duration

### Controversies

- Some argue that cases that underwent malignant transformation were actually **oral lichenoid dysplasia** (OLD) misdiagnosed as OLP — true OLP by strict diagnostic criteria may have negligible transformation risk
- The presence of epithelial dysplasia excludes a diagnosis of OLP (by definition) — raising the question of whether "dysplastic OLP" should be reclassified as lichenoid dysplasia
- Despite debate, current WHO recommendation is to **follow all OLP patients long-term** with biopsy of any suspicious change

### Surveillance Protocol

- Clinical review every **6–12 months**
- Re-biopsy any area that develops:
  - New erythematous or erosive change
  - Thickened/verrucous appearance
  - Persistent ulceration not responding to treatment
  - Unilateral change in a previously bilateral lesion

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. OLP is a T-cell mediated autoimmune attack on the basal cell layer of oral epithelium
> 2. Bilateral symmetrical reticular white striae (Wickham's striae) on buccal mucosa is the hallmark
> 3. Six clinical types: reticular, papular, plaque-like, erosive, ulcerative, bullous
> 4. Definitive diagnosis requires both clinical AND histological criteria (van der Meij & van der Waal 2003)
> 5. Histology: band-like lymphocytic infiltrate, basal cell degeneration, Civatte bodies, no dysplasia
> 6. Topical corticosteroids are first-line treatment (Cochrane 2020 evidence)
> 7. Malignant transformation rate is approximately 1.14% (Gonzalez-Moles 2019 meta-analysis)
> 8. Erosive/ulcerative OLP on the tongue carries highest transformation risk
> 9. All OLP patients require long-term surveillance with biopsy of suspicious changes
> 10. Screen all OLP patients for Hepatitis C

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Bilateral symmetrical distribution distinguishes OLP from lichenoid contact reaction (unilateral, near a restoration)
> - Always perform perilesional biopsy (not from the ulcer centre) — send one sample for histology and one for direct immunofluorescence
> - Desquamative gingivitis has three main causes: LP, pemphigus, and pemphigoid — biopsy is essential to differentiate
> - The presence of dysplasia on biopsy EXCLUDES a diagnosis of OLP — reclassify as lichenoid dysplasia and manage more aggressively
> - Tacrolimus ointment is an excellent second-line option for localised refractory lesions but requires monitoring (theoretical concern of long-term malignancy risk)
