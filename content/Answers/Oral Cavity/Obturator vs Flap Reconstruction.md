> Obturator versus flap reconstruction in upper alveolus defect

> The choice between prosthetic obturation and free flap reconstruction for maxillectomy defects remains one of the most debated topics in head and neck reconstruction. Brown et al. ## Introduction

Resection of the upper alveolus and maxilla for oral or sinonasal malignancy creates an oroantral or oronasal communication that impairs speech, swallowing, and facial aesthetics. Two principal reconstructive strategies exist: prosthetic obturation (obturator) and surgical reconstruction (free flap). The choice depends on defect extent, patient factors, and institutional expertise. Neither approach is universally superior — the Brown classification provides a systematic framework for matching defect to reconstruction.

## Brown Classification of Maxillectomy Defects

### Original Classification (Brown 2000) and Revision (Brown & Shaw 2010)

#### Vertical Component

| Class | Defect | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | Maxillectomy not causing oroantral fistula | Alveolectomy, medial maxillectomy |
| **II** | Subtotal maxillectomy (not involving orbit) | Low maxillectomy, infrastructure |
| **III** | Total maxillectomy (involving orbit floor, orbit preserved) | Classical total maxillectomy |
| **IV** | Total maxillectomy + orbital exenteration | Radical maxillectomy |
| **V** | Orbitomaxillary defect (orbital contents removed, palate intact) | Expanded in 2010 revision |
| **VI** | Nasomaxillary defect (nasoethmoid, frontal bone) | Expanded in 2010 revision |

#### Horizontal Component (Palatal Extent)

| Subclass | Description |
|---|---|
| **a** | Palate only (alveolus not involved) |
| **b** | Less than or equal to half the palate (unilateral) |
| **c** | Greater than half the palate (crosses midline) |
| **d** | Total palatectomy (bilateral, complete) |

### Reconstruction Algorithm by Defect Class

| Defect | Preferred Reconstruction |
|---|---|
| Class I | Obturator or local flap; no oroantral communication |
| Class II (a-b) | **Obturator preferred** — low defects, orbit intact |
| Class II (c-d) | Free flap (extensive palatal loss) |
| Class III (a-b) | Obturator or free flap (surgeon/patient preference) |
| Class III (c-d) | **Free flap preferred** — extensive defect |
| Class IV | Free flap (complex defect + orbital exenteration) |
| Class V-VI | Free flap + craniofacial reconstruction |

## Prosthetic Obturation

### Phases of Obturator Rehabilitation (Jatin Shah)

| Phase | Timing | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediate obturator** | Intraoperative | Wired to teeth; retains surgical packing; aids swallowing and pulmonary clearance; obviates feeding tube |
| **Interim obturator** | 1-2 weeks | Clasped to remaining teeth; allows wound inspection |
| **Definitive obturator** | 6-8 weeks | Clasped to remaining teeth; completely replaces hard palate and dentition; restores speech and mastication |

### Technique

- **Preoperative dental impressions** taken for fabrication
- Surgeon marks anticipated resection on dental cast → prosthodontist fabricates immediate obturator
- Extended posteriorly if soft palate resection anticipated
- **Bolus extension** into maxillectomy cavity improves voice quality (obliterates resonating air space)

### Advantages of Obturator

- Single-stage (no donor site morbidity)
- Allows **direct wound surveillance** — cavity inspected at each visit
- Removable for cleaning and wound care
- Lower operative time and hospital stay
- No microvascular expertise required
- Good results for small-moderate defects (Class II a-b, III a-b)
- **Dental rehabilitation integrated** into prosthesis

### Disadvantages of Obturator

- Requires adequate **dentition for retention** (edentulous patients = poor retention)
- Needs lifelong prosthodontic follow-up and periodic replacement
- Oral hygiene maintenance burden
- Appearance concerns (visible clasp on teeth)
- Oral-nasal communication = nasal regurgitation when removed
- **Poor results for large defects** (>50% palatal resection, bilateral, total palatectomy)
- Social embarrassment — must be removed for cleaning

## Free Flap Reconstruction

### Common Flaps for Maxillary Reconstruction

| Flap | Type | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Radial forearm (RFFF)** | Fasciocutaneous | Small palatal defects, thin pliable tissue |
| **Anterolateral thigh (ALT)** | Fasciocutaneous | Medium defects, versatile bulk |
| **Rectus abdominis** | Myocutaneous | Large defects requiring bulk |
| **Fibula** | Osteocutaneous | Defects requiring bone (alveolar reconstruction, dental implants) |
| **Scapula** | Osteocutaneous | Complex midface defects |
| **Iliac crest (DCIA)** | Osteocutaneous | Maxillary alveolar ridge for dental implants |

### Advantages of Free Flap

- **Permanent closure** — eliminates oroantral/oronasal communication
- No need for lifelong prosthetic maintenance
- Better for extensive defects (Class II c-d, III c-d, IV)
- Provides vascularised tissue (important if postoperative RT planned)
- Bone-containing flaps allow **dental implant placement**
- Superior speech and swallowing for large defects (Moreno 2010)

### Disadvantages of Free Flap

- Donor site morbidity
- Longer operative time (8-12 hours)
- Requires microvascular expertise
- **Wound surveillance difficult** — cannot directly visualise resection cavity
- Flap failure risk (2-5%)
- May require secondary debulking or revision
- Obliterates cavity → recurrence harder to detect early

## Comparative Outcomes

### Moreno et al. 2010 (n=113)

| Outcome | Obturator (n=73) | Free Flap (n=40) | P value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Speech intelligibility | Comparable | Comparable | NS |
| Diet score | Comparable | Comparable | NS |
| Speech (defect >50% palate) | Worse | **Better** | 0.019 |
| Diet (defect >50% palate) | Worse | **Better** | 0.043 |

**Key conclusion**: for moderate defects, outcomes are equivalent; for **extensive defects (>50% palatal resection), free flap is superior**.

### Rogers et al. 2003 (n=28)

- No statistically significant QOL differences between groups
- Borderline trends: obturator patients more concerned about appearance, more oral pain/soreness

### Breeze et al. 2016 (n=39, Prospective)

- Significant QOL decrease after treatment (P<0.001) but **no significant difference** between obturator and flap groups
- Neither defect size nor postoperative RT adversely affected QOL

## Decision-Making Algorithm

### Favour Obturator

- Small to moderate defect (Class II a-b)
- Adequate remaining dentition for retention
- Patient preference for reversible option
- Comorbidities precluding long surgery
- Need for wound surveillance (recurrence risk)
- No plans for dental implant rehabilitation

### Favour Free Flap

- Large defect (>50% palate, crossing midline, Class II c-d and above)
- Edentulous patient (poor obturator retention)
- Orbital exenteration component (Class IV)
- Soft palate resection (velopharyngeal insufficiency)
- Plans for dental implant rehabilitation (osteocutaneous flap)
- Adjuvant RT planned (vascularised tissue tolerates RT better)
- Patient preference for permanent reconstruction

> [!abstract] Key Points
> - The **Brown classification** (vertical I-VI, horizontal a-d) provides a systematic framework for matching defect to reconstruction
> - For small-moderate defects (Class II a-b), obturator and free flap achieve **comparable** speech, swallowing, and QOL outcomes
> - For extensive defects (>50% palatal resection), free flap provides **superior** speech and diet outcomes
> - Obturator advantages: no donor site, allows wound surveillance, lower morbidity, single-stage
> - Free flap advantages: permanent closure, tolerates RT better, enables dental implants, better for large defects

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Immediate obturator** should be fabricated preoperatively from dental impressions and placed intraoperatively — eliminates need for NG tube and allows immediate oral intake
> - Obturator retention depends on **remaining dentition** — in edentulous patients, zygomatic implants or free flap reconstruction should be considered
> - The bolus extension of the obturator into the maxillectomy cavity is critical for **voice quality** — it obliterates the resonating air space
> - When planning free flap reconstruction for dental rehabilitation, **fibula flap** provides the best bone stock for osseointegrated implant placement
> - Direct wound surveillance is a major advantage of obturators — for patients at high recurrence risk, obturator allows **cavity inspection at every follow-up**
