## Introduction

Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) of the mandible is one of the most devastating complications of radiotherapy for head and neck cancer. It is defined as exposed, non-healing bone in an irradiated field that fails to heal over a period of 3–6 months in the absence of tumour recurrence. The mandible is far more susceptible than the maxilla because of its predominantly end-arterial blood supply (inferior alveolar artery) compared to the rich collateral supply of the maxilla. ORN occurs in approximately 5–15% of patients receiving radical-dose radiotherapy to the oral cavity, and its incidence is related to radiation dose, fractionation, volume of mandible irradiated, and pre-existing dental disease. Management ranges from conservative wound care to radical resection with microvascular reconstruction, depending on the stage and severity.

## Pathophysiology

### Marx's "Three H" Theory (1983)

Marx proposed that ORN results from radiation-induced tissue damage characterised by:
1. **Hypovascularity** — endarteritis obliterans reduces blood supply to bone
2. **Hypocellularity** — radiation destroys osteoblasts, osteocytes, and fibroblasts
3. **Hypoxia** — reduced vascularity leads to chronic tissue hypoxia

This creates a metabolic environment where normal tissue turnover and wound healing cannot occur. Any injury (dental extraction, trauma, denture irritation) exceeds the tissue's capacity to repair, leading to progressive bone necrosis. Importantly, Marx demonstrated that ORN is **not** primarily an infection — it is a problem of impaired wound healing. Infection is secondary to exposed necrotic bone, not the primary cause.

### Delanian and Lefaix Fibro-Atrophic Theory (2004)

This newer theory reframes ORN as part of the radiation-induced fibro-atrophic (RIF) process, a three-phase continuum:

1. **Pre-fibrotic phase** — endothelial cell damage, inflammation, free radical generation
2. **Constitutive organised phase** — abnormal fibroblast activity, excessive collagen deposition, tissue fibrosis
3. **Late fibro-atrophic phase** — tissue remodelling leads to fragile, poorly vascularised, atrophic tissue prone to necrosis

The RIF theory opened the door to **antioxidant-based therapy** (PENTOCLO) as an alternative to HBO, targeting the underlying fibrotic process rather than simply attempting to increase oxygen delivery.

## Risk Factors

| Factor | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Radiation dose >60 Gy | Direct dose-dependent bone damage |
| Mandible in radiation field | End-arterial blood supply, thin periosteum |
| Dental extraction post-RT | Trauma exceeds healing capacity |
| Poor oral hygiene | Chronic infection, periodontal disease |
| Tobacco and alcohol use | Impaired vascularity, poor healing |
| Brachytherapy | High focal doses to mandible |
| Concurrent chemotherapy | Additive mucosal and vascular damage |

### Mandible vs Maxilla

The mandible is far more susceptible to ORN because:
- Blood supply is predominantly from the inferior alveolar artery (end-arterial)
- Dense cortical bone with limited periosteal vascularity
- The maxilla has rich collateral supply from multiple branches of the internal maxillary artery

## Clinical Presentation

- **Exposed bone** — non-healing exposed bone through oral mucosa or skin
- **Pain** — often the presenting symptom
- **[[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]]** — from fibrosis of masticatory muscles and TMJ
- **Infection** — secondary to exposed bone; purulent discharge
- **Fistula** — orocutaneous or oronasal communication
- **Pathological fracture** — in advanced cases
- **Reduced sensation** — inferior alveolar nerve involvement

### Differential Diagnosis

The critical differential is **tumour recurrence** — biopsy of the soft tissue surrounding exposed bone is mandatory to exclude malignancy before attributing the findings to ORN.

## Staging Systems

### Notani Classification (2003)

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| I | ORN confined to alveolar bone |
| II | ORN limited to mandible above the inferior alveolar canal |
| III | ORN extending below the inferior alveolar canal ± skin fistula ± pathological fracture |

### Marx Staging

| Stage | Description | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| I | Exposed bone, responds to HBO | HBO alone (30 dives) |
| II | Does not respond to HBO stage I | HBO + sequestrectomy + HBO |
| III | Full-thickness necrosis, fistula, fracture | Resection + HBO + reconstruction |

## Prevention

Prevention is more effective than treatment and should be a priority in every patient receiving head and neck radiotherapy:

1. **Pre-radiotherapy dental assessment** — comprehensive evaluation by a dental oncologist/oral surgeon
2. **Extraction of non-restorable teeth** — perform **before** radiotherapy, allowing 14–21 days healing prior to RT commencement
3. **Fluoride carriers** — custom trays for daily topical fluoride application to prevent radiation caries
4. **Oral hygiene education** — meticulous lifelong oral care
5. **Avoid post-RT extractions** — if unavoidable, use atraumatic technique with antibiotic cover; HBO may be considered pre-extraction
6. **IMRT** — intensity-modulated radiotherapy reduces dose to mandible compared to conventional techniques
7. **Dental follow-up** — regular lifelong dental surveillance

## Management

### Conservative Management (Grade I–II)

- **Local wound care** — saline irrigation, debridement of loose sequestra
- **Antibiotics** — culture-directed; clindamycin or amoxicillin-clavulanate are commonly used empirically
- **Chlorhexidine rinses** — reduce bacterial load
- **Nutritional optimisation** — adequate protein and caloric intake
- **Smoking cessation** — essential for healing
- **Pain management** — including neuropathic pain agents if needed

### Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBO)

**Marx Protocol:**
- 20–30 pre-operative sessions at 2.0–2.4 ATA, 90 minutes of 100% O₂
- 10 post-operative sessions after any surgical intervention
- Mechanism: promotes [[Answers/General/Tumour Angiogenesis|angiogenesis]] and osteogenesis in hypoxic tissue

**Evidence — conflicting:**
- Marx's original data (1983, 1985) strongly supported HBO for ORN prevention and treatment
- **ORN96 Trial (Annane 2004):** Randomised, placebo-controlled, double-blind trial — showed **no benefit** of HBO for established ORN (recovery 19% HBO vs 32% placebo); stopped early for potential harm
- HBO remains controversial — may be useful for **prevention** (pre-extraction in irradiated fields) but evidence for **treatment** of established ORN is weak
- Combined DAHANCA-21/NWHHT data (2021) also showed no benefit for HBO

### PENTOCLO Protocol (Delanian)

Based on the fibro-atrophic theory, this medical regimen combines:
- **Pentoxifylline** 800 mg/day — antifibrotic, improves microcirculation
- **Tocopherol** (vitamin E) 1000 IU/day — antioxidant, reduces free radical damage
- **Clodronate** 1600 mg/day (5 days/week) — bisphosphonate that inhibits osteoclast activity

Phase II trial showed complete resolution of refractory ORN in a significant proportion of patients. PENTOCLO represents a paradigm shift from surgical to medical management of early-stage ORN. Advantages: non-invasive, oral medication, can be used long-term.

### Surgical Management (Grade III / Refractory)

**1. Sequestrectomy**
- Removal of loose, non-viable bone fragments
- Appropriate for limited disease with intact mandibular continuity
- Often combined with HBO or PENTOCLO

**2. [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Marginal Mandibulectomy|marginal mandibulectomy]]**
- Removal of necrotic alveolar bone preserving the inferior border
- Indicated when disease is limited to the superior aspect of the mandible
- Maintains mandibular continuity and function

**3. [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Types of Mandibulectomy|segmental mandibulectomy]] with Free Flap Reconstruction**
- Indicated for full-thickness necrosis, pathological fracture, or orocutaneous fistula
- Free fibula osteocutaneous flap is the gold standard for reconstruction
- Provides vascularised bone graft that is not radiation-damaged
- Allows dental rehabilitation with [[Answers/General/Osseointegrated Implants|osseointegrated implants]] implants
- This is the definitive treatment for advanced (Notani grade III) or refractory ORN

**4. Reconstruction Options**

| Flap | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| Free fibula | Gold standard; long bone stock; skin paddle | Donor site morbidity |
| Iliac crest | Excellent bone height for implants | Limited pedicle length |
| Scapula | Combined bone + soft tissue | Patient positioning |
| [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Regional Flaps in Oral Reconstruction|pectoralis major flap]] | Reliable; no microsurgery | No bone; muscle atrophy |

## Treatment Algorithm

1. **Exclude tumour recurrence** — biopsy mandatory
2. **Grade I–II:** Conservative management ± HBO ± PENTOCLO
3. **Grade II not responding to conservative measures:** Sequestrectomy ± HBO
4. **Grade III (full-thickness, fracture, fistula):** Segmental mandibulectomy + free fibula flap reconstruction
5. **All grades:** Optimise nutrition, stop smoking, dental hygiene, long-term follow-up

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. ORN is defined as exposed non-healing bone in an irradiated field for >3 months without tumour recurrence
> 2. Marx's "three H" theory: hypovascularity, hypocellularity, hypoxia — ORN is a wound healing failure, not primarily an infection
> 3. Delanian's fibro-atrophic theory reframed ORN as a fibrotic disease, leading to PENTOCLO (pentoxifylline + tocopherol + clodronate)
> 4. The mandible is more susceptible than the maxilla due to its end-arterial blood supply (inferior alveolar artery)
> 5. ORN96 trial (Annane 2004): landmark RCT showed no benefit of HBO for established ORN — challenged decades of HBO-based practice
> 6. Prevention is paramount: pre-RT dental assessment, extraction of non-restorable teeth 14–21 days before RT, lifelong fluoride therapy
> 7. Notani classification: Grade I (alveolar), Grade II (above IA canal), Grade III (below IA canal ± fracture ± fistula)
> 8. Advanced ORN (Grade III) requires segmental mandibulectomy with free fibula flap reconstruction
> 9. Always biopsy to exclude tumour recurrence before diagnosing ORN
> 10. IMRT reduces mandibular dose and may decrease ORN incidence compared to conventional RT techniques

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The single most important preventive measure for ORN is pre-radiotherapy dental assessment and extraction of questionable teeth — this cannot be done retrospectively
> - When a patient presents with exposed bone in an irradiated field, your first thought should be "Is this recurrence?" — biopsy before labelling it as ORN
> - The ORN96 trial (Annane 2004) is a game-changer for exam answers — know it well. It showed HBO did not help established ORN and was potentially harmful
> - PENTOCLO is the modern medical management of choice for early ORN — it targets the underlying fibrotic pathology rather than simply increasing oxygen delivery
> - For advanced ORN with pathological fracture or fistula, the only reliable treatment is resection and free fibula reconstruction — bringing in non-irradiated, well-vascularised tissue
