## Introduction

Access mandibulotomy (also called mandibular swing or lip-splitting mandibulotomy) is a surgical technique in which the mandible is temporarily divided to provide access to tumours in the posterior oral cavity, oropharynx, parapharyngeal space, and skull base. Unlike mandibulectomy (where the bone is resected as part of the oncological procedure), an access mandibulotomy preserves the mandible — the bone is cut, swung laterally to provide wide exposure, and then rigidly fixed back in its anatomical position at the end of the operation. This distinction is critical: access mandibulotomy is a reversible access procedure, not a resection.

## Indications

### Primary Indications

1. **Posterior oral tongue carcinoma** — tumours of the posterior one-third of the mobile tongue that cannot be adequately exposed transorally
2. **Base of tongue carcinoma** — the mandibular swing provides the widest access to tongue base tumours
3. **Tonsillar and lateral oropharyngeal tumours** — especially when transoral access is insufficient
4. **Soft palate tumours** with posterior extension
5. **Parapharyngeal space tumours** — both pre-styloid and post-styloid compartments
6. **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Infratemporal Fossa Spread|infratemporal fossa]] tumours** — extended mandibular swing
7. **Nasopharyngeal tumours** — selected cases requiring lateral access

### When Access Mandibulotomy Is Preferred Over Transoral Surgery

- Limited mouth opening ([[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]])
- Large tumour requiring wide en bloc resection
- Tumour proximity to the carotid sheath requiring controlled exposure
- Need for combined tumour resection + neck dissection through a single approach
- Tumour extending to the parapharyngeal space

### Contraindications

- Tumour directly invading the mandible (requires mandibulectomy, not access mandibulotomy)
- Previously irradiated mandible (high risk of [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Osteoradionecrosis of the Mandible|osteoradionecrosis]] and non-union)
- Patient unfit for prolonged surgery
- Tumour amenable to adequate transoral resection (TORS or conventional)

## Technique

### Step 1: Incision

- **Lip-split incision** — vertical incision through the lower lip in the midline, extending down through the chin crease (important for cosmesis)
- Extended submandibularly into a neck crease incision for combined neck dissection
- The lip split should follow the midline precisely and curve around the chin point to minimise cosmetic deformity
- **Alternative:** Visor flap (bilateral submental incision without lip split) — avoids lip scar but provides less exposure

### Step 2: Soft Tissue Elevation

- Periosteum of the anterior mandible elevated
- Mental nerve identified and preserved bilaterally
- Genioglossus and geniohyoid muscle attachments divided on the side of the mandibulotomy

### Step 3: Mandibulotomy

**Two main approaches:**

| Feature | Midline | Paramedian |
|---|---|---|
| Osteotomy site | Between central incisors | Between lateral incisor and canine |
| Tooth extraction | Central incisor | None (through interdental space) |
| Genioglossus | Divided | Preserved on one side |
| Geniohyoid | Divided | Preserved on one side |
| Bone healing | Good (cancellous bone) | Good |
| Function | Temporary tongue dysfunction | Better tongue function preservation |
| Complication rate | ~20% | ~20% (no significant difference) |

- **Staircase or step osteotomy** — preferred over straight cut; provides rotational stability and prevents vertical displacement
- Osteotomy performed with oscillating saw under copious irrigation
- **Pre-drill plate holes** before making the osteotomy to ensure accurate reduction at closure
- Place miniplate(s) or reconstruction plate before osteotomy, mark screw holes, remove plate, perform osteotomy

### Step 4: Mandibular Swing

- The mandible is swung laterally on a soft tissue hinge (masseter, medial pterygoid attachments)
- This exposes the posterior oral cavity, oropharynx, and parapharyngeal space
- The lingual nerve and hypoglossal nerve are identified and preserved (unless involved by tumour)

### Step 5: Tumour Resection

- Wide en bloc resection with adequate margins under direct vision
- The access provides excellent exposure for controlled resection around critical neurovascular structures (internal carotid artery, internal jugular vein, lower cranial nerves)

### Step 6: Reconstruction and Mandibular Closure

- Reconstruct the soft tissue defect (free flap, local flap, or primary closure depending on defect size)
- Reduce the mandible to its anatomical position
- Fix with pre-bent miniplates and monocortical screws (typically 2.0 mm system)
- **Rigid fixation is essential** for mandibular union
- Close [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]], lip, and skin in layers

## Complications

### Mandibulotomy-Specific Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Malocclusion | 5–15% | Accurate pre-plating; IMF if needed |
| Hardware exposure | 5–10% | Observation or plate removal |
| Non-union | 1–3% | Revision fixation ± bone graft |
| Osteomyelitis | 2–5% | Antibiotics, debridement |
| Tooth damage/loss | 5–10% | Careful osteotomy planning |
| Lip scar/deformity | Variable | Midline incision in chin crease |
| Mental nerve injury | 5–10% | Usually neuropraxia; recovers |
| ORN (post-RT) | 10–15% | Avoid mandibulotomy in irradiated bone |

### Key Points About Complications

- **Overall complication rate:** ~20% (Dubner 1991) — but most are minor
- **Non-union is rare** (<3%) with rigid internal fixation
- **Malocclusion** is the most functionally significant complication — prevented by pre-plating
- **Adjuvant radiotherapy** significantly increases complication rates, particularly hardware exposure and delayed union
- **Step osteotomy** reduces malunion risk compared to straight osteotomy

## Comparison With Alternative Approaches

| Approach | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| Mandibular swing | Widest exposure; oncologically sound | Lip scar; mandibulotomy complications |
| Transoral robotic (TORS) | No external incision; faster recovery | Limited to smaller tumours; learning curve |
| Lateral pharyngotomy | No mandibulotomy needed | Limited exposure for large tumours |
| Visor flap | No lip split | Limited posterior access |
| Lingual release | Preserves mandible and lip | Restricted access to tongue base |

## Special Considerations

### Posterior Oral Tongue Carcinoma

For the AMRITA 2020 question on surgical approaches to posterior oral tongue carcinoma, the access mandibulotomy/mandibular swing is the traditional open approach that provides the best exposure. However, transoral robotic surgery (TORS) has increasingly replaced mandibulotomy for selected cases. The choice depends on tumour size, mouth opening, surgeon expertise, and need for concurrent neck dissection. See also [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Tongue Reconstruction Algorithm|Tongue Reconstruction Algorithm]] for reconstructive considerations.

### Combined With Neck Dissection

The lip-split mandibulotomy incision is designed to incorporate a neck dissection through the same approach — the submandibular extension connects with a neck crease incision, allowing en bloc resection of the primary tumour and ipsilateral neck contents.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Access mandibulotomy is a reversible access procedure — the mandible is temporarily divided and rigidly fixed, NOT resected
> 2. Primary indication: tumours of the posterior oral cavity, oropharynx, parapharyngeal space, and skull base requiring wide exposure
> 3. Midline vs paramedian mandibulotomy: no significant difference in complication rates (~20%); paramedian preserves genioglossus/geniohyoid
> 4. Step/staircase osteotomy is preferred over straight cut for rotational stability
> 5. Pre-plating (marking screw holes before osteotomy) is essential to prevent malocclusion
> 6. Overall complication rate ~20% but non-union is rare (<3%) with rigid fixation
> 7. Contraindicated in previously irradiated mandible due to high ORN and non-union risk
> 8. Malocclusion is the most functionally significant complication — prevented by precise pre-plating technique
> 9. TORS has increasingly replaced mandibulotomy for smaller oropharyngeal tumours
> 10. Spiro et al. (1981) at MSK established the mandibular swing as the standard access procedure for posterior oral/oropharyngeal cancers

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The key exam distinction: access mandibulotomy = bone preserved; mandibulectomy = bone resected as part of tumour clearance. Never confuse the two
> - Always pre-plate before cutting — this is the single most important technical step for preventing malocclusion. Place the plate, mark the holes, remove the plate, cut the bone, then at closure the screws go back into the pre-drilled holes for perfect anatomical reduction
> - The chin crease incision for the lip split is critical for cosmesis — a poorly placed lip scar is immediately visible and permanent
> - If the mandible has been previously irradiated, avoid mandibulotomy if at all possible — consider transoral approaches or lateral pharyngotomy instead
> - For the exam, emphasise that mandibular swing provides the widest access to the oropharynx and parapharyngeal space — it remains the gold standard open approach despite the rise of TORS
