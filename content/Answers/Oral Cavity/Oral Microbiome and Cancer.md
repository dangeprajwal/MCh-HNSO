## Overview

The oral cavity harbours one of the most diverse microbial communities in the human body — over **700 bacterial species** colonise the oral mucosa, tongue, gingiva, and saliva. Emerging evidence suggests that the **oral microbiome** plays a significant role in oral cancer pathogenesis through chronic inflammation, carcinogen metabolism, immune modulation, and direct genotoxic effects. This is an extension of the established link between *Helicobacter pylori* and gastric cancer to the oral cavity context.

## The Oral Microbiome

### Normal Composition

| Phylum | Predominant Genera | Location |
|---|---|---|
| Firmicutes | Streptococcus, Veillonella | Tongue, buccal mucosa |
| Bacteroidetes | Prevotella, Porphyromonas | Subgingival plaque |
| Proteobacteria | Neisseria, Haemophilus | Saliva, tongue |
| Actinobacteria | Actinomyces, Rothia | Teeth, gingiva |
| Fusobacteria | Fusobacterium | Subgingival, tongue |

### Dysbiosis in Oral Cancer

The oral microbiome of cancer patients shows significant **dysbiosis** compared to healthy controls:

| Finding | Detail |
|---|---|
| Increased | Fusobacterium, Porphyromonas, Prevotella, Peptostreptococcus |
| Decreased | Streptococcus, Rothia, Haemophilus |
| Overall pattern | Shift from aerobic commensals to anaerobic pathobionts |
| Diversity | Reduced alpha diversity in tumour tissue vs normal mucosa |

## Mechanisms of Microbial Carcinogenesis

### 1. Chronic Inflammation

The most well-established mechanism:
- Periodontal pathogens (*Porphyromonas gingivalis*, *Fusobacterium nucleatum*) induce **chronic inflammatory response**
- NF-κB activation → IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α, PGE2 production
- Chronic inflammation promotes: cell proliferation, angiogenesis, inhibition of apoptosis, DNA damage
- **Periodontitis** independently associated with increased oral cancer risk (OR 2.0–5.0)

### 2. Direct Genotoxic Effects

| Bacterium | Genotoxic Mechanism |
|---|---|
| *Porphyromonas gingivalis* | Activates ERK1/2-Ets1, MMP-9; promotes cell invasion; inhibits apoptosis |
| *Fusobacterium nucleatum* | FadA adhesin activates β-catenin/Wnt pathway → proliferation |
| *Treponema denticola* | Produces hydrogen sulphide and acetaldehyde |
| Various oral bacteria | Nitrosamine production from nitrate reduction |

### 3. Carcinogen Metabolism

Oral bacteria can **metabolise procarcinogens into active carcinogens**:
- **Acetaldehyde production:** Oral bacteria (especially *Neisseria* and *Streptococcus*) metabolise ethanol to acetaldehyde — a Group 1 carcinogen (IARC)
- Salivary acetaldehyde levels after alcohol consumption are 10–100× higher than blood levels
- This explains the **synergistic effect of alcohol and poor oral hygiene** on oral cancer risk
- Bacterial nitrate reductase converts dietary nitrates to nitrites → N-nitroso compounds

### 4. Immune Modulation

- *P. gingivalis* can evade immune surveillance and promote an immunosuppressive microenvironment
- Bacterial biofilms trigger **Th17 responses** → IL-17 production → tumour-promoting inflammation
- Dysbiotic microbiome alters Treg/Th17 balance, favouring pro-tumorigenic immunity
- PD-L1 expression may be influenced by microbial composition

### 5. Epithelial Barrier Disruption

- Periodontal pathogens degrade **E-cadherin** and tight junction proteins
- Barrier disruption allows carcinogen penetration into deeper tissue layers
- *P. gingivalis* promotes **epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT)** through ZEB1 upregulation

## Key Bacteria Implicated

### *Porphyromonas gingivalis*

The strongest candidate for a direct carcinogenic role:
- Activates cell proliferation (ERK1/2 pathway)
- Inhibits apoptosis (Bcl-2 upregulation, JAK1/STAT3 activation)
- Promotes invasion through MMP-9 induction
- Activates Notch1 signalling in gingival epithelial cells
- Detected at significantly higher levels in OSCC tissue than adjacent normal mucosa

### *Fusobacterium nucleatum*

Best established in colorectal cancer, increasingly implicated in oral cancer:
- FadA adhesin binds E-cadherin → β-catenin nuclear translocation → Wnt pathway activation
- Promotes chemoresistance through autophagy activation
- Enriched in tumour tissue and associated with advanced T-stage
- May create an immunosuppressive tumour microenvironment

### *Candida albicans*

A fungal organism with established carcinogenic potential:
- Produces carcinogenic **nitrosamines**
- Chronic candidiasis associated with oral leukoplakia → SCC transformation
- **Candidal leukoplakia** has higher malignant transformation rate than non-candidal leukoplakia

## Clinical Evidence

### Periodontitis and Oral Cancer Risk

| Study Type | Finding |
|---|---|
| Meta-analyses | Periodontitis associated with 2–5× increased oral cancer risk |
| Tooth loss studies | Loss of >10 teeth associated with increased HNSCC risk |
| Mechanism | Chronic inflammation + altered microbiome + carcinogen exposure |

### Poor Oral Hygiene as Independent Risk Factor

- Poor oral hygiene is an **independent risk factor** for oral cancer after adjusting for tobacco and alcohol
- The effect is most pronounced in the **floor of mouth and lateral tongue** — the "cancer-prone crescent" where carcinogen-laden saliva pools

## Diagnostic and Therapeutic Implications

### Microbiome as Biomarker

- Salivary microbiome profiling may serve as a **non-invasive screening tool**
- Specific bacterial signatures (elevated *Fusobacterium*, decreased *Streptococcus*) distinguish OSCC from controls
- Sensitivity 80–90%, specificity 70–85% in pilot studies

### Therapeutic Modulation

| Strategy | Rationale | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Periodontal treatment | Reduce chronic inflammation and dysbiosis | Supported by epidemiological data |
| Probiotics | Restore healthy microbiome composition | Preclinical |
| Antibiotics (targeted) | Eliminate specific pathobionts | Experimental |
| Microbiome transplant | Restore community structure | Very early research |
| Improve oral hygiene | Reduce carcinogen-metabolising bacteria | Public health intervention |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The oral cavity harbours >700 bacterial species; oral cancer patients show significant dysbiosis with increased anaerobic pathobionts
> 2. *Porphyromonas gingivalis* is the strongest candidate for direct carcinogenic role — promotes proliferation, inhibits apoptosis, activates invasion
> 3. *Fusobacterium nucleatum* activates β-catenin/Wnt pathway through FadA adhesin — enriched in tumour tissue
> 4. Oral bacteria metabolise ethanol to acetaldehyde (Group 1 carcinogen) — explaining the alcohol-oral hygiene synergy in cancer risk
> 5. Chronic inflammation from periodontitis is the most established mechanism — NF-κB activation, pro-inflammatory cytokines, DNA damage
> 6. Periodontitis independently increases oral cancer risk 2–5× after adjusting for tobacco and alcohol
> 7. Poor oral hygiene is an independent risk factor — most relevant for floor of mouth and lateral tongue cancers
> 8. *Candida albicans* produces nitrosamines and is associated with increased leukoplakia transformation rates
> 9. Salivary microbiome profiling is a potential non-invasive screening biomarker (sensitivity 80–90%)
> 10. Periodontal treatment and oral hygiene improvement represent modifiable interventions for cancer risk reduction

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **"Treat the teeth to prevent the tumour"** — periodontal disease management is a modifiable cancer risk factor alongside tobacco and alcohol cessation
> 2. For the exam, draw parallels with **H. pylori → gastric cancer** when discussing oral microbiome → oral cancer
> 3. The **acetaldehyde pathway** is the exam-friendly mechanism: alcohol → bacterial ADH → acetaldehyde (carcinogen) → DNA adducts → mutation
> 4. *P. gingivalis* is the oral cancer equivalent of *H. pylori* — remember its pathways: ERK1/2, JAK/STAT3, MMP-9, anti-apoptosis
> 5. Poor oral hygiene compounds the risk from tobacco and alcohol — this is actionable public health knowledge for oral cancer prevention programmes
