## Introduction

Chemoprevention is the use of natural or synthetic agents to reverse, suppress, or prevent the progression of premalignant lesions to invasive cancer, or to prevent the development of second primary tumours (SPTs) in patients already treated for a first cancer. The concept is rooted in the biology of [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Field Cancerization|field cancerization]] — the entire upper aerodigestive tract mucosa is exposed to carcinogens and harbours genetically altered fields that can independently give rise to multiple cancers. Despite decades of research and initially promising results with retinoids, no chemoprevention agent has achieved routine clinical use in oral cancer. The field has shifted from empirical agent testing to molecularly-targeted approaches and biomarker-guided patient selection.

## Levels of Chemoprevention

| Level | Target Population | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | Healthy individuals at risk | Prevent development of premalignant lesions |
| Secondary | Patients with premalignant lesions | Reverse or prevent malignant transformation |
| Tertiary | Patients treated for first cancer | Prevent second primary tumours |

Most oral cancer chemoprevention trials have focused on **secondary** (oral leukoplakia/erythroplakia) and **tertiary** (SPT prevention) chemoprevention.

## Retinoid Chemoprevention

### Biological Rationale

Retinoids (vitamin A derivatives) regulate epithelial cell differentiation, proliferation, and apoptosis through nuclear retinoid receptors (RAR and RXR). Squamous carcinogenesis is associated with loss of RAR-beta expression, and retinoids can restore normal differentiation in dysplastic epithelium. This provided the rationale for the earliest chemoprevention trials.

### Key Retinoid Trials — Chronological Summary

**1. Hong Trial (1986) — The Landmark Study**
- 44 patients with oral leukoplakia randomised to high-dose 13-cis-retinoic acid (1–2 mg/kg/day) vs placebo for 3 months
- **67% clinical response** (vs 10% placebo) and **54% histological reversal of dysplasia** (vs 10% placebo)
- However, 50% relapsed within 3 months of stopping therapy
- Toxicity was significant: cheilitis, facial erythema, hypertriglyceridaemia, conjunctivitis

**2. Lippman Maintenance Trial (1993)**
- Phase IIb trial: 3 months induction with high-dose 13cRA followed by randomisation to 9 months of low-dose 13cRA (0.5 mg/kg/day) vs beta-carotene
- Low-dose 13cRA was significantly superior to beta-carotene in maintaining response
- Established the concept that **sustained therapy is needed** — withdrawal leads to relapse
- Lower toxicity with low-dose regimen

**3. EUROSCAN Trial (van Zandwijk, 2000)**
- 2,592 patients with curatively treated head and neck or lung cancer
- 2×2 factorial design: retinyl palmitate and/or N-acetylcysteine vs placebo for 2 years
- **No benefit** in survival, event-free survival, or SPT prevention
- The largest negative chemoprevention trial

**4. Khuri Phase III Trial (2006)**
- 1,190 patients with stage I–II head and neck cancer (curatively treated)
- Low-dose isotretinoin (30 mg/day) vs placebo for 3 years
- **No difference in SPT rate, recurrence, or survival**
- Subgroup analysis: current smokers had higher SPT rates regardless of treatment arm
- Effectively ended the era of retinoid-based tertiary chemoprevention

### Summary of Retinoid Evidence

The retinoid chemoprevention story follows a characteristic arc: initial small positive trial → larger confirmatory trials fail. High-dose retinoids produce short-term responses in leukoplakia but cannot be sustained due to toxicity, relapse upon withdrawal, and no proven cancer prevention benefit in large trials.

## Other Chemoprevention Agents

### Curcumin

Curcumin (diferuloylmethane) is the active polyphenol in turmeric (Curcuma longa) and has been extensively studied for cancer chemoprevention:

**Mechanisms of action:**
- Inhibits NF-κB signalling pathway — the master regulator of inflammation and survival
- Suppresses COX-2 and iNOS expression → reduces prostaglandin and nitric oxide production
- Induces apoptosis via mitochondrial pathway and caspase activation
- Inhibits angiogenesis (downregulates VEGF)
- Antioxidant activity — scavenges reactive oxygen species
- Modulates cell cycle (G2/M arrest)
- Inhibits matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) → reduces invasion

**Clinical evidence:**
- Phase I trials demonstrate safety up to 8 g/day orally
- Poor oral bioavailability is the major limitation — extensive first-pass metabolism
- Topical application in oral leukoplakia shows histological improvement in small studies
- No large RCT has demonstrated cancer prevention benefit
- Nano-formulations and liposomal delivery systems are being developed to improve bioavailability

**Current status:** Promising preclinical agent with excellent safety profile but limited clinical evidence due to bioavailability challenges. Not recommended as standard chemoprevention.

### Green Tea Polyphenols (EGCG)

- Epigallocatechin-3-gallate (EGCG) is the principal active catechin
- Mechanisms: antioxidant, pro-apoptotic, anti-angiogenic, EGFR inhibition
- Phase II trials in oral leukoplakia show clinical and histological responses in some patients
- Mixed berry preparations containing green tea polyphenols also studied
- Not established as standard chemoprevention

### COX-2 Inhibitors

- Rationale: COX-2 is overexpressed in oral premalignant and malignant lesions
- Ketorolac oral rinse — negative in a randomised trial (did not reduce prostaglandin levels or oral lesion size)
- Celecoxib — studied in oral premalignant lesions; early trials showed some biomarker modulation but no definitive cancer prevention benefit
- Cardiovascular safety concerns have limited enthusiasm for COX-2 inhibitors as chemopreventive agents

### EGFR-Targeted Agents

- Rationale: EGFR is overexpressed in >90% of HNSCC and in premalignant lesions
- **EPOC Trial (William 2016):** erlotinib vs placebo in LOH-positive oral premalignant lesions
  - 3-year cancer-free survival: erlotinib 70% vs placebo 74% — **no benefit**
  - However, LOH status validated as a cancer risk biomarker (LOH-positive: 74% vs LOH-negative: 87% cancer-free survival)
- First "precision medicine" chemoprevention trial — demonstrated the principle of molecular selection even though the agent failed

### Beta-Carotene

- Initial enthusiasm based on epidemiological studies showing association between low dietary intake and cancer risk
- Beta Carotene and Retinol Efficacy Trial (CARET) and ATBC trial: beta-carotene **increased** lung cancer risk in smokers
- Not effective for oral cancer chemoprevention
- No longer recommended

### Other Agents Under Investigation

- **Bowman-Birk inhibitor (BBI)** — soybean-derived protease inhibitor; phase IIb trials in oral leukoplakia
- **Pioglitazone** — PPARγ agonist; preclinical anti-tumour activity
- **Metformin** — epidemiological data suggest reduced cancer risk; mechanism involves AMPK activation and mTOR inhibition
- **Berberine** — plant alkaloid with NF-κB inhibition; early-phase studies

## Why Chemoprevention Has Failed

Several fundamental challenges explain the persistent failure of chemoprevention in oral cancer:

1. **Genetic heterogeneity** — premalignant lesions harbour diverse clonal populations; a single agent cannot target all pathways
2. **Insufficient exposure** — agents that work in vitro often fail to achieve effective tissue concentrations in vivo
3. **Relapse upon withdrawal** — most agents suppress rather than eliminate the genetically altered field
4. **Trial design** — cancer prevention requires large sample sizes, long follow-up, and precise surrogate endpoints; these trials are expensive and slow
5. **Bioavailability** — oral agents (curcumin, green tea) undergo extensive first-pass metabolism
6. **Toxicity** — effective doses of retinoids cause unacceptable side effects for long-term prophylactic use

## Current Status and Future Directions

- **No agent is currently recommended** for routine chemoprevention in oral cancer
- The field has shifted from empirical agent testing to **molecularly-guided approaches:**
  - Loss of heterozygosity (LOH) profiling to identify high-risk premalignant lesions
  - TP53 mutation status as a selection biomarker
  - Immune checkpoint status for potential immunoprevention
- **Immunoprevention** with anti-PD-1/PD-L1 agents is the newest frontier
- **Combination strategies** targeting multiple pathways simultaneously may overcome single-agent limitations
- Tobacco cessation remains the **single most effective cancer prevention intervention** — more effective than any pharmacological agent

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The retinoid chemoprevention story is a cautionary tale: small positive trials → large negative trials. Always demand phase III evidence before adopting preventive strategies
> - When asked about curcumin in exams, emphasise the NF-κB pathway inhibition (its principal mechanism) and poor bioavailability (the reason it hasn't translated clinically)
> - The EPOC trial's lasting contribution was not erlotinib (which failed) but the validation of LOH as a molecular risk stratifier — this is the future of chemoprevention: treat the right patients, not all patients
> - Field cancerization explains why chemoprevention is theoretically attractive — the entire mucosal field is at risk, not just the visible lesion
> - For exam purposes, know the chronological retinoid trial sequence: Hong 1986 (positive) → Lippman 1993 (maintenance) → EUROSCAN 2000 (negative) → Khuri 2006 (negative, definitive)
