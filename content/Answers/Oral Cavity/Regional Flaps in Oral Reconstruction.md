## Overview

Regional (locoregional or pedicled) flaps remain essential in oral reconstruction, particularly when free tissue transfer is unavailable, contraindicated, or unnecessary for the defect. They are especially valuable in resource-limited settings, in patients unfit for prolonged microvascular surgery, and as salvage options when free flaps fail.

## Classification of Regional Flaps

| Type | Blood Supply | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Random | Subdermal plexus | Buccal advancement, tongue flap |
| Axial | Named artery | Nasolabial, submental |
| Musculocutaneous | Perforators through muscle | Pectoralis major, platysma |
| Fasciocutaneous | Fascial plexus | Supraclavicular artery flap |

## Commonly Used Regional Flaps for Oral Cavity

### 1. Pectoralis Major Myocutaneous Flap (PMMF)

The **workhorse pedicled flap** for head and neck reconstruction since Ariyan's description in 1979.

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Blood supply | Thoracoacromial artery (pectoral branch) |
| Arc of rotation | Reaches oral cavity, oropharynx, hypopharynx |
| Skin paddle | Up to 10 × 15 cm from chest wall |
| Bulk | Substantial — suitable for large composite defects |

**Indications in oral reconstruction:**
- Salvage after free flap failure
- Through-and-through cheek defects (composite)
- Large [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]] or tongue defects when free flap not feasible
- Mandibular reconstruction (soft tissue coverage over reconstruction plate)
- Patients unfit for microvascular surgery

**Limitations:**
- Bulky in females (breast tissue)
- Limited reach to anterior oral cavity
- Hair-bearing skin paddle in males
- Donor site morbidity (shoulder weakness, chest contour deformity)
- Higher complication rate than free flaps for intraoral defects (fistula 15–25%)

### 2. Submental Artery Island Flap

Described by Martin et al. (1993), this has become increasingly popular for oral reconstruction.

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Blood supply | Submental artery (branch of facial artery) |
| Skin paddle | Up to 7 × 16 cm |
| Pedicle length | 6–8 cm |
| Tissue type | Thin, pliable skin ± anterior digastric/mylohyoid |

**Indications:**
- Floor of mouth defects (ideal match — thin, pliable)
- Buccal mucosa defects
- Lip reconstruction (colour and texture match)
- Lower facial skin defects
- Tongue defects (small to moderate)

**Oncological concern:**
- **Level IA nodes** are in the submental triangle — flap harvest may compromise nodal clearance
- Contraindicated in **cN+ level I disease** or tumours with high risk of submental nodal metastasis
- For cN0 oral cancers, the flap can be harvested after completion of level I dissection with [[Answers/General/Frozen Section in Surgical Margins|frozen section]] confirmation of negative nodes

**Advantages:** Thin, pliable, excellent colour match for intraoral use, reliable blood supply, single-field surgery (same operative field as tumour resection).

### 3. Nasolabial Flap

An axial flap based on the **nasolabial artery** (branch of facial artery).

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Blood supply | Nasolabial artery |
| Types | Superiorly based; inferiorly based |
| Skin paddle | Up to 2 × 8 cm |
| Best for | Small intraoral defects |

**Indications:**
- Small floor of mouth defects
- Anterior alveolar ridge defects
- Lip defects (local advancement)
- Palatal fistula closure

**Two-stage procedure:** Inferiorly based nasolabial flap for intraoral use requires division of the pedicle at 3 weeks (second stage). Superiorly based variants are one-stage.

### 4. Platysma Myocutaneous Flap

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Blood supply | Submental artery (superiorly based) or occipital artery |
| Skin paddle | Up to 6 × 14 cm |
| Reach | Floor of mouth, buccal mucosa, tonsillar fossa |

**Limitations:** Unreliable in previously irradiated patients; platysma is thin and may not survive in the distal portion; limited arc of rotation.

### 5. Infrahyoid Myocutaneous Flap

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Blood supply | Superior thyroid artery |
| Muscle component | Sternohyoid, sternothyroid, thyrohyoid |
| Skin paddle | Up to 6 × 10 cm |
| Best for | Small-moderate oral/oropharyngeal defects |

**Advantages:** Thin, pliable, good colour match, single-field surgery. Useful in resource-limited settings as an alternative to free flaps for moderate defects.

### 6. Supraclavicular Artery Island Flap (SCAIF)

A resurgent flap gaining popularity:

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Blood supply | Supraclavicular artery (branch of transverse cervical) |
| Skin paddle | Up to 10 × 25 cm |
| Arc of rotation | Reaches oral cavity, oropharynx, hypopharynx |
| Tissue type | Thin, pliable fasciocutaneous flap |

**Advantages over PMMF:** Thinner, better contour, less donor morbidity, excellent reach. Increasingly used as the first-line pedicled flap for oral and oropharyngeal defects.

### 7. Temporalis Muscle Flap

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Blood supply | Deep temporal artery |
| Best for | Maxillary defects, palatal reconstruction |
| Technique | Coronoid process osteotomy allows increased reach |

Used for infrastructure maxillectomy defects and palatal reconstruction. Can be combined with an [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Obturator vs Flap Reconstruction|obturator reconstruction]] prosthesis.

## Comparison of Regional Flaps for Oral Defects

| Flap | Best Defect Site | Skin Match | Bulk | Reliability |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PMMF | Large composite | Poor (hair) | High | High |
| Submental | FOM, buccal | Excellent | Low-moderate | High |
| Nasolabial | Small FOM, alveolus | Good | Low | High |
| SCAIF | Moderate oral/OPC | Good | Low-moderate | Moderate-high |
| Infrahyoid | Small-moderate oral | Good | Low | Moderate |
| Platysma | FOM, buccal | Moderate | Low | Moderate |
| Temporalis | Maxilla, palate | N/A (muscle) | Moderate | High |

## Regional Flaps vs Free Flaps

| Parameter | Regional Flaps | Free Flaps |
|---|---|---|
| Operating time | Shorter (2–4 hrs) | Longer (6–10 hrs) |
| Microsurgical expertise | Not needed | Essential |
| Flap failure rate | 5–10% | 2–5% |
| Fistula rate (oral) | 15–25% | 5–15% |
| Tissue quality | Variable | Tailored to defect |
| Donor morbidity | Moderate | Site-specific |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Resource requirement | Low | High |

### When to Choose Regional Over Free Flaps

- Free flap failure requiring salvage
- Patient unfit for prolonged anaesthesia (ASA III–IV)
- No microvascular expertise or facilities available
- Small-moderate defects where regional flap provides adequate reconstruction
- Vessel-depleted neck (previous surgery, RT, bilateral neck dissection)
- Patient preference for shorter surgery

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Pectoralis major myocutaneous flap (PMMF) is the workhorse pedicled flap — based on thoracoacromial artery; reaches oral cavity and oropharynx
> 2. Submental artery island flap provides excellent thin tissue for FOM and buccal defects — but contraindicated with cN+ level I disease
> 3. Nasolabial flap (nasolabial artery) is ideal for small intraoral defects — inferiorly based type requires second-stage pedicle division
> 4. Supraclavicular artery island flap (SCAIF) is gaining favour as first-line pedicled flap — thinner and less morbid than PMMF
> 5. Infrahyoid flap (superior thyroid artery) is useful in resource-limited settings for moderate oral defects
> 6. Temporalis muscle flap is the regional option for maxillary/palatal reconstruction
> 7. Regional flaps have higher fistula rates (15–25%) than free flaps (5–15%) for intraoral defects
> 8. Regional flaps are indicated when free flaps fail, microvascular expertise is unavailable, or patient fitness precludes prolonged surgery
> 9. PMMF limitations: bulky, hair-bearing, limited anterior reach, higher complication rate than free flaps
> 10. Submental flap can be safely used in cN0 oral cancer after level I dissection with frozen section node clearance

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **PMMF is your safety net** — always plan it as a backup when performing complex free flap reconstructions; marking the skin paddle preoperatively takes 2 minutes
> 2. The **submental flap** is the closest tissue match for intraoral lining — if the neck is N0, use it preferentially over PMMF for FOM and buccal defects
> 3. **SCAIF is replacing PMMF** in many centres as the go-to pedicled flap — better tissue match, less bulk, lower donor morbidity
> 4. For **small anterior FOM defects**, a nasolabial flap provides excellent reconstruction without the morbidity of a free flap — remember it usually needs a second stage for pedicle division
> 5. In the exam, remember Ariyan (1979) for PMMF and Martin (1993) for submental flap — these are commonly asked eponyms
