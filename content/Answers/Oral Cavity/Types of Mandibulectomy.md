## Introduction

The mandible is frequently involved in oral cavity cancers due to its anatomical proximity to the gingiva, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]], retromolar trigone, and buccal mucosa. The fundamental question in surgical management is **when to preserve and when to sacrifice the mandible**. The historical "commando" operation (composite resection with routine segmental mandibulectomy) has been replaced by individualised decision-making based on tumour biology, pattern of invasion, imaging, and patient factors. No lymphatic channels pass through the mandible — therefore, routine sacrifice of uninvolved mandible for en-bloc lymphatic clearance is not justified.

## Pathways of Mandibular Invasion

### In Dentate Patients

- Tumour reaches the mandible at the **point of abutment** — the junction of attached and reflected mucosa (gingivobuccal or gingivolingual sulcus)
- Enters through **dental pores** in the alveolar ridge → cancellous bone
- **Cortical bone inferior to tooth roots remains uninvolved** initially
- The periosteum, inferior alveolar canal, and inferior cortex act as barriers

### In Edentulous Patients

- Alveolar ridge atrophy → tumour reaches cortical bone more easily through cortical defects
- **Pipestem mandible** (severely atrophic): mandibular canal near surface, minimal bone stock
- Tumour may enter through mental foramen or dental pores

### Patterns of Invasion (Wong et al. 2000)

| Pattern | Histology | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Erosive** | Broad advancing front, osteoclast-mediated resorption | Better prognosis |
| **Infiltrative** | Finger-like projections of tumour into bone | 4× increased risk of death |

## Classification of Mandibulectomy Types

### 1. Marginal (Rim) Mandibulectomy

**Definition:** Resection of the alveolar portion of the mandible while preserving the inferior border and continuity of the mandible.

**Types:**
- **Superior marginal** — removal of alveolar crest (most common)
- **Lateral marginal** — removal of the lateral/buccal cortex
- **Lingual marginal** — removal of the lingual cortex (for lingual soft tissue tumours)
- **Reverse marginal** — removal of lingual cortex from the inner surface (for FOM tumours abutting lingual plate)

**Indications:**
- Tumour abutting the mandible without gross bone invasion
- Tumour limited to alveolar process (superficial erosive invasion)
- Adequate residual mandibular height (≥1 cm inferior cortex)
- Dentate patients with sufficient bone stock below tooth roots

**Contraindications:**
- Gross invasion of cancellous bone or inferior alveolar canal
- Infiltrative pattern of bone invasion
- Severely atrophic (pipestem) mandible — fracture risk too high
- Previous radiotherapy to the mandible — compromised vascularity
- Tumour encasing the mandible

**Technical principles (Jatin Shah):**
- [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Marginal Mandibulectomy|marginal mandibulectomy]] should be performed in a **smooth curve** (not right-angled cuts) to distribute stress evenly and prevent fracture
- Exposed cancellous bone after resection should be covered with soft tissue (buccal fat pad or local flap)
- Minimum 1 cm residual mandibular height is required

**Advantages:**
- Preserves mandibular continuity → no need for bony reconstruction
- Better cosmesis and function (mastication, occlusion)
- Shorter operative time, lower morbidity
- Equivalent oncologic outcome when appropriately selected

**Disadvantages:**
- Risk of pathological fracture if inadequate bone remains
- Not suitable for edentulous/atrophic mandibles
- May be inadequate if tumour is more extensive than anticipated

### 2. Segmental Mandibulectomy

**Definition:** Resection of a full-thickness segment of the mandible, creating a discontinuity defect.

**Indications (Jatin Shah):**
1. Gross invasion of mandible by oral cancer
2. Invasion of inferior alveolar nerve or canal by tumour
3. Massive soft tissue disease adjacent to the mandible (where adequate access requires bone removal)
4. Primary malignant tumour of the mandible (e.g., osteosarcoma, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Ameloblastoma of Mandible|ameloblastoma]])
5. Tumour that has metastasised to the mandible

**Subtypes by location:**

| Type | Segment Resected | Reconstruction |
|---|---|---|
| **Lateral** | Body (posterior to mental foramen) | Fibula FFF or plate |
| **Anterior** | Symphysis/parasymphysis | Fibula FFF essential (Andy Gump deformity if not reconstructed) |
| **Hemimandibulectomy** | Half the mandible (body + ramus) | Fibula FFF with condylar component |
| **Subtotal** | >Half the mandible | Complex reconstruction needed |

**Advantages:**
- Ensures oncologically adequate resection for invasive tumours
- Clear margins more reliably achieved

**Disadvantages:**
- Creates discontinuity defect → requires bony reconstruction
- Significant functional impairment (mastication, speech, deglutition) if not reconstructed
- "Andy Gump" deformity if anterior segment not reconstructed
- Longer operative time, higher morbidity
- Donor site morbidity from free flap harvest

### 3. Hemimandibulectomy

**Definition:** Resection of the entire half of the mandible from the symphysis to the condyle (or including the condyle = total hemimandibulectomy).

**Indications:**
- Extensive tumour involving the body and ramus
- Tumour extending to the [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Masticator Space|masticator space]]/[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Infratemporal Fossa Spread|infratemporal fossa]]
- Recurrent tumour after prior mandibular surgery

### 4. Total Mandibulectomy

**Definition:** Resection of the entire mandible.

**Indications:**
- Extremely rare; reserved for bilateral tumour involvement
- Devastating functional consequences — reconstruction extremely challenging

### 5. Inner Table Mandibulectomy (Sagittal Split)

**Definition:** Removal of the medial (lingual) cortex and cancellous bone while preserving the lateral cortex and inferior border.

**Indications:**
- Selected floor of mouth tumours with lingual cortex abutment
- No invasion through to lateral cortex

## Mandibular Defect Classification (Urken)

| Symbol | Segment | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **C** | Condyle | Condylar segment |
| **R** | Ramus | Ascending ramus |
| **B** | Body | Body (posterior to canine) |
| **S** | Symphysis | Symphysis (canine to canine) |

Defects are described by combining symbols, e.g., BSB = body + symphysis + contralateral body. This classification guides reconstruction planning — anterior (S-containing) defects are the most functionally significant and mandate bony reconstruction.

## Reconstruction by Defect Type

| Defect | Preferred Reconstruction |
|---|---|
| Marginal (any site) | No bony reconstruction needed |
| Lateral body (B) | Fibula FFF or reconstruction plate |
| Anterior (S, SB, BSB) | Fibula FFF (mandatory) |
| Hemimandible (CRBS) | Fibula FFF with condylar component |

## Decision-Making Algorithm

1. **Tumour not abutting mandible** → No mandibulectomy; soft tissue resection only
2. **Tumour abutting mandible, no radiographic invasion** → Marginal mandibulectomy (if adequate bone stock)
3. **Superficial erosive invasion on imaging** → Marginal mandibulectomy (if technically feasible)
4. **Gross invasion of cancellous bone or IAN canal** → Segmental mandibulectomy
5. **Massive soft tissue disease with encasement** → Segmental mandibulectomy
6. **Pipestem mandible or prior radiation** → Segmental mandibulectomy (marginal too risky)
7. **Intraoperative [[Answers/General/Frozen Section in Surgical Margins|frozen section]]** of the marrow cavity should guide extent of resection

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. No lymphatic channels pass through the mandible — routine composite resection is not justified
> 2. Two patterns of bone invasion: erosive (better prognosis) and infiltrative (4× worse survival)
> 3. Marginal mandibulectomy preserves continuity; requires ≥1 cm residual height and smooth curved cuts
> 4. Segmental mandibulectomy indications: gross invasion, IAN involvement, massive soft tissue disease, primary bone tumour
> 5. Pipestem mandible and prior RT are contraindications for marginal mandibulectomy
> 6. Anterior (symphyseal) defects mandate bony reconstruction to prevent Andy Gump deformity
> 7. Fibula free flap is the gold standard for segmental defect reconstruction
> 8. Urken classification (C-R-B-S) guides reconstruction planning
> 9. Teeth near the tumour should NOT be extracted preoperatively — opens dental sockets and risks tumour implantation into the mandibular canal

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - When performing marginal mandibulectomy, **never make right-angled cuts** — always use smooth curves to prevent stress fractures
> - In dentate patients, the intact inferior cortex below tooth roots provides the biological rationale for marginal mandibulectomy
> - If frozen section of the mandibular marrow is positive, convert to segmental mandibulectomy intraoperatively
> - A bridging reconstruction plate without bone graft can be used as a temporising measure in patients unfit for free flap, but long-term plate exposure and fracture rates are high
> - The "commando" concept of routine composite resection is obsolete — individualise based on tumour biology and imaging
