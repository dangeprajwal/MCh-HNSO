> [!cite] Landmark Articles

> Reichart et al. (1995) published the largest pooled analysis of 3,677 ameloblastoma cases, defining epidemiology, site distribution, and recurrence patterns that remain the reference standard. Carlson and Marx (2006) established segmental resection with 1.5 cm margins as the gold standard for solid ameloblastoma, demonstrating recurrence rates of 0–5% compared to 50–90% with curettage. Lau and Samman (2006) performed a systematic review demonstrating lower recurrence with resection versus conservative treatment for unicystic ameloblastoma, helping stratify treatment by subtype. Kurppa et al. (2014) were the first to demonstrate BRAF V600E mutations in 63% of ameloblastomas, providing the rationale for targeted therapy with vemurafenib in unresectable or recurrent cases. Mendenhall et al. (2007) provided a comprehensive review of ameloblastoma management including the emerging role of adjuvant radiotherapy for unresectable and recurrent cases.

## Overview

Ameloblastoma is the **most common clinically significant odontogenic tumour**, arising from the odontogenic epithelium. Though histologically benign, it is **biologically aggressive** with locally destructive behaviour and a propensity for recurrence after inadequate excision. It essentially recapitulates the development of the early tooth-forming apparatus without forming enamel.

## Epidemiology

- Accounts for approximately **1% of all jaw tumours** and **11–18% of odontogenic tumours**
- Peak incidence: **3rd–4th decade** of life
- No gender predilection
- Equal racial distribution

### Site Distribution

| Site | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Mandible | 80% |
| Maxilla | 20% |

**Mandibular distribution:** 70% molar and ascending ramus; 20% premolar region; 10% incisor region.

## WHO Classification (2017)

The WHO (2017) classification divides ameloblastoma into:

| Type | Frequency | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Conventional (solid/multicystic) | 85% | Multiloculated, aggressive; highest recurrence |
| Unicystic | 10% | Young adults; mandibular molar-ramus; less aggressive |
| Peripheral (extraosseous) | 2–5% | Soft tissue only; excision curative |
| Metastasizing | <1% | Histologically benign but distant metastases (lung) |

### Histological Subtypes (Conventional)

| Pattern | Description |
|---|---|
| Follicular | Nests of epithelium with peripheral palisading and central stellate reticulum |
| Plexiform | Anastomosing strands of odontogenic epithelium |
| Acanthomatous | Squamous metaplasia centrally |
| Granular cell | Granular cytoplasmic change |
| Desmoplastic | Dense collagenous stroma |
| Basal cell | Basaloid cell pattern |

The **follicular** pattern is the most common. All histological subtypes of conventional ameloblastoma share similar aggressive behaviour.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms and Signs
- **Painless jaw swelling** — most common presentation
- Slowly progressive over months to years
- Facial asymmetry in advanced cases
- Loosening or displacement of teeth
- Pathological fracture (large tumours)
- Paraesthesia of inferior alveolar nerve (rare — if present, consider malignant transformation)

### Radiological Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Classic appearance | **"Soap-bubble"** multiloculated radiolucency |
| Multicystic type | Multiple loculations with scalloped margins |
| Unicystic type | Unilocular well-defined radiolucency |
| Root resorption | "Knife-edge" resorption of adjacent tooth roots |
| Cortical expansion | Buccal and lingual cortex thinning/perforation |
| CT/MRI | Enhancing solid component, cystic areas |

**OPG (orthopantomogram)** is the initial screening investigation. **CT with 3D reconstruction** is essential for surgical planning, demonstrating extent and cortical integrity. **MRI** helps differentiate solid from cystic components.

## Molecular Biology

Recent advances have identified key mutations:

| Mutation | Frequency | Subtype | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| BRAF V600E | 46–63% | Conventional mandibular | Potential target for vemurafenib |
| SMO (Hedgehog pathway) | 39–88% | Maxillary predominance | Target for vismodegib |
| FGFR2 | Rare | — | Tyrosine kinase pathway |
| KRAS | Rare | Metastasizing type | Aggressive behaviour |

**BRAF V600E** is the single most common mutation in conventional ameloblastoma and has therapeutic implications — vemurafenib and dabrafenib have shown dramatic responses in case reports of unresectable/recurrent ameloblastoma.

## Management

### Principles

The optimal treatment for ameloblastoma is **complete excision at initial presentation**. Inadequate initial treatment leads to recurrence requiring progressively more extensive resection.

### Conservative vs Radical Treatment

| Approach | Technique | Recurrence Rate | Indications |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conservative | Enucleation ± curettage | 50–90% | NOT recommended for solid type |
| Conservative | Enucleation + Carnoy's solution | 10–30% | Unicystic (selected cases) |
| Conservative | Marsupialization → enucleation | 10–20% | Large unicystic in young patients |
| Radical | [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Marginal Mandibulectomy|marginal mandibulectomy]] | 5–15% | Small, localized tumours away from inferior border |
| Radical | [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Types of Mandibulectomy|segmental mandibulectomy]] | 0–5% | Standard for solid/multicystic type |

### Treatment by Type

**Conventional (Solid/Multicystic) Ameloblastoma:**
- **Segmental mandibulectomy** with 1–1.5 cm bone margin beyond radiographic extent
- Curettage/enucleation → **unacceptably high recurrence** (50–90%) — NOT recommended
- Immediate reconstruction with **vascularized fibula free flap** for mandibular continuity defects
- Pre-plating technique for accuracy

**Unicystic Ameloblastoma:**
- **Luminal type** (no mural invasion): Enucleation + Carnoy's solution application to cavity walls; recurrence rate 10–20%
- **Mural type** (invasion into cyst wall): Treat as conventional — resection recommended
- **Marsupialization** as first step for large unicystic lesions in young patients → allows shrinkage → delayed enucleation

**Peripheral Ameloblastoma:**
- Excision with clear soft tissue margins
- Curettage of underlying bone
- Recurrence rate: <5%
- Excellent prognosis

### Maxillary Ameloblastoma

- More aggressive behaviour than mandibular — thinner bone barriers allow rapid spread to nasal cavity, maxillary sinus, orbit, skull base
- **Partial or total maxillectomy** required
- Higher recurrence rate than mandibular ameloblastoma
- Consider [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Oral Cancer|adjuvant RT]] for close/positive margins in maxillary cases

## Reconstruction

| Defect | Reconstruction Option | Advantages |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral mandible defect | Fibula free flap | Gold standard; 25 cm bone stock; allows [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Dental Implants after Mandibular Reconstruction|dental implants]] |
| Anterior mandible (arch) | Fibula with double-barrel | Restores height; implant-ready |
| Posterior body/ramus | Fibula or iliac crest | Bulk for masticatory function |
| Soft tissue only (peripheral) | Primary closure | Minimal defect |

**[[Answers/Oral Cavity/3D Models in Mandibular Reconstruction|3D planning and patient-specific cutting guides]]** improve accuracy of reconstruction and reduce operative time.

## Recurrence and Follow-Up

### Recurrence Patterns
- **Peak recurrence:** 2–5 years post-surgery, but can occur up to 10–15 years
- **Local recurrence** — soft tissue or residual bone
- Multifocal recurrence possible after inadequate initial surgery

### Prognostic Factors for Recurrence

| Factor | Higher Recurrence Risk |
|---|---|
| Treatment type | Conservative > radical |
| Histological type | Conventional > unicystic |
| Margin status | Involved > clear |
| Location | Maxilla > mandible |
| Age | Older adults with maxillary tumours |

### Follow-Up Protocol
- Clinical and radiographic (OPG) every 6 months for 5 years, then annually for 10 years
- **CT/MRI** for any clinical suspicion of recurrence
- Total follow-up: minimum **10 years** recommended due to late recurrences

## Malignant Ameloblastoma

### Ameloblastic Carcinoma
- Malignant transformation with cytological atypia, mitoses, necrosis
- Aggressive behaviour with potential for metastasis
- Treatment: radical resection + adjuvant RT ± chemotherapy

### Metastasizing Ameloblastoma
- Histologically benign primary but with **distant metastases** (most commonly lung)
- Extremely rare (<1%)
- Diagnosed retrospectively when metastases show benign ameloblastomatous histology
- Treatment: resection of primary + pulmonary metastasectomy when feasible

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Ameloblastoma is the most common clinically significant odontogenic tumour — histologically benign but biologically aggressive
> 2. 80% occur in the mandible (70% molar-ramus region); peak incidence in the 3rd–4th decade
> 3. Classic radiographic appearance is the multiloculated "soap-bubble" radiolucency with root resorption
> 4. WHO 2017 classifies into conventional (85%), unicystic (10%), peripheral (2–5%), and metastasizing (<1%)
> 5. BRAF V600E mutation found in 46–63% of conventional ameloblastoma — vemurafenib shows promising responses
> 6. Segmental mandibulectomy with 1–1.5 cm margins is the gold standard for conventional ameloblastoma (recurrence 0–5%)
> 7. Enucleation/curettage has unacceptably high recurrence (50–90%) for solid type — NOT recommended
> 8. Unicystic ameloblastoma may be treated conservatively (enucleation + Carnoy's) if luminal type; mural invasion requires resection
> 9. Maxillary ameloblastoma is more aggressive — partial/total maxillectomy needed; consider adjuvant RT for close margins
> 10. Follow-up for minimum 10 years — recurrences can present up to 15 years post-treatment

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Never curet a solid ameloblastoma** — inadequate initial treatment creates a worse surgical problem later with multifocal recurrence in soft tissues
> 2. In young patients with large unicystic ameloblastoma, **marsupialization first** allows tumour shrinkage, preserving jaw growth and reducing the extent of eventual surgery
> 3. Always get **CT with 3D reconstruction** before surgery — OPG alone underestimates the extent of cortical perforation and soft tissue extension
> 4. For large mandibular ameloblastoma, **pre-plate the fibula** using 3D-printed cutting guides — this dramatically improves occlusal and contour outcomes
> 5. If a patient with unresectable or multiply recurrent ameloblastoma — request **BRAF V600E testing**; vemurafenib has produced dramatic shrinkage in case series
