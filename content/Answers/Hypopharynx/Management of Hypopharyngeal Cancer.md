## Surgical Anatomy of the Hypopharynx

The hypopharynx (laryngopharynx) extends from the level of the hyoid bone (pharyngoepiglottic fold) superiorly to the inferior border of the cricoid cartilage inferiorly, where it is continuous with the cervical oesophagus. It has three subsites:

**1. Pyriform sinus (most common subsite, ~65–85% of hypopharyngeal cancers)**
The pyriform sinus is a paired funnel-shaped recess lying lateral to the larynx on each side. It has three walls:
- **Medial wall**: formed by the aryepiglottic fold and the lateral surface of the arytenoid and cricoid cartilages. Tumours on the medial wall spread early to the larynx (paraglottic space, posterior cricoarytenoid muscle) and are associated with vocal cord fixation.
- **Lateral wall**: abutting the thyroid cartilage medially and the thyrohyoid membrane superiorly. Tumours here tend to invade cartilage relatively early.
- **Apex**: the most inferior part, at the level of the cricoid cartilage. Apex involvement is associated with submucosal spread to the postcricoid region and cervical oesophagus, and carries a worse prognosis.

**2. Posterior pharyngeal wall**
Extends from the floor of the vallecula to the pharyngo-oesophageal junction. Tumours here spread along the prevertebral fascia and tend to present at an advanced stage with limited surgical options.

**3. Postcricoid region**
The anterior wall of the hypopharynx at the level of the arytenoid cartilages and cricoid. Almost exclusively affects women in association with iron-deficiency anaemia (Plummer-Vinson/Paterson-Brown-Kelly syndrome). Has strong tendency for submucosal spread, often requiring total laryngopharyngo-oesophagectomy.

**Lymphatic drainage**: Rich bilateral lymphatic network. The pyriform sinus drains primarily to levels II, III, and IV. Retropharyngeal nodes (level VII) are commonly involved, particularly for posterior pharyngeal wall and postcricoid tumours. The incidence of occult nodal metastasis for clinically N0 pyriform sinus cancer is 30–40%.

## AJCC 8th Edition Staging — Hypopharynx

**T-classification:**
- **T1**: Tumour limited to one subsite of the hypopharynx AND ≤2 cm in greatest dimension
- **T2**: Tumour invades more than one subsite of the hypopharynx OR an adjacent site, OR measures >2 cm but ≤4 cm, WITHOUT fixation of the hemilarynx
- **T3**: Tumour >4 cm OR with fixation of the hemilarynx OR extension to the oesophagus
- **T4a**: Moderately advanced local disease — tumour invades any of: thyroid/cricoid cartilage, hyoid bone, thyroid gland, central compartment soft tissue (includes strap muscles and subcutaneous fat)
- **T4b**: Very advanced local disease — tumour invades prevertebral fascia, encases carotid artery, or involves mediastinal structures

**N-classification (clinical, AJCC 8th edition):**
- N1: Single ipsilateral node ≤3 cm, ENE(−)
- N2a: Single ipsilateral node >3 cm but ≤6 cm, ENE(−)
- N2b: Multiple ipsilateral nodes, none >6 cm, ENE(−)
- N2c: Bilateral or contralateral nodes, none >6 cm, ENE(−)
- N3a: Any node >6 cm, ENE(−)
- N3b: Any node with clinical ENE(+)

## T2 N0 Pyriform Sinus Cancer: Treatment Options

### Patient Assessment
Before treatment planning a full assessment is mandatory:
- **History**: dysphagia, odynophagia, referred otalgia (Arnold's nerve reflex via CN X), hoarseness (suggests medial wall or apex involvement)
- **Examination**: indirect laryngoscopy, flexible nasoendoscopy assessing vocal cord mobility, pooling of secretions in pyriform sinus
- **Imaging**: CT neck and chest (cartilage invasion, nodal disease), MRI (soft tissue extension, prevertebral fascia)
- **Endoscopy under anaesthesia**: bimanual palpation, assessment of tumour extent, biopsy. Rigid oesophagoscopy to rule out synchronous oesophageal tumour
- **Audiogram, dental assessment, PFTs, nutritional assessment**

### Option 1: Conservation (Organ-Preserving) Surgery

**Partial pharyngectomy ± hemilaryngectomy** is appropriate for carefully selected T2 pyriform sinus cancers where the vocal cord is mobile, the apex is not involved, and the contralateral larynx and hypopharynx are normal.

**Surgical approach — Lateral pharyngotomy:**
- External approach through the neck; the thyroid ala is retracted or partially resected
- Pharynx entered lateral to the larynx
- Tumour excised with a cuff of normal mucosa (minimum 1 cm margins)
- If medial wall is involved, partial or hemilaryngectomy (resection of ipsilateral arytenoid, aryepiglottic fold, and adjacent portion of the pyriform sinus) is added
- Primary closure of the pharynx if adequate mucosa remains; rarely requires reconstruction for T2 lesions
- Neck dissection (ipsilateral levels II–IV) performed concurrently

**Oncological outcomes (conservation surgery for T2 pyriform):**
- 5-year local control: 70–80%
- Functional results: voice preservation in >90%; swallowing recovery in most patients within weeks

**Contraindications to conservation surgery:**
- Apex or postcricoid involvement (risk of incomplete resection)
- Vocal cord fixation (T3 by definition)
- Cartilage invasion on imaging
- Inadequate pulmonary reserve (aspiration risk)
- Contralateral hypopharyngeal involvement

### Option 2: Concurrent Chemoradiotherapy (CRT) — Organ Preservation

For T2 N0 pyriform sinus cancer in patients who wish to preserve laryngeal function and are not surgical candidates, or where surgical margins would be compromised, concurrent CRT is a well-established alternative.

**Regimen:**
- **Radiotherapy**: 66–70 Gy in 33–35 fractions over 7 weeks (IMRT technique preferred to spare contralateral parotid and spinal cord)
- **Chemotherapy**: Cisplatin 100 mg/m² on days 1, 22, and 43 (three-cycle concurrent regimen)

**Outcomes for T2 N0:**
- 5-year locoregional control: 75–85%
- Organ preservation: achieved in ~80–85% of T2 tumours
- 5-year overall survival: 60–70% (similar to surgical cohorts)

**Patient selection for CRT:**
- ECOG performance status 0–1
- Adequate renal function (creatinine clearance ≥50 mL/min for cisplatin)
- No cartilage destruction on imaging
- Functional larynx pre-treatment (adequate swallowing and airway)

### Option 3: Radiotherapy Alone

Reserved for patients unfit for cisplatin (age >70, comorbidities, renal impairment). Alternative radiosensitisers include carboplatin or cetuximab. Outcomes are inferior to concurrent CRT.

### Multidisciplinary Decision for T2 N0

The MDT discussion must weigh:
- Functional outcome (voice quality, swallowing, aspiration risk)
- Oncological outcome (locoregional control rates are equivalent between conservation surgery and CRT for T2 N0)
- Patient preference (permanent tracheostomy with surgery is avoided with both options; risk of chronic aspiration post-surgery vs xerostomia/dysphagia post-CRT)
- Institutional expertise

For most T2 N0 pyriform sinus cancers, **concurrent CRT is the preferred primary treatment** given equivalent oncological outcomes and avoidance of surgical morbidity. Conservation surgery is an excellent option in specialised centres for lateralised, apex-free T2 tumours with mobile cord.

## T3/T4a Hypopharyngeal SCC: Treatment Options

### T3 Pyriform Sinus Cancer

T3 disease (hemilarynx fixation, or tumour >4 cm, or oesophageal extension) represents a treatment dilemma between oncological adequacy and functional preservation.

**Option A: Total Laryngopharyngectomy (TLP)**
- Surgical removal of the larynx, hypopharynx, and adjacent structures
- Reconstruction: primary closure (if adequate mucosa remains), pectoralis major myocutaneous flap, free jejunal transfer, or radial forearm free flap (tubed) depending on defect
- Permanent tracheostomy; voice rehabilitation via tracheoesophageal puncture (TEP) or electrolarynx
- Adjuvant RT (60–66 Gy) or CRT (positive margins, ENE) added postoperatively
- Neck dissection: bilateral levels II–IV

**Option B: Organ Preservation — Induction Chemotherapy Followed by RT (EORTC 24891 Protocol)**
- Induction PF (cisplatin 100 mg/m² day 1 + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/day continuous infusion days 1–5) × 2–3 cycles
- Patients with complete or near-complete response (≥50% tumour regression) → definitive RT 70 Gy
- Non-responders → total laryngopharyngectomy
- EORTC 24891 (Lefebvre 1996): equivalent OS; 35% long-term larynx preservation

**Option C: Concurrent CRT (Current Standard for Resectable T3)**
- Cisplatin 100 mg/m² × 3 cycles concurrent with RT 70 Gy
- Evidence basis: RTOG 91-11 established concurrent CRT as superior to induction chemotherapy followed by RT for larynx preservation (applies to larynx; extrapolated to hypopharynx in many centres)
- Organ preservation rate: ~60–70% for T3

### T4a Pyriform Sinus Cancer

**T4a is defined as moderately advanced local disease** with invasion of thyroid or cricoid cartilage, hyoid bone, thyroid gland, or central compartment soft tissue. It is potentially resectable.

**Preferred treatment: Total Laryngopharyngectomy + adjuvant CRT**
- Surgery provides the best chance of locoregional control for T4a with cartilage destruction, which does not respond well to RT
- Adjuvant cisplatin-based CRT is mandatory if positive margins (R1) or ENE on pathology (RTOG 9501 / EORTC 22931 level I evidence)
- If margins are clear and no ENE: adjuvant RT alone (60–66 Gy)

**Organ preservation for T4a — when acceptable:**
- T4a without cartilage destruction (e.g., invasion of soft tissue only) in a patient with adequate pre-treatment laryngeal function may be offered concurrent CRT
- Induction TPF followed by CRT (sequential protocol): in responders, this avoids laryngectomy in ~50–60% of cases

**T4b: Unresectable disease**
- Concurrent CRT (platinum-based) is standard
- Consider cetuximab-based CRT if unfit for cisplatin
- Palliative intent if poor PS

## T4N1 Pyriform Sinus Cancer: Assessment and Treatment Allocation

### Clinical Assessment

**History:**
- Duration and progression of dysphagia, referred otalgia, voice change, neck mass
- Weight loss, nutritional status
- Performance status (ECOG/Karnofsky)
- Comorbidities (renal, cardiac, pulmonary — relevant to treatment choice)
- Tobacco and alcohol history

**Examination:**
- Full head and neck examination with flexible nasoendoscopy
- Assessment of vocal cord mobility (immobile cord = T3–T4)
- Palpation of neck nodes: size, number, fixity, ENE
- Trismus (pterygoid involvement — T4b feature if from carotid/skull base)
- Pooling of secretions in pyriform sinus on endoscopy is pathognomonic

**Investigations:**
- **CT neck and chest (with contrast)**: assess tumour extent, cartilage invasion (cortical erosion, breakthrough, sclerosis), nodal characteristics, distant metastases. CT chest mandatory for lung secondaries and synchronous primary
- **MRI neck**: superior soft tissue delineation; assess prevertebral fascia, carotid artery encasement (T4b features), and true extent of submucosal spread
- **PET-CT**: for staging, particularly for occult nodal disease and distant metastases in advanced disease
- **Panendoscopy under GA**: rigid laryngoscopy, rigid oesophagoscopy (rule out synchronous oesophageal primary, assess extent of tumour, obtain biopsy)
- **PFTs**: relevant if thoracic surgery (oesophagectomy) may be needed; also pre-anaesthetic assessment
- **Nutritional assessment**: dietitian review, consider NG tube or PEG if dysphagia severe pre-treatment

### Staging T4N1 Pyriform Sinus Cancer

A T4N1 lesion is Stage IVA disease (AJCC 8th edition) if T4a, N1, M0. This represents moderately advanced resectable disease.

**Key distinction: T4a vs T4b**
- T4a (resectable): cartilage invasion, strap muscle involvement, thyroid gland invasion — amenable to surgical resection
- T4b (unresectable): prevertebral fascia involvement, carotid encasement (>270° contact), mediastinal involvement

For T4a N1 M0 (Stage IVA):

### Treatment Allocation

**Standard recommendation: Total Laryngopharyngectomy + bilateral neck dissection + adjuvant treatment**

**Surgery:**
- Total laryngopharyngectomy with ipsilateral therapeutic neck dissection (levels II–V)
- Contralateral elective neck dissection (levels II–IV) given high risk of bilateral nodal spread in pyriform sinus cancer
- Reconstruction based on defect: primary closure if adequate mucosa; pectoralis major myocutaneous flap for partial defects; free jejunal transfer or gastric pull-up for circumferential pharyngeal defects
- Permanent tracheostomy; TEP at time of surgery or as secondary procedure

**Pathological findings guiding adjuvant therapy:**
- **Positive margins (R1) OR extranodal extension (ENE)**: adjuvant concurrent CRT (cisplatin 100 mg/m² × 3 cycles + RT 60–66 Gy). Level I evidence: EORTC 22931 (Bernier 2004) and RTOG 9501 (Cooper 2004)
- **Clear margins, N1 without ENE**: adjuvant RT alone (60–63 Gy to primary bed; 54–60 Gy elective to neck)
- **Adverse pathological features** (>1 positive node, perineural invasion, lymphovascular invasion): consider CRT over RT alone

**Organ preservation — is it appropriate for T4N1?**
- Organ preservation protocols (induction chemotherapy → RT, or concurrent CRT) are generally **not recommended for T4a with radiographic cartilage destruction**
- Rationale: cartilage-invaded larynx rarely functions adequately even if preserved; RT cannot sterilise cartilage invasion reliably; residual/recurrent disease in a destroyed larynx is difficult to salvage
- Exception: T4a without radiographic cartilage breakthrough (soft tissue invasion only), functional larynx pre-treatment, and patient preference after informed consent — may be offered CRT with upfront counselling about salvage laryngectomy

## Neck Management in Hypopharyngeal Cancer

| Nodal Status | Management |
|---|---|
| cN0 (clinical and radiological) | Elective ND levels II–IV ipsilateral; consider contralateral ND given 30–40% bilateral nodal risk |
| cN+ (therapeutic ND) | Selective or modified radical ND of involved levels; bilateral if bilateral nodes |
| Post-CRT complete nodal response | Observation (PET-CT at 12 weeks) or planned ND if ≥N2b at presentation |
| Post-CRT residual nodal disease | Salvage neck dissection |

Retropharyngeal nodes (level VII): irradiated in the RT field but not accessible to surgical dissection; important to include in radiation planning volumes.

## Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Decision Framework

The hypopharynx MDT must include head and neck surgeon, radiation oncologist, medical oncologist, radiologist, speech and language therapist, dietitian, and clinical nurse specialist. Key decision factors:

1. **Tumour factors**: T stage, subsite, cartilage invasion, oesophageal extension, nodal burden
2. **Functional factors**: pre-treatment vocal cord mobility, swallowing function (videofluoroscopy if needed), aspiration risk
3. **Patient factors**: age, PS, comorbidities (cardiac, pulmonary, renal), patient preference
4. **Institutional factors**: availability of microsurgical reconstruction, expertise in conservation surgery

The fundamental oncological principle is that **no organ preservation strategy should compromise survival**. In T4a with cartilage destruction, survival is the primary goal and surgery is preferred.

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> The EORTC 24891 trial, conducted by Lefebvre and colleagues, was the first prospective randomised controlled trial to demonstrate that organ-preserving treatment is feasible in hypopharyngeal cancer without compromising overall survival. Patients with resectable squamous cell carcinoma of the pyriform sinus or aryepiglottic fold were randomised to either conventional treatment (total laryngopharyngectomy + postoperative RT) or an organ-preservation arm (induction cisplatin–5-FU × 2–3 cycles, followed by definitive RT in complete responders and surgery in non-responders). Median follow-up was 10 years. Overall survival was equivalent between arms (overall HR for death 0.85, 95% CI 0.65–1.11), with 35% of the organ-preservation arm alive with a functional larynx at 3 years. This trial established induction chemotherapy followed by RT as a valid strategy for hypopharyngeal organ preservation and formed the oncological basis for subsequent trials.
>
> The GORTEC 99-02 trial (Pointreau 2009) refined the induction chemotherapy regimen by randomising 220 patients with locally advanced laryngeal or hypopharyngeal cancer to TPF (docetaxel 75 mg/m² day 1 + cisplatin 75 mg/m² day 1 + 5-FU 750 mg/m²/day × 5 days) versus PF (cisplatin 100 mg/m² day 1 + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/day × 5 days). Responders received definitive RT; non-responders underwent total laryngectomy. The 3-year actuarial larynx preservation rate was 70.3% with TPF versus 57.5% with PF (p=0.03), and the overall response rate to induction chemotherapy was 80% vs 59.2% (p=0.002). Overall survival was similar between arms. On the basis of these results, TPF induction became the standard regimen for organ preservation protocols in larynx and hypopharynx cancer, replacing PF as the preferred induction regimen.

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
>
> Hypopharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma most commonly arises in the pyriform sinus and carries a poor prognosis due to late presentation, rich lymphatic drainage, and propensity for submucosal spread. T2 N0 pyriform sinus cancer offers the choice between conservation surgery (partial pharyngectomy ± hemilaryngectomy via lateral pharyngotomy) and concurrent chemoradiotherapy, with equivalent oncological outcomes; the MDT decision hinges on vocal cord mobility, apex involvement, and patient suitability for each modality. T3 and T4a disease is treated with total laryngopharyngectomy as the surgical standard, but organ preservation with induction TPF chemotherapy (GORTEC 99-02) or concurrent CRT is offered to appropriately selected patients with intact laryngeal function and no cartilage destruction. For T4a tumours, cartilage invasion on imaging is a relative contraindication to organ preservation as the functional larynx cannot be reliably salvaged and RT cannot sterilise bone-equivalent cartilage. T4N1 (Stage IVA) disease requires comprehensive assessment with CT, MRI, PET-CT, and panendoscopy to confirm resectability; the standard treatment is total laryngopharyngectomy with bilateral neck dissection followed by adjuvant CRT if positive margins or extranodal extension are present. The landmark EORTC 24891 trial (Lefebvre 1996) established oncological equivalence of organ-preservation strategies in hypopharyngeal cancer, and GORTEC 99-02 (Pointreau 2009) demonstrated TPF induction is superior to PF for achieving larynx preservation. Neck management must address bilateral levels II–IV given the high rate of occult nodal metastasis, and retropharyngeal nodes should be included in radiation target volumes.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
>
> 1. The pyriform sinus apex is the most important subsite to assess: apex involvement predicts submucosal spread to the postcricoid region and cervical oesophagus, mandating wider resection or change in reconstruction strategy.
> 2. Referred otalgia in a patient with hypopharyngeal cancer is mediated by Arnold's nerve (auricular branch of CN X) and is a red flag symptom indicating a deeply infiltrating lesion.
> 3. Pooling of secretions in the pyriform sinus on flexible nasoendoscopy is a pathognomonic sign of a hypopharyngeal mass obstructing the pharyngeal lumen.
> 4. For T2 N0 pyriform sinus cancer, the choice between conservation surgery and CRT is driven primarily by apex involvement (contraindication to conservation surgery) and vocal cord mobility.
> 5. In organ preservation protocols, non-responders to induction chemotherapy should proceed directly to total laryngopharyngectomy — attempting radiotherapy in a poor responder risks both suboptimal oncological control and a damaged, functionally useless larynx that is harder to salvage.
> 6. T4a with cartilage destruction (cortical breakthrough on CT, marrow signal on MRI) is best treated with upfront surgery; cartilage-invaded larynx rarely achieves adequate function even if preserved by CRT, and complication rates of salvage laryngectomy after RT to an irradiated cartilage are high.
> 7. GORTEC 99-02 established TPF as superior to PF for larynx preservation (70.3% vs 57.5% at 3 years), but overall survival was similar, confirming the primary endpoint benefit is functional, not survival-based.
> 8. The incidence of occult nodal metastasis in clinically N0 pyriform sinus cancer is 30–40%; elective ipsilateral neck dissection (levels II–IV) is therefore always indicated, and bilateral ND should be considered given the rich bilateral lymphatic drainage.
> 9. Adjuvant concurrent CRT (cisplatin + 60–66 Gy) is mandatory after surgery when pathology shows positive margins or extranodal extension — these two indications have the highest level of evidence (EORTC 22931, RTOG 9501) for benefit of adding chemotherapy to adjuvant RT.
> 10. Postcricoid cancer differs from pyriform sinus cancer epidemiologically (female predominance, association with Plummer-Vinson syndrome) and surgically (frequently requires total pharyngo-laryngo-oesophagectomy with gastric pull-up or free jejunal reconstruction).
