> Steiner et al. (2001) published the seminal retrospective review of 129 previously untreated patients with piriform sinus carcinoma (pT1-pT4) undergoing transoral CO2 laser microsurgery between 1981 and 1996, demonstrating comparatively low local recurrence rates and high recurrence-free survival, establishing TLM as a curative-intent organ-preservation option for selected hypopharyngeal cancers. Martin et al. (2008) reported a prospective series of 172 patients with previously untreated hypopharyngeal SCC (piriform sinus n=150) treated by TLM, finding five-year local control rates of 84% for pT1 and 70% for pT2 disease with larynx preservation in the majority, confirming organ-preserving TLM as oncologically valid with morbidity rates lower than radical open surgery. Weiss et al. (2017) reported the largest published series of 211 patients with hypopharyngeal SCC treated at the Göttingen centre by TLM across all T-stages, demonstrating five-year local control rates of 88.1% (pT1), 74.8% (pT2), 77.3% (pT3), and 61.8% (pT4a) with an overall five-year survival of 55%, confirming TLM as a definitive treatment modality across a broad T-stage range. Lane et al. (2020) performed a systematic review and meta-analysis of TLM for hypopharyngeal carcinoma pooling data from 15 studies; pooled five-year overall survival was 54%, disease-specific survival 72%, local control 78%, laryngeal preservation 97%, and gastrostomy independence 95%, establishing the evidence base for TLM as the preferred minimally invasive approach. O'Malley et al. (2006) published the first clinical series of transoral robotic surgery (TORS) using the da Vinci system for base-of-tongue neoplasms, establishing the feasibility, safety, and oncologic principles of robotic transoral resection that subsequently informed TORS applications in the hypopharynx.

## Overview: Minimally Invasive Approaches to the Hypopharynx

Historically, hypopharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma was managed by total laryngopharyngectomy with reconstruction, carrying significant functional morbidity. Two minimally invasive endoscopic platforms now offer organ-preserving surgical alternatives:

1. **Transoral Laser Microsurgery (TLM)** — the established gold standard for endoscopic hypopharyngeal resection, pioneered by Steiner at the University of Göttingen
2. **Transoral Robotic Surgery (TORS)** — emerging platform with enhanced visualisation and articulation; results in hypopharynx are evolving

Both are categorised under the broader term **Endoscopic Laryngopharyngeal Surgery (ELPS)** — a term encompassing all transoral endoscopic resective procedures of the larynx and pharynx.

## Transoral Laser Microsurgery (TLM)

### Concept and Principles

TLM is based on the principle that tumours can be resected transorally under microscopic control using a CO2 laser, with piecemeal resection performed as needed to achieve adequate deep margins without compromising the ability to assess them. Steiner's key innovation was demonstrating that cutting through tumour to assess depth of invasion — rather than always performing en bloc resection — does not compromise locoregional control provided all margins are eventually cleared.

### Instrumentation

| Instrument | Role |
|---|---|
| **Weerda distending laryngoscope** | Primary scope for hypopharynx; two-bladed design distends the hypopharyngeal lumen for simultaneous exposure and suspension |
| **Kleinsasser laryngoscope** | Alternative; narrower blade for glottic exposure |
| **Lindholm laryngoscope** | For supraglottic and high hypopharyngeal access |
| **Operating microscope** | Carl Zeiss OPMI with 400 mm objective; provides 6–25× magnification |
| **CO2 laser micromanipulator** | Coupled to microscope; delivers focused beam at 5–8 watts (continuous or superpulse mode) |
| **Cold steel instruments** | Scissors, curved forceps, suction-monopolar device; for haemostasis and tissue manipulation |
| **Bipolar diathermy forceps** | Haemostasis of vessels >0.5 mm |
| **Suction cannulae** | Smoke evacuation and field clearance |
| **Suspension arm (Kleinsasser/Lewy)** | Attaches laryngoscope to operating table rail for hands-free suspension laryngoscopy |

### Patient Positioning

- **Supine** with neck extended (shoulder roll beneath scapulae; head on a ring or neurosurgical head rest)
- **Neck extended** to align oral, pharyngeal, and laryngeal axes
- Anaesthesia via **microlaryngoscopy tube** (5–6 mm cuffed, laser-safe) or **jet ventilation** (Hunsaker Mon-Jet tube); tube positioned in trachea out of the operative field
- Laser-safe endotracheal tube (e.g. Sheridan, Laser-Flex) mandatory; saline-soaked pledgets protect cuff

### Patient Selection Criteria

| Criterion | Requirement |
|---|---|
| T-stage | T1–T2; selected small T3 in high-volume centres |
| Subsite | Piriform sinus (medial wall, superior/middle third); posterior pharyngeal wall |
| Apex of piriform sinus | Must be clear — apex involvement implies cricoarytenoid joint involvement (T4) |
| Post-cricoid region | Not involved — exposure to post-cricoid is poor transorally |
| Mouth opening | Adequate (≥3.5 cm interincisal distance) |
| Cervical spine | No severe arthritis or fusion limiting neck extension |
| Exposure on EUA | Adequate tumour visualisation at pre-operative examination under anaesthesia |
| Neck disease | N0 or N+ amenable to staged neck dissection |
| General fitness | Adequate pulmonary reserve (post-resection swallowing may cause temporary aspiration) |

**Contraindications:**
- T4 disease (cartilage or pre-vertebral fascia invasion)
- Post-cricoid or bilateral/circumferential pyriform sinus involvement
- Inadequate transoral exposure at EUA
- Severe trismus or cervical spine disease
- Prior high-dose irradiation to the field (relative; salvage TLM used in selected cases)

### Surgical Technique

**Step 1: Suspension Laryngoscopy**
Weerda distending laryngoscope introduced under general anaesthesia with the patient supine and neck extended. Blades advanced to expose the pyriform sinus under direct vision. Suspension arm applied to provide hands-free access.

**Step 2: Mapping and Marking**
Tumour boundaries identified under white light and narrow-band imaging (NBI) if available. Mucosal margins marked with laser at low power.

**Step 3: Resection**
- Begin at the superior margin of the tumour at the pharyngoepiglottic fold
- Resect in systematic sequence: superior mucosal margin → lateral wall → medial wall → deep margin
- Piecemeal sectioning through tumour permitted to assess depth — specimens labelled and oriented for pathology
- Deep margin assessed at the level of the inferior constrictor muscle; the paraglottic space and thyroid cartilage must not be violated unless mandated
- **Piriform apex resection** requires care to avoid cricoarytenoid joint entry; if apex invaded, procedure is contraindicated or must convert to open approach
- **Medial wall resection** includes part of the aryepiglottic fold; arytenoid preservation is the norm

**Step 4: Frozen Section Margin Control**
Intraoperative frozen sections of mucosal and deep margins guide additional resection. All mucosal margins should be clear (≥5 mm recommended; at minimum >2 mm). Positive frozen section → re-resection until clear.

**Step 5: Wound Management**
Primary closure not routinely performed for small defects — **healing by secondary intention** provides satisfactory results without stenosis risk. For larger defects (>3 cm), a split-skin graft or free flap may be used if stenosis is anticipated.

**Step 6: Neck**
Neck dissection (selective levels II–IV ipsilateral; bilateral for midline tumours) performed as a **staged procedure**, typically 2–6 weeks later. TLM and neck dissection are not performed simultaneously, as simultaneous transcervical and transoral approaches create a risk of oro-cutaneous communication.

### Outcomes of TLM for Hypopharyngeal Cancer

| Parameter | Outcome (pooled data — Lane 2020) |
|---|---|
| 5-year Overall Survival | 54% (95% CI 50–58%) |
| 5-year Disease-Specific Survival | 72% (95% CI 68–77%) |
| Local Control Rate | 78% (95% CI 72–85%) |
| Laryngeal Preservation Rate | 97% |
| Gastrostomy Independence | 95% |
| Tracheostomy Dependency | 3% |

**Subgroup outcomes by T-stage (Weiss 2017, n=211):**

| T-stage | 5-year Local Control |
|---|---|
| pT1 | 88.1% |
| pT2 | 74.8% |
| pT3 | 77.3% |
| pT4a | 61.8% |

### Advantages of TLM for Hypopharyngeal Cancer

1. **No external incision** — avoids pharyngotomy, neck scarring, and open surgical morbidity
2. **Shorter hospital stay** — typically 3–7 days versus 2–4 weeks for open surgery
3. **Laryngeal function preserved** — swallowing and voice largely maintained in T1–T2 disease
4. **Rapid functional recovery** — oral feeding resumed within days; no mandatory tracheostomy
5. **Salvageability** — failure can be treated by open surgery, re-TLM, or chemoradiation; prior TLM does not compromise subsequent treatment
6. **Cost-effective** — reduced ICU requirements and reconstruction costs
7. **Equivalent oncological outcomes** to open surgery in selected T1–T2 disease

### Disadvantages and Limitations

1. **Restricted to T1–T2 (selected T3)** — not appropriate for advanced disease
2. **Post-cricoid tumours cannot be resected** — poor transoral exposure
3. **Staged neck dissection required** — simultaneous neck surgery is not safe
4. **Expertise-dependent** — steep learning curve; high-volume centres essential
5. **Limited visualisation** — cannot assess submucosal extension beyond margins visible at laryngoscopy
6. **Piecemeal resection** — orientation of specimen challenging; requires experienced pathologist

### Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Intraoperative bleeding | 2–5% | Bipolar haemostasis; packing; convert to open |
| Post-operative haemorrhage | 1–3% | Return to theatre; compression/ligation |
| Airway oedema/compromise | 2–5% | Temporary tracheostomy; systemic steroids |
| Aspiration/pneumonia | 5–15% | Swallowing therapy; nasogastric feeding; most transient |
| Pharyngocutaneous fistula | Rare (<1%) with TLM; higher if prior radiation | Conservative management |
| Pharyngeal stenosis | 3–8% (larger defects) | Dilatation; repeat endoscopy |
| Dental trauma | Uncommon | Dental guard; careful laryngoscope introduction |
| Cervical spine injury | Rare | Careful neck positioning; avoid excessive extension |

## Transoral Robotic Surgery (TORS) for the Hypopharynx

### Overview

TORS uses the **da Vinci Surgical System** (Intuitive Surgical) to provide high-definition three-dimensional visualisation, articulating wrist instruments with seven degrees of freedom, and tremor filtration. First applied to base-of-tongue tumours by O'Malley, Weinstein, and colleagues at the University of Pennsylvania (2006), its application to the hypopharynx has expanded cautiously.

### System Components and Setup

| Component | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Surgeon console** | Remote; 3D HD vision; master controllers |
| **Patient-side cart** | 3–4 robotic arms docked to retractor system |
| **Instruments used** | 5 mm Maryland bipolar forceps; 5 mm monopolar cautery scissors; 8 mm fenestrated bipolar forceps |
| **Camera arm** | 8.5 mm dual-channel endoscope (0° or 30°) |
| **Retractor/exposure** | FK (Feyh-Kastenbauer) retractor or Crowe-Davis mouth gag with TORS-specific blade |

### Specific Challenges in the Hypopharynx vs Oropharynx

The hypopharynx presents unique access challenges compared to the oropharynx (where TORS is well established for tonsil and base-of-tongue tumours):

| Challenge | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Narrow working space** | Pyriform sinus and post-cricoid region are narrow; robotic arms have less freedom of movement |
| **Proximity to larynx** | Arytenoids and cricoarytenoid joint at immediate risk; injury causes dysphonia or aspiration |
| **Depth of field** | Pyriform apex lies deep and inferiorly; visualisation limited even with 30° scope |
| **Instrument clashing** | 5 mm and 8 mm arms clash in a narrow hypopharyngeal lumen |
| **FK retractor limitations** | May not expose post-cricoid adequately; exposure varies with anatomy |

### Indications for TORS in Hypopharynx

- T1–T2 pyriform sinus carcinoma (medial and lateral walls, superior to apex)
- Selected posterior pharyngeal wall tumours
- Not currently recommended for post-cricoid region or T3–T4 disease

### TORS vs TLM: Comparative Considerations

| Feature | TLM | TORS |
|---|---|---|
| Visualisation | 2D microscopic (6–25×) | 3D HD (10×) |
| Instrument articulation | Limited (fixed beam laser) | 7 degrees of freedom |
| Haptic feedback | Tactile (surgeon holds instruments) | None (robotic) |
| Learning curve | Steep but well-defined | Steep; less data in hypopharynx |
| Evidence base | Mature (30+ years, large series) | Emerging (smaller series) |
| Cost | Moderate (laser maintenance) | High (robotic system) |
| Availability | Widely available in specialist centres | Fewer centres have robotic platform |
| Best for hypopharynx | Yes (established) | Selected cases (developing) |

## Endoscopic Surgery in the Context of Organ Preservation

Endoscopic surgery represents the **surgical arm of organ preservation** for hypopharyngeal cancer — an alternative to CRT-based organ preservation (EORTC 24891 approach). Key distinctions:

| Feature | TLM-Based Preservation | CRT-Based Preservation |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Surgical excision + staged neck | Non-surgical; definitive CRT |
| Larynx preservation | 97% in T1–T2 | 42–57% overall (higher T-stage) |
| Swallowing | Better in T1–T2 (no radiation sequelae) | Long-term dysphagia 30–50% |
| Recurrence management | Salvage options preserved | Salvage surgery feasible |
| Best stage | T1–T2 | T2–T3 (larger tumours) |
