> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Larsson, Sandström, and Westling (1975) published the definitive epidemiological study from northern Sweden — a region historically endemic for Plummer-Vinson syndrome — demonstrating that PVS as a consequence of sideropenic anaemia significantly influenced the pattern of hypopharyngeal and oral cancer, with a markedly elevated female-to-male ratio in hypopharyngeal cancer compared to other regions, and that postcricoid carcinoma was relatively common in areas of high PVS prevalence; this study provided the strongest population-level evidence linking iron deficiency anaemia and PVS to upper alimentary tract malignancy. Novacek (2006) published a comprehensive narrative review of Plummer-Vinson syndrome in the Orphanet Journal of Rare Diseases, synthesising the pathophysiology, epidemiology, clinical features, management, and malignant potential of the condition; the review described PVS as a premalignant condition predisposing to SCC of the oral cavity, pharynx, and proximal oesophagus, and emphasised the declining incidence as nutritional iron deficiency has improved in Western populations. Kelly (1919), in the original description from the UK, first reported the association of oesophageal spasm with anaemia and glossitis in women — providing the historical anchor for what became the Patterson-Brown-Kelly/Plummer-Vinson eponym. Atmatzidis et al. (1994) reviewed postcricoid carcinoma arising in the setting of PVS, detailing the surgical management — total laryngopharyngectomy with reconstruction — and demonstrating that late presentation and submucosal spread are the principal reasons for poor prognosis despite aggressive treatment.

## Definition and Synonyms

**Plummer-Vinson syndrome (PVS)** is a rare condition defined by the classic triad of:
1. **Iron deficiency anaemia**
2. **Dysphagia** (progressive, initially to solids)
3. **Postcricoid oesophageal/pharyngeal web**

**Synonyms:**
- **Paterson-Brown-Kelly syndrome** (UK nomenclature — after Donald Ross Paterson and Adam Brown-Kelly, who first described the triad in 1919)
- **Sideropenic dysphagia** (descriptive; preferred by some gastroenterologists)
- **Patterson-Kelly syndrome** (variant spelling)

The condition was independently described by Henry Stanley Plummer and Porter Paisley Vinson at the Mayo Clinic (USA) around the same period, giving rise to the dual eponymous nomenclature used on either side of the Atlantic.

## Epidemiology

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| **Sex** | Predominantly women (female:male ratio approximately 7:1 to 9:1) |
| **Age** | Middle-aged women (4th–6th decade); occasionally postmenopausal |
| **Geography** | Historically prevalent in Northern Europe (Scandinavia, UK); high incidence in Sweden (especially northern), Finland, and pre-welfare-state UK |
| **Incidence trend** | Markedly declining in developed nations with improved nutrition, iron supplementation, and food fortification |
| **India** | Cases reported; associated with chronic nutritional iron deficiency in reproductive-age women |
| **Rare** | Currently considered a rare condition; large series rarely reported |

The declining incidence in Sweden — once the epicentre of PVS — following widespread iron supplementation provided natural epidemiological evidence that iron deficiency is the primary aetiological factor.

## Pathophysiology

The unifying mechanism is **iron deficiency at the tissue level**, leading to a cascade of mucosal and structural changes:

```
Iron Deficiency
      │
      ▼
Impaired oxidative phosphorylation in rapidly dividing epithelial cells
(iron-dependent cytochrome oxidase and other haem-containing enzymes)
      │
      ▼
Mucosal atrophy and epithelial fragility
(all mucosal surfaces: oral, pharyngeal, oesophageal)
      │
      ▼
Inflammation and loss of mucosal integrity
      │
      ▼
Submucosal fibrosis and web formation at pharyngo-oesophageal junction
      │
      ▼
Intermittent/progressive mechanical obstruction (dysphagia)
```

**Why postcricoid?** The pharyngo-oesophageal junction (postcricoid region) is the area of maximum physiological narrowing in the upper aerodigestive tract. Atrophic mucosa at this site is most susceptible to web formation due to the high mechanical stress of repeated swallowing.

**Iron and mucosal carcinogenesis:** Iron deficiency causes mucosal dysplasia through persistent mitotic stress and impaired DNA repair in epithelial cells — the same mechanism predisposing to SCC. This is the biological basis for the premalignant potential of PVS.

## The Postcricoid Web

The postcricoid web is the pathognomonic structural lesion of PVS:

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| **Location** | Anterior > posterior wall of the postcricoid hypopharynx / proximal oesophagus |
| **Composition** | Thin fold of hyperkeratotic or normal mucosa with submucosal fibrosis; not a true muscle or cartilaginous structure |
| **Radiological appearance** | Thin shelf-like filling defect at or just below the cricopharyngeus on barium swallow; best seen on lateral projection |
| **Endoscopic appearance** | Pale, thin mucosal fold traversing (partially or completely) the lumen; often eccentric (anterior); occasionally circumferential |
| **Behaviour** | Intermittently obstructing at first; progressive fibrosis leads to fixed stenosis; may rupture and reform |
| **Multiplicity** | Usually single; occasionally multiple |
| **Distinction from carcinoma** | Web is smooth, thin, and enhances the mucosa uniformly on imaging; carcinoma has irregular mucosa, mass effect, and paraglottic/thyroid cartilage involvement |

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

| Symptom | Details |
|---|---|
| **Dysphagia** | Progressive; initially to solids (lumpy foods, bread), later semi-solids; usually insidious onset over months to years |
| **Globus sensation** | Sensation of a foreign body in the throat |
| **Sore mouth/tongue** | Glossitis-related burning and discomfort |
| **Fatigue, pallor** | Symptoms of iron deficiency anaemia |
| **Hair thinning** | Alopecia from iron deficiency |

### Signs

| Sign | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Koilonychia** | Spoon-shaped nails; highly characteristic of chronic iron deficiency |
| **Glossitis** | Smooth, erythematous, depapillated tongue (atrophic glossitis) |
| **Angular stomatitis (cheilitis)** | Fissuring and cracking at the corners of the mouth |
| **Pallor** | Conjunctival and palmar pallor from anaemia |
| **Alopecia** | Diffuse hair thinning; more prominent in prolonged iron deficiency |
| **Achlorhydria** | Reduced or absent gastric acid (in some patients); may contribute to reduced iron absorption — a self-perpetuating cycle |
| **Splenomegaly** | Occasional, from chronic anaemia |

## Investigations

### Haematological

| Investigation | Expected Finding |
|---|---|
| **Full blood count** | Microcytic hypochromic anaemia (Hb low, MCV <80 fL, MCH <27 pg) |
| **Blood film** | Microcytes, hypochromic cells, anisocytosis, poikilocytosis, target cells |
| **Serum ferritin** | Low (<15 µg/L); most sensitive marker of iron deficiency |
| **Serum iron** | Low |
| **TIBC (total iron binding capacity)** | Elevated (in iron deficiency; reduced in anaemia of chronic disease) |
| **Transferrin saturation** | Reduced (<20%) |
| **Reticulocyte count** | Normal or low before treatment; rises after iron supplementation |

### Imaging

| Investigation | Finding in PVS |
|---|---|
| **Barium swallow (lateral view)** | Thin shelf-like filling defect in the postcricoid region at rest; best seen during swallowing; may only be demonstrated on rapid-sequence fluoroscopy |
| **CT neck and chest** | Used to exclude carcinoma if clinical suspicion; no specific PVS finding on CT |
| **MRI** | Not routine for PVS diagnosis |

### Endoscopy

| Investigation | Role |
|---|---|
| **Flexible nasopharyngoscopy** | Assess pyriform sinuses, postcricoid region; look for mucosal changes, erosions, or suspicious lesions |
| **Oesophago-gastro-duodenoscopy (OGD)** | Gold standard; directly visualises and biopsies the web; allows simultaneous dilatation; must biopsy any suspicious area |
| **EUA laryngoscopy** | If suspicious mucosal lesion; allows wider field assessment and biopsy under anaesthesia |

**Biopsy** of the web and surrounding mucosa is mandatory to exclude dysplasia or in situ carcinoma at endoscopy.

## Management

### 1. Iron Replacement Therapy

First-line treatment; corrects the underlying deficiency:
- **Oral ferrous sulphate** 200 mg three times daily (or ferrous fumarate 210 mg twice daily)
- Duration: Minimum 3 months after Hb normalises to replete stores; often continued for 6–12 months
- Monitor: FBC at 4 and 12 weeks; serum ferritin to confirm store repletion
- Iron replacement alone resolves or reduces the web in many patients and substantially relieves dysphagia

### 2. Endoscopic Web Dilatation

For webs causing persistent dysphagia despite iron repletion, or those causing significant luminal narrowing:
- **Endoscopic dilatation** (Savary-Gilliard bougies or balloon dilatation via OGD) — preferred technique
- Single or repeated sessions as needed
- Web may rupture spontaneously during endoscopy (diagnostic disruption)
- Risk of perforation is low but higher in fibrotic/tight webs; monitor post-procedure

### 3. Nutritional Support

- Dietary advice to maximise dietary iron intake (red meat, leafy vegetables, vitamin C to enhance absorption)
- Avoid tea/coffee with meals (reduce absorption)
- Correct any coexisting folate or B12 deficiency

### 4. Surveillance for Malignancy

**Mandatory ongoing surveillance** given the premalignant nature of PVS:
- Endoscopy (OGD ± flexible laryngoscopy) every 12–24 months or if new symptoms develop
- Any change in dysphagia pattern, voice change, weight loss, or ear pain warrants urgent re-evaluation
- Surveillance continues even after apparent resolution of the web

## Malignant Transformation and Association with Hypopharyngeal/Postcricoid SCC

### Risk Profile

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Type of cancer** | Squamous cell carcinoma of postcricoid hypopharynx; also oral cavity and proximal oesophagus |
| **Relative risk** | Significantly elevated — estimated 10–15× increased risk vs. general population |
| **Incidence in PVS** | Historically 4–16% (older Swedish series); more recent studies report lower rates (6–7%) with improved iron status and surveillance |
| **Sex predilection** | Postcricoid SCC is the only head and neck SCC with female predominance — PVS is the major aetiological explanation |
| **Latency** | Cancer may develop years to decades after PVS diagnosis; some patients present with postcricoid SCC as the first manifestation of undiagnosed PVS |

### Pathogenesis of Malignant Transformation

```
Chronic iron deficiency
        │
        ▼
Mucosal atrophy and dysplasia (persistent mitotic stress)
        │
        ▼
Field defect across upper aerodigestive mucosa
        │
        ▼
Accumulation of oncogenic mutations (TP53, CDKN2A pathway)
        │
        ▼
SCC — postcricoid (most common) or oral cavity/oesophagus
```

### Features of Postcricoid SCC in PVS

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| **Location** | Postcricoid region (anterior wall of lower hypopharynx and upper cervical oesophagus) |
| **Submucosal spread** | Extensive; histologically tumour spreads submucosally beyond visible mucosal margin — explains poor outcome despite resection |
| **Stage at presentation** | Often advanced (T3–T4, N+) — postcricoid carcinoma is radiologically and clinically occult until large |
| **Prognosis** | Poor (5-year overall survival 15–30%); worse than pyriform sinus SCC |
| **Treatment** | Total laryngopharyngectomy with pharyngeal reconstruction (gastric pull-up, free jejunal flap, or ALT flap) + postoperative RT/CRT |
| **Lymphatic drainage** | Paratracheal and retropharyngeal nodes (level VI); bilateral cervical nodes |

### Staging of Postcricoid SCC

Postcricoid SCC is staged as hypopharyngeal SCC per AJCC 8th edition:
- **T1:** ≤2 cm, limited to one subsite
- **T2:** >2 cm or more than one subsite without laryngeal fixation
- **T3:** >4 cm or laryngeal fixation
- **T4a:** Thyroid/cricoid cartilage, hyoid bone, thyroid gland, oesophagus, or central compartment soft tissue
- **T4b:** Pre-vertebral fascia, carotid, mediastinum

### Treatment of PVS-Associated Postcricoid Carcinoma

**Standard surgical treatment:**
- **Total laryngopharyngectomy** (larynx + entire hypopharynx + cervical oesophagus)
- **Bilateral selective neck dissection** (levels II–VI including paratracheal dissection)
- **Reconstruction:** Gastric pull-up preferred for circumferential defects extending below the thoracic inlet; free jejunal flap for shorter defects; ALT (tubed) as alternative
- **End tracheostomy** (permanent)
- **Postoperative CRT** in most (ECE, positive margins — as per RTOG 9501/EORTC 22931 criteria)

**Non-surgical option:** Definitive concurrent CRT in T1–T2N0; outcomes inferior to pyriform sinus SCC due to difficult radiation targeting and propensity for paratracheal nodal failure.

**Prognosis:**
- Overall poor; late presentation is the dominant driver
- 5-year OS: T1–T2 ~40–50%; T3–T4 ~15–25%
- Submucosal extension leads to high rates of mucosal margin positivity even after total laryngopharyngectomy

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Plummer-Vinson syndrome (Paterson-Brown-Kelly syndrome in the UK; sideropenic dysphagia) is defined by the triad of iron deficiency anaemia, progressive dysphagia, and a postcricoid oesophageal web. It predominantly affects middle-aged women, with a historical epicentre in northern Sweden and the UK, and its incidence has declined markedly following improvements in nutrition and iron supplementation. The pathophysiology rests on iron deficiency impairing rapidly dividing mucosal epithelial cells through cytochrome oxidase dysfunction, leading to mucosal atrophy, submucosal fibrosis, and web formation at the physiologically narrow postcricoid region. Clinical features include progressive dysphagia (initially to solids), koilonychia, glossitis, angular stomatitis, alopecia, and achlorhydria; the diagnosis is confirmed by barium swallow demonstrating a thin anterior postcricoid web and by OGD with biopsy. Management involves oral iron replacement and endoscopic web dilatation, with mandatory long-term surveillance given the significantly elevated risk — estimated 10–15 times — of developing SCC of the postcricoid region, oral cavity, or proximal oesophagus. Postcricoid SCC arising in PVS is uniquely characterised by extensive submucosal spread, frequent late presentation, and female predominance; it requires total laryngopharyngectomy with gastric pull-up or free flap reconstruction, bilateral neck dissection, and adjuvant chemoradiation, with an overall poor prognosis (5-year OS 15–30%). The syndrome illustrates the principle that nutritional deficiencies can drive field cancerisation across the upper aerodigestive tract, and the examiner expects the candidate to explain both the pathophysiology of web formation and the biological basis for malignant transformation.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The UK term is **Paterson-Brown-Kelly syndrome**; the USA term is **Plummer-Vinson syndrome** — the same condition with different eponyms on either side of the Atlantic; "sideropenic dysphagia" is the most mechanistically accurate name.
> 2. The classic triad is **iron deficiency anaemia + dysphagia + postcricoid web** — remember all three; the web is the structural lesion, the anaemia is the cause, and the dysphagia is the symptom.
> 3. **Koilonychia** (spoon nails) and **glossitis** are the most important signs to elicit on examination — both directly reflect chronic iron deficiency in peripheral and mucosal tissues.
> 4. The postcricoid web is best demonstrated on **lateral barium swallow** — CT misses it; endoscopy is diagnostic and therapeutic (dilatation at the same sitting).
> 5. **PVS is the only condition that gives a female predominance to head and neck SCC** — postcricoid carcinoma is more common in women than men because of PVS; all other HNSCCs have marked male predominance.
> 6. **Serum ferritin** is the most sensitive and specific test for iron deficiency — it is the first to fall and rises last with repletion; use it to confirm both diagnosis and treatment adequacy.
> 7. **Submucosal spread** in postcricoid SCC explains the high positive margin rates and poor outcomes despite total laryngopharyngectomy — frozen section control of mucosal margins alone is insufficient.
> 8. **Gastric pull-up** is the reconstruction of choice for postcricoid SCC requiring total laryngopharyngectomy with oesophageal resection; it provides a one-anastomosis pharyngo-gastric repair and avoids the need for a free flap in the irradiated neck.
> 9. The incidence of PVS-associated postcricoid SCC **has fallen dramatically in Sweden** following iron fortification of food — a natural experiment demonstrating that iron deficiency is genuinely the aetiological driver, not merely an association.
> 10. **Surveillance endoscopy** every 12–24 months is mandatory after PVS diagnosis — early detection of in situ or T1 postcricoid carcinoma dramatically improves the otherwise dismal prognosis of this tumour.
