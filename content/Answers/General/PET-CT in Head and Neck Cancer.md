## Introduction

Positron emission tomography combined with computed tomography (PET-CT) has become the optimal imaging modality for head and neck cancer management, combining the metabolic sensitivity of PET with the anatomical detail of CT. In surveillance, its high negative predictive value (NPV 95-96%) makes it particularly valuable as a "rule-out" test for recurrence. The PET-NECK trial has further established PET-CT-guided surveillance as the standard of care after chemoradiation for advanced disease.

## Principles of FDG-PET

### Physics and Radiopharmaceutical

- **18F-Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG)**: glucose analogue labelled with positron-emitting fluorine-18
- Exploits the **Warburg effect**: malignant cells have increased glucose metabolism and GLUT-1 transporter expression
- FDG is phosphorylated by hexokinase but cannot undergo further glycolysis → **metabolic trapping** in cells with high glucose uptake
- Positron annihilation produces two 511 keV photons at 180° → detected by coincidence ring detectors
- **Half-life of F-18**: 110 minutes

### Standardised Uptake Value (SUV)

- **SUV** = tissue radioactivity concentration / (injected dose / body weight)
- **SUVmax**: maximum SUV in the lesion — most commonly reported parameter
- Generally, SUVmax **>2.5** is considered suspicious for malignancy (not absolute)
- Limitations: affected by blood glucose level, time post-injection, body composition, partial volume effect

### PET-CT Fusion

- **Combined PET-CT** acquires both datasets in a single session
- CT provides attenuation correction for PET AND anatomical co-registration
- Improves anatomic localisation, reduces equivocal findings, and alters management in **18-30% of cases** (Schoder 2005, Harrison)

## Roles of PET-CT in HNC

### A. Initial Staging

| Role | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Primary tumour detection | Superior to CT/MRI for metabolic characterisation |
| Nodal staging | Higher sensitivity than CT/MRI (PET 87-90% vs CT 75-80%) |
| Distant metastasis screening | Whole-body capability — detects lung, liver, bone metastases |
| Synchronous primary detection | Identifies second primaries in 5-8% of patients |
| Unknown primary (CUP) | Identifies primary site in 10-72% of CUP cases |
| Treatment plan alteration | Alters management in **30%** of initial workup cases |

### B. Post-Treatment Surveillance (Primary Role)

#### Timing of Post-Treatment PET-CT

| Timing Post-RT | Sensitivity | Specificity | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| <8 weeks | Lower (inflammation) | Lower (false positives) | **Too early** — avoid |
| 8-10 weeks | Moderate | Moderate | Acceptable minimum |
| **≥12 weeks** | **Highest** | **Highest** | **Optimal timing** |

- Post-radiation inflammation causes **false-positive** FDG uptake in the first 8-10 weeks
- **NCCN recommendation**: first post-treatment PET-CT at **12 weeks** (3 months) after completion of RT/CRT

#### Diagnostic Performance for Surveillance

**Isles et al. 2008 Meta-analysis (Post-treatment PET):**

| Site | Sensitivity | Specificity | PPV | NPV |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Primary site | 94% | 82% | 75% | **95%** |
| Neck | 74% | 88% | 49% | **96%** |

**Gupta et al. 2011 Meta-analysis:**

| Site | Sensitivity | Specificity | PPV | NPV |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Primary site | 79.9% | 87.5% | 58.6% | **95.1%** |
| Neck | 72.7% | 87.6% | 52.1% | **94.5%** |

**Key conclusion**: PET-CT is an excellent **"rule-out" test** — a negative PET-CT reliably excludes recurrence (NPV ~95%). However, the moderate PPV means positive findings require biopsy confirmation.

#### Risk Stratification (Moeller et al.)

- Patients stratified into **high-risk** (HPV-negative, non-oropharyngeal, tobacco history) and **low-risk** categories
- In high-risk patients: PET-CT PPV for residual nodal disease = **75%** vs CT alone 37.5%
- PET-CT outperforms CT alone particularly in the **high-risk** subgroup

### C. PET-NECK Trial (Mehanna et al. 2016, NEJM)

**The Landmark RCT for Post-CRT Neck Management**

- **Design**: multicentre RCT, 564 patients with N2-N3 HNSCC treated with CRT
- **Randomisation**: planned neck dissection vs PET-CT-guided surveillance
- **PET-CT group**: PET-CT at 12 weeks → ND only if incomplete/equivocal response
- **Results**:
  - 2-year OS: **84.9%** (PET-CT) vs **81.5%** (planned ND) — **non-inferior**
  - Only **19%** of PET-CT group required ND (vs 100% in planned ND group)
  - PET-CT-guided approach saved **£1,492 per patient**
  - No difference in QOL between groups
- **Conclusion**: PET-CT-guided surveillance is the **standard of care** after CRT for N2-N3 neck — planned ND offers no survival advantage and adds morbidity/cost

### D. Other Roles

| Role | Application |
|---|---|
| **Response assessment** | Mid-treatment PET (adaptive RT) — experimental |
| **Radiation planning** | PET-defined GTV for RT target delineation |
| **Prognostic** | Pretreatment SUVmax correlates with outcomes (controversial) |
| **Recurrence detection** | Higher sensitivity than CT/MRI for post-treatment recurrence |
| **Distant metastasis workup** | Whole-body screening before salvage surgery |

## Limitations and False Positives/Negatives

### Causes of False Positives

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Post-radiation inflammation | Increased FDG uptake in inflamed/healing tissue |
| Post-surgical changes | Granulation tissue, wound healing |
| Infection/abscess | Inflammatory cell glucose uptake |
| Physiological uptake | Tonsils, salivary glands, brown fat, muscle tension |
| Benign tumours | Warthin tumour, pleomorphic adenoma |

### Causes of False Negatives

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Small tumour (<8-10 mm) | Below spatial resolution (~4 mm) |
| Low-grade malignancy | Low metabolic activity (e.g., adenoid cystic) |
| Hyperglycaemia | Competitive inhibition of FDG uptake |
| Mucinous tumours | Low cellularity |
| Recent chemotherapy | Metabolic stunning |

### Micrometastatic Nodal Disease

- PET-CT spatial resolution ~4 mm → **cannot detect micrometastases** (<2 mm)
- Sensitivity for occult nodal metastases in N0 neck is **only 40-50%**
- PET-CT does NOT replace [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Sentinel Node Biopsy in Oral Cancer|sentinel node biopsy]] or [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Node Negative Neck in Oral Cancer|elective neck dissection]] for cN0 staging

## Surveillance Protocol

| Timepoint | Recommended Imaging |
|---|---|
| Baseline (pre-treatment) | PET-CT for staging |
| 12 weeks post-CRT | PET-CT (first surveillance scan) |
| 6 months | Clinical exam + CT or MRI |
| 12 months | PET-CT or CT/MRI based on risk |
| Years 2-5 | Clinical exam ± imaging based on symptoms |
| N2-N3 post-CRT | PET-CT at 12 weeks → ND only if positive/equivocal (PET-NECK protocol) |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> - PET-CT combines metabolic sensitivity of FDG-PET with anatomical detail of CT — alters management in 18-30% of cases
> - Post-treatment PET-CT has NPV ~95% — an excellent "rule-out" test for recurrence; moderate PPV means positive findings require biopsy
> - **Optimal timing**: ≥12 weeks post-RT to avoid false positives from post-radiation inflammation
> - **PET-NECK trial**: PET-CT-guided surveillance after CRT is non-inferior to planned ND for N2-N3 disease, with significant cost savings
> - PET-CT cannot detect micrometastases (<2 mm) — does not replace elective ND or SNB for cN0 neck staging

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - A **negative PET-CT at 12 weeks** post-CRT is highly reassuring (NPV 95%) — avoids unnecessary neck dissection in 80% of patients
> - **Never perform PET-CT <8 weeks** post-RT — false positive rates are unacceptably high due to post-radiation inflammatory changes
> - In HPV-negative, tobacco-positive patients (**high-risk group**), PET-CT has double the PPV of CT alone for detecting residual nodal disease
> - For CUP (unknown primary with cervical metastasis), PET-CT identifies the primary in up to **72%** of cases — should be performed before panendoscopy and directed biopsies
> - **Hyperglycaemia** reduces FDG uptake → ensure fasting blood glucose <200 mg/dL before PET-CT; ideally <150 mg/dL for optimal image quality
