## Introduction

Occupational cancers are malignancies that arise as a consequence of workplace exposure to carcinogenic agents. In the head and neck, occupational carcinogens primarily affect the **sinonasal tract** and **larynx**, with smaller contributions to cancers of the oral cavity, pharynx, salivary glands, and thyroid. The occupation-attributable fraction for sinonasal cancers is estimated at 20-46% overall and up to 77% for sinonasal adenocarcinomas specifically.

Montgomery's Chapter 1 provides a comprehensive table of aetiological factors in head and neck cancer, listing occupational agents including asbestos, man-made fibres, textiles, wood dust, plastics, mustard gas, naphthalene, chloromethyl methyl ether (CME), bischloromethyl ether (BCME), pesticides, sulphuric acid, leather workers, paint and print workers, car mechanics, nickel refiners, metal workers, and coal and stone dust — with specific associations for laryngeal, sinonasal, and pharyngeal cancers.

Scott-Brown's Chapter 7 notes that several carcinogenic compounds have been identified, with inhalation of these carcinogens being responsible for around 40% of reported sinonasal malignancies.

---

## Part A: IARC Classification of Occupational Carcinogens

The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classifies carcinogens into groups based on the strength of evidence:

| IARC Group | Definition | Examples in HNC |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Group 1 | Carcinogenic to humans | Wood dust, nickel compounds, formaldehyde, leather dust, chromium (VI), asbestos, mustard gas |
| Group 2A | Probably carcinogenic | Benzo[a]pyrene, vinyl chloride |
| Group 2B | Possibly carcinogenic | Nickel metallic, welding fumes |

Loomis et al. (2018) identified 47 known occupational carcinogens (IARC Group 1) by 2017, compared with 28 in 2004. Cancers of the lung and other respiratory sites, followed by skin, account for the largest proportion. An estimated 666,000 fatal work-related cancers occur annually worldwide.

## Part B: Occupational Carcinogens by Head and Neck Subsite

### 1. Sinonasal Cancer — The Most Common Occupational HNC

The sinonasal tract is the head and neck subsite most strongly associated with occupational carcinogenesis due to the direct deposition of inhaled carcinogens on the nasal and sinus mucosa.

**Hardwood dust — the most important occupational carcinogen:**

As described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 7, foremost among occupational hazards is exposure to hardwoods in the furniture industry. It is thought that biologically active compounds in wood dust impair mucociliary clearance and predispose to carcinogenesis. Acheson et al. (1968) reported high rates of nasal cavity and sinus adenocarcinoma in the High Wycombe area (Buckinghamshire, UK) and proposed that this was secondary to wood dust exposure. Workers exposed to hardwood have a **70 times increased incidence** of sinonasal adenocarcinoma, particularly of the ethmoid sinuses. The UK Government recognizes this as an occupational risk and as such it is a prescribed disease under the 1959 National Insurance Regulations.

Scott-Brown's further notes that the type of wood is significant:
- **Hardwood** (oak, beech, mahogany) → increases risk of **adenocarcinoma** (intestinal-type)
- **Softwood** (pine, spruce) → more commonly associated with **squamous cell carcinoma**
- **African mahogany** appears to be the most carcinogenic — Bantu tribesmen who burn this wood have the highest incidence of maxillary sinus cancer in the world

Stell and Maran's Chapter 6 adds that intestinal-type adenocarcinoma is associated with cumulative exposure to hardwood dust and leather dust, and the tumour typically occurs in the ethmoid sinus. The tumour may be morphologically indistinguishable from metastatic colorectal adenocarcinoma.

**Other sinonasal occupational carcinogens:**

| Agent | IARC Group | Cancer Type | Occupations |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Hardwood dust** | 1 | Adenocarcinoma (ethmoid) | Furniture makers, carpenters, joiners |
| **Leather dust** | 1 | Adenocarcinoma | Shoe/boot manufacture |
| **Nickel compounds** | 1 | SCC (250x increased risk) | Nickel refining, smelting |
| **Chromium (VI)** | 1 | SCC | Chromate production, plating |
| **Formaldehyde** | 1 | SCC | Embalming, pathology, textiles |
| **Isopropyl alcohol** | 1 | SCC | Production (strong acid process) |
| **Mustard gas** | 1 | SCC | Military/chemical industry |
| **Radium** | 1 | Various | Watch dial painters (historical) |
| **Textile dust** | — | SCC | Textile manufacturing |

Scott-Brown's Chapter 7 notes that exposure to nickel increases risk of developing sinonasal SCC 250-fold, with Pedersen et al. reporting a latent interval of 18-36 years between exposure and tumour development.

Stell and Maran's states that other substances linked to sinonasal cancer include glues, formaldehyde, solvents used in furniture and shoe production, nickel and chromium dust, mustard gas, isopropyl alcohol, and radium.

### 2. Laryngeal Cancer

Stell and Maran's Chapter 1 states that laryngeal cancer is associated with nickel and mustard gas exposure, and there may also be association with asbestos exposure. Machinists and car mechanics are at increased risk. Long-term exposure to sulphuric and hydrochloric acid in battery plant workers increases risk.

Montgomery's Chapter 1 lists the following occupational associations with laryngeal cancer:

| Agent | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| **Asbestos** | Stell and McGill (1973) first reported the association; evidence sufficient for IARC Group 1 classification for laryngeal cancer |
| **Nickel refining** | Increased risk of glottic and supraglottic SCC |
| **Mustard gas** | Historical: mustard gas workers had increased laryngeal cancer risk |
| **Sulphuric acid mists** | IARC Group 1 — strong acid mists cause laryngeal cancer |
| **Man-made fibres** | Probable association |
| **CME/BCME** | Chloromethyl ethers — associated with laryngeal cancer |
| **Pesticides** | Increased risk |
| **Coal/stone dust** | Miners, construction workers |
| **Paint, print workers** | Solvent and chemical exposure |
| **Rubber manufacturing** | Increased risk |

### 3. Other Head and Neck Subsites

**Oral cavity and oropharynx:**
- Wood dust exposure is associated with the risk of oral cancer, as well as pharyngeal and laryngeal cancer (Stell and Maran's)
- Occupations involving exposure to organic chemicals and coal products are also at increased risk
- Vinyl chloride — associated with oral cavity and pharynx cancer (Montgomery)

**Salivary glands:**
- Stell and Maran's notes that working with certain metals (nickel alloy dust) and minerals (silica dust) may increase the risk for salivary gland cancer
- Exposure to radioactive substances also increases risk

**Thyroid:**
- Radiation exposure — both therapeutic (for benign conditions) and accidental (Chernobyl, atomic bomb)
- Montgomery lists Chernobyl exposure specifically for paediatric thyroid cancer

**Lip:**
- Outdoor workers (farmers, fishermen, construction workers) — chronic UV radiation exposure

## Part C: Pathogenesis of Occupational HNC

Occupational carcinogens cause head and neck cancer through several mechanisms:

1. **Direct mucosal contact:** Inhaled particulates (wood dust, nickel, formaldehyde) deposit on the sinonasal and laryngeal mucosa, causing chronic irritation, inflammation, and DNA damage
2. **Impaired mucociliary clearance:** Wood dust particles impair ciliary function, prolonging carcinogen contact time with the mucosa (Scott-Brown's)
3. **DNA adduct formation:** Formaldehyde and chromium (VI) form covalent DNA adducts, causing mutations — particularly in TP53
4. **Oxidative stress:** Nickel and chromium compounds generate reactive oxygen species (ROS), causing oxidative DNA damage
5. **Chronic inflammation:** Tumour-promoting inflammation (Hanahan and Weinberg, 2011) — chronic occupational irritation creates a pro-tumour microenvironment
6. **Epigenetic changes:** Nickel compounds cause aberrant DNA methylation and [[Answers/General/Epigenetics in Oncogenesis|histone modifications]], silencing [[Answers/General/Tumour Suppressor Genes|tumour suppressor genes]]

**Latency period:** The interval between first occupational exposure and cancer development is typically **20-40 years** — nickel-related sinonasal SCC has a latent interval of 18-36 years (Scott-Brown's). This has important medicolegal implications.

## Part D: Prevention and Medicolegal Aspects

### Prevention Strategies

Stell and Maran's emphasizes that environmental protective measures include adequate ventilation and the use of respirators to reduce occupational exposure to airborne carcinogens:

1. **Workplace engineering controls:** Enclosed dust extraction systems, wet processing, adequate ventilation
2. **Personal protective equipment:** Respirators, masks — particularly in woodworking, nickel refining, formaldehyde exposure
3. **Occupational health surveillance:** Regular nasal examination and nasal biopsies in high-risk workers (furniture industry, nickel refining)
4. **Regulatory standards:** Maximum workplace exposure limits for Group 1 carcinogens
5. **Substitution:** Replacing hazardous materials with safer alternatives where possible

### Medicolegal Considerations

- Sinonasal adenocarcinoma in woodworkers is a **prescribed occupational disease** in the UK (Industrial Injuries Disablement Benefit)
- Scott-Brown's states it remains the duty of a doctor to inform patients of their rights
- A detailed occupational history is mandatory in all patients presenting with sinonasal cancer
- The long latency period (20-40 years) means patients may present decades after the causative exposure has ceased
- Documented evidence of workplace exposure strengthens medicolegal claims

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Occupational exposures account for 20-46% of sinonasal cancers and up to 77% of sinonasal adenocarcinomas
> 2. Hardwood dust is the most important occupational carcinogen — 70x increased risk of ethmoid adenocarcinoma in furniture workers (Acheson et al., 1968)
> 3. Hardwood → adenocarcinoma (intestinal-type, ethmoid); softwood → SCC — the type of wood determines the histology
> 4. Nickel compounds increase sinonasal SCC risk 250-fold with a latent interval of 18-36 years
> 5. IARC Group 1 sinonasal carcinogens: wood dust, leather dust, nickel, chromium (VI), formaldehyde, mustard gas
> 6. Laryngeal cancer is associated with asbestos (Stell & McGill, 1973), nickel, mustard gas, and sulphuric acid mists
> 7. 47 known occupational carcinogens (IARC Group 1) identified by 2017 — respiratory cancers most common
> 8. Latency period is typically 20-40 years — patients present decades after exposure cessation
> 9. Sinonasal intestinal-type adenocarcinoma may be indistinguishable from metastatic colorectal cancer — always take occupational history
> 10. Sinonasal adenocarcinoma in woodworkers is a prescribed occupational disease in the UK — doctors must inform patients of their rights

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - ALWAYS take a detailed occupational history in every patient with sinonasal cancer — ask specifically about woodworking, furniture manufacture, shoe/leather work, nickel/chromium exposure, and formaldehyde
> - A patient with ethmoid adenocarcinoma should be asked about hardwood dust exposure even if it was decades ago — the latency is 20-40 years
> - Intestinal-type adenocarcinoma of the ethmoid + history of furniture work = classic exam association
> - African mahogany is the most carcinogenic wood — remember "mahogany = maxillary" (the Bantu tribesmen burn it, causing maxillary sinus cancer)
> - Nickel causes SCC, not adenocarcinoma — distinguish from wood dust which causes adenocarcinoma
> - Asbestos and laryngeal cancer: IARC has classified this as Group 1 (sufficient evidence) — this is a common exam question that many candidates get wrong by only associating asbestos with mesothelioma
