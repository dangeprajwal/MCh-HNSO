## Introduction

Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) is a cytogenetic technique that uses fluorescently labelled DNA probes to detect and localize specific nucleic acid sequences on chromosomes or within tissue sections. Montgomery Chapter 2 states that "cytogenetic analysis of head and neck tumors has improved dramatically in the last 15 years due to FISH technology." Harrison Chapter 2 notes that "arguably, the greatest utility of ISH relative to head and neck cancers is in the identification of **[[Answers/General/EBV Testing in Head and Neck Cancer|Epstein-Barr virus]] virus-encoded RNA (EBER)** in nasopharyngeal carcinomas and in the detection of **high-risk HPV** in oropharyngeal carcinomas." FISH has become an indispensable tool in head and neck oncology for detecting gene amplification, chromosomal deletions, viral integration, and diagnostic translocations.

## Part A: Principles of FISH

### Basic Technique

1. **Probe Design:** A DNA probe complementary to the target genomic sequence is labelled with a fluorescent dye (fluorochrome)
2. **Denaturation:** Both the probe and the target DNA in the tissue section are denatured to single strands by heating
3. **Hybridization:** The labelled probe binds (hybridizes) to its complementary target sequence on the chromosome or in the cell nucleus
4. **Washing:** Unbound probe is washed away
5. **Visualization:** Fluorescence microscopy reveals the probe signals as bright coloured dots within the nucleus

### Signal Interpretation

Harrison Chapter 2 explains: "Aside from genes mapping to the sex chromosomes, **two copies** of each gene are present in normal cells (diploid genome); FISH/CISH generate **two signals** for any given probe."

| Pattern | Signals | Interpretation |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Normal (diploid) | 2 signals | Normal gene copy number |
| Deletion | 0 or 1 signal | Gene/chromosomal loss (e.g., p16 deletion) |
| High polysomy | 3-5 signals | Extra chromosome copies (aneuploidy) |
| Gene amplification | >5 signals or signal clusters | Multiple gene copies (e.g., EGFR, Cyclin D1) |
| Translocation | Split signal or fusion signal | Chromosomal rearrangement (e.g., Ewing sarcoma) |

### Advanced FISH Variants

Montgomery Chapter 2 describes several variants:

| Variant | Technique | Application |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **M-FISH (Multiplex FISH)** | Five fluorochromes simultaneously; computer analysis differentiates colours | Identifies origin of every chromosome in complex karyotypes |
| **SKY (Spectral Karyotyping)** | Similar to M-FISH; spectral imaging analysis | 24-colour display of all chromosomes simultaneously |
| **CGH (Comparative Genomic Hybridization)** | Tumour + normal DNA labelled differently; hybridized to normal chromosomes | Screens entire genome for gains/losses in single experiment |
| **CISH (Chromogenic ISH)** | Enzyme-based chromogen instead of fluorescence | Permanent signal; standard light microscopy; cheaper |
| **RNA ISH (RNAscope)** | Probes target mRNA transcripts | Detects transcriptionally active genes (e.g., HPV E6/E7) |

### Advantages of FISH Over Conventional Cytogenetics

Harrison Chapter 2 emphasizes:
- Can be applied to **interphase nuclei** — no need for tissue culturing or mitotic cells
- Works on **fresh, frozen, cytologic, FFPE, and archival tissue** including tissue [[Answers/General/PCR and Microarray Analysis|microarray]]
- **Simultaneous multi-target detection** with different fluorochromes
- **Quantitative** — counts individual gene copies per cell
- Overcomes limitations of G-banding for complex karyotypes with multiple marker chromosomes (Montgomery Ch2)

## Part B: Applications of FISH in Head and Neck Cancer

### 1. EGFR Gene Copy Number

- Montgomery Ch2: Up to **24%** of HNSCCs display erbB-1 gene amplification/rearrangement by FISH
- Chung et al. (2006): **58% FISH-positive** (high polysomy and/or amplification) in 75 HNSCC samples
- FISH-positive group had significantly worse PFS and OS
- Harrison Ch5: "Patients with more than two copies per cell of the EGFR gene experienced a higher risk of progression and death"
- **No activating EGFR mutations** found (unlike lung cancer)

### 2. Cyclin D1 (11q13) Amplification

- Harrison Ch5: "Cyclin D1 is the most commonly amplified oncogene in HNSCC, with approximately **35%** of tumours revealing increased gene copy number in FISH analysis"
- Namazie et al. (2002): Dual-colour FISH detected cyclin D1 amplification in **30%** of 103 patients; correlated with recurrence and reduced survival
- Montgomery: EMS1 amplification (also at 11q13) occurs in **20%** and independently predicts early recurrence

### 3. p16/CDKN2A Deletion

- Montgomery Ch2: "Using FISH, p16 deletion was detected in **52%** of patients with HNSCC and was significantly associated with development of **distant metastases**"
- Namazie et al. (2002): p16 deletion by FISH correlated with poor survival at 36 months

### 4. HPV Detection

Harrison Ch2 and Jatin Shah describe the role of ISH in HPV detection:

| Method | Target | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes |
|--------|--------|------------|-------------|-------|
| DNA ISH | HPV DNA | Moderate | High | Type-specific probes available; localizes to cell nuclei |
| RNA ISH (RNAscope) | HPV E6/E7 mRNA | High | High | Detects transcriptionally ACTIVE virus; superior to DNA ISH (Ukpo 2011) |

- **Practical Algorithm (Dreyer 2013):** p16 IHC as initial screen → positive cases confirmed by DNA ISH or RNA ISH
- Harrison: "A type-specific probe is very sensitive for detecting [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-16]], and a consensus ISH probe can detect 15 HPV types"

### 5. EBER Detection for Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma

- Harrison identifies this as one of the two greatest utilities of ISH in HNC
- EBER ISH detects Epstein-Barr virus-encoded RNA in NPC tissue
- Essential for confirming EBV-associated NPC, especially undifferentiated type

### 6. Diagnostic Translocations

Jatin Shah describes FISH for specific sarcomas in the head and neck:

| Tumour | Translocation | FISH Application |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| Ewing sarcoma | t(11;22)(q24;q12) | Diagnostic confirmation |
| Rhabdomyosarcoma | Various | Molecular subtyping |

### Chromosomal Aberrations in HNSCC Detected by FISH/CGH

Montgomery Chapter 2 catalogues the most frequent chromosomal abnormalities:

| Chromosomal Change | Frequency | Associated Gene/Significance |
|-------------------|-----------|------------------------------|
| Loss of 3p13-q24 | >60% | Multiple TSGs |
| Loss of 8p22-p23 | 65% | TSGs |
| Loss of 18q22-23 | >60% | DCC, SMAD4 |
| Loss of 9p21-p24 | 43% | p16/CDKN2A |
| Loss of 4p | 43% | TSGs |
| Gain of 11q13-23 | 30-40% | Cyclin D1, EMS1 |
| Gain of 3q21-qter | 30-40% | PIK3CA |
| Gain of 7p | 30-40% | EGFR |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. FISH uses fluorescently labelled DNA probes to detect gene amplification, deletion, translocation, and viral sequences — works on FFPE/archival tissue without requiring cell culture
> 2. In HNSCC, key FISH applications: EGFR gene copy number (58% positive, predicts poor PFS/OS), cyclin D1 amplification (30-35%), p16 deletion (52%, predicts distant metastasis)
> 3. For HPV detection, RNA ISH (RNAscope) targeting E6/E7 mRNA is superior to DNA ISH as it identifies transcriptionally ACTIVE virus (Ukpo 2011)
> 4. EBER ISH for EBV in nasopharyngeal carcinoma and HPV ISH in oropharyngeal SCC are the two greatest utilities of ISH in head and neck cancer (Harrison Ch2)
> 5. Advanced variants (M-FISH, SKY, CGH) enable whole-genome screening for gains/losses, identifying the complex chromosomal landscape of HNSCC (losses at 3p, 8p, 9p, 18q; gains at 3q, 7p, 11q13)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Two Signals = Normal:** In a diploid cell, FISH shows exactly 2 signals per gene — more signals indicate amplification/polysomy, fewer indicate deletion
> - **EGFR FISH vs IHC:** FISH detects gene copy number but Pectasides et al. (2011) showed that EGFR protein level (IHC/AQUA), not FISH alone, is the stronger prognostic marker — gene amplification doesn't always translate to protein overexpression
> - **HPV Detection Hierarchy:** p16 IHC (screening) → DNA ISH or RNA ISH (confirmation) → RT-PCR for E6/E7 (gold standard) — FISH/ISH sits in the middle of this algorithm
> - **p16 Deletion by FISH Predicts Distant Metastasis:** This is a frequently tested association — 52% of HNSCC show p16 deletion, and it specifically predicts distant (not locoregional) failure
> - **FISH Advantage Over PCR:** FISH preserves tissue architecture and shows the signal within individual cells — you can see WHERE in the tissue the abnormality exists, unlike PCR which gives only an aggregate result
