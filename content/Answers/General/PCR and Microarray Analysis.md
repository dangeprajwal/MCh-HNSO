# PCR and Microarray Analysis in Head and Neck Cancer

**Source:** Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 3 (Molecular Biology — Molecular Diagnostics, Molecular Staging, Microarray Analysis); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 1 (Pathology — Molecular Techniques: PCR, ISH, DNA Microarrays); Scott-Brown's Otorhinolaryngology Volume 3, Chapter 13 (Oropharyngeal Tumours — PCR, qRT-PCR for HPV Detection)

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) and DNA microarray technology represent the two most transformative molecular techniques in cancer diagnostics. Mullis et al. (1986) published the first description of the polymerase chain reaction — a technique for exponential amplification of specific DNA sequences in vitro — which earned Kary Mullis the 1993 Nobel Prize in Chemistry and revolutionized molecular biology. Saiki et al. (1988) published the landmark Science paper introducing thermostable Taq DNA polymerase for PCR, enabling automated thermal cycling and making PCR practical for routine laboratory use. Schena et al. (1995) published the first DNA microarray paper in Science, demonstrating that complementary DNA immobilized on glass slides could simultaneously monitor expression of thousands of genes — launching the era of genomics. Chung et al. (2004) applied microarray-based gene expression profiling to 60 HNSCC tumours, identifying four distinct molecular subtypes with different survival outcomes and achieving 80% accuracy in predicting lymph node metastasis — the landmark molecular classification of HNSCC. van de Vijver et al. (2002) validated the 70-gene microarray prognostic signature in 295 breast cancer patients, establishing the paradigm that microarray-based molecular signatures outperform clinicopathological factors in outcome prediction — a model subsequently applied to HNSCC.

## Introduction

Molecular techniques have transformed the diagnosis, staging, and prognostication of head and neck cancers. Stell and Maran's Chapter 3 states: the identification of tumour-specific somatic molecular alterations in HNSCC, coupled with the development of highly sensitive molecular analytic techniques such as the **polymerase chain reaction (PCR)**, provides several opportunities for improved molecular diagnostics of HNSCC.

Harrison's Chapter 1 describes: molecular techniques used in evaluating for chromosomal aberrations include **polymerase chain reaction (PCR)**, comparative genomic hybridization (CGH), in situ hybridization (ISH), and **DNA microarrays**.

---

## Part A: Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

### Principle

Harrison's Chapter 1 explains: PCR is used for isolating and **exponentially amplifying a fragment or sequence of interest of DNA** via enzymatic replication. PCR is an in vitro technique; it can be performed without restrictions on the form of DNA, and it can be extensively modified to perform a wide array of genetic manipulations.

Scott-Brown's Chapter 13 describes: following the separation of double-strand DNA (dsDNA) into single-strand DNA (ssDNA), the presence of the target DNA is detected using small targeted DNA sequences (**primers**) designed to bind to complementary sequences on either side of the gene under investigation. Repeated cycles will **exponentially augment** single copies of the specific gene.

### Steps of PCR

| Step | Temperature | Process |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Denaturation** | 94-98°C | dsDNA separates into ssDNA |
| **2. Annealing** | 50-65°C | Primers bind to complementary sequences |
| **3. Extension** | 72°C | Taq polymerase synthesizes new DNA strand |
| **Repeat** | 25-35 cycles | Exponential amplification (2ⁿ copies) |

Harrison's notes: most PCR methods typically amplify DNA fragments of up to **10 kilobase pairs (kb)**, although some techniques allow for amplification of fragments up to 40 kb in size.

### Variants of PCR

| Variant | Principle | Application in HNSCC |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Consensus PCR** | Detects presence of target DNA | HPV DNA detection (presence/absence) |
| **Real-time PCR (qPCR)** | Quantifies DNA copy number in real time | HPV viral load quantification |
| **RT-PCR** | Reverse transcribes RNA → cDNA → PCR | Gene expression analysis |
| **qRT-PCR** | Quantitative RT-PCR | HPV E6/E7 mRNA detection — gold standard for HPV biological activity |
| **Methylation-specific PCR** | Detects methylated CpG islands | Promoter hypermethylation analysis |
| **LOH analysis** | PCR-based microsatellite analysis | Molecular staging of [[Answers/General/Surgical Margins in Head and Neck Cancer|surgical margins]] |

### Applications of PCR in HNSCC

**1. HPV Detection:**
Scott-Brown's Chapter 13 states: consensus PCR simply detects the presence of HPV DNA, whilst **real-time PCR is able to quantify viral load**. qRT-PCR constitutes the **current gold standard assay** for determining HPV biological activity — it detects transcriptionally active E6 and E7 mRNA, moving beyond merely detecting HPV DNA presence.

**2. Molecular Diagnostics:**
Stell and Maran's notes: premalignant lesions containing 3p14 or 9p21 alterations (detected by PCR) have a significantly higher likelihood of evolving into HNSCC (**37%**) compared to lesions without these changes (**6%**).

**3. Molecular Staging of Margins:**
Stell and Maran's describes: several studies demonstrated a correlation between the presence of **genetically abnormal cells in histologically benign mucosa** within surgical margins and a higher risk for local recurrence. PCR-detected molecular alterations in histologically benign lymph node aspirates were associated with reduced survival.

**4. Methylation Analysis:**
Stell and Maran's states: PCR-based analysis of promoter hypermethylation events may improve sensitivity and specificity over traditional LOH analysis. Tumour-specific methylation events can be detected in **saliva and serum**.

## Part B: DNA Microarray Analysis

### Principle

Stell and Maran's Chapter 3 provides a schematic description: tumour and normal reference RNA (or DNA) is reverse-transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) and **differentially labelled** with a red fluorescent and green fluorescent agent, respectively. Equal amounts of labelled tumour and reference DNA are hybridized onto chips dotted with several thousands of individual gene sequences. Using a computer-assisted microscope and spectral analysis, the **green-to-red colour ratio** for each dot (gene) is calculated.

Harrison's confirms: DNA microarrays utilize a **multiplex-spotted technology** where thousands of oligonucleotide probes corresponding to human genes are spotted on a solid surface (gene chip) or on microscopic beads. The intensity of the signals is proportional to the abundance of specific cDNA in a given specimen.

### Interpretation of Microarray Data

| Colour Ratio | Meaning |
|--------------|---------|
| **Red > Green (ratio >1)** | Gene overexpressed in tumour |
| **Green > Red (ratio <1)** | Gene underexpressed in tumour |
| **Red = Green (ratio = 1)** | Equal expression in tumour and reference |

### Applications of Microarray in HNSCC

**1. Molecular Classification:**
Stell and Maran's describes: Chung et al. investigated the gene expression profile of 60 HNSCC and identified a **high-risk gene expression profile predictive of lymph node metastasis (80% accuracy)**. HNSCC can be stratified into several different subgroups based on divergent global molecular profiles, some of which are associated with poor clinical outcome.

Chung et al. (2004) identified **four distinct HNSCC subtypes:**
- EGFR-pathway subtype
- Mesenchymal-enriched subtype
- Normal epithelium-like subtype
- Antioxidant enzyme subtype

**2. Prognostic Signatures:**
Stell and Maran's cites the paradigm-setting breast cancer example: van de Vijver and colleagues identified a **70-gene poor prognosis signature** that outperformed clinicopathological factors in the prediction of distant metastasis. The molecular signature was an independent predictor of disease outcome in 295 patients. In HNSCC, Roepman and colleagues identified and validated **102 predictor genes** that predicted lymph node metastasis with **86% accuracy** compared to **68% accuracy** of clinical diagnosis.

**3. Predictor Identification:**
Stell and Maran's notes: Ganly and colleagues identified and externally validated **MDM2 and ERBB2 (Her2/Neu)** as predictors of regional recurrence after chemoradiation therapy of laryngeal carcinoma.

### Limitations

Stell and Maran's acknowledges: reproducibility issues associated with **RNA-based microarray analysis** may be overcome by recently developed improvements in DNA-based microarray analysis. Despite promising findings, the predictive value of individual or combined molecular markers **remains insufficient for routine clinical use** in HNSCC.

## Part C: Comparison of Molecular Techniques

| Feature | PCR | Microarray | Next-Generation Sequencing |
|---------|-----|------------|---------------------------|
| **Target** | Single gene/sequence | Thousands of genes | Entire genome/exome |
| **Principle** | Amplification | Hybridization | Sequencing by synthesis |
| **Throughput** | Low (1 target) | High (thousands) | Very high (millions) |
| **Sensitivity** | Very high (single copy) | Moderate | High |
| **Quantitation** | qPCR: yes | Semi-quantitative | Quantitative |
| **Sample requirement** | Very small (ng) | Moderate (μg) | Moderate |
| **Clinical use in HNSCC** | HPV detection, margins, LOH | Research; limited clinical | TCGA; emerging clinical |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. PCR exponentially amplifies specific DNA sequences — most sensitive molecular technique; can amplify a single copy of DNA (Harrison)
> 2. qRT-PCR is the GOLD STANDARD for determining HPV biological activity — detects transcriptionally active E6/E7 mRNA (Scott-Brown's)
> 3. DNA microarrays simultaneously analyse THOUSANDS of genes using fluorescent hybridization on gene chips — revolutionized molecular profiling
> 4. Chung et al. (2004) identified FOUR distinct molecular subtypes of HNSCC by microarray with 80% accuracy for predicting nodal metastasis
> 5. The 70-gene microarray signature outperformed clinicopathological staging in breast cancer (van de Vijver, 2002) — paradigm for HNSCC
> 6. Roepman identified 102 genes predicting lymph node metastasis with 86% accuracy vs 68% clinical accuracy (Stell and Maran's)
> 7. PCR-detected molecular alterations in histologically benign surgical margins predict higher local recurrence risk
> 8. PCR variants: consensus PCR (presence/absence), qPCR (quantification), RT-PCR (RNA expression), methylation-specific PCR
> 9. Despite promising findings, molecular markers remain INSUFFICIENT for routine clinical use in HNSCC — validation is ongoing (Stell and Maran's)
> 10. Microarray interpretation: red/green ratio >1 = overexpressed; <1 = underexpressed; =1 = equal expression

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, know the THREE steps of PCR: denaturation (94°C) → annealing (50-65°C) → extension (72°C) — repeated 25-35 cycles
> - qRT-PCR for HPV E6/E7 mRNA is the gold standard — distinguish from consensus PCR (detects DNA only) and qPCR (quantifies DNA load)
> - Microarray = simultaneous analysis of thousands of genes; PCR = one gene at a time — complementary techniques
> - The clinical utility of PCR in HNSCC is primarily in HPV detection, molecular margin analysis, and methylation-based liquid biopsy
> - Know the Chung classification: 4 HNSCC subtypes by gene expression — this is analogous to the molecular subtypes of breast cancer
> - Limitation of microarray: requires bioinformatic analysis, has reproducibility issues with RNA, and results are not yet validated for routine clinical decisions in HNSCC
