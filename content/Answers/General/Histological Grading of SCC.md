# Histological Grading of Squamous Cell Carcinoma

**Source:** Scott-Brown's Otorhinolaryngology Volume 3, Chapter 4 (TNM Staging — Histopathological Grading); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 1 (Pathology — Histologic Differentiation and Grade, Worst Pattern of Invasion); Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 4 (Assessment — Grading), Chapter 5 (Prognostic Factors)

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> Histological grading of squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) has evolved from Broders' (1920) whole-tumour differentiation-based system to modern approaches focusing on the invasive tumour front. Broders (1920) published the foundational paper establishing the first histological grading system, dividing 537 lip SCCs into four grades based on the ratio of differentiated to undifferentiated cells — proving for the first time that numerical tumour grading could predict prognosis. Bryne et al. (1992) demonstrated that malignancy grading of the deep invasive margins of oral SCC has high prognostic value, while conventional Broders' grading of the whole biopsy had none — shifting the paradigm to invasive front grading. Bryne (1998) published the comprehensive review establishing the theoretical basis for why the invasive front is the most important area for prognostication. [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Histological Risk Assessment in Oral Cancer|Brandwein-Gensler risk model]] et al. (2005) developed the histologic risk assessment model for oral SCC based on worst pattern of invasion (WPOI), [[Answers/General/Perineural Invasion|perineural invasion]], and lymphocytic host response — showing it is strongly predictive of local recurrence and survival, while margin status alone is not. The WPOI-5 pattern (dispersed tumour satellites ≥1 mm from main tumour) from this model was subsequently incorporated into the [[Answers/Oral Cavity/AJCC 8th Edition Oral Cavity Staging|AJCC 8th edition staging]] edition staging system.

## Introduction

The histological grading of squamous cell carcinoma represents **estimation by the pathologist of the expected biological behaviour of the neoplasm** (Scott-Brown's Chapter 4; Stell and Maran's Chapter 4). Although grading is a common practice, its role in treatment planning has evolved significantly over the past century.

Scott-Brown's states: it has been suggested that such information in conjunction with other characteristics of the primary tumour would be useful in the rational approach to therapy. Others have reserved doubts as to the validity of the method because of its subjective nature. Although grading of SCC of the upper aerodigestive tract mucosa is a common practice, it has **not evolved as an important factor** in planning therapeutic strategies.

Stell and Maran's Chapter 7 concurs: tumour grade, or degree of differentiation, **does not appear to be of great significance in HNSCC**.

However, modern grading approaches focusing on the invasive tumour front and worst pattern of invasion have shown considerably greater prognostic value.

## Part A: Conventional Grading Systems

### 1. Broders' Grading System (1920)

The first histological grading system for SCC was developed by **Albert C. Broders** of the Mayo Clinic. Scott-Brown's Chapter 4 notes that Broders published his grading system in 1920 and subsequently applied it to sites of the head and neck.

| Grade | Description | % Undifferentiated Cells |
|-------|-------------|-------------------------|
| **Grade I** | Well differentiated | <25% |
| **Grade II** | Moderately differentiated | 25-50% |
| **Grade III** | Poorly differentiated | 50-75% |
| **Grade IV** | Undifferentiated/anaplastic | >75% |

**Assessment based on:** Proportion of differentiated (keratinizing) cells to undifferentiated cells throughout the entire tumour mass.

**Limitations:**
- Subjective nature with poor interobserver reproducibility
- Evaluates entire tumour mass — ignores tumour heterogeneity
- Does not account for the invasive front, which is the most biologically active zone
- Poor correlation with prognosis in many HNSCC sites

### 2. WHO/TNM Grading System

The current standard grading incorporated into the UICC/AJCC TNM system (reproduced in Stell and Maran's Table 4.3):

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **GX** | Grade of differentiation cannot be assessed |
| **G1** | Well differentiated |
| **G2** | Moderately differentiated |
| **G3** | Poorly differentiated |
| **G4** | Undifferentiated |

Harrison's Chapter 1 explains: a **well-differentiated squamous carcinoma** has obvious keratinization and intercellular bridges. As a tumour becomes less differentiated, it "loses" its histologic similarities to its cell of origin. A **poorly differentiated SCC** has little, if any, keratinization, and intercellular bridges become less distinct or absent.

### Prognostic Significance of Conventional Grading

Scott-Brown's and Stell and Maran's both cite the same systematic review:
- **3294 patients** analysed
- **46% of poorly differentiated tumours** had nodal metastasis at presentation vs only **28% of differentiated tumours**
- **3.4% of poorly differentiated tumours** had distant metastases vs **1.8% of well-differentiated tumours**
- Primary and nodal recurrence rates rose for poorly differentiated tumours
- Survival fell significantly for poorly differentiated tumours
- Patients with well-differentiated tumours are at low risk of distant metastases; poorly differentiated at high risk

## Part B: Invasive Front Grading — Bryne's System (1992)

### Rationale

Bryne (1998) established the theoretical basis: molecular and morphological characteristics at the **invasive front** (tumour-host interface) reflect tumour prognosis better than other parts of the tumour. Key molecular events at the invasive front include:
- Gains and losses of adhesion molecules
- Secretion of proteolytic enzymes
- Increased cell proliferation
- Initiation of [[Answers/General/Tumour Angiogenesis|angiogenesis]]

Bryne et al. (1992) proved that **invasive front grading is an independent prognostic factor** (p < 0.01 in Cox multivariate analysis), while conventional Broders' grading of the whole biopsy had **no prognostic value** (p = 0.38).

### Bryne's Invasive Front Malignancy Grading System

| Parameter | Score 1 | Score 2 | Score 3 | Score 4 |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Degree of keratinization** | Highly keratinized | Moderately keratinized | Minimal keratinization | No keratinization |
| **Nuclear polymorphism** | Little | Moderate | Abundant | Extreme |
| **Pattern of invasion** | Pushing, well-defined borders | Infiltrating, solid cords/bands | Small groups or cords (<15 cells) | Marked, widespread dissociation |
| **Lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate** | Marked | Moderate | Slight | Absent |

**Total score:** Sum of all four parameters
- **Grade I (Low malignancy):** 4-8 points
- **Grade II (Intermediate):** 9-12 points
- **Grade III (High malignancy):** 13-16 points

**Key principle:** Only cells at the **deepest invasive margin** of the tumour are graded — NOT the entire tumour mass.

Scott-Brown's confirms: a non-cohesive invasive front is an indicator of the probability of positive cervical lymph nodes.

## Part C: Worst Pattern of Invasion — Brandwein-Gensler Risk Model (2005)

### WPOI Classification

Harrison's Chapter 1 describes in detail the Brandwein-Gensler worst pattern of invasion (WPOI) system:

| WPOI | Description | Risk |
|------|-------------|------|
| **1** | Invasion in broad pushing front | Low |
| **2** | Finger-like broad pushing pattern or separate large tumour islands | Low |
| **3** | Invasive tumour islands at periphery >15 cells/island | Intermediate |
| **4** | Invasive tumour islands at periphery <15 cells/island or single cell filing pattern | High |
| **5** | Satellites of dispersed tumour infiltrates ≥1 mm distance from main tumour mass | Highest |

### Histologic Risk Assessment Model

Harrison's Chapter 1 provides the complete scoring system (Table 1.12):

| Variable | Score 0 | Score 1 | Score 3 |
|----------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Perineural invasion** | None | Small nerves | Large nerves |
| **Lymphocytic response** | Continuous band | Patchy | Absent |
| **Worst pattern of invasion** | WPOI 1, 2, or 3 | — | WPOI 4 or 5 |

**Risk stratification:**
- **Low risk:** Total score = 0
- **Intermediate risk:** Total score = 1-2
- **High risk:** Total score ≥ 3

Brandwein-Gensler et al. (2005) showed: this risk assessment was significantly associated with **local recurrence** (p = 0.0004) and **overall survival** (p < 0.0001). Importantly, **margin status alone was NOT independently predictive** of local recurrence or survival — a paradigm-shifting finding.

### WPOI-5 and AJCC 8th Edition

WPOI-5 (dispersed tumour satellites ≥1 mm from the main tumour at the tumour-host interface) has been incorporated into the **AJCC 8th edition** Cancer Staging Manual as a recognized prognostic marker for oral cavity SCC, reflecting its strong association with occult cervical metastases and poor outcomes.

## Part D: Other Histopathological Features Beyond Grade

### Features at the Invasive Front

Scott-Brown's and Stell and Maran's emphasize that several features at the invasive front carry prognostic significance beyond formal grading:

| Feature | Prognostic Significance |
|---------|------------------------|
| **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Depth of Invasion in Oral Cancer|depth of invasion]] (DOI)** | Now part of AJCC 8th edition T staging for oral cavity |
| **Pattern of invasion** | Cohesive front = better; non-cohesive/dissociated = worse |
| **Perineural invasion** | Highly prognostically significant; indication for [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Oral Cancer|adjuvant RT]] |
| **Lymphovascular invasion** | Predictor of nodal metastasis and poor survival |
| **Tumour budding** | Small clusters (<5 cells) at invasive front; emerging prognostic marker |
| **Host immune response** | Dense lymphocytic infiltrate = favourable |
| **Tumour-stroma ratio** | High stroma ratio associated with poor outcome |

### Grading vs Site-Specific Behaviour

Stell and Maran's Chapter 7 provides an important caveat: conventional tumour grade does not appear to be of great significance in HNSCC overall. However:
- **Depth of invasion** has an adverse effect on prognosis, especially for oral tongue
- **Endophytic/ulcerative tumours** have worse prognosis than exophytic tumours at the same T stage
- **Tumour volume** may be more significant than diameter for organ-sparing therapy response

## Part E: Comparison of Grading Systems

| Feature | Broders (1920) | Bryne (1992) | Brandwein-Gensler (2005) |
|---------|---------------|-------------|-------------------------|
| **Area graded** | Entire tumour mass | Deep invasive front only | Worst pattern at interface |
| **Parameters** | % differentiation only | 4 parameters (keratinization, nuclear polymorphism, invasion pattern, lymphocytes) | 3 parameters (WPOI, PNI, lymphocytic response) |
| **Prognostic value** | Limited | Independent prognostic factor | Strongest predictor of LR and OS |
| **AJCC incorporation** | G1-G4 in TNM | Not formally adopted | WPOI-5 in AJCC 8th edition |
| **Clinical utility** | Descriptive; adjunctive to TNM | Better prognostication | Guides adjuvant RT decisions |

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Broders (1920) developed the first histological grading: 4 grades based on % undifferentiated cells in the entire tumour — foundational but limited prognostic value
> 2. WHO/TNM grading (GX, G1-G4) remains the standard descriptive system but has NOT evolved as an important factor in therapeutic planning
> 3. Bryne (1992) shifted the paradigm to INVASIVE FRONT grading — proven as independent prognostic factor while whole-biopsy grading was not (p = 0.38)
> 4. The invasive tumour front is the most biologically active zone — where adhesion molecule changes, proteolysis, proliferation, and angiogenesis occur
> 5. Brandwein-Gensler (2005) risk model (WPOI + PNI + lymphocytic response) is the most prognostically powerful — margin status alone was NOT predictive
> 6. WPOI-5 (dispersed tumour satellites ≥1 mm from main mass) is the highest-risk invasion pattern and is incorporated into AJCC 8th edition
> 7. Poorly differentiated tumours have 46% nodal metastasis rate vs 28% for differentiated tumours (systematic review of 3294 patients)
> 8. Bryne's 4-parameter system (keratinization, nuclear polymorphism, invasion pattern, lymphocytic infiltrate) grades only cells at the deepest invasive margin
> 9. Key features beyond formal grade: depth of invasion (now in T staging), PNI, LVI, tumour budding, and host immune response
> 10. Non-cohesive invasive front is an indicator of the probability of positive cervical lymph nodes (Scott-Brown's)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, describe the EVOLUTION: Broders (1920) → Bryne (1992) → Brandwein-Gensler (2005) — each advancing from whole-tumour to invasive front to worst pattern
> - The key conceptual shift: the INVASIVE FRONT is more important than the bulk of the tumour — Bryne proved this; Brandwein-Gensler refined it
> - Know the WPOI 1-5 classification — particularly WPOI-5 (dispersed satellites ≥1 mm) which is now in AJCC 8th edition
> - Remember: conventional grade (G1-G4) "does not appear to be of great significance in HNSCC" — this is directly from Stell and Maran's
> - The Brandwein-Gensler model guides adjuvant RT decisions for oral cavity SCC — high-risk patients (score ≥3) should receive adjuvant RT regardless of margin status
> - Depth of invasion is now part of T staging in AJCC 8th edition oral cavity — ≥5 mm DOI upstages the tumour; this is more impactful than histological grade
