# Factors Involved in Decision Making in Head and Neck Cancer

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Factors involved in decision making in head and neck (KIDWAI 2020)

**Source:** Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 7 (Treatment Options in Head and Neck Cancer); Jatin Shah - Head and Neck Surgery and Oncology, Chapter 1 (Introduction); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 5 (Pretreatment Evaluation); Montgomery - Principles and Practice of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 7 (Nursing Care — MDT)

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> Treatment decision-making in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) is among the most complex in oncology, requiring integration of tumour factors, patient factors, and healthcare system factors within a multidisciplinary framework. Piccirillo (2000) published the landmark study demonstrating the importance of comorbidity in head and neck cancer, showing that 21% of HNC patients had moderate-to-severe comorbidity and that comorbidity severity significantly affected overall survival — leading to the development of the Adult Comorbidity Evaluation-27 (ACE-27) instrument. Wheless et al. (2010) conducted the first prospective study evaluating the clinical impact of a multidisciplinary head and neck tumour board, finding that diagnosis, stage, or treatment plan was changed in 27% of cases — establishing the evidence base for MDT decision-making. Piccirillo et al. (2004) extended the comorbidity work in a JAMA publication demonstrating that comorbidity is an independent prognostic factor across cancer sites, including head and neck, and should be incorporated into staging systems. Prades et al. (2015) published the definitive systematic review of MDT impact on cancer outcomes, confirming improved survival in head and neck, colorectal, breast, and lung cancer with structured multidisciplinary care. Liu et al. (2020) provided the most recent evidence that implementation of a dedicated multidisciplinary tumour board significantly improved 5-year disease-specific survival in HNSCC.

## Introduction

Decision-making in head and neck cancer is among the most complex in all of oncology. As Stell and Maran's Chapter 7 states: the **heterogeneous nature of HNSCC**, as well as the diversity of patient characteristics and consequent paucity of level 1 evidence, **precludes the use of simple algorithms**, and each case must be discussed on its own merits. Evidence-based decisions depend more on retrospective outcome studies than randomized controlled trials in most areas, and there are very few trials with a surgical arm.

Jatin Shah's Chapter 1 concurs: selection of either surgery or radiotherapy depends on the site, size, and stage of the primary tumour as well as its proximity to bone and its depth of infiltration into the underlying soft tissues. Additional factors influencing the choice of initial treatment are **complications and sequelae of treatment, the patient's compliance with treatment, convenience of the recommended therapy for the patient, the cost of treatment, and the competency of the treatment delivery team** in executing the recommended therapy.

Stell and Maran's provides the definitive classification of decision-making factors in a **three-category framework** (Table 7.1):

| Category | Factors |
|----------|---------|
| **Patient factors** | Age, performance status, comorbidities, pulmonary reserve, occupation, compliance, socioeconomic background |
| **Tumour factors** | Site, stage, invasion of adjacent structures, histological grade, depth of infiltration, previous treatment, nodal/distant metastases |
| **Healthcare factors** | Local surgical/RT/chemo expertise, reconstruction capability, support services, cost, convenience, compliance, complications, competence |

## Part A: Tumour-Related Factors

### TNM Staging

The TNM system remains the cornerstone of treatment selection. Stell and Maran's explains: the TNM staging system divides patients according to the **size and anatomical extent of the primary tumour and its metastatic spread**, facilitating counselling regarding prognosis and treatment. The AJCC and UICC agreed on a unified system in 1987.

However, the textbook acknowledges significant limitations: HNSCC represents a heterogeneous population, and the predictive power of any one tumour stage leaves a great deal to be desired.

### Beyond TNM: Pathological and Molecular Factors

| Factor | Prognostic Significance |
|--------|------------------------|
| **Depth of invasion** | Adverse effect on prognosis, especially oral tongue |
| **Tumour volume** | May be more significant than diameter for organ-sparing therapy response |
| **Pattern of invasion** | Pushing interfaces → better prognosis; infiltrating cords → worse |
| **Perineural invasion** | Highly prognostically significant |
| **Lymphovascular invasion** | Highly prognostically significant |
| **Extracapsular spread (ENE)** | Potentially the most significant nodal prognostic factor |
| **Tumour grade** | Does NOT appear to be of great significance in HNSCC |
| **HPV status** | HPV+ oropharyngeal SCC has dramatically better prognosis |
| **EGFR expression** | Overexpression related to poor prognosis; altered in 90% of HNSCC |

### HPV Status

Stell and Maran's notes the dramatic increase in oropharyngeal cancers in younger populations, around 60% testing positive for HPV-16 infection. These cancers tend to be poorly differentiated with basaloid features but have an **excellent response to therapy**, possibly explained by functional wild-type p53 activity in this subset.

### Resectability

The T4a/T4b distinction (introduced in 2002) is a critical decision point. As Stell and Maran's explains: a tumour is only deemed truly unresectable when the surgeon doubts the ability to remove all gross tumour on anatomical grounds or believes local control will not be achieved by surgery followed by adjuvant radiotherapy. Three criteria define T4b status across most sites:
1. **Vascular encasement/invasion** — carotid artery involvement
2. **Prevertebral fascia invasion** — posterior extension
3. **Skull base invasion** — intracranial extension

Importantly, T4b does not imply incurability — nasopharyngeal cancer extending intracranially may achieve excellent results with concurrent chemoradiotherapy.

## Part B: Patient-Related Factors

### Performance Status and Comorbidity

Stell and Maran's states: the patient's physical condition may play a major role in determining which treatment pathway is most appropriate. HNSCC patients typically are of **advanced age** and have **multiple comorbidities** as they share risk factor exposure with systemic diseases such as hypertension, myocardial infarction, cerebrovascular events, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease.

Key performance status tools include:
- **Karnofsky Performance Scale (KPS):** 0-100% scale (Table 7.3 in Stell and Maran's)
- **ECOG/WHO Performance Status:** 0-5 scale
- **ACE-27 (Adult Comorbidity Evaluation-27):** Developed by Piccirillo; grades comorbidity severity

As comorbidities increase, the proportion of patients receiving **no treatment increases in parallel**. Aged patients are more likely to receive substandard treatment — 38% of those over 60 years and 64% over 80 years received suboptimal treatment in one series, with predictable adverse outcomes.

### Pulmonary Function

Inadequate pulmonary function will **preclude many types of partial laryngeal surgery** where aspiration is a significant risk. This is a critical factor in decision-making for conservation laryngeal surgery.

### Nutritional Status

Patients with head and neck cancer are frequently malnourished, which has adverse prognostic implications. Nutritional support given preoperatively for **7-10 days decreases postoperative complications by approximately 10%** in malnourished patients with weight loss ≥10%.

### Other Patient Factors

| Factor | Impact on Decision-Making |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **Immunosuppression (HIV)** | Severe adverse effect on prognosis |
| **Fanconi anaemia** | 500-700× increase in HNSCC risk; majority HPV-associated |
| **Occupation** | Determines tolerance for facial disfigurement or speech alteration |
| **Socioeconomic status** | Poorer prognosis in lower SES (reflects access to care) |
| **Social support** | Married patients willing to undergo more aggressive treatment |
| **Alcohol/tobacco status** | Priorities for CNS intervention before treatment |

### Patient Preferences

Stell and Maran's emphasizes: the patient's wishes are of course the **final determining factor** in choice of treatment. The textbook provides a striking statistic: **46% of medical staff believed their patients would sacrifice survival for better voice and quality of life, whereas only 20% of patients agreed**. Patients with advanced HNSCC consistently prioritize cure and long-term survival over quality of life issues far more than their peers and healthcare professionals.

## Part C: Healthcare System Factors

### The 5 Cs (Stell and Maran's)

Stell and Maran's introduces the **5 Cs** that determine treatment selection from a healthcare system perspective:

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Cost** | Financial burden of treatment pathway |
| **Convenience** | Willingness to travel daily for 6-7 weeks (RT); logistical feasibility |
| **Compliance** | Patient's ability to adhere to treatment schedule |
| **Complications** | Expected morbidity of treatment modality |
| **Competence** | Local expertise in surgical/RT/medical oncology techniques |

Jatin Shah identifies these same factors: complications and sequelae of treatment, the patient's compliance, convenience of recommended therapy, cost of treatment, and competency of the treatment delivery team.

### Local Expertise

Stell and Maran's notes: in the ideal situation, the full range of therapies should be available. In practice, local expertise tends to be stronger in some areas than others, and support services may be inadequate for some of the more demanding options.

## Part D: The Multidisciplinary Team

### Structure and Function

Stell and Maran's states: the sheer complexity and diversity of the anatomy, physiology, and pathology of the multiple potential tumour subsites **necessitates a team approach to HNSCC**. No single clinician can be expected to have the requisite expertise.

Harrison's Chapter 5 confirms: multidisciplinary tumour board participation in evaluation and treatment planning is now the **standard of care** for management of head and neck cancer patients. Evidence demonstrates multiple benefits: improved staging accuracy, greater accordance with clinical practice guidelines, improved communication, enhanced cost-effectiveness, shorter time to treatment, and improved satisfaction.

### MDT Core Members (Montgomery, Box 7.1)

Montgomery's Chapter 7 lists the supporting MDT care team:

| Core Members | Support Services |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Head and neck surgeon | Clinical nurse specialist |
| Radiation oncologist | Dietitian |
| Medical oncologist | Speech and language therapist |
| Neuroradiologist | Physiotherapist |
| Head and neck pathologist | Social worker |
| Reconstructive surgeon | Psychotherapist |
| Maxillofacial prosthodontist | Palliative care team |

### Impact of MDT on Outcomes

Harrison's reports: a prospective study evaluated 120 consecutive patients presented at a multidisciplinary head and neck tumour board — diagnosis, stage, or treatment was changed in approximately **one out of every four patients** reviewed. Review of outside imaging by dedicated specialists led to changes in 41% of cases, with 34% having a change in T, N, or M staging.

## Part E: Treatment Selection by Stage

### General Principles (Jatin Shah)

| Stage | General Approach |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Early (I-II)** | Single-modality treatment (surgery OR RT) — outcomes comparable |
| **Advanced (III-IV)** | Multidisciplinary treatment: surgery + adjuvant RT/CRT |
| **Unresectable (T4b)** | Non-surgical: concurrent chemoradiotherapy |
| **Incurable/metastatic** | Palliative: symptom control, chemotherapy, immunotherapy |

### Treatment Goals (Stell and Maran's)

The primary goal is either **curative or palliative**. Secondary goals include:
1. Preservation of form and function
2. Restoration of form and function where preservation is not possible
3. Minimization of treatment sequelae
4. Prevention of second primary tumours (smoking/alcohol cessation)

As Stell and Maran's cautions: we must not compromise elimination of the disease in our quest to retain 'function', as **an organ containing residual tumour will soon become more of a burden to the patient than the primary radical resection**.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Decision-making in HNSCC involves three categories of factors: tumour-related, patient-related, and healthcare system-related (Stell and Maran's Table 7.1)
> 2. TNM staging is the cornerstone but has significant limitations — depth of invasion, PNI, LVI, ENE, HPV status, and EGFR expression are important beyond-TNM factors
> 3. Comorbidity is an independent prognostic factor (Piccirillo, 2000) — ACE-27 grades comorbidity; moderate-severe comorbidity present in 21% of HNC patients
> 4. Patient preferences are the FINAL determining factor — 80% of patients prioritize survival over quality of life (only 20% would sacrifice survival for voice/QoL)
> 5. The 5 Cs of healthcare factors: Cost, Convenience, Compliance, Complications, Competence
> 6. MDT discussion is the standard of care — alters diagnosis/stage/treatment in 27% of cases (Wheless et al., 2010)
> 7. Early-stage HNSCC: single-modality (surgery or RT); advanced-stage: multidisciplinary combination therapy
> 8. HPV status is now a critical decision-making factor — HPV+ oropharyngeal SCC has excellent prognosis and distinct treatment implications
> 9. Nutritional status must be addressed: 7-10 days preoperative supplementation reduces complications by 10% in malnourished patients
> 10. Resectability (T4a vs T4b) is a key decision point — T4b does not imply incurability (e.g., NPC responds well to CCRT)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, structure your answer using Stell and Maran's three-category framework: tumour factors, patient factors, healthcare factors — then discuss MDT
> - The 5 Cs mnemonic (Cost, Convenience, Compliance, Complications, Competence) from Stell and Maran's is a high-yield exam answer
> - Key quote for exams: "no single clinician can be expected to have the requisite expertise" — justifies the MDT approach
> - Remember the counterintuitive patient preference data: clinicians OVERESTIMATE patients' willingness to trade survival for quality of life
> - Inadequate pulmonary reserve is a specific contraindication to conservation laryngeal surgery — a frequently tested clinical scenario
> - HPV status has fundamentally changed decision-making: de-escalation trials for HPV+ disease are now a major research direction
