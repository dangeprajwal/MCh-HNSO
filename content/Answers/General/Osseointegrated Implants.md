## Introduction

Osseointegrated implants have revolutionized the dental and prosthetic rehabilitation of head and neck cancer patients. The concept, pioneered by **Per-Ingvar Branemark** in the 1950s-60s, is based on the discovery that titanium implanted into bone forms a direct structural and functional connection — osseointegration — without an intervening fibrous tissue layer. Stell and Maran's Chapter 57 provides the most comprehensive account: optical chambers made of titanium implanted into rabbit tibias could not be removed because bone grew directly against the titanium frames. Harrison Chapter 9 notes that osseointegrated implants have had a "dramatic impact" on rehabilitation, with bite force and masticatory efficiency in hemimandibulectomy patients with free-flap reconstruction and primary implant placement being comparable to non-cancer patients.

## Part A: Principles of Osseointegration

### Definition

Osseointegration is defined as the **direct structural and functional connection between living, ordered bone and the surface of a load-carrying implant** at the light microscopic level (Branemark 1977). There is no intervening fibrous tissue — only a very thin layer of proteoglycan (up to 500 nm) between bone and implant surface (Stell Ch57).

### The Titanium-Bone Interface

Stell and Maran's Chapter 57 describes the tissue interface:

- **Titanium Oxide Layer:** A thin oxide layer covers pure titanium after contact with air — this oxide is inert, practically insoluble, and resistant to body fluids
- **Biocompatibility:** Bone predictably grows on the surface of titanium. Contaminated surfaces impede osteoblast colonization
- **Healing Sequence:**

| Time | Event |
|------|-------|
| Immediate | Acute inflammatory response |
| Days 1-3 | Macrophage debris removal |
| Days 3-7 | Mesenchymal cell migration |
| Week 1 | Osteoid production |
| Week 3 | Woven bone formation |
| Week 7 | Lamellar bone remodelling |

### Requirements for Successful Osseointegration

1. **Biocompatible Implant Material:** Titanium is the gold standard, but aluminium oxide ceramics, tantalum, stainless steel, and cobalt/nickel alloys can also osseointegrate (Stell Ch57)
2. **Minimally Traumatic Placement:** Gentle surgical technique with controlled drilling speed to avoid thermal necrosis
3. **Primary Stability:** The implant must be mechanically stable at insertion
4. **Undisturbed Healing:** A period of 3-6 months without loading before prosthesis placement

### Bone Quality and Implant Success

Stell Ch57 notes that mandibular implants have **higher success rates** than maxillary implants due to the greater proportion of cortical bone. Bone density varies significantly by anatomical site.

## Part B: Applications in Head and Neck Oncology

### Intraoral (Dental) Implants

**After Mandibulectomy (Jatin Shah Ch8):**
- Osseointegrated implants with a fixed denture should be considered in edentulous patients after [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Marginal Mandibulectomy|marginal mandibulectomy]]
- If adequate vertical height of remaining mandible is available, implants can be inserted **at the time of mandibulectomy** or later
- If postoperative RT is required, **extreme caution** should be exercised placing implants in irradiated bone

**In Reconstructed Mandible (Harrison Ch9):**
- In normal mandible/maxilla: success rate **90-95%**
- In reconstructed mandible: may be lower depending on quantity and quality of recipient bone
- **Fibula free flap:** Bicortical configuration and vertical height (average 15.2 mm) are ideal for endosteal implant placement
- Chiapasco et al. (2006): Implants in fibula free flaps achieve survival comparable to native bone

### Extraoral (Craniofacial) Implants

Montgomery describes applications for facial prostheses:

| Site | Application | Key Considerations |
|------|------------|-------------------|
| Temporal bone | BAHA, auricular prostheses | Good bone stock |
| Orbit | Orbital prosthesis after exenteration | Upper outer quadrant preferred; minimum 3-4 mm bone depth |
| Nose | Nasal prosthesis after rhinectomy | Pyriform rim, nasal bridge |
| Zygoma | Zygomaticus implants for nasal reconstruction | Complex anatomy |

- Prosthesis attachment: **press-stud (ball/socket) or magnet** retention
- FDA approved extraoral implants since 1993
- Heal unloaded for **4-6 months** (Harrison Ch9)

### Prosthetic Rehabilitation Principles

Montgomery notes three principles govern prosthetic success:
1. **Support** — adequate bone stock for implant anchorage
2. **Stability** — resistance to displacement during function
3. **Retention** — resistance to removal along the path of insertion

## Part C: Osseointegrated Implants in Irradiated Patients

### The Challenge

Radiotherapy impairs bone vascularity, reduces osteocyte viability, and slows the osseointegration process. This is the most clinically relevant issue for HNC patients.

### Evidence

| Study | n (Implants) | Survival Rate | Key Finding |
|-------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| Adell 1981 (non-irradiated) | 2,768 | 91% (mandible), 81% (maxilla) | Baseline reference |
| Granstrom 2005 (irradiated) | 631 | Lower than non-irradiated | Higher failure with high dose and longer time since RT |
| Schiegnitz 2014 (meta-analysis) | Multiple studies | **83%** (range 34-100%) | Irradiated native bone > irradiated grafted bone |
| Scott-Brown Ch31 | Review | 83% mean, >=5 years follow-up | "Radiotherapy reduces success but use is still of great value" |

### Practical Considerations

- **Timing:** Montgomery notes osseointegration in irradiated orbital bone may take **up to 1 year** (vs 3-6 months normally)
- **[[Answers/General/Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy|hyperbaric oxygen therapy]] (HBO):** Scott-Brown's states there is "currently **inadequate information** to support routine use" of HBO before implant placement in irradiated bone
- **Dose Effect:** Higher RT dose correlates with higher implant failure (Granstrom 2005)
- **Site Matters:** Highest failure rates in frontal bone, zygoma; better outcomes in mandible even after RT

### Contraindications

Stell Ch57 lists:
- Immunosuppression
- Psychiatric disorders (compliance issues)
- **Smoking** — has detrimental effect on osseointegration
- Poorly controlled diabetes
- Recent high-dose radiotherapy to implant site

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Osseointegration (Branemark 1977) is the direct bone-titanium interface without fibrous tissue ��� titanium oxide layer is inert and biocompatible; healing sequence takes 7 weeks to lamellar bone
> 2. In normal bone, implant success rates are 90-95%; mandibular > maxillary due to greater cortical bone proportion
> 3. In irradiated patients, mean implant survival is 83% (meta-analysis, Schiegnitz 2014) — reduced but still valuable for rehabilitation
> 4. Fibula free flap is ideal for implant placement due to bicortical configuration and adequate vertical height (15.2 mm) — survival comparable to native bone (Chiapasco 2006)
> 5. Extraoral implants (temporal, orbital, nasal) enable prosthetic rehabilitation after major ablative surgery; FDA approved since 1993; retained by press-stud or magnet

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Primary vs Delayed Placement:** Implants can be placed at the time of mandibulectomy (primary) or later — primary placement avoids a second surgery but requires careful treatment planning with the prosthodontist preoperatively
> - **The RT Dilemma:** If [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Oral Cancer|adjuvant RT]] is anticipated, exercise extreme caution with implant placement in the radiation field — consider delaying implant placement until after RT completion and healing (Jatin Shah Ch8)
> - **HBO Is Not Proven:** Despite theoretical rationale, Scott-Brown's states evidence is inadequate to support routine HBO before implant placement — don't assume it's standard of care
> - **Smoking Must Stop:** Smoking has a direct detrimental effect on osseointegration — cessation is mandatory before implant placement
> - **Bite Force Recovery:** Harrison reports that hemimandibulectomy patients with iliac crest free flap and primary implants achieve bite force comparable to non-cancer patients — this is the strongest functional outcome evidence
