## Introduction

Smoking cessation is one of the most impactful interventions in head and neck cancer (HNC) management. Continued smoking after diagnosis increases recurrence risk 2-6 fold, doubles the risk of second primary tumours, worsens treatment toxicity, and reduces overall survival. Nicotine replacement therapy forms the cornerstone of pharmacological cessation support, aiming to reduce withdrawal symptoms while patients break the behavioural habit.

## Nicotine Pharmacology and Dependence

### Mechanism of Addiction

- Nicotine binds **α4β2 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs)** in the ventral tegmental area (VTA) of the mesolimbic system
- Stimulates **dopamine release** in the nucleus accumbens → reward and reinforcement
- Tolerance develops rapidly — receptor upregulation with chronic exposure
- Withdrawal symptoms: irritability, anxiety, difficulty concentrating, increased appetite, craving — onset within 2-3 hours of last cigarette
- **Fagerström Test for Nicotine Dependence (FTND)**: validated 6-item scale; score ≥6 indicates high dependence requiring pharmacotherapy

### Nicotine in Head and Neck Cancer

- Nicotine itself is **not a direct carcinogen** but promotes tumour progression through:
  - Activation of nAChRs on tumour cells → proliferation, anti-apoptosis
  - Stimulation of angiogenesis via VEGF upregulation
  - Promotion of epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT)
- Despite these concerns, NRT use during cessation provides **net clinical benefit** — risk of continued smoking far outweighs theoretical NRT risks

## Forms of Nicotine Replacement Therapy

### NRT Products

| Product | Dose Range | Onset of Action | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Transdermal patch | 7-21 mg/24h | Slow (1-2 hours) | Steady-state delivery, once daily |
| Chewing gum | 2-4 mg/piece | Moderate (15-30 min) | Ad libitum dosing, buccal absorption |
| Sublingual tablet | 2 mg | Moderate (15-20 min) | Discreet, fixed dosing |
| Nasal spray | 0.5 mg/spray | Rapid (5-10 min) | Closest to cigarette nicotine delivery |
| Oral inhaler | 10 mg cartridge | Moderate (15-20 min) | Addresses hand-to-mouth ritual |
| Lozenge | 2-4 mg | Moderate (20-30 min) | For patients who cannot chew gum |

### Prescribing Principles

- **Patch**: provides baseline nicotine replacement; start at 21 mg/24h for heavy smokers (>10 cigarettes/day), step down over 8-12 weeks
- **Combination NRT**: patch + short-acting form (gum/spray/lozenge) is **superior to monotherapy** — OR 1.34 vs single NRT (Cochrane 2018)
- Duration: minimum **8-12 weeks**, can extend to 6 months for high-dependence patients
- Dose according to FTND score and number of cigarettes per day

### Special Considerations in HNC

- **Oral mucositis**: gum and lozenges may be poorly tolerated during/after RT; prefer patch or nasal spray
- **Post-laryngectomy**: inhaler device not effective (no nasal airflow); use patch, gum, or sublingual tablet
- **Trismus**: limits use of gum; prefer patch, lozenge, or sublingual tablet
- **Xerostomia**: affects gum efficacy (requires saliva for nicotine release); prefer patch

## Non-Nicotine Pharmacotherapies

### Varenicline (Champix/Chantix)

- **Mechanism**: partial agonist at α4β2 nAChR — provides moderate dopamine stimulation (reduces craving) while blocking nicotine binding (reduces reward from smoking)
- **Dosing**: 0.5 mg OD × 3 days → 0.5 mg BD × 4 days → 1 mg BD × 11 weeks
- **Efficacy**: OR 2.24 vs placebo; superior to single NRT (OR 1.57) and bupropion (OR 1.59) — Cahill network meta-analysis 2013
- **Jorenby et al. (2006)**: continuous abstinence at 52 weeks — varenicline 23% vs bupropion 15% vs placebo 10%
- **Side effects**: nausea (30%), insomnia, vivid dreams; FDA black box warning for neuropsychiatric events removed in 2016
- **Contraindications**: severe renal impairment (dose reduction), caution in psychiatric illness

### Bupropion Sustained-Release (Zyban)

- **Mechanism**: norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor (NDRI); also weak nAChR antagonist
- **Dosing**: 150 mg OD × 3 days → 150 mg BD × 7-12 weeks; start 1-2 weeks before quit date
- **Efficacy**: OR 1.64 vs placebo — Hurt et al. (1997): 44% quit rate at 7 weeks vs 19% placebo
- **Side effects**: insomnia, dry mouth, seizure risk (0.1%)
- **Contraindications**: seizure disorder, eating disorders, concurrent MAOIs
- **Advantage**: prevents weight gain associated with cessation

## Comparison of Cessation Pharmacotherapies

| Agent | Mechanism | OR vs Placebo | 12-month Quit Rate | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NRT monotherapy | nAChR agonist | 1.50-1.60 | 15-20% | First-line, widely available |
| Combination NRT | Patch + short-acting | 1.93 | 25-30% | Heavy smokers, FTND ≥6 |
| Varenicline | Partial α4β2 agonist | 2.24 | 23-25% | Highest single-agent efficacy |
| Bupropion SR | NDRI + nAChR antagonist | 1.64 | 18-20% | Weight concerns, depression |
| Combination NRT + varenicline | Dual mechanism | ~2.5 | 30-35% | Refractory smokers |

## Smoking Cessation in HNC: Clinical Importance

### Impact of Continued Smoking

- **Recurrence**: continued smoking increases local recurrence risk 2-6× compared to quitters
- **Second primary tumours**: risk remains elevated for 5 years; cessation reduces risk by 50% after 5 years
- **Treatment toxicity**: increased mucositis severity, reduced RT response, higher complication rates
- **Survival**: 5-year OS reduced by 20-30% in persistent smokers vs quitters
- **Wound healing**: smoking impairs flap vascularity; cessation ≥4 weeks preoperatively recommended

### The 5A Framework for Cessation Counselling

1. **Ask** — screen every patient for tobacco use at every visit
2. **Advise** — clear, personalised advice to quit
3. **Assess** — readiness to quit (stages of change model)
4. **Assist** — pharmacotherapy (NRT/varenicline/bupropion) + behavioural counselling
5. **Arrange** — follow-up within 1 week of quit date, then at 1, 3, 6, 12 months

### Barriers Specific to HNC Patients

- Heavy nicotine dependence (mean 30+ pack-years)
- Concurrent alcohol dependence (dual addiction)
- Cancer-related psychological distress and fatalism
- Pain — nicotine has analgesic properties; patients self-medicate
- Social isolation and limited support networks
- "Teachable moment" of cancer diagnosis may be leveraged

### Evidence for Cessation Interventions in HNC

- **Gritz et al. (1993)**: RCT in 186 HNC patients — provider-delivered cessation advice + NRT vs usual care; 70% attempted quitting but sustained abstinence was only 33% at 12 months, highlighting need for intensive support
- Combined pharmacotherapy + behavioural counselling achieves best outcomes
- HNC patients who quit have **improved QOL**, better voice outcomes post-RT, and reduced xerostomia severity

> [!abstract] Key Points
> - NRT increases quit rates by 50-60% vs placebo; combination NRT (patch + short-acting) is superior to monotherapy
> - Varenicline is the most effective single agent (OR 2.24); superior to bupropion (OR 1.64) and single NRT
> - In HNC patients, choice of NRT formulation must account for mucositis, trismus, xerostomia, and airway status
> - The 5A framework provides structured cessation counselling at every clinical encounter
> - Continued smoking after HNC diagnosis increases recurrence 2-6×, doubles second primary risk, and reduces OS by 20-30%

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Combination NRT** (patch + gum/lozenge) should be first-line for heavy smokers — equivalent efficacy to varenicline
> - Start cessation pharmacotherapy **at diagnosis**, not after treatment completion — the "teachable moment" is most effective early
> - For patients undergoing concurrent chemoradiation with mucositis, the **transdermal patch** is the best-tolerated NRT option
> - Nicotine itself does not cause cancer — the benefits of NRT for cessation far outweigh theoretical concerns about tumour promotion
> - **Preoperative cessation ≥4 weeks** reduces wound complications; even 24-48 hours improves CO levels and oxygen-carrying capacity
