## Introduction

Chyle leak (chylous fistula) is an uncommon but potentially serious complication of head and neck surgery, occurring in 0.5-1.4% of thyroidectomies and 2-8% of neck dissections. It results from iatrogenic injury to the thoracic duct or its tributaries, most commonly during dissection of the left supraclavicular fossa (level IV). The thoracic duct's considerable anatomical variability makes it vulnerable to inadvertent damage. If unrecognized, chyle leak can lead to significant fluid and electrolyte losses, nutritional depletion, immunosuppression, wound healing impairment, and prolonged hospitalization.

---

## Part A: Anatomy of the Thoracic Duct

The thoracic duct originates from the cisterna chyli at the level of L1-L2, traverses the posterior thoracic cavity behind the oesophagus, and enters the neck crossing between the internal jugular vein (IJV) and the anterior scalene muscle on the left side. It terminates at the junction of the left IJV and left subclavian vein, or drains directly into the IJV.

**Key anatomical points:**

- The majority of chyle leaks (95%) occur on the **left side** — corresponding to the thoracic duct termination
- Approximately **5% occur on the right** — from the right lymphatic duct
- **2-4 litres** of chyle flow through the thoracic duct daily; flow varies with dietary fat intake
- The duct is thin-walled and often has multiple terminal branches — making it vulnerable to injury during level IV/V dissection

### Chyle Composition

Chyle is a milky-white lymphatic fluid formed in the intestinal lacteals from digested fat. It contains:
- Chylomicrons (long-chain triglycerides)
- Protein, immunoglobulins, lymphocytes
- Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)
- Electrolytes

---

## Part B: Prevention

Prevention through meticulous surgical technique is the best strategy:

1. **Careful dissection in level IV/V** — use bipolar cautery and clip or ligate all tissue pedicles in the supraclavicular fossa
2. **Identify the thoracic duct** — actively seek it during left-sided neck dissections, especially low in the neck
3. **Intraoperative leak detection:** At the conclusion of surgery, examine the supraclavicular region carefully. Use manoeuvres to increase intrathoracic pressure:
   - **Valsalva manoeuvre** (anaesthesiologist applies positive pressure ventilation)
   - **Trendelenburg positioning**
   - These increase chyle flow and help identify small leaks
4. **If creamy/milky fluid is seen intraoperatively:** Identify the source and ligate the duct with non-absorbable sutures or clips
5. **Ligate all lymphatic channels** entering the thoracic duct — divide and ligate individually rather than mass ligation

---

## Part C: Diagnosis

### Clinical Features
- Milky or creamy fluid in the surgical drain — usually apparent within 24-48 hours when oral feeding resumes
- High drain output (may exceed expected volume)
- Fluid becomes milky after fat-containing meals

### Biochemical Confirmation

| Test | Diagnostic Threshold |
|------|---------------------|
| Triglyceride level > 110 mg/dL | Diagnostic of chyle leak |
| Triglyceride level < 50 mg/dL | Usually excludes chyle leak (unless malnourished/fasted) |
| Triglyceride 50-110 mg/dL | Equivocal — test for chylomicrons |
| Chylomicron presence | Confirmatory |

### Classification by Output

| Category | Output | Management |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Low output | < 500 mL/24 hours | Conservative management |
| High output | > 500 mL/24 hours | May require intervention |
| Very high output | > 1000 mL/24 hours | Consider early surgical intervention |

---

## Part D: Management

### Conservative Management (First Line)

The mainstay of management is nutritionally based — reducing chyle formation allows the fistula to heal:

1. **Fat-free or MCT-based diet:** Medium-chain triglycerides (MCTs) are absorbed directly into the portal venous system, bypassing the thoracic duct. Long-chain triglycerides (LCTs) enter the bloodstream via chyle. Replacing LCTs with MCTs reduces chyle production while maintaining nutrition.

2. **Pressure dressing** over the supraclavicular wound

3. **Drain maintenance** — keep drain in situ until output decreases

4. **Fluid and electrolyte replacement** — monitor and replace losses

5. **If dietary modification fails:** Consider total parenteral nutrition (TPN) to achieve complete bowel rest — but this should not be first-line except in very high-output leaks (>1000 mL)

### Pharmacological Management

- **Octreotide (somatostatin analogue):** Reduces intestinal secretion and chyle flow. Published evidence from 2001-2015 includes multiple case reports and series demonstrating effectiveness. Typical dose: 100-200 mcg subcutaneously three times daily.
- **Orlistat:** Lipase inhibitor that reduces fat absorption — adjunct to dietary modification

### Surgical Management

Indicated when conservative management fails (typically after 5-7 days of high output) or for very high-output leaks:

1. **Neck re-exploration:** Identify and ligate the thoracic duct directly — the leak site may be difficult to identify; use Trendelenburg position and positive pressure ventilation
2. **Thoracoscopic thoracic duct ligation:** Emerging as the preferred surgical approach for complicated or high-output leaks — less invasive than open thoracic surgery
3. **Local tissue sealants:** Fibrin glue, oxidized cellulose (Surgicel), microfibrillar collagen (Avitene) applied to the leak site
4. **Muscle flap coverage** of the supraclavicular fossa

### Management Algorithm

```
Chyle leak confirmed
    │
    ├── Low output (<500 mL/day)
    │   └── Fat-free/MCT diet + pressure dressing
    │       └── Usually resolves in 2-5 days
    │
    ├── High output (500-1000 mL/day)
    │   └── MCT diet or TPN + Octreotide
    │       ├── Resolving → continue conservative
    │       └── Not resolving in 5-7 days → surgical exploration
    │
    └── Very high output (>1000 mL/day)
        └── TPN + Octreotide + early surgical exploration
            └── Thoracoscopic duct ligation or neck re-exploration
```

## Part E: Complications of Untreated Chyle Leak

- **Nutritional depletion:** Loss of protein, fats, fat-soluble vitamins, electrolytes
- **Immunosuppression:** Loss of lymphocytes and immunoglobulins increases infection risk
- **Wound healing impairment:** Local tissue damage from chyle
- **Electrolyte imbalance:** Hyponatraemia, hypocalcaemia
- **Prolonged hospitalization** and delayed adjuvant treatment

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Chyle leak occurs in 2-8% of neck dissections; 95% are left-sided (thoracic duct termination)
> 2. 2-4 litres of chyle flow daily through the thoracic duct; flow increases with dietary fat
> 3. Diagnosis: drain fluid triglyceride > 110 mg/dL is diagnostic; < 50 mg/dL excludes
> 4. Classification: low output (< 500 mL) vs. high output (> 500 mL) vs. very high (> 1000 mL)
> 5. Prevention: meticulous level IV/V dissection + Valsalva/Trendelenburg manoeuvre at end of surgery
> 6. Conservative management: MCT-based diet replaces LCTs, reducing chyle formation while maintaining nutrition
> 7. Octreotide (100-200 mcg SC TDS) reduces chyle flow — supported by multiple case series
> 8. Surgical intervention indicated if conservative management fails after 5-7 days or for very high output
> 9. Thoracoscopic thoracic duct ligation is emerging as the preferred surgical approach
> 10. Untreated chyle leak leads to nutritional depletion, immunosuppression, and wound healing failure

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Always perform Valsalva manoeuvre before closing a left-sided neck dissection — it takes 30 seconds and prevents a week-long complication
> - MCT oil can be used as a cooking medium — patients absorb it via the portal system, not the thoracic duct
> - If drain fluid becomes milky after the first oral feed, suspect chyle leak — send triglyceride levels immediately
> - Do not rush to TPN for low-output leaks — MCT diet alone resolves most cases within 2-5 days
> - Right-sided chyle leaks (5%) do occur — maintain vigilance during bilateral neck dissections
> - Early dietetic input is essential — a dietitian should be involved from the day of diagnosis
