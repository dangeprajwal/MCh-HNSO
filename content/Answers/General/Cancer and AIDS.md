(2001) analyzed 302,834 HIV/AIDS adults from the AIDS-Cancer Match Registry, finding standardized incidence ratios (SIR) of 258 for Kaposi's sarcoma and 78 for non-Hodgkin lymphoma, with each 100-cell drop in CD4 count increasing KS risk by 36%. ## Introduction

The intersection of HIV/AIDS and head and neck cancer represents an increasingly important clinical challenge. HIV-associated immunosuppression predisposes patients to both AIDS-defining malignancies (Kaposi's sarcoma, non-Hodgkin lymphoma) and non-AIDS-defining cancers (HNSCC, skin cancers). Harrison states that HIV-infected patients have an **increased relative risk of 2 to 3** to develop HNSCC. Stell and Maran's reports that in a New York study, **HIV infection was present in 5% of HNC patients**, rising to **over 20% in patients under 45 years** — with HIV-positive patients presenting with larger, more advanced tumours. The widespread use of highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) since 1996 has dramatically altered the spectrum of HIV-associated malignancies, with marked reduction in KS and NHL but an emerging increase in non-AIDS-defining cancers as patients live longer.

## Part A: AIDS-Defining Head and Neck Cancers

### 1. Kaposi's Sarcoma (KS)

Montgomery Chapter 24 provides the most comprehensive coverage:

- **Aetiology:** Caused by complex interaction between **Human Herpesvirus 8 (HHV-8)** and immunologic/environmental factors. HHV-8 is found in all KS cells and is contracted by oral exposure to infectious saliva
- **Epidemiology:** Formerly the most frequent malignant lesion in AIDS patients — up to 95% of all AIDS patients had KS. In the USA, KS is **10 times more common in homosexual men** with HIV than in other HIV subgroups
- **Head and Neck Presentation:**
  - Oral cavity and oropharynx are typical sites of extracutaneous spread
  - **Hard palate** is the most common intraoral site
  - Appearance: red to violaceous nodules, macules, or plaques that may ulcerate
  - Also affects gingiva and tongue
- **Histology:** Fascicles of spindle cells and numerous slit-like spaces containing red blood cells (Montgomery Ch11)

**Treatment (Montgomery Ch24):**

| Extent | Treatment Options |
|--------|------------------|
| Limited cutaneous | Topical alitretinoin gel, intralesional chemotherapy, RT, laser, cryotherapy |
| Extensive/visceral | Liposomal anthracyclines (daunorubicin, doxorubicin), paclitaxel |
| All patients | Initiate/optimize HAART — fundamental to management |

**Impact of HAART:** Franceschi et al. (2008) demonstrated from the Swiss HIV Cohort (12,959 patients, 596 KS cases, 1984-2006):
- KS incidence fell abruptly after 1996
- HR declined to **0.06** (95% CI: 0.02-0.17) at 7-10 years after HAART initiation
- 63.5% of KS in HAART users arose in those who had stopped or used HAART <6 months
- Harrison confirms: HAART has reduced KS rate by **up to 50%**

### 2. Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma (NHL)

- AIDS-defining cancer with SIR of 78 (Frisch 2001)
- Common head and neck presentations: Waldeyer's ring (tonsils, nasopharynx), cervical lymphadenopathy, salivary glands, paranasal sinuses
- Typically aggressive subtypes: diffuse large B-cell lymphoma, Burkitt lymphoma
- Associated with **[[Answers/General/EBV Testing in Head and Neck Cancer|Epstein-Barr virus]] virus (EBV)** co-infection in many cases
- Incidence has also decreased with HAART, but less dramatically than KS

## Part B: Non-AIDS-Defining Head and Neck Cancers in HIV

### Head and Neck Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Beachler et al. (2014) using NA-ACCORD pooled data demonstrated:

| Cancer Type | SIR in HIV | Significance |
|-------------|-----------|--------------|
| HPV-related HNSCC | 3.2 | Significantly elevated |
| HPV-unrelated HNSCC | 3.0 | Significantly elevated |

**Key Features in HIV-Positive Patients:**
- Present at **younger age** (Purgina 2011)
- More **aggressive disease** and advanced stage at presentation
- **Worse prognosis** despite similar treatment
- Lower CD4 count significantly associated with increased HNSCC risk (Beachler 2014)
- Montgomery Ch12: "HIV also probably accelerates development of SCC in high-risk patients"

**Critical Finding (Harrison):** The prognostic advantage of HPV/p16-positive status **disappears in the HIV-positive population**. Mourad et al. reported that among 73 HIV-positive SCCHN patients, there was no outcome difference between [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-positive]] vs HPV-negative — suggesting that HIV status is an important independent prognostic factor that overrides the HPV benefit.

### Other HIV-Associated HN Malignancies

| Malignancy | HIV Association |
|-----------|----------------|
| Lymphoepithelial lesions of salivary glands | Increased frequency; often bilateral (Jatin Shah Ch13) |
| Non-melanoma skin cancer | Markedly increased risk; more aggressive and invasive (Montgomery Ch25) |
| Merkel cell carcinoma | Increased risk in immunosuppression |
| Nasopharyngeal carcinoma | EBV-driven; increased in HIV |
| Hodgkin disease | SIR elevated in immunosuppression (Frisch 2001) |

### Radiation Sensitivity in HIV

Montgomery notes that "reduced levels of scavenger molecules (in such conditions as HIV/AIDS) may also increase reactions" to radiation — suggesting potential for increased RT toxicity in HIV-positive patients requiring dose modification or enhanced supportive care.

## Part C: Clinical Implications

### Screening and Surveillance

- HIV-positive patients should undergo regular oral and head/neck examination
- Low threshold for biopsy of oral lesions, particularly palatal lesions
- Maintain awareness that HNC may present at younger ages in HIV

### Treatment Considerations

- **HAART Optimization:** Fundamental to management of all HIV-associated malignancies
- **CD4 Count Monitoring:** Lower CD4 increases cancer risk and impacts treatment tolerance
- **Treatment Modifications:** Consider increased RT toxicity; chemotherapy interactions with antiretrovirals (particularly CYP3A4 interactions with protease inhibitors)
- **Multidisciplinary Approach:** HIV physician, oncologist, and head and neck surgeon coordination essential

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. HIV-infected patients have a 2-3x increased relative risk of HNSCC (Harrison); both HPV-related (SIR 3.2) and HPV-unrelated (SIR 3.0) cancers are elevated
> 2. Kaposi's sarcoma (HHV-8 driven, hard palate most common intraoral site) was formerly in 95% of AIDS patients but HAART reduced HR to 0.06 at 7-10 years (Swiss Cohort)
> 3. The prognostic advantage of HPV-positive status DISAPPEARS in HIV-positive HNSCC patients — HIV overrides the HPV survival benefit
> 4. HIV-positive HNC patients present younger, with more advanced disease, and have worse prognosis despite similar treatment (Purgina 2011)
> 5. In the New York study, HIV was present in 5% of all HNC patients and >20% of HNC patients under 45 years (Stell and Maran's)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Purple Palatal Lesion in a Young Patient:** Always consider Kaposi's sarcoma — ask about HIV status; the hard palate is the most common intraoral KS site
> - **HAART Is Therapy:** Optimizing antiretroviral therapy is the single most important intervention for HIV-associated KS — not just an adjunct but fundamental treatment
> - **HPV Paradox in HIV:** Don't assume HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer has a favourable prognosis in an HIV-positive patient — the survival advantage is lost
> - **Bilateral Salivary Gland Enlargement + HIV:** Think lymphoepithelial lesions — they occur with increased frequency and are often bilateral in HIV patients (Jatin Shah Ch13)
> - **Drug Interactions:** Protease inhibitors (CYP3A4 inhibitors) can dramatically alter chemotherapy metabolism — always involve HIV physician in treatment planning
