# Pre-operative Embolization in Head and Neck Surgery

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Pre-operative embolization (KIDWAI 2020)

**Source:** Jatin Shah - Head and Neck Surgery and Oncology, Chapter 6 (Skull Base — Preoperative Workup), Chapter 14 (Paragangliomas), Chapter 15 (Soft Tissue Tumours); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 27 (Paragangliomas — Embolization); Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 14 (Paragangliomas), Chapter 30 (Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma)

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> Preoperative embolization has become a standard adjunct to surgery for hypervascular head and neck tumours, particularly paragangliomas and juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibromas (JNA). Valavanis (1986) published the foundational article establishing indications, patient selection, goals, and precautions for preoperative embolization of head and neck tumours — reporting on 39 patients with 44 paragangliomas and demonstrating significant improvements in surgical conditions. Persky et al. (2002) described the combined endovascular and surgical approach as a team treatment for 47 patients with 53 head and neck paragangliomas, establishing the multidisciplinary paradigm. Chaloupka et al. (1999) introduced the concept of direct puncture therapeutic embolization (DPTE) for hypervascular head and neck neoplasms as an alternative to transarterial embolization. Jackson et al. (2015) published the first systematic review and meta-analysis of preoperative embolization for carotid body paragangliomas, confirming significantly less estimated blood loss compared with surgery alone. Kothari et al. (2023) provided the most comprehensive systematic review of preoperative embolization techniques in JNA, reporting PVA particles as the most commonly used embolic material and an embolization complication rate of 3.16%.

## Introduction

Preoperative embolization is a catheter-based interventional radiology procedure that reduces the vascularity of hypervascular tumours prior to surgical resection. In head and neck surgery, it is primarily indicated for **paragangliomas** (carotid body tumours, glomus vagale, glomus jugulare/tympanicum) and **juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibromas (JNA)**, as well as selected meningiomas, hemangiomas, and other hypervascular lesions.

Jatin Shah's Chapter 6 states: direct preoperative angiographic studies and **embolization are considered in highly vascular lesions and neurovascular tumors**. Harrison's Chapter 27 confirms: embolization is a **very useful adjunct to surgery** of large paragangliomas, and when done by an experienced endovascular team, carries a very low morbidity rate.

## Part A: Indications

### Tumours Requiring Preoperative Embolization

| Tumour | Indication | Textbook Reference |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Paragangliomas** (carotid body, glomus vagale, glomus jugulare) | Standard for larger tumours; reduces intraoperative haemorrhage | Stell Ch14; Harrison Ch27; Jatin Shah Ch14 |
| **Juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma** | Reduces blood loss during endoscopic or open resection | Stell Ch30; Jatin Shah Ch8 |
| **Hemangiomas** | Facilitates safe resection; thromboses vascular spaces | Jatin Shah Ch15 |
| **Meningiomas** (skull base) | Reduces vascularity of convexity/skull base meningiomas | Harrison Ch35 |
| **Hypervascular metastases** | Selected cases (renal, thyroid metastases) | — |
| **Carotid blowout syndrome** | Emergent embolization (therapeutic, not preoperative) | Stell Ch23 |

### Goals of Preoperative Embolization (Valavanis, 1986)

1. **Reduce intraoperative blood loss** — the primary goal
2. **Shorten operative time**
3. **Control surgically inaccessible feeding vessels** — deep branches not accessible during surgery
4. **Improve visualization** — less bleeding allows better identification of tissue planes
5. **Increase chances of complete resection**
6. **Reduce need for transfusion**
7. **Minimize risk to cranial nerves** — better dissection planes

Harrison's Chapter 27 explains: the advantages of embolization are **tumour shrinkage and decreased blood flow** that in large tumours have profound benefits. Less intraoperative bleeding is generally associated with a much easier dissection — more obvious tissue planes and less risk to normal anatomic structures, especially cranial nerves.

## Part B: Technique

### Pre-Embolization Assessment

1. **Diagnostic angiography** — maps the arterial supply (external carotid, internal carotid, vertebral contributions)
2. **Identify dangerous anastomoses** — particularly external-to-internal carotid anastomoses that may allow embolic particles to reach the cerebral circulation
3. **Assess contralateral venous drainage** — especially for glomus jugulare tumours (Harrison emphasizes checking for contralateral hypoplastic/absent jugular system as a contraindication)
4. **[[Answers/General/Balloon Occlusion Test|balloon occlusion test]] testing** — if carotid sacrifice is contemplated

### Embolization Technique

Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 describes: embolization must be **superselective**, in that only the artery feeding the tumour is embolized. In the temporal bone, multiple cannulations may be required to ensure that all compartments of the tumour are devascularized.

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| **1. Access** | Femoral artery puncture; guide catheter to common/external carotid artery |
| **2. Superselective catheterization** | Microcatheter navigated into individual feeding arteries |
| **3. Embolization** | Injection of embolic material into tumour vasculature |
| **4. Control angiography** | Post-embolization run to confirm devascularization (absence of "tumour blush") |
| **5. Surgery** | Within 24-48 hours of embolization |

### Embolic Materials

| Material | Type | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|----------|------|-----------|---------------|
| **PVA particles** | Particulate | Most commonly used (80% in JNA); reliable | May recanalize |
| **Gelfoam** | Temporary | Short-term occlusion; biodegradable | Temporary; tumour may revascularize |
| **Onyx (EVOH)** | Liquid | Excellent penetration; controllable | Requires experience; catheter may be trapped |
| **Cyanoacrylate (NBCA)** | Liquid adhesive | Permanent occlusion; rapid | Difficult to control; risk of distal migration |
| **Coils** | Metallic | Precise large vessel occlusion | Does not embolize tumour parenchyma |

### Critical Timing

Harrison's Chapter 27 is emphatic: it is essential that **surgery be performed within 48 hours of embolization** to avoid recruitment of collateral circulation. The inflammatory tumour reaction that has been reported following embolization is countered by administration of steroids.

Stell and Maran's Chapter 30 concurs for JNA: selective cannulation of the feeders of the tumour should be carried out **48 hours prior to the resection**.

## Part C: Tumour-Specific Applications

### Paragangliomas

**Carotid Body Tumour:**
- Jatin Shah's Chapter 14 states: preoperative embolization of the tumour is required for larger carotid body tumours
- Primary blood supply: ascending pharyngeal artery, branches of external carotid
- Jackson et al. (2015) meta-analysis: significantly less estimated blood loss with embolization

**Glomus Jugulare/Tympanicum:**
- Stell and Maran's Chapter 14: preoperative embolization has become a **standard procedure** in larger paraganglioma surgery
- Studies comparing embolized vs non-embolized procedures showed **highly significant reductions** in intraoperative haemorrhage and operation time
- Reserved for larger tumours (complication rate ~1%)
- Even with embolization, patients often lose >2 units of blood during resection of large jugular paragangliomas

**Glomus Vagale:**
- Jatin Shah describes delineation of vascular supply and consideration of preoperative embolization
- Feeds from ascending pharyngeal artery and branches of occipital artery

### Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma

Stell and Maran's Chapter 30 states: as the angiofibroma is a vascular tumour, **preoperative selective embolization is one option to reduce blood loss** during resection. Although the embolization procedure has a small risk of leading to cerebral ischaemia, in established centres the benefit outweighs the risk.

- Primary blood supply: internal maxillary artery (sphenopalatine branch)
- Additional feeders: ascending pharyngeal, facial, vidian arteries
- Bilateral supply common in larger tumours
- Kothari et al. (2023): PVA particles used in 80%; surgery within 24-48 hrs; complication rate 3.16%

### Hemangiomas

Jatin Shah's Chapter 15 provides detailed case examples of preoperative embolization for hemangiomas:
- Selective external carotid angiography delineates vascular supply
- Terminal branches of feeding arteries are selectively catheterized and embolized
- The surgical specimen demonstrates **thrombosed vascular spaces** resulting from preoperative embolization
- Jatin Shah states: preoperative embolization clearly **minimizes the blood loss, facilitates performance of the surgical procedure, and increases the safety** of the operation

## Part D: Complications and Precautions

### Complications

| Complication | Mechanism | Frequency |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Intracranial stroke** | Escape of embolic particles via ECA-ICA anastomoses or flow reversal | Uncommon (~1%) |
| **Cranial nerve palsy** | Vasa nervorum occlusion; tissue oedema | Often temporary |
| **Tumour swelling** | Inflammatory reaction post-embolization | Common; managed with steroids |
| **Pain** | Tumour infarction and oedema | Common |
| **Groin haematoma** | Femoral artery puncture site | Minor |
| **Vessel dissection** | Catheter-related injury | Rare |

Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 states: while uncommon (approximately 1%), complications of embolization include **intracranial stroke and cranial nerve neuropathy** (often temporary, due to tissue oedema or partial occlusion of the vasa nervorum), and for this reason, it is reserved for larger tumours.

Harrison's Chapter 27 highlights: the obvious risk of embolization is the escape of embolizing particles into the cerebral circulation, which can occur through **external-internal carotid anastomoses** or flow reversal from the arterial supply of the tumour.

### Key Dangerous Anastomoses

| Anastomosis | Pathway |
|-------------|---------|
| **Middle meningeal → ophthalmic** | Via meningo-lacrimal artery |
| **Ascending pharyngeal → vertebral** | Via musculospinal branches |
| **Ascending pharyngeal → ICA** | Via caroticotympanic artery |
| **Occipital → vertebral** | Via muscular branches at C1-C2 |

### Precautions

1. **Superselective catheterization only** — avoid proximal embolization
2. **Provocative testing** — consider lidocaine injection to test for cranial nerve supply before embolization
3. **Avoid ICA feeders** — do not embolize internal carotid artery branches
4. **Surgery within 48 hours** — prevents collateral recruitment
5. **Steroid administration** — counters inflammatory tumour reaction
6. **Multidisciplinary team** — experienced interventional neuroradiologist essential

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Preoperative embolization is a standard adjunct to surgery for hypervascular head and neck tumours — primarily paragangliomas and juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibromas
> 2. Primary goals: reduce intraoperative blood loss, shorten operative time, improve visualization, and decrease risk to cranial nerves
> 3. Embolization must be SUPERSELECTIVE — only the artery feeding the tumour is embolized (Stell and Maran's)
> 4. Surgery MUST be performed within 48 hours of embolization to avoid collateral circulation recruitment (Harrison)
> 5. PVA particles are the most commonly used embolic material (80% in JNA); other options include Gelfoam, Onyx, NBCA, and coils
> 6. Complications are uncommon (~1%): intracranial stroke (via ECA-ICA anastomoses) and cranial nerve neuropathy (usually temporary)
> 7. Dangerous ECA-ICA anastomoses must be identified before embolization to prevent cerebral embolization
> 8. Jackson et al. (2015) meta-analysis confirms significantly reduced blood loss and operative time with embolization for carotid body paragangliomas
> 9. Jatin Shah: preoperative embolization clearly minimizes blood loss, facilitates surgery, and increases safety
> 10. Embolization is reserved for LARGER tumours — smaller paragangliomas may not require it

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, know the THREE main indications: paragangliomas, JNA, and hemangiomas — and the key principle of superselective embolization
> - The 48-hour rule is critical: surgery must follow within 48 hours of embolization — delay allows collateral vessel recruitment
> - The most dangerous complication is cerebral embolization via ECA-ICA anastomoses — know the key anastomoses (middle meningeal-ophthalmic, ascending pharyngeal-vertebral)
> - For glomus jugulare: check contralateral jugular venous drainage — hypoplastic/absent contralateral jugular system is a contraindication to surgical sacrifice
> - Even after successful embolization, expect >2 units blood loss for large jugular paragangliomas — always cross-match blood
> - Steroids are administered post-embolization to counter the inflammatory tumour reaction
