## Introduction

Tobacco use is the single most important modifiable risk factor for head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC). The risk for HNSCC in smokers is approximately 10 times higher than in never-smokers, with a dose-response relationship. Approximately 70-80% of new HNSCC diagnoses are associated with tobacco and alcohol use, which have a synergistic (multiplicative) effect on cancer risk. Cigarette smoke contains more than 60 established carcinogens, while unburned (smokeless) tobacco contains at least 16. The devastating link between tobacco products and cancer results from a powerful combination of two factors — nicotine (causing addiction) and carcinogens (causing DNA damage).

## Part A: Mechanism of Tobacco Carcinogenesis

### The Multistep Process

Tobacco carcinogenesis follows a well-defined multistep pathway:

```
Tobacco exposure → Carcinogen uptake → Metabolic activation
→ DNA adduct formation → DNA miscoding/mutations
→ Oncogene activation + Tumour suppressor inactivation
→ Loss of growth control → Invasive carcinoma
```

### Key Carcinogens in Tobacco

There are three major classes of carcinogens relevant to HNSCC:

**1. Tobacco-Specific Nitrosamines (TSNAs)**
- **NNK** (4-(methylnitrosamino)-1-(3-pyridyl)-1-butanone) — the most potent TSNA
- **NNN** (N'-nitrosonornicotine) — present in both smoke and smokeless tobacco
- Formed from nicotine during curing and combustion
- Require metabolic activation by cytochrome P450 enzymes (CYP1A1, CYP2A6)

**2. Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs)**
- **Benzo[a]pyrene** — the prototype PAH carcinogen
- Generated during combustion of organic material
- Activated by CYP1A1/CYP1B1 to form diol epoxides that bind to DNA

**3. Aromatic Amines**
- 4-aminobiphenyl, 2-naphthylamine
- Activated by N-acetyltransferase enzymes
- Particularly implicated in bladder cancer but also contribute to HNSCC

### Metabolic Activation and DNA Adduct Formation

Carcinogens in tobacco are mostly **procarcinogens** — they require metabolic activation to become DNA-damaging agents:

1. **Phase I metabolism (activation):** Cytochrome P450 enzymes (CYP1A1, CYP1B1, CYP2A6) convert procarcinogens to reactive electrophilic metabolites
2. **Phase II metabolism (detoxification):** Glutathione S-transferases (GSTM1, GSTT1) and other conjugating enzymes detoxify these metabolites
3. **DNA adduct formation:** If reactive metabolites escape detoxification, they bind covalently to DNA bases, forming DNA adducts
4. **If unrepaired:** DNA adducts cause miscoding during replication → permanent mutations

### Genetic Susceptibility

Individual variation in carcinogen metabolism explains why not all smokers develop cancer:

| Gene | Function | Risk Association |
|------|----------|-----------------|
| GSTM1 (null) | Reduced detoxification | Increased HNSCC risk |
| GSTT1 (null) | Reduced detoxification | Increased HNSCC risk |
| CYP1A1 variants | Enhanced activation | Increased HNSCC risk |
| EPHX1 variants | Altered metabolism | Associated with HNSCC |
| ALDH2 variants | Impaired alcohol metabolism | Synergistic with tobacco |
| ADH1B/ADH7 variants | Alcohol metabolism | Modifies tobacco-alcohol interaction |

## Part B: Molecular Consequences

### Oncogene Activation
- **EGFR overexpression** — seen in 80-90% of HNSCC; drives proliferation
- **KRAS mutations** — less common in HNSCC than lung cancer
- **Cyclin D1 amplification** — promotes cell cycle entry
- **PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway activation** — promotes survival signaling

### Tumour Suppressor Inactivation
- **TP53 mutations** — the most frequent genetic alteration in tobacco-related HNSCC (60-80%); specific mutation patterns correlate with tobacco use
- **p16/CDKN2A inactivation** — by deletion, mutation, or promoter hypermethylation
- **Rb pathway disruption** — loss of cell cycle control

### Epigenetic Changes
- Promoter hypermethylation of p14, p15, p16 — seen in tobacco/betel-quid chewers
- These epigenetic changes are detectable in precancerous lesions, preceding invasive cancer

## Part C: Field Cancerization

The concept of **field cancerization** was established by Slaughter et al. (1953) in a study of >700 HNSCC cases:

- The grossly normal epithelium adjacent to invasive carcinoma frequently shows dysplasia or carcinoma in situ
- The entire upper aerodigestive tract mucosa is "condemned" by chronic repetitive carcinogen exposure
- Explains the high rate of synchronous and metachronous second primary tumours
- Molecular evidence: identical genetic alterations found in matched dysplastic and malignant lesions (clonal cancerization)
- Some second primaries show distinct genetic profiles — supporting independent initiation events from separate carcinogen exposure

## Part D: Biomarkers of Tobacco Carcinogenesis

### Exposure Biomarkers
- **Cotinine** (nicotine metabolite) — in blood, urine, saliva; best marker of current tobacco exposure
- **NNAL** (NNK metabolite) — urinary NNAL reflects tobacco-specific carcinogen exposure
- **Exhaled carbon monoxide** — quick screening for active smoking

### Effect Biomarkers
- **DNA adducts** — direct measurement of carcinogen-DNA binding; correlate with cancer risk
- **Protein adducts** (haemoglobin/albumin) — reflect cumulative carcinogen exposure
- **Micronuclei frequency** — reflects chromosomal damage; higher in tobacco users

### Susceptibility Biomarkers
- **GSTM1/GSTT1 null genotype** — impaired detoxification capacity
- **CYP polymorphisms** — enhanced carcinogen activation
- **DNA repair capacity** — reduced repair increases mutation accumulation

## Part E: Betel Quid and Smokeless Tobacco

Particularly relevant to the Indian subcontinent:

- **Betel quid** (paan) containing areca nut, slaked lime ± tobacco is an independent risk factor for HNSCC
- Tobacco-free betel quid increases cumulative oral cancer risk by >60%
- **Areca nut** alone is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen (IARC)
- Betel quid has synergistic effects with tobacco and alcohol
- Associated with oral submucous fibrosis — a precancerous condition with malignant transformation rate of 7-13%
- Specific epigenetic changes (p14, p15, p16 hypermethylation) detected in precancerous lesions from betel-quid chewers

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Cigarette smoke contains 60+ carcinogens; the three major classes are TSNAs, PAHs, and aromatic amines
> 2. NNK and benzo[a]pyrene are the most potent tobacco carcinogens relevant to HNSCC
> 3. Carcinogenesis is a multistep process: procarcinogen → metabolic activation → DNA adducts → mutations → cancer
> 4. CYP enzymes activate carcinogens (Phase I); GSTs detoxify them (Phase II) — genetic polymorphisms explain individual susceptibility
> 5. TP53 mutation is the most frequent genetic alteration in tobacco-related HNSCC (60-80%)
> 6. HNSCC risk is 10-fold higher in smokers; tobacco and alcohol have a synergistic (multiplicative) effect
> 7. Field cancerization (Slaughter 1953): entire aerodigestive mucosa is "condemned" by chronic carcinogen exposure
> 8. DNA adducts and urinary NNAL are the most promising biomarkers for carcinogen exposure assessment
> 9. GSTM1/GSTT1 null genotypes confer increased HNSCC risk through impaired carcinogen detoxification
> 10. Betel quid (with or without tobacco) is an independent carcinogen — particularly relevant in India and Southeast Asia

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Tobacco and alcohol are synergistic, not just additive — a patient who both smokes and drinks has a multiplicatively higher risk than either alone
> - TP53 mutation patterns differ between tobacco-related and HPV-related HNSCC — missense mutations in tobacco-users vs. wild-type p53 degraded by HPV E6
> - Field cancerization explains why patients with one HNSCC need lifelong surveillance for second primaries
> - Cotinine levels can objectively verify smoking status — useful when patients underreport tobacco use
> - In India, always ask specifically about betel quid, paan, gutka, and khaini — patients may not consider these as "tobacco use"
> - Smoking cessation even after HNSCC diagnosis improves treatment outcomes, reduces second primary risk, and should be actively supported
