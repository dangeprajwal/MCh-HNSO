## Introduction

The molecular biology of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) has been transformed over the past two decades by extraordinary advances in genomic and proteomic technologies. Where once the molecular understanding of HNSCC was limited to observations about individual genes (such as the overexpression of EGFR first noted in the 1980s, or the frequent mutation of TP53 identified in the 1990s), the advent of next-generation sequencing (NGS) has enabled comprehensive, unbiased characterization of the entire mutational landscape of HNSCC at a resolution that was previously unimaginable. The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) Network's landmark 2015 paper provided the definitive genomic profile of 279 HNSCC cases, revealing the full spectrum of genetic alterations, distinguishing the fundamentally different molecular landscapes of [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-positive]] and HPV-negative disease, and identifying potential therapeutic targets.

In parallel with genomic advances, proteomics — the large-scale study of the complete protein complement of a cell, tissue, or organism — has offered complementary insights that cannot be obtained from DNA or RNA analysis alone. Because proteins are the functional effectors of cellular processes, and because post-translational modifications (phosphorylation, glycosylation, ubiquitination) critically regulate protein function without being detectable at the DNA or mRNA level, proteomic profiling provides a more direct window into the functional state of cancer cells. Proteomic biomarker discovery has been particularly promising in HNSCC because of the accessibility of tumour tissue and body fluids (saliva, blood) for non-invasive sampling.

The identification of recurring molecular alterations — TP53 mutation, CDKN2A inactivation, EGFR overexpression, PIK3CA amplification, CCND1 amplification, and NOTCH1 loss — has provided the foundation for targeted therapies and the emerging paradigm of precision medicine in HNSCC. However, it must be acknowledged that despite the depth of molecular characterization now available, the clinical translation has been relatively limited compared to other tumour types — cetuximab (anti-EGFR) and immune checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1) remain the only molecularly targeted agents with regulatory approval for HNSCC. Understanding the current state of molecular markers, NGS, and proteomics is nonetheless essential for the MCh examination and for appreciating where the field is heading.

## Part A: Key Molecular Markers in HNSCC

### Established Markers

| Marker | Alteration | Frequency | Significance |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| TP53 | Missense mutation | 60-80% (HPV−) | Most common alteration; loss of [[Answers/General/Cell Cycle and Cancer|cell cycle]] control and [[Answers/General/Apoptosis|apoptosis]]; associated with worse prognosis |
| EGFR | Overexpression | 80-90% | Most commonly overexpressed protein; target for cetuximab; associated with poor prognosis |
| CDKN2A (p16) | Deletion/methylation | 50-60% (HPV−) | Loss of CDK4/6 inhibition; overexpressed in HPV+ (surrogate marker) |
| Cyclin D1 (CCND1) | Amplification | 30-40% | Drives G1→S transition; associated with aggressive disease |
| PIK3CA | Mutation/amplification | 20-30% | Activates [[Answers/General/PI3-Kinase Pathway|PI3K/AKT pathway]]/mTOR survival pathway; therapeutic target |
| NOTCH1 | Inactivating mutation | 10-15% | Novel tumour suppressor in HNSCC; discovered via NGS |

### Emerging Markers

- **PD-L1 (Combined Positive Score):** Predicts response to immunotherapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab)
- **Tumour Mutational Burden (TMB):** Higher TMB associated with better immunotherapy response
- **FGFR alterations:** Potential therapeutic target
- **Circulating tumour DNA (ctDNA):** Liquid biopsy for monitoring treatment response and detecting recurrence

## Part B: FISH (Fluorescent In Situ Hybridization)

### Principle

FISH is a cytogenetic technique that uses fluorescent-labelled DNA probes to detect and localize specific DNA sequences on chromosomes within intact cells or tissue sections.

### Steps

1. **Probe design:** Fluorescent-labelled DNA probe complementary to target sequence
2. **Denaturation:** Both probe and target DNA are heated to separate double strands
3. **Hybridization:** Probe binds to complementary target sequence on the chromosome
4. **Washing:** Remove unbound probe
5. **Visualization:** Fluorescence microscopy reveals probe location as coloured signals

### Applications in HNC

| Application | Target | Clinical Use |
|-------------|--------|-------------|
| HPV detection | HPV DNA | Confirmatory test for HPV status |
| EGFR amplification | EGFR gene (7p12) | Predict response to cetuximab |
| Cyclin D1 amplification | CCND1 (11q13) | Prognostic marker |
| p16/CDKN2A deletion | 9p21 | Confirm homozygous deletion |
| HER2 amplification | ERBB2 (17q12) | Salivary duct carcinoma — trastuzumab eligibility |

### Advantages and Limitations

**Advantages:**
- Performed on FFPE tissue sections — no need for fresh tissue
- Maintains tissue architecture — correlates genetic changes with morphology
- Can detect gene amplification, deletion, and translocation
- Can be used on interphase cells (no cell culture needed)

**Limitations:**
- Requires specific probes — cannot discover novel alterations
- Limited to pre-defined targets
- Technical expertise and fluorescence microscopy required
- Signals fade over time (not permanent)

## Part C: Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) in HNSCC

### What is NGS?

NGS (also called massively parallel sequencing) enables simultaneous sequencing of millions of DNA fragments, allowing comprehensive analysis of the entire genome (whole-genome sequencing), all coding regions (whole-exome sequencing), or targeted gene panels.

### Key NGS Findings in HNSCC (TCGA 2015)

The Cancer Genome Atlas comprehensive analysis of 279 HNSCC cases revealed:

**HPV-Negative HNSCC:**
- TP53 mutation: 84%
- CDKN2A inactivation: 57%
- PIK3CA mutation/amplification: 34%
- CCND1 amplification: 31%
- EGFR amplification: 15%
- NOTCH1 mutation: 26%
- Loss of 3p, 8p; gain of 3q, 5p, 8q

**HPV-Positive HNSCC:**
- TP53 wild-type (degraded by E6)
- PIK3CA mutation: 56% (helical domain mutations)
- TRAF3 loss: 22%
- E2F1 amplification: 19%
- Fewer total mutations than HPV−

### Clinical Applications of NGS

| Application | Description |
|-------------|------------|
| Tumour profiling | Identify actionable mutations for targeted therapy |
| TMB calculation | Predict immunotherapy response |
| ctDNA monitoring | Liquid biopsy for minimal residual disease and recurrence |
| HPV integration mapping | Determine HPV integration sites and their biological significance |
| Companion diagnostics | Guide selection of clinical trial eligibility |

## Part D: Proteomics in Head and Neck Cancer

### What is Proteomics?

Proteomics is the large-scale study of the entire complement of proteins (the proteome) expressed by a cell, tissue, or organism. Unlike genomics (which studies DNA) or transcriptomics (which studies mRNA), proteomics directly measures the functional molecules — proteins — that drive cellular processes.

### Proteomic Technologies

| Technology | Principle | Application |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| 2D-PAGE | Protein separation by charge and mass | Differential protein expression |
| Mass spectrometry (MS) | Protein identification by mass/charge ratio | Biomarker discovery, protein identification |
| MALDI-TOF | Matrix-assisted laser ionization + MS | Rapid protein profiling |
| LC-MS/MS | Liquid chromatography + tandem MS | High-throughput biomarker identification |
| Protein [[Answers/General/PCR and Microarray Analysis|microarray]] | Antibody-based high-throughput detection | Screening multiple proteins simultaneously |
| RPPA | Reverse-phase protein array | Quantitative protein expression profiling |

### Proteomic Biomarker Candidates in HNSCC

- **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Salivary Biomarkers in Oral Cancer|salivary biomarkers]]:** IL-8, IL-6, M2BP, MRP14 — potential for non-invasive [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Cancer Screening|oral cancer screening]]
- **Serum biomarkers:** SCC antigen, CYFRA 21-1 — monitoring treatment response
- **Tissue markers:** S100A proteins, annexins, heat shock proteins — diagnostic and prognostic significance
- **Exosomal proteins:** Emerging area — tumour-derived exosomes carry protein signatures detectable in saliva and blood

### Advantages of Proteomics over Genomics

- Proteins are the functional molecules — gene expression does not always correlate with protein levels
- Post-translational modifications (phosphorylation, glycosylation) affect protein function and are not detectable by genomics
- Protein-based biomarkers can be measured in body fluids (saliva, blood) — enabling non-invasive screening

## Part E: Targeted Therapies Based on Molecular Markers

| Target | Drug | Mechanism | Evidence |
|--------|------|-----------|---------|
| EGFR | Cetuximab | Monoclonal antibody blocking EGFR | Bonner 2006: improved OS when added to RT |
| PD-1 | Pembrolizumab, Nivolumab | Immune checkpoint inhibitor | KEYNOTE-048: first-line for R/M HNSCC |
| PD-L1 | CPS score guides treatment | Biomarker for immunotherapy | CPS ≥1 required for pembrolizumab |
| PI3K/mTOR | Alpelisib, Everolimus | PI3K/mTOR pathway inhibition | Under investigation in clinical trials |
| CDK4/6 | Palbociclib, Ribociclib | Cell cycle inhibition | Under investigation in HNSCC |
| FGFR | Various inhibitors | Fibroblast growth factor receptor | Emerging therapeutic target |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. EGFR overexpression (80-90%) is the most consistent molecular marker in HNSCC — target for cetuximab
> 2. TP53 is mutated in 84% of HPV-negative HNSCC — the most frequently mutated gene
> 3. TCGA 2015 provided the definitive genomic landscape: TP53, CDKN2A, PIK3CA, CCND1, NOTCH1 as key drivers
> 4. HPV+ and HPV− HNSCC have fundamentally different genomic profiles
> 5. NOTCH1 was discovered as a novel tumour suppressor in HNSCC through NGS (Agrawal 2011)
> 6. FISH detects specific gene amplifications/deletions on FFPE tissue — used for HPV, EGFR, Cyclin D1, HER2
> 7. NGS enables comprehensive tumour profiling, TMB calculation, and ctDNA monitoring
> 8. Proteomics studies the functional protein landscape — complements genomics by detecting post-translational modifications
> 9. Salivary proteomics offers potential for non-invasive oral cancer screening (IL-8, IL-6, M2BP)
> 10. PD-L1 CPS and TMB are the key biomarkers guiding immunotherapy decisions in HNSCC

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Always request p16 IHC for oropharyngeal SCC and PD-L1 CPS for recurrent/metastatic HNSCC — these are the two biomarkers that directly change treatment decisions
> - NGS panels (e.g., FoundationOne) can identify actionable mutations, but most HNSCC patients currently lack targeted therapy options beyond EGFR and PD-1/PD-L1
> - FISH is more specific than IHC for detecting true gene amplification — but IHC is faster and cheaper for screening
> - Liquid biopsy (ctDNA) is emerging as a powerful tool for monitoring minimal residual disease after treatment — watch this space
> - Cetuximab works regardless of EGFR expression level — the biomarker for EGFR-targeted therapy is not yet optimized
> - In salivary duct carcinoma, always check HER2 by FISH — trastuzumab may be indicated if amplified
