## Introduction

Xerostomia — the subjective sensation of oral dryness resulting from salivary gland hypofunction — is the most common long-term complaint following radiotherapy for head and neck cancer. It profoundly impairs quality of life through its effects on eating, [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]], speech, taste, dental health, and sleep. Normal daily saliva production is 0.5-1.5 litres, produced predominantly (90%) by the three pairs of major salivary glands: parotid, submandibular, and sublingual, with the remainder from the [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Minor Salivary Gland Tumours of the Oral Cavity|minor salivary gland tumours]] distributed throughout the oral mucosa.

As described in Montgomery's Principles and Practice (Chapter 4), the position and size of the parotid gland means that it is frequently included in the radiation treatment field for HNSCC. The serous acini, which produce watery saliva, are more sensitive to the effects of radiation than the mucous acini — resulting in the production of thick, tenacious saliva. The decrease in the quantity and quality of saliva may lead to a change in oral flora and accelerated dental caries. Salivary gland damage from radiation is dose-dependent: significant reduction in salivary output occurs with doses as low as 10-15 Gy, and doses >40 Gy to a major salivary gland typically result in permanent, irreversible hypofunction.

The management of xerostomia encompasses three approaches: **prevention** (minimizing salivary gland dose through radiation technique), **pharmacological stimulation** (cholinergic agonists, cytoprotective agents), and **symptomatic relief** (saliva substitutes, oral care). Current strategies also include surgical approaches such as salivary gland transfer.

## Part A: Radiation-Related Prevention Strategies

### 1. Intensity-Modulated Radiation Therapy (IMRT) — Parotid Sparing

IMRT is the single most important advance in xerostomia prevention. By generating concavities in the dose distribution, IMRT permits significant dose reduction to the parotid glands while maintaining full therapeutic dose to the tumour and at-risk nodal volumes. As described in Harrison's Chapter 5, efforts to reduce xerostomia through IMRT have concentrated on sparing the contralateral parotid gland, which is often not in the primary tumour volume.

**Key evidence:**

The **PARSPORT trial** (Nutting et al., Lancet Oncology 2011) was the first phase III multicentre RCT comparing parotid-sparing IMRT to conventional RT in oropharyngeal and hypopharyngeal cancer:
- Grade ≥2 xerostomia at 12 months: **38% (IMRT) vs. 74% (conventional RT)** — p<0.001
- Significant improvement in patient-reported xerostomia and dry mouth-specific QOL
- No difference in locoregional control between IMRT and conventional RT

**Dose-volume thresholds:**

| Structure | Constraint | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| Contralateral parotid | Mean dose < 25-26 Gy | Salivary recovery possible (Deasy et al., 2010) |
| At least one parotid | Mean dose < 20 Gy | Best chance of salivary preservation |
| Submandibular glands | Mean dose < 35 Gy | Contributes to resting saliva; sparing improves overall salivary function |

Harrison notes that the correlation between salivary output from major salivary glands and patient-reported xerostomia symptoms is significant but not very high — partly because the minor salivary glands (distributed throughout the oral mucosa and not specifically spared by IMRT) make an important contribution to mucosal lubrication. The oral cavity mucosal dose was found to be an independent factor in patients' reported xerostomia after IMRT.

**Recovery over time:** University of Michigan data demonstrated that patient-reported xerostomia improved significantly over 2 years following parotid-sparing IMRT. By 2 years following irradiation, xerostomia reported by patients receiving parotid-sparing bilateral neck radiation was only slightly worse than that reported by patients receiving unilateral RT only.

### 2. Submandibular Gland Transfer (Surgical Prevention)

Surgical transfer of the contralateral submandibular gland to the submental space — outside the planned radiation field — preserves a functioning major salivary gland. This procedure is performed at the time of neck dissection, before adjuvant radiotherapy. Evidence from multiple series shows significant preservation of stimulated and unstimulated salivary flow rates compared to non-transfer controls. However, it is only applicable when:
- The contralateral submandibular gland is not involved by tumour
- The submental region can be excluded from the RT field
- The patient is planned for [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Oral Cancer|adjuvant RT]] (not primary RT)

### 3. Proton Beam Therapy

Proton therapy offers superior dose conformality to IMRT through the Bragg peak phenomenon, potentially further reducing salivary gland doses. Early clinical data suggest reduced xerostomia rates compared to photon IMRT, but large-scale prospective comparative studies are ongoing.

## Part B: Pharmacological Prevention

### 1. Amifostine (Ethyol)

As described in Montgomery's textbook, amifostine is a free radical scavenger that accumulates preferentially in salivary tissue. It has been shown to reduce both acute and chronic xerostomia without adversely affecting the anti-tumour effects of radiotherapy.

**Pivotal trial (Brizel et al., 2000):** In a randomized trial of 315 HNC patients receiving RT:
- Acute grade ≥2 xerostomia: **51% (amifostine) vs. 78% (control)** — p<0.001
- 1-year grade ≥2 xerostomia: **35% vs. 57%** — p=0.002
- No difference in tumour control or survival
- Recommended dose: 200 mg/m² IV 15-30 minutes before each RT fraction

**Limitations:** Amifostine is expensive, causes nausea, vomiting, and hypotension, and rarely allergic reactions. These side effects, combined with the logistical burden of daily IV administration before each RT fraction, have limited its routine adoption — particularly since IMRT (which achieves similar or better xerostomia reduction without pharmacological side effects) has become standard.

## Part C: Pharmacological Treatment (Symptomatic)

### 1. Pilocarpine

A muscarinic (cholinergic) agonist that stimulates residual salivary gland function. As noted in Montgomery's textbook, oral pilocarpine can improve xerostomia symptoms, although unpleasant cholinergic side effects (visual disturbance, bowel disturbance, bladder disturbance, sweating) are often limiting.

- **Dose:** 5 mg orally three times daily, increased to 10 mg TDS if tolerated
- **Onset:** Takes 8-12 weeks for maximal effect — patients must be counselled to persist
- **Requirement:** Residual functioning salivary tissue must be present — pilocarpine cannot stimulate glands that have been destroyed by high-dose radiation
- **Contraindications:** Uncontrolled asthma, narrow-angle glaucoma, urinary obstruction

### 2. Cevimeline

Another muscarinic agonist with higher M3 receptor selectivity than pilocarpine — fewer GI side effects but similar efficacy. Dose: 30 mg orally three times daily.

### 3. Saliva Substitutes and Oral Moisturizers

- **Artificial saliva sprays/gels:** Carboxymethylcellulose-based or mucin-based preparations
- **Regular sipping of water:** The simplest and most effective symptomatic measure
- **Biotene products:** Enzyme-based oral rinses (lactoperoxidase, lysozyme, glucose oxidase)
- **Sugar-free gum/lozenges:** Stimulate residual gland function through mastication

### 4. Acupuncture

Evidence from multiple small trials and systematic reviews suggests that acupuncture may improve subjective xerostomia symptoms and stimulated salivary flow rates. The mechanism may involve parasympathetic nervous system stimulation and neuropeptide-mediated vasodilation in salivary glands. While the evidence base is growing, acupuncture is considered a complementary approach rather than a primary treatment.

## Part D: Investigational Strategies

| Strategy | Mechanism | Status |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Salivary gland stem cell therapy | Transplant of salivary gland stem/progenitor cells to regenerate damaged glands | Preclinical/early clinical |
| Gene therapy (aquaporin-1) | Adenoviral vector delivers AQP1 gene to irradiated glands → restores water transport | Phase I clinical trial completed |
| Botulinum toxin | Injection into parotid glands to reduce sialorrhoea (opposite indication); being explored for redirecting salivary flow | Experimental |
| Low-level laser therapy (LLLT) | Photobiomodulation of salivary gland tissue → stimulates regeneration | Small clinical trials |
| Salivary gland organoids | In vitro culture and transplantation of salivary gland organoids | Preclinical |

## Part E: Dental Consequences and Oral Care

Xerostomia dramatically increases the risk of dental caries (radiation caries), periodontal disease, oral candidiasis, and [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Osteoradionecrosis of the Mandible|osteoradionecrosis]]. All patients receiving head and neck RT should:

- Undergo comprehensive dental evaluation and extraction of unsalvageable teeth BEFORE RT
- Use daily fluoride trays (1% sodium fluoride gel) — lifelong
- Maintain meticulous oral hygiene with soft brush, fluoride toothpaste
- Be treated for oral candidiasis promptly (nystatin or fluconazole)
- Avoid sugar-containing foods and drinks
- Have regular dental review (every 3-6 months) for life

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Xerostomia is the most common chronic complaint after HNC radiotherapy — profoundly impairs QOL
> 2. IMRT with parotid sparing is the most effective prevention: PARSPORT showed 38% vs 74% grade ≥2 xerostomia at 12 months
> 3. Critical dose constraint: contralateral parotid mean dose < 25-26 Gy for salivary recovery
> 4. Serous acini (watery saliva) are more radiosensitive than mucous acini (thick saliva)
> 5. Amifostine reduces xerostomia but is limited by cost, side effects, and logistical burden
> 6. Pilocarpine 5-10 mg TDS stimulates residual gland function — takes 8-12 weeks for maximal effect
> 7. Submandibular gland transfer to the submental space preserves a functioning gland outside the RT field
> 8. Minor salivary glands contribute to mucosal lubrication — not specifically spared by IMRT
> 9. Salivary recovery after IMRT continues to improve over 2 years post-treatment
> 10. Lifelong fluoride tray use is mandatory to prevent radiation caries — dental extraction after RT risks ORN

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Always discuss parotid-sparing IMRT constraints with the radiation oncologist at the treatment planning stage — not after the plan is finalized
> - Pilocarpine only works if there is residual salivary gland tissue — it is useless if both parotids received >40 Gy
> - The submandibular gland contributes 70% of resting (unstimulated) saliva — sparing or transferring it may be more important than parotid sparing for baseline oral comfort
> - Patients should sip water regularly throughout the day and keep a water bottle at the bedside — this simple measure provides more relief than most pharmacological interventions
> - Pre-RT dental extractions must be completed at least 2-3 weeks before RT starts — extracting teeth DURING or AFTER RT risks osteoradionecrosis
> - Gene therapy with aquaporin-1 is the most promising investigational approach — a phase I trial has been completed with encouraging results
