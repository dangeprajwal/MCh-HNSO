## Introduction

LASER (Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation) technology has revolutionized the surgical management of head and neck cancers, particularly through transoral laser microsurgery (TLM). Since Strong and Jako's pioneering use of the CO2 laser in 1972, and Steiner's expansion to malignant disease in the 1980s, TLM has become established as a primary treatment modality for early and selected advanced laryngeal, hypopharyngeal, and oropharyngeal cancers. Stell and Maran's Chapter 7 notes that for early glottic cancer, rates of local control, voice preservation, and survival with transoral laser excision appear **equivalent to radiotherapy and open partial laryngectomy**. Harrison Chapter 6 describes the birth of endoscopic head and neck surgery (eHNS) in 1972 when Strong and Jako used the CO2 laser for benign and early limited cancers, with Steiner and Ambrosch later expanding indications throughout the upper aerodigestive tract.

## Part A: Principles of Laser Therapy in Head and Neck Cancer

### Basic Laser Physics

Harrison Chapter 6 provides the physics of laser technology relevant to head and neck surgery:

- **Laser Principle:** A laser produces a collimated, coherent, monochromatic beam of light at a specific wavelength, determined by the lasing medium
- **Tissue Interaction:** The wavelength determines the chromophore (target molecule) — for the CO2 laser (wavelength 10,600 nm), the chromophore is **water** in tissue cells
- **Mechanism:** Water absorbs the laser energy, cells vaporize at 100 degrees C, enabling precise incision with simultaneous coagulation of small vessels
- **Pulsed Mode:** Reduces thermal injury to surrounding tissue by allowing heat dissipation between pulses. The repeat-delay setting (0.1-0.3 seconds) further reduces collateral thermal damage
- **Spot Size:** Small focal diameter enables minimal carbonization, low infection, reduced swelling, and minimal scarring (Stell Ch7)

### Principles of TLM (Transoral Laser Microsurgery)

Stell and Maran's Chapter 8 and Harrison Chapter 6 describe the revolutionary philosophy introduced by **Steiner and Ambrosch**:

1. **Transtumoral Resection:** Unlike conventional en bloc oncological resection, TLM deliberately cuts through the tumour — this allows visualization of the cut surface to delineate tumour depth and individually adjust the safety margin
2. **Blockwise (Piecemeal) Resection:** Tumours removed in planned pieces, enabling resection from anatomically inaccessible areas while preserving uninvolved tissue
3. **Margin Assessment:** Up to 8 marginal biopsies taken from the resection bed (Scott-Brown Ch13: superior, anterosuperior, posterosuperior, superior deep, inferior, anteroinferior, posteroinferior, inferior deep)
4. **Function Preservation:** Resection governed by the specific anatomy of each individual tumour, minimizing removal of uninvolved tissue to preserve function
5. **Steiner's Claim:** Laser seals lymphatics during cutting, theoretically preventing tumour cell dissemination (Stell Ch8)

### Advantages of TLM

Stell and Maran's Chapter 8 (Box 8.4) compares TLM to both open surgery and radiotherapy:

| TLM vs Open Surgery | TLM vs Radiotherapy |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Outpatient possible | Similar oncologic results |
| Shorter operating time | Shorter treatment time |
| Less overtreatment | Lower costs |
| Better voice quality | Repeatable procedure |
| Lower morbidity | Leaves RT as salvage option |
| No feeding tube needed | No cumulative dose limit |
| No tracheostomy needed | Pathological staging possible |
| Similar oncologic results | Immediate margin assessment |

### Laser Safety

Montgomery Chapter 6 details the safety protocols for laser surgery:

- **Laser-Resistant Tubes:** Mallinckrodt Laserflex (metal) or Xomed Lasershield (metallic-coated silicone)
- **Cuff Protection:** Inflate with **saline + methylene blue** (blue dye signals cuff breach)
- **Avoid N2O:** Supports combustion — use air/oxygen mixture
- **Low FiO2:** Maintain the lowest possible inspired oxygen fraction
- **Fire Triad:** Combustible material + oxidizing agent + ignition source — all three must be controlled
- **Jet Ventilation:** Ideal for laser microsurgery (best surgical access) but carries risks of difficult CO2 monitoring and barotrauma; not suitable for obese patients
- **Eye Protection:** Appropriate wavelength-specific goggles for all operating room personnel

## Part B: Types of Lasers in Head and Neck Cancer

Harrison Chapter 6 describes three laser types used in transoral laser microsurgery:

| Laser Type | Wavelength | Chromophore | Key Properties | Primary Use |
|------------|-----------|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| CO2 | 10,600 nm | Water | Precise cutting, minimal thermal spread, absorbed superficially | Gold standard for TLM — larynx, oropharynx, hypopharynx, oral cavity |
| KTP (Potassium Titanyl Phosphate) | 532 nm | Haemoglobin | Excellent haemostasis, fibre-deliverable, green light | Vascular lesions, photocoagulation, angiolytic treatment of vocal fold lesions |
| Thulium | 2,013 nm | Water | Similar to CO2 but fibre-deliverable, smooth vaporization, excellent haemostasis | Emerging alternative to CO2 — delivered via small glass fibre |

### Additional Laser Types in Oncology

| Laser | Wavelength | Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Nd:YAG | 1,064 nm | Deep tissue coagulation, debulking obstructive tumours, palliative airway management |
| Diode | 800-980 nm | Soft tissue surgery, interstitial photocoagulation |
| PDT Lasers | Variable (630-690 nm) | [[Answers/General/Photodynamic Therapy|photodynamic therapy]] — activate photosensitizers for superficial mucosal cancers |

### Technological Advances

- **OmniGuide Fibre-Optic CO2 Laser:** Overcomes the line-of-sight limitation of conventional CO2 laser, allowing "around-corners" work (Harrison Ch6)
- **Scanning Robotic Micromanipulator:** Remacle et al. introduced the "digital acublade" that reduces the thermal injury zone to just **12.5 micrometers** (Harrison Ch6)
- **HeNe Aiming Beam:** The CO2 laser beam is invisible; a visible helium-neon laser provides the aiming guide

## Part C: Role of CO2 Laser in Laryngopharyngeal Cancers

### ELS Classification of Endoscopic Cordectomies

Scott-Brown's Chapter 14 (Table 14.4) provides the **European Laryngological Society classification** (Remacle et al. 2000), which is essential for standardized reporting:

| ELS Type | Name | Depth of Resection |
|----------|------|-------------------|
| Type I | Subepithelial | Epithelium through superficial lamina propria |
| Type II | Subligamental | Epithelium, Reinke's space, vocal ligament |
| Type III | Transmuscular | Through vocalis muscle |
| Type IV | Total cordectomy | Vocal process to anterior commissure, depth to internal perichondrium |
| Type Va | Extended | Includes contralateral vocal fold and anterior commissure |
| Type Vb | Extended | Includes arytenoid |
| Type Vc | Extended | Includes subglottis |
| Type Vd | Extended | Includes ventricle and false cords |
| Type VI | Anterior commissurectomy | Bilateral anterior cordectomy with subglottic mucosa |

### CO2 Laser Technique for Laryngeal Cancer

Jatin Shah Chapter 10 describes the detailed surgical technique:

- **Setup:** Laser-safe double-cuff endotracheal tube, CO2 laser coupled to operating microscope with micromanipulator, 0/30/70-degree telescopes for visualization
- **Power:** Continuous mode at **3-5 Watts** for vocal cord lesions
- **Small Lesions:** Excised en bloc with adequate margin
- **Larger Lesions:** Stepwise segmental excision — initial exploratory incision through tumour centre to assess depth (Steiner technique)
- **Healing:** Defect heals by secondary intention
- **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Leukoplakia Management|leukoplakia]]:** CO2 laser with handheld piece, 15W continuous beam, 1-2 mm tissue destruction depth; haemostasis by defocusing the laser beam (Jatin Shah Ch8)

### Oncologic Outcomes by Subsite

#### Glottic Cancer (Stell Ch8)

| Stage | n (Steiner Series) | 5-Year Local Control | Larynx Preservation |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|-------------------|
| T1a | 158 | 96% | 97.6-99% |
| T1b | 30 | 85% | 97.6-99% |
| T2a | 129 | 84% | 86-96% |
| T2b | 115 | 70% | 86-96% |
| T3 | 95 | 60% (RFS) | — |

- **T1a Lesions:** Laser surgery ideally suited — 96% local control (Stell Ch8). Jatin Shah Ch10 confirms endoscopic resection is ideal for lesions confined to mucosa of one vocal cord without anterior commissure involvement
- **Anterior Commissure:** Stell Ch7 warns that anterior commissure involvement yields inferior local control and voice quality with laser
- **T1b:** Jatin Shah notes T1b lesions are generally not suitable for endoscopic resection
- **T3 Glottic:** Steiner's series of 95 patients showed 5-year overall survival 58%, recurrence-free survival 60% — comparable to chemoradiotherapy (Stell Ch7: survival 50-65% for T3)

#### Supraglottic Cancer (Stell Ch8)

| Stage | n (Steiner Series) | 5-Year Local Control | Larynx Preservation |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|-------------------|
| T1 | 23 | 95% | 96-99% |
| T2 | 72 | 85% | 96-99% |
| T3 | 76 | 79% | 84-95% |
| T4 | 45 | 69% | 84-95% |

- Results comparable to open supraglottic laryngectomy but with **no clinically relevant aspiration** — superior functional outcomes (Stell Ch8)
- Ambrosch (2007) confirmed TLM achieves functional larynx preservation in 70-80% of cases across all stages

#### Hypopharyngeal Cancer (Montgomery Ch13, Stell Ch8)

Stell Chapter 8 notes that transoral laser proved "surprisingly effective" for hypopharyngeal cancer:

- **Steiner's Piriform Sinus Series (n=129):** 87% local control, 5-year OS 71% (stage I/II), 47% (stage III/IV), **99% organ preservation rate**
- **Functional Results:** 73% required feeding tube for median 7 days only — excellent [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]] outcomes
- No patient required permanent laryngectomy; only 4 temporary tracheostomies
- Montgomery Ch13 confirms criteria for patient selection: freely mobile true cords and arytenoids, no apex involvement, no thyroid cartilage invasion, no postcricoid involvement

### TLM vs Radiotherapy for Early Glottic Cancer

Scott-Brown's Chapter 14 provides the most comprehensive comparison:

- **UK EaStER Trial:** Attempted to randomize TLM vs RT for T1 glottic cancer but **failed to recruit** — patients preferred TLM (day-case, rapid recovery, minimal impact on swallowing/voice)
- **SEER Database (n=10,429):** "Local surgery only" and "local surgery + radiation" had **lower hazards of death** than "radiation only" (5-year DSS 91% and 90% vs 83% for RT only)
- **Health Economics:** TLM dominates RT — cheaper (GBP 623 vs GBP 2,654) and more effective (higher QALYs) over a 10-year horizon. In the American system, RT was **15.5 times more costly**
- **Salvage After Recurrence:** At least 25-30% of T2 patients recur after RT, and most require **total laryngectomy** for salvage. TLM recurrence rate is 7-15%, and all salvage options (re-excision, RT, or open surgery) remain available
- **Post-RT Salvage:** Laser resection achieves 60-70% local control rates in the salvage setting (Stell Ch8)

### Complications of TLM

Montgomery Chapter 14 reports complication rates:

| Complication | Incidence |
|-------------|-----------|
| Haemorrhage | 2.6-8% |
| Pneumonia | 0-11.5% |
| Cervical emphysema | 1% |
| Perichondritis | 0.7% |
| Laryngeal synechiae | 0-8.3% |
| Airway ignition | 0.3% |

Complications are more common with extensive tumours, less experienced surgeons, and patients with diabetes.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The CO2 laser (10,600 nm, water chromophore) is the gold standard for transoral laser microsurgery — introduced by Strong and Jako (1972), expanded to malignancy by Steiner (1980s)
> 2. The Steiner-Ambrosch philosophy of transtumoral, piecemeal resection challenges en bloc dogma but is oncologically safe — deliberately cutting through tumour to optimize margin assessment
> 3. The ELS classification (Remacle 2000, Types I-VI) standardizes cordectomy extent from subepithelial (Type I) to anterior commissurectomy (Type VI)
> 4. For T1a glottic cancer, TLM achieves 96% local control with 97-99% larynx preservation; for T3, 5-year OS is 58% — comparable to chemoradiotherapy
> 5. TLM is cost-effective, preserves all future treatment options, and in the SEER analysis (n=10,429) showed superior disease-specific survival vs RT alone

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Anterior Commissure Involvement:** Laser gives inferior local control and voice quality — consider RT preferentially (Stell Ch7)
> - **T1b Glottic:** Generally not suitable for endoscopic laser resection — bilateral disease limits functional outcomes (Jatin Shah Ch10)
> - **Key TLM Advantage:** If recurrence occurs after TLM, ALL treatment options remain open (re-excision, RT, open surgery); after primary RT, salvage often requires total laryngectomy
> - **Laser Safety Essentials:** Saline + methylene blue in cuff, laser-safe tube, avoid N2O, lowest FiO2 — the fire triad must always be controlled (Montgomery Ch6)
> - **Hypopharynx Surprise:** Steiner's TLM for piriform sinus SCC achieved 99% organ preservation and 87% local control — but strict selection criteria are essential (freely mobile cords, no cartilage invasion)
