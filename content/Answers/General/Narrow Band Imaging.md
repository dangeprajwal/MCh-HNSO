## Introduction

Narrow band imaging (NBI) is a novel endoscopic optical imaging technique that enhances visualization of mucosal and submucosal microvascular patterns without the use of dyes or contrast agents. By filtering white light into specific narrow-band wavelengths, NBI dramatically improves the contrast between blood-containing vessels and surrounding mucosa, enabling identification of neoangiogenic patterns associated with precancerous and malignant lesions. In head and neck oncology, NBI has emerged as a powerful tool for early detection of superficial squamous cell carcinoma, assessment of surgical margins, surveillance after treatment, and detection of unknown primaries in cervical lymph node metastasis.

---

## Part A: Physical Basis of NBI

### Principle

NBI technology is based on the knowledge that the depth of light penetration into tissue is determined by its wavelength, and that haemoglobin selectively absorbs certain wavelengths. By using special optical filters, white light is narrowed to two specific wavelengths:

- **Blue light (415 nm):** Penetrates only the superficial mucosal layers and is maximally absorbed by haemoglobin in superficial capillaries — highlights **intrapapillary capillary loops (IPCLs)**
- **Green light (540 nm):** Penetrates deeper into the submucosa and highlights **subepithelial venules and arterioles**

In combination, these two narrow-band wavelengths produce a very high-contrast image of the tissue surface that is further enhanced when used with high-definition television (HDTV) cameras.

### What NBI Reveals

Normal mucosa shows a regular, homogeneous vascular pattern. Neoplastic transformation is accompanied by **neoangiogenesis**, producing characteristic abnormal microvascular patterns:

- **Intrapapillary capillary loops (IPCLs):** Abnormal, dilated, tortuous capillary loops running perpendicular to the mucosal surface — the hallmark finding in dysplasia and carcinoma
- **Brown dots/spots:** Superficial IPCL changes visible with blue light
- **Thickened subepithelial vessels:** Irregular deeper vasculature visible with green light

---

## Part B: IPCL Classification (Ni Classification)

Ni et al. (2011) proposed the first classification system for laryngeal IPCL patterns observed with NBI:

| Type | Vascular Pattern | Pathological Correlation |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|
| I | Thin, oblique, arborescent vessels | Normal mucosa |
| II | Enlarged, tortuous loops | Inflammatory/benign |
| III | Irregular, dilated IPCLs | Low-grade dysplasia |
| IV | Small dot-like IPCLs | Moderate dysplasia |
| Va | Solid/hollow brownish speckles | Severe dysplasia/CIS |
| Vb | Irregular, tortuous, destructive IPCLs | Microinvasive carcinoma |
| Vc | Brownish spots with irregular surface | Invasive carcinoma |

**Key diagnostic threshold:** Types I-IV are considered non-malignant; Type V (Va, Vb, Vc) indicates malignancy.

- For **Type Va** (severe dysplasia/CIS): NBI sensitivity 100%, specificity 79.5%
- For **Types Vb-Vc** (invasive carcinoma): NBI sensitivity 83.8%, specificity 100%

The European Laryngological Society (ELS) subsequently proposed a simplified classification distinguishing **longitudinal vascular changes (LVC)** from **perpendicular vascular changes (PVC)**, where PVC represent IPCLs and are associated with neoplastic stimulation.

## Part C: Clinical Applications in Head and Neck Oncology

### 1. Laryngeal Cancer

NBI has achieved its greatest clinical impact in laryngeal oncology:

- **Sensitivity 89%, specificity 93%** for diagnosis of primary laryngeal lesions
- Superior performance in post-radiation/chemoradiation surveillance, reducing the need for tissue biopsy
- Helps distinguish between benign scarring and recurrent tumour after treatment
- Improves assessment of superficial tumour extent and margins during transoral laser microsurgery

### 2. Oral Cancer

NBI enhances detection of:
- Early oral mucosal lesions that are invisible under white light
- Mucosal margins during surgery
- Field cancerization — multifocal premalignant changes

### 3. Unknown Primary Detection

NBI has proven particularly valuable for detecting occult primary tumours in patients presenting with cervical lymph node metastasis from an unknown primary:
- A meta-analysis of studies showed NBI identified additional primaries missed by conventional endoscopy
- Recommended during panendoscopy in conjunction with bilateral tonsillectomy and tongue base mucosectomy

### 4. Post-Treatment Surveillance

After surgery or radiotherapy, distinguishing recurrence from post-treatment changes (scarring, inflammation, oedema) is challenging with white light. NBI significantly improves:
- Detection of superficial recurrence in previously irradiated fields
- Differentiation of neovascularization (recurrence) from fibrosis (scar)
- Higher values of sensitivity/specificity in the post-treatment setting compared to primary diagnosis

### 5. Meta-Analysis Evidence

A systematic review and meta-analysis (Towns et al., 2020) of NBI in laryngeal cancer showed:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Pooled sensitivity | 88.5% |
| Pooled specificity | 95.6% |
| Positive likelihood ratio | 12.33 |
| Negative likelihood ratio | 0.11 |
| Diagnostic odds ratio | 121.26 |
| AUC (SROC curve) | 96.94% |

## Part D: Advantages and Limitations

### Advantages

- No dyes or contrast agents needed — simply a filter switch on the endoscope
- Real-time imaging — immediate visual feedback during endoscopy
- NBI mode is a standard feature on modern videoendoscopes (Olympus systems)
- Can be used in outpatient clinic (flexible NBI nasopharyngoscope) or in the operating room (rigid endoscopy)
- Repeatable — no cumulative effects
- Enhances margin assessment during transoral laser surgery

### Limitations

- **Learning curve:** Requires training to recognize aberrant microvascular patterns and IPCL classification
- **Superficial depth of penetration:** Cannot assess deep tumour invasion — limited to mucosal/submucosal assessment
- **False positives:** Granulation tissue, inflammation, and scarring can mimic neoplastic vascular patterns
- **Equipment requirement:** Requires NBI-compatible endoscopes (Olympus EVIS EXERA series)
- **Inadequate in keratinized lesions:** Thick leukoplakia obscures the underlying vasculature

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. NBI uses filtered blue (415 nm) and green (540 nm) light to enhance mucosal vascular patterns without dyes
> 2. Haemoglobin absorption of narrow-band light creates high-contrast images of IPCLs and subepithelial vessels
> 3. Ni classification (Types I-V) provides a standardized IPCL grading system; Type V indicates malignancy
> 4. NBI sensitivity 88.5%, specificity 95.6% for laryngeal cancer diagnosis (meta-analysis evidence)
> 5. Muto et al. RCT demonstrated 100% sensitivity for superficial HN SCC — significantly superior to white light
> 6. Clinical applications: early detection, margin assessment, unknown primary detection, post-RT surveillance
> 7. NBI is particularly valuable in post-treatment surveillance — differentiates recurrence from scar
> 8. NBI mode is a standard feature on modern Olympus videoendoscopes — no additional equipment needed
> 9. Main limitations: learning curve, superficial depth only, false positives from inflammation/scarring
> 10. NBI is recommended during panendoscopy for unknown primaries alongside bilateral tonsillectomy

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - NBI is most useful for flat, superficial lesions — it does not replace tissue biopsy for definitive diagnosis
> - In the post-RT larynx, NBI significantly reduces unnecessary biopsies by distinguishing recurrence from fibrosis
> - The Ni classification Type V threshold (Va/Vb/Vc) has the highest clinical utility — learn to recognize it
> - Combine NBI with HDTV for maximum resolution — standard definition cameras diminish the benefit
> - NBI cannot penetrate through thick leukoplakia — if the surface is heavily keratinized, biopsy regardless
> - For unknown primaries, always perform NBI-guided panendoscopy before committing to radiotherapy fields
