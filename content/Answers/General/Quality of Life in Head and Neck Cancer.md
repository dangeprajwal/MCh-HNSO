> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> The concept of systematically measuring QOL in head and neck cancer was formalized through several foundational instruments developed in the 1990s. Hassan and Weymuller (1993) created the University of Washington QOL scale (UW-QOL), a concise disease-specific tool preferred by 97% of patients over longer questionnaires. Cella et al. (1993) validated the FACT-G general cancer QOL measure, which became the parent instrument for the FACT-HN head and neck module. The EORTC QLQ-H&N35, validated by Bjordal et al. (1999) in a 500-patient multinational study, became the most widely used QOL instrument in HNC clinical trials. List et al. (1996) developed the clinician-rated Performance Status Scale (PSS-HN) covering diet, speech, and public eating domains. More recently, Laraway and Rogers (2012) provided a structured review of UW-QOL outcomes across published studies, confirming it as one of the two most frequently reported QOL instruments in HNC research alongside the EORTC QLQ-H&N35.

## Introduction

Quality of life (QOL) in head and neck cancer encompasses the physical, functional, psychological, and social well-being of patients before, during, and after treatment. Head and neck cancers and their treatment uniquely affect fundamental human functions — breathing, speaking, [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]], chewing, and facial appearance — making QOL assessment particularly important in this population. Treatment decisions in HNC increasingly require balancing oncologic outcomes with preservation of function and QOL.

**Health-Related Quality of Life (HRQOL)** is a multidimensional concept that specifically measures how disease and its treatment affect a patient's perceived well-being across physical, psychological, social, and functional domains. Unlike generic QOL (which includes financial, spiritual, and environmental factors), HRQOL focuses on health-specific domains measurable through validated instruments.

---

## Part A: Domains of QOL Assessment

QOL assessment in HNC typically covers four core domains:

1. **Physical well-being:** Pain, fatigue, nausea, appetite, sleep disturbance
2. **Functional well-being:** Swallowing, speech, chewing, shoulder function, diet normalcy
3. **Emotional/psychological well-being:** Anxiety, depression, distress, body image, fear of recurrence
4. **Social well-being:** Social contact, family relationships, employment, eating in public, sexuality

### Head and Neck-Specific Functional Domains

HNC QOL instruments additionally assess site-specific concerns:

- **Swallowing and diet** — ability to eat normal diet, aspiration risk
- **Speech and communication** — understandability, voice quality
- **Appearance and disfigurement** — facial form, scarring
- **[[Answers/General/Xerostomia Management|xerostomia]] and taste** — salivary function post-RT
- **Shoulder function** — post-neck dissection morbidity
- **[[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]]** — mouth opening limitation
- **Social eating** — willingness and ability to eat with others

## Part B: Major QOL Instruments in Head and Neck Cancer

### 1. EORTC QLQ-C30 + QLQ-H&N35

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Developer | European Organisation for Research and Treatment of Cancer |
| Structure | QLQ-C30 (30 items, 5 functional + 3 symptom scales + global QOL) + H&N35 module (35 items, 7 scales + 11 single items) |
| Administration | Self-reported; takes 10-15 minutes |
| H&N35 scales | Pain, swallowing, senses, speech, social eating, social contact, sexuality |
| Strengths | Most widely validated; used in 244+ studies; multinational validation in 500 patients; sensitive to site, stage, and treatment changes |
| Limitations | Lengthy (65 items total); low internal consistency for speech and senses scales; requires licensing |

The EORTC QLQ-H&N35 has been updated to the **QLQ-HN43** (43 items), validated in 812 patients from 28 institutions across 18 countries.

### 2. University of Washington QOL (UW-QOL)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Developer | Hassan and Weymuller (1993), University of Washington |
| Structure | 12 domains scored 0-100 each + 3 global questions |
| Domains | Pain, appearance, activity, recreation, swallowing, chewing, speech, shoulder, taste, saliva, mood, anxiety |
| Administration | Self-reported; takes 5-10 minutes |
| Strengths | Concise; 97% patient preference; criterion validity 0.849; excellent responsiveness to change; free to use |
| Limitations | Less comprehensive than EORTC; fewer psychosocial items; ceiling effects in some domains |

The UW-QOL is the most commonly used QOL instrument in clinical practice due to its brevity. It has been used in 142+ published studies.

### 3. FACT-G + FACT-HN

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Developer | Cella et al. (1993), FACIT.org |
| Structure | FACT-G (27 items, 4 domains) + HN subscale (12 additional items) = 39 items total |
| Domains | Physical, social/family, emotional, functional well-being + HN-specific items (eating, speech, appearance) |
| Administration | Self-reported; takes 10-15 minutes |
| Strengths | Well-validated; 4-domain structure; available in multiple languages; used in 86+ HNC studies |
| Limitations | Requires licensing; HN module less granular than EORTC H&N35 for site-specific symptoms |

### 4. Performance Status Scale for Head and Neck (PSS-HN)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Developer | List et al. (1990, 1996) |
| Structure | 3 clinician-rated subscales, each scored 0-100 |
| Subscales | Normalcy of Diet, Understandability of Speech, Eating in Public |
| Administration | Clinician-rated; takes 2-3 minutes |
| Strengths | Quick; clinician-rated (objective); correlates with Karnofsky; reveals functional limitations missed by KPS |
| Limitations | Only 3 domains; no patient-reported component; no psychosocial assessment |

### 5. Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS) and ECOG/WHO Scale

| Feature | KPS | ECOG/WHO |
|---------|-----|----------|
| Scale | 0-100 (10-point increments) | 0-5 |
| Assessment | Global functional status | Global activity level |
| Strengths | Universally known; quick | Simpler; widely used in trials |
| Limitations | Not disease-specific; misses HNC-specific morbidity; 43% of patients with good KPS have dietary limitations | Same; less granular |

## Part C: Comparison of Instruments

| Feature | EORTC QLQ-H&N35 | UW-QOL | FACT-HN | PSS-HN |
|---------|-----------------|--------|---------|--------|
| Items | 65 (with C30) | 15 | 39 | 3 subscales |
| Time | 10-15 min | 5-10 min | 10-15 min | 2-3 min |
| Reporter | Patient | Patient | Patient | Clinician |
| Domains | 7 scales + singles | 12 domains | 4+HN | 3 subscales |
| Best for | Clinical trials | Clinical practice | Clinical trials | Quick clinic screen |
| Licensing | Required | Free | Required | Free |

**There is no single gold-standard QOL instrument.** The choice depends on the clinical or research context. For clinical trials, EORTC QLQ-H&N35 or FACT-HN is preferred. For busy clinical settings, UW-QOL or PSS-HN is more practical.

## Part D: Factors Affecting QOL in HNC

### Patient Factors
- **Age:** Older patients consistently report better QOL scores than younger patients
- **Gender:** Males have more mental illness, alcoholism, malnutrition; females have worse pretreatment QOL and greater post-treatment functional impairments
- **Marital status:** Independently associated with QOL — married patients fare better
- **Socioeconomic status:** Income correlates with QOL outcomes

### Disease and Treatment Factors
- **Tumour site:** More posterior tumours (tongue base, hypopharynx) have worse QOL
- **Stage:** Increasing T-stage and N-stage correlate with worse QOL
- **Treatment modality:** Combined modality treatment (surgery + RT/CRT) leads to worse QOL than single modality
- **Radiotherapy effects:** Xerostomia, dysphagia, and taste loss are major long-term QOL determinants
- **Time since treatment:** QOL generally improves over the first 12 months post-treatment, then plateaus

### QOL After Laryngeal Cancer Treatment

QOL assessment after laryngeal cancer is particularly important given the choice between organ preservation (CRT) and surgery (laryngectomy). Key considerations:

- Total laryngectomy patients report worse communication but better swallowing QOL compared to CRT patients
- Voice rehabilitation method (TEP vs. esophageal speech) significantly affects communication QOL
- CRT patients have more long-term dysphagia and xerostomia affecting QOL
- Depression rates are higher in laryngectomy patients (up to 40%)

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. HRQOL is a multidimensional concept covering physical, functional, emotional, and social domains
> 2. EORTC QLQ-H&N35 is the most validated and widely used HNC QOL instrument (244+ studies, 7 scales)
> 3. UW-QOL is the most practical clinical tool — 12 domains, 5-10 minutes, 97% patient preference, free to use
> 4. FACT-HN assesses 4 core domains + HN-specific items; requires licensing
> 5. PSS-HN is the only clinician-rated instrument — covers diet, speech, public eating in 2-3 minutes
> 6. No single gold-standard QOL instrument exists — choice depends on context (trial vs. clinic)
> 7. Older patients report better QOL; posterior tumours and advanced stage predict worse QOL
> 8. Post-treatment QOL generally improves over 12 months then plateaus
> 9. The EORTC QLQ-H&N35 has been updated to QLQ-HN43 (validated in 812 patients, 18 countries)
> 10. KPS and ECOG miss HNC-specific morbidity — 43% of patients with good KPS have dietary limitations

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For clinical trials, use EORTC QLQ-C30 + H&N35 (or HN43) — this is the standard expected by reviewers
> - For busy outpatient clinics, UW-QOL is the best balance of brevity and comprehensiveness
> - Always assess QOL at baseline (pre-treatment), 3 months, 6 months, and 12 months post-treatment
> - QOL at 3 months post-treatment is a key predictor of long-term QOL outcome
> - In laryngeal cancer, always compare organ preservation vs. surgical QOL — they differ in which domains are affected, not overall QOL
> - Depression screening should be part of QOL assessment — rates of depression reach 40% in laryngectomy patients
