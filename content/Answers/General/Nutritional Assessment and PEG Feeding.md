> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> Nutritional management of head and neck cancer patients is guided by several key publications. The ESPEN guidelines (Arends et al., 2017) represent the most comprehensive evidence-based recommendations for cancer nutrition, covering screening, assessment, intervention, and monitoring. Bossola (2015) provided a narrative review specifically examining the evidence for prophylactic versus reactive enteral feeding during chemoradiotherapy, concluding that prophylactic feeding does not clearly offer survival or nutritional advantages over reactive approaches. McClelland et al. Wiggenraad et al. (2007) demonstrated that prophylactic PEG with early tube feeding limits weight loss to a mean of only 2.8% during CRT. Langius et al. (2013) showed that critical weight loss (>5%) during radiotherapy is an independent prognostic factor for overall survival.

## Introduction

Malnutrition is one of the most common and consequential comorbidities in head and neck cancer patients, yet it remains frequently underrecognized and undertreated. Studies have consistently estimated that between 44% and 57% of patients with head and neck cancer experience significant difficulties with eating or unintentional weight loss even before treatment commences — a prevalence far higher than in most other cancer types. This pre-existing nutritional compromise reflects the combined effects of tumour-related obstruction and pain (which directly impair eating and [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]]), the catabolic effects of the cancer itself (tumour-induced systemic inflammation, increased metabolic rate, and cytokine-mediated muscle wasting), and the lifestyle factors that predispose to head and neck cancer (heavy alcohol use, poor diet, social deprivation, and dental neglect).

Treatment for HNC — whether surgery, radiotherapy, chemoradiotherapy, or multimodality combinations — further and often dramatically compromises nutritional status. Surgical resection of oral, pharyngeal, or laryngeal tumours directly impairs the mechanical ability to chew, swallow, and protect the airway. Radiotherapy to the head and neck causes progressive mucositis (typically from week 2-3, peaking at 1-2 weeks post-treatment), [[Answers/General/Xerostomia Management|xerostomia]] (salivary gland damage), taste disturbance (dysgeusia), fibrosis, and [[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]] — all of which impair oral intake for months to years after treatment. The addition of concurrent chemotherapy (particularly cisplatin) amplifies these toxicities while adding nausea, vomiting, and renal toxicity. The result is a "nutritional perfect storm" in which pre-existing compromise is compounded by treatment-related toxicity, leading to a downward spiral of weight loss, muscle wasting, treatment interruptions, impaired wound healing, immunosuppression, and reduced survival.

Nutritional intervention in head and neck cancer therefore aims to identify, prevent, and treat malnutrition at every stage of the cancer pathway — from initial diagnosis through treatment, rehabilitation, and long-term survivorship. Nutritional status has been recognized as a long-term prognostic factor in head and neck cancer for almost 30 years, and critical weight loss (>5% during treatment) has been shown to be an independent predictor of worse overall survival. A specialist head and neck dietitian should be a key member of the multidisciplinary team, and nutritional screening, assessment, and intervention should be integrated into standard clinical pathways for every patient.

## Part A: Impact of Malnutrition in HNC

Malnutrition is defined as a BMI < 18.5 kg/m², or unintentional weight loss > 10% over 6 months (or > 5% in 3 months) combined with reduced BMI or low fat-free mass index. In HNC patients, malnutrition is associated with:

- Delayed wound healing and increased post-operative complications and infections
- Impaired cardiac and respiratory function due to muscle weakness
- Treatment interruptions
- Decreased response rates to chemotherapy
- Increased risk of loco-regional failure
- Increased length of hospital stay
- Depression and poor [[Answers/General/Quality of Life in Head and Neck Cancer|quality of life]]
- Increased mortality rate

**Critical weight loss (>5%) during radiotherapy is an independent prognostic factor for overall survival.**

## Part B: Nutritional Screening and Assessment

### Screening Tools

| Tool | Description | Strengths |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| PG-SGA | Patient-Generated Subjective Global Assessment | Gold standard for cancer; patient self-generated |
| MST | Malnutrition Screening Tool (2 questions: weight + appetite) | Quick, simple; compares favourably with PG-SGA |
| MUST | Malnutrition Universal Screen Tool | Widely used in UK; not validated for cancer |
| SGA | Subjective Global Assessment | Based on nutritional history + physical examination |

Screening should be performed at initial presentation and repeated at regular intervals during the patient pathway.

### Full Nutritional Assessment

Following screening, a comprehensive assessment should include:

- **Diagnosis:** Tumour site and staging; nutritional implications of planned treatment
- **Anthropometry:** Weight history, % weight loss, BMI, hand grip strength (muscle mass)
- **Dietary intake:** Current ability to chew and swallow; food avoidance patterns; % energy from alcohol
- **Biochemistry:** Serum albumin, pre-albumin (though these are poor markers of nutritional status in acute illness)
- **Functional status:** Performance status, physical activity, functional impairment
- **Psychosocial factors:** Access to food, cooking skills, social support, financial status, lifestyle factors

### Estimated Nutritional Requirements

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Energy | 25-30 kcal/kg/day (higher if complications) |
| Protein | 1-1.5 g/kg/day |
| Fluid | 30-35 mL/kg/day (more if infection/excessive losses) |
| Vitamins/minerals | As per RDA unless deficient |

## Part C: Nutritional Interventions

### Pre-Treatment Optimization

- Optimize malnourished patients before treatment
- Even patients with reasonably normal intake may need dietetic intervention to meet energy/protein requirements
- Consider pre-operative carbohydrate loading (ERAS principle) to reduce catabolic response

### During Surgical Treatment

- Post-operative enteral feeding (nasogastric or PEG) typically commenced within 24-48 hours
- ERAS principles: early post-operative feeding reduces insulin resistance and accelerates recovery
- Most patients can be admitted on the day of surgery

### During Radiotherapy/Chemoradiotherapy

- Side effects impact nutrition from weeks 2-3 of treatment, peaking 1-2 weeks after completion
- Mucositis, xerostomia, dysphagia, taste changes, and nausea are the main nutritional barriers
- Independent predictors of severe toxicity: older age, advanced T-stage, larynx/pharynx primary, neck dissection after CRT
- Regular dietetic counselling throughout treatment is essential

### Immunonutrition

Feed containing omega-3 fatty acids, arginine, and nucleotides:
- Thought to upregulate host immune response, modulate inflammation, and improve protein synthesis
- Multiple RCTs and meta-analyses show reduced infection rates and hospital stay in GI cancer patients
- Greatest benefit seen in malnourished patients
- Evidence in HNC is growing but not yet definitive

## Part D: Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy (PEG)

### Indications for PEG in HNC

- Inability to maintain adequate oral nutrition during/after treatment
- Anticipated prolonged dysphagia (>2-3 weeks)
- Severe mucositis preventing oral intake
- Post-surgical patients with extensive resections affecting swallowing
- Prophylactic placement before CRT in high-risk patients

### Prophylactic vs. Reactive PEG

| Feature | Prophylactic PEG | Reactive PEG |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| Timing | Before treatment | When needed during/after treatment |
| Weight loss | Reduces malnourished patients | Similar average weight loss |
| QOL at 6 months | Improved | Similar |
| Treatment breaks | Potentially fewer | May have more |
| Long-term dependence | Greater PEG dependence | Less dependence |
| Survival | No proven advantage | No proven advantage |
| Risk | Procedural complications; may discourage oral intake | Emergency placement; delay in nutritional support |

**Current evidence:** Prophylactic PEG does not clearly offer advantages over reactive PEG in terms of nutritional outcomes, treatment interruptions, or survival. However, it may reduce the number of malnourished patients and improve QOL at 6 months. The choice should be individualized based on risk factors.

### Risk Factors Favouring Prophylactic PEG

- Advanced T-stage (T3-T4)
- Oropharyngeal or hypopharyngeal primary
- Concurrent chemoradiotherapy planned
- Pre-treatment weight loss > 5%
- Poor performance status
- Low BMI or pre-existing malnutrition
- Bilateral neck irradiation planned

### PEG Management Principles

- Early tube feeding should start before significant weight loss occurs
- PEG feeding frequency should increase as oral intake diminishes
- Oral intake should be encouraged alongside PEG feeding whenever safe to do so
- PEG should be removed when sustained adequate oral intake is established
- Swallowing exercises should continue during PEG feeding to prevent disuse atrophy

### Alternatives to PEG

- **Nasogastric tube (NGT):** Short-term (<4 weeks); easier placement; less comfortable; risk of displacement
- **Radiologically Inserted Gastrostomy (RIG):** Alternative when PEG endoscopy not possible (e.g., obstructing tumour)
- **Jejunostomy:** For patients with gastric pathology

---

## Part E: Rehabilitation and Survivorship

- Nutritional rehabilitation should continue after treatment completion
- Weight and dietary intake should be monitored at regular follow-up
- Swallowing rehabilitation and dietetic counselling work in parallel
- Many patients experience long-term dietary changes due to xerostomia, taste alteration, and fibrosis
- Address micronutrient deficiencies (vitamin D, iron, B12) common in long-term survivors

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. 44-57% of HNC patients have eating difficulties or weight loss before treatment even starts
> 2. Critical weight loss (>5%) during RT is an independent prognostic factor for overall survival
> 3. PG-SGA is the gold standard nutritional screening tool for cancer patients
> 4. Estimated requirements: 25-30 kcal/kg/day energy, 1-1.5 g/kg/day protein
> 5. Malnutrition increases complications, treatment breaks, loco-regional failure, and mortality
> 6. Prophylactic PEG reduces malnourished patients but shows no clear survival advantage over reactive PEG
> 7. Risk factors for prophylactic PEG: advanced T-stage, oropharyngeal primary, CRT, pre-existing weight loss
> 8. ESPEN guidelines (Arends 2017) are the definitive evidence-based recommendations for cancer nutrition
> 9. Immunonutrition (omega-3, arginine, nucleotides) may reduce infections — greatest benefit in malnourished patients
> 10. PEG should be removed when sustained adequate oral intake is re-established; oral intake encouraged alongside PEG

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Always screen for malnutrition at first contact — don't wait until the patient has already lost significant weight
> - A BMI in the normal range does not exclude malnutrition — look for % weight loss and muscle wasting
> - Encourage oral intake alongside PEG feeding whenever safe — prolonged PEG dependence with oral disuse leads to worse long-term swallowing outcomes
> - Pre-operative carbohydrate loading (ERAS) reduces post-surgical insulin resistance and catabolic response
> - In India, vegetarian patients may have inadequate protein intake even with reasonable caloric intake — specifically address protein sources
> - Long-term survivors need ongoing nutritional monitoring — xerostomia and taste changes can persist for years
