KDIGO AKI Work Group (2012) published the definitive international clinical practice guideline for AKI, establishing the standardized KDIGO staging system (stages 1-3) based on serum creatinine rise and urine output criteria — this guideline unified the prior RIFLE and AKIN classifications and is now the universal standard. Goren et al. (2021) published the joint ADQI/POQI consensus report establishing the framework for postoperative AKI in non-cardiac surgery, defining PO-AKI as AKI occurring within 7 days of surgery and outlining prevention, detection, and management strategies. Bellomo et al. (2004) published the original RIFLE criteria for AKI classification — the foundational work that was subsequently refined into AKIN and finally KDIGO staging.

## Introduction

Postoperative anuria — defined as urine output of **less than 50 mL in 24 hours** — is the most severe form of acute kidney injury (AKI) and represents a surgical emergency requiring immediate evaluation and management. In the context of head and neck surgery, AKI is an important complication due to prolonged operative times, significant fluid shifts, potential blood loss, and the frequent use of nephrotoxic agents including iodinated contrast and cisplatin.

Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 notes that acute renal failure occurs as a complication of septic shock in approximately **50%** of cases. Mulvey et al. (2017) found that progressive renal insufficiency after HNC surgery carries an **odds ratio of 21.28 for 30-day mortality** — one of the strongest predictors of postoperative death.

---

## Part A: Classification and Definitions

### KDIGO Staging of Acute Kidney Injury (2012)

| Stage | Serum Creatinine Criteria | Urine Output Criteria |
|-------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **1** | Rise ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hrs OR 1.5-1.9× baseline within 7 days | <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 6-12 hours |
| **2** | 2.0-2.9× baseline | <0.5 mL/kg/hr for ≥12 hours |
| **3** | ≥3.0× baseline OR creatinine ≥4.0 mg/dL OR initiation of RRT | <0.3 mL/kg/hr for ≥24 hrs OR anuria for ≥12 hours |

### Definitions of Reduced Urine Output

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Normal urine output** | >0.5 mL/kg/hr (≈30-50 mL/hr in adults) |
| **Oliguria** | <0.5 mL/kg/hr (<400 mL/24 hrs) |
| **Severe oliguria** | <0.3 mL/kg/hr |
| **Anuria** | <50 mL/24 hrs (or no urine output) |

## Part B: Causes of Postoperative Anuria

### The Traditional Classification: Pre-renal, Renal, Post-renal

| Category | Mechanism | Common Causes in HNC Surgery |
|----------|-----------|------------------------------|
| **Pre-renal (70%)** | Decreased renal perfusion | Hypovolaemia (blood loss, inadequate fluid replacement), hypotension, cardiogenic shock, septic shock |
| **Renal/Intrinsic (20%)** | Parenchymal kidney damage | Acute tubular necrosis (ATN), cisplatin nephrotoxicity, contrast nephropathy, nephrotoxic drugs (aminoglycosides, NSAIDs) |
| **Post-renal (10%)** | Urinary tract obstruction | Blocked catheter, bilateral ureteric obstruction (rare), prostatic obstruction |

### Causes Specific to Head and Neck Surgery

**1. Hypovolaemia (most common):**
Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 describes the ATLS classification of haemorrhagic shock: Class III (30-40% blood volume loss) produces urine output of only **5-15 mL/hr**, while Class IV (>40% loss) results in **negligible** urine output. Major HNC procedures (composite resection, free flap reconstruction) frequently involve significant blood loss.

**2. Cisplatin Nephrotoxicity:**
Cisplatin is the backbone of concurrent chemoradiation in HNSCC. It causes dose-dependent nephrotoxicity through direct tubular cell injury, oxidative stress, and inflammation. van der Vorst et al. (2019) found that AKI during cisplatin-based CRT leads to **long-term renal impairment**. Stell and Maran's Chapter 30 notes that pretreatment with amino acid infusion can mitigate nephrotoxicity.

**3. Iodinated Contrast Nephropathy:**
Stell and Maran's Chapter 5 states that iodinated contrast media is nephrotoxic and reported as inducing acute renal failure in **1-6% of unselected patients** and **up to 50% in high-risk populations**. An iso-osmolar or low-osmolar non-ionic contrast medium is preferred in patients with renal impairment and diabetes; hydration before and after the examination is required.

**4. Sepsis:**
Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 states: complications from sepsis include acute renal failure (ARF) in approximately **50%** of septic shock cases. Sepsis is a significant risk in HNC patients due to contaminated wounds (oral/pharyngeal), indwelling catheters, and immunocompromised status.

**5. Rhabdomyolysis:**
Prolonged operative positioning in lengthy HNC procedures (>8 hours for free flap surgery) can cause muscle compression injury and rhabdomyolysis, with myoglobin-mediated renal tubular damage.

## Part C: Evaluation of Postoperative Anuria

### Systematic Approach

| Step | Action | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| **1. Check catheter** | Flush/irrigate; rule out kinking, blockage, displacement | Exclude post-renal obstruction (commonest correctable cause) |
| **2. Assess volume status** | JVP, skin turgor, mucous membranes, HR, BP, fluid balance chart | Identify hypovolaemia |
| **3. Haemodynamic review** | Heart rate, blood pressure, central venous pressure (if available) | Differentiate hypovolaemic from cardiogenic/septic shock |
| **4. Review drug chart** | Identify nephrotoxic agents: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, cisplatin, ACE inhibitors | Remove/modify offending agents |
| **5. Blood investigations** | Urea, creatinine, electrolytes, blood gases, FBC, CRP, CK | Diagnose AKI, hyperkalaemia, acidosis, rhabdomyolysis |
| **6. Urine investigations** | Urinary sodium, osmolality, sediment, myoglobin | Differentiate pre-renal from intrinsic renal |
| **7. Imaging** | Renal ultrasound | Exclude bilateral obstruction (hydronephrosis) |

### Differentiating Pre-renal from Intrinsic Renal AKI

| Parameter | Pre-renal | Intrinsic (ATN) |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Urine sodium (mmol/L)** | <20 | >40 |
| **Urine osmolality (mOsm/kg)** | >500 | <350 |
| **FENa (%)** | <1% | >2% |
| **Urine/plasma creatinine ratio** | >40 | <20 |
| **Urine sediment** | Normal/hyaline casts | Muddy brown granular casts |
| **Response to fluid** | Urine output improves | No improvement |

**FENa** (fractional excretion of sodium) = (Urine Na × Plasma Cr) / (Plasma Na × Urine Cr) × 100

## Part D: Management

### Immediate Management

**1. Exclude catheter blockage** — the simplest and most commonly overlooked cause.

**2. Fluid resuscitation** (for pre-renal AKI):
Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 describes initial management of shock: crystalloid resuscitation guided by haemodynamic parameters. For Class III-IV haemorrhage, crystalloid and blood products are required.

**3. Stop nephrotoxic drugs:** Discontinue NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, ACE inhibitors/ARBs; review cisplatin dosing if on concurrent CRT.

**4. Treat underlying cause:**
- Sepsis → antibiotics, source control
- Cardiogenic shock → cardiology consultation
- Rhabdomyolysis → aggressive fluid resuscitation, urinary alkalinization

**5. Monitor and correct electrolytes:**
- **Hyperkalaemia** is the most dangerous complication of anuria — can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias
- Emergency treatment: calcium gluconate (cardioprotection), insulin-dextrose, nebulized salbutamol

**6. Renal replacement therapy (RRT):** Indicated for:
- Refractory hyperkalaemia (K⁺ >6.5 mmol/L with ECG changes)
- Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1)
- Fluid overload unresponsive to diuretics
- Uraemic complications (encephalopathy, pericarditis)

### Prevention in Head and Neck Surgery

| Strategy | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Adequate hydration** | Pre-operative and intraoperative IV fluids; avoid prolonged fasting |
| **Avoid nephrotoxic agents** | Minimize NSAIDs; use low-osmolar contrast with pre-hydration |
| **Cisplatin hydration protocol** | Aggressive IV hydration before, during, and after cisplatin; maintain UO >100 mL/hr |
| **Monitor urine output** | Indwelling catheter for all major HNC procedures; hourly monitoring |
| **Goal-directed fluid therapy** | Optimize intravascular volume during lengthy free flap procedures |
| **Early identification** | Serial creatinine monitoring; KDIGO criteria application |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Anuria = <50 mL urine in 24 hours; the most severe form of AKI requiring emergency evaluation
> 2. KDIGO staging (2012) is the standard: Stage 1 (creatinine rise ≥0.3 mg/dL) → Stage 3 (anuria ≥12 hrs or RRT initiation)
> 3. Pre-renal causes (hypovolaemia) account for ~70% of postoperative AKI — most common and most responsive to treatment
> 4. ALWAYS check the catheter first — blocked/kinked catheters are the simplest correctable cause of apparent anuria
> 5. Progressive renal insufficiency after HNC surgery carries OR 21.28 for 30-day mortality — one of the strongest predictors of death (Mulvey et al., 2017)
> 6. Cisplatin nephrotoxicity is a major concern in HNSCC — dose-dependent tubular damage; aggressive hydration protocol is essential
> 7. Contrast nephropathy affects 1-6% of unselected patients and up to 50% of high-risk patients (Stell and Maran's)
> 8. FENa <1% = pre-renal; FENa >2% = intrinsic renal (ATN) — this is the key discriminating test
> 9. Hyperkalaemia is the most dangerous complication of anuria — emergency treatment: calcium gluconate → insulin-dextrose → salbutamol → RRT
> 10. Renal replacement therapy is indicated for refractory hyperkalaemia, severe acidosis, fluid overload, and uraemic complications

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, use the structured approach: (1) check catheter, (2) assess volume status, (3) review drugs, (4) blood/urine tests, (5) imaging
> - The PRE-RENAL vs INTRINSIC distinction is the highest-yield concept: FENa <1% + urine Na <20 + urine osmolality >500 = pre-renal (will respond to fluid)
> - In HNC patients, always think of FOUR specific causes: (1) hypovolaemia from blood loss, (2) cisplatin nephrotoxicity, (3) contrast nephropathy, (4) sepsis
> - Cisplatin hydration protocol: 1-2L NS pre-hydration → cisplatin with mannitol → 1L post-hydration; target UO >100 mL/hr
> - Rhabdomyolysis from prolonged operative positioning is an underrecognized cause in lengthy HNC free flap procedures (>8 hours)
> - The "fluid challenge" is both diagnostic AND therapeutic for pre-renal AKI: 250-500 mL crystalloid bolus → observe urine output response
