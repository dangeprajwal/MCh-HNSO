## Introduction

Apoptosis (from the Greek, meaning "falling off," as leaves from a tree) is a genetically programmed, energy-dependent form of cell death that plays a fundamental role in tissue homeostasis, embryonic development, and immune regulation. The term was coined by Kerr, Wyllie, and Currie in 1972 to describe a distinct morphological pattern of controlled cell deletion, complementary to mitosis in regulating cell populations.

Unlike necrosis — which is a passive, disordered process resulting from acute cellular injury, characterized by cell swelling, membrane rupture, and inflammation — apoptosis is an orderly, regulated process. Apoptotic cells undergo cell shrinkage, chromatin condensation (pyknosis), nuclear fragmentation (karyorrhexis), membrane blebbing, and formation of apoptotic bodies that are rapidly phagocytosed without triggering an inflammatory response.

As described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 30, normal cells constantly audit the balance between incoming survival and death signals, requiring constant "reassurance" from their environment to prevent activation of death signalling pathways. DNA damage — including that induced by radiotherapy or chemotherapy — leads to a block in proliferation (cell cycle arrest) and an evaluation of the cell's capacity for repair. If the damage exceeds the repair capacity, the balance of survival and death signals swings in favour of the death signals and the cell commits to programmed cell death. Loss of normal apoptotic pathway signalling is an extremely common event in cancer — and indeed, the best known cancer-associated gene (p53) is intimately involved in controlling apoptosis and is functionally inactivated in the majority of head and neck cancers.

Hanahan and Weinberg (2000) identified **evading apoptosis** as one of the six original hallmarks of cancer, underscoring its central importance in tumour development and progression.

## Part A: Mechanisms of Apoptosis — The Two Pathways

There are essentially two main effector pathways for apoptosis, as described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 6: the **extrinsic** pathway, regulated by cell surface receptor signalling; and the **intrinsic** pathway, regulated by intracellular stimuli such as DNA damage, hypoxia, and metabolic stresses.

### 1. The Intrinsic (Mitochondrial) Pathway

This pathway is activated by intracellular stress signals — DNA damage, oxidative stress, hypoxia, growth factor withdrawal, or oncogene activation. It is regulated by the **BCL-2 family** of proteins.

**Key steps:**
1. Cellular stress activates pro-apoptotic BH3-only proteins (BID, BIM, BAD, PUMA, NOXA)
2. BH3-only proteins activate the effector proteins BAX and BAK
3. BAX/BAK oligomerize and form pores in the outer mitochondrial membrane → **mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP)**
4. Release of cytochrome c from the mitochondrial intermembrane space into the cytoplasm
5. Cytochrome c binds APAF-1 (apoptotic protease-activating factor 1) → formation of the **apoptosome**
6. The apoptosome activates **caspase-9** (initiator caspase)
7. Caspase-9 activates **caspase-3** and **caspase-7** (executioner caspases)
8. Executioner caspases cleave cellular substrates → orderly cell dismantling

**BCL-2 family — the arbiters of intrinsic apoptosis:**

| Pro-Apoptotic | Anti-Apoptotic |
|--------------|----------------|
| BAX, BAK (effectors) | BCL-2, BCL-xL, MCL-1 |
| BIM, BID, BAD, PUMA, NOXA (BH3-only sensors) | BCL-W, A1/BFL-1 |

As described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 10, the BCL-2 group contains pro-apoptotic proteins such as BAX, BAD, and BAK, and anti-apoptotic proteins such as BCL-2, BCL-xL, and survivin. The balance between these opposing forces determines whether a cell lives or dies. Anti-apoptotic BCL-2 proteins sequester BAX/BAK and prevent MOMP; pro-apoptotic BH3-only proteins displace this interaction, freeing BAX/BAK to oligomerize and induce cell death.

**The role of p53 in the intrinsic pathway:**

Harrison's Chapter 3 describes p53 as a transcription factor with tumour suppressor function that negatively regulates the cell cycle and serves to protect the integrity of the genome. Activation of p53 occurs in response to a variety of cellular stressors such as DNA damage, hypoxia, and cell cycle aberrations. p53 mediates apoptosis through transactivation of pro-apoptotic genes including PUMA, NOXA, and BAX, presumably when the extent of DNA damage exceeds the capacity of cellular repair mechanisms. Thus, p53 protects the cell from propagating mutations to subsequent generations and is considered the "guardian of the genome."

Loss of p53 function contributes to tumour aggressiveness by promoting resistance to radiation and chemotherapy, accelerated growth in hypoxic conditions, and tumour neovascularization. As described in Harrison, p53 mutant cell lines exhibit more resistance to ionizing radiation and certain chemotherapeutic agents due to a deficiency in DNA damage-induced apoptosis. Transfection with an adenoviral vector containing a functional p53 gene resulted in a dose-dependent increase in radiation sensitivity, restoration of the G1 checkpoint, and induction of apoptosis.

### 2. The Extrinsic (Death Receptor) Pathway

This pathway is activated by extracellular death ligands binding to cell surface **death receptors** — members of the tumour necrosis factor receptor (TNFR) superfamily. The seminal review by Ashkenazi and Dixit (1998) defined this pathway.

**Key death receptor-ligand pairs:**

| Death Receptor | Ligand |
|---------------|--------|
| FAS (CD95/APO-1) | FAS Ligand (FASL) |
| TRAIL-R1 (DR4) | TRAIL (TNF-related apoptosis-inducing ligand) |
| TRAIL-R2 (DR5) | TRAIL |
| TNFR1 | TNF-α |

**Key steps:**
1. Death ligand binds to death receptor → receptor trimerization
2. Recruitment of adaptor protein **FADD** (FAS-associated death domain) to the intracellular death domain
3. FADD recruits procaspase-8 → formation of the **DISC** (death-inducing signalling complex)
4. Procaspase-8 undergoes autoproteolytic activation → active **caspase-8** (initiator caspase)
5. Caspase-8 directly activates executioner **caspase-3** and **caspase-7**
6. Alternatively, caspase-8 cleaves BID to truncated BID (tBID), which activates the intrinsic pathway → amplification loop

As described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 6, FADD links death receptors such as FAS with the intracellular survival regulatory machinery via NF-κB and with caspase effectors of apoptosis such as caspase-8. CASP8 encodes the critical effector caspase-8 for the extrinsic apoptotic signalling pathway in head and neck cancers. CASP8 inactivating mutations — found predominantly in oral cavity tumours — were often found associated with wild-type TP53 (thus p53 is competent to induce apoptosis through the intrinsic pathway, but caspase-8 which acts through the extrinsic pathway is not functional).

### 3. The Executioner Caspases — The Final Common Pathway

Both pathways converge on the activation of **executioner caspases** (caspase-3, -6, -7), which cleave over 400 cellular substrates including:
- ICAD (inhibitor of caspase-activated DNase) → releases CAD → DNA fragmentation
- Lamin → nuclear envelope breakdown
- Cytoskeletal proteins → cell shrinkage
- PARP (poly-ADP ribose polymerase) → prevents DNA repair

**Inhibitors of apoptosis proteins (IAPs):** XIAP, cIAP1, cIAP2, and survivin directly inhibit caspases. They are counteracted by **SMAC/DIABLO** (second mitochondria-derived activator of caspases), released from mitochondria during MOMP, which binds IAPs and relieves their caspase-inhibitory activity.

## Part B: Apoptosis versus Necrosis

| Feature | Apoptosis | Necrosis |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Stimulus | Physiological or programmed | Pathological (injury, ischaemia) |
| Energy requirement | ATP-dependent | Passive, ATP-independent |
| Cell morphology | Shrinkage, blebbing | Swelling, lysis |
| Nucleus | Pyknosis, karyorrhexis | Karyolysis |
| Membrane integrity | Maintained until late | Early loss |
| Organelles | Intact | Disrupted |
| Inflammation | Absent (no leakage) | Present (content release) |
| End products | Apoptotic bodies | Cell debris |
| Phagocytosis | Rapid, by neighbours | By macrophages |

## Part C: Evasion of Apoptosis in Head and Neck Cancer

Scott-Brown's Chapter 30 states that by circumventing normal apoptotic signalling, cancer cells are better able to sustain DNA damage without its causing cell death and are more likely to be intrinsically resistant to anticancer treatments. Multiple mechanisms of apoptosis evasion operate in HNSCC:

### 1. TP53 Mutation (50-80% of HNSCC)

The most common mechanism. As described in Montgomery Chapter 2, alterations of the p53 gene by allelic losses, point mutations, deletion, or inactivation disrupt its role as guardian of the genome by impairing the cell's ability to repair and undergo apoptosis in response to DNA damage. Harrison Chapter 6 notes that p53 is involved in the regulation of diverse cellular processes including cell cycle, apoptosis, autophagy, DNA repair, and metabolism, and that mutations generally render the gene nonfunctional and provide growth advantage to cancer cells.

In HPV-positive HNSCC, p53 is functionally inactivated not by mutation but by the viral oncoprotein E6, which targets p53 for proteasomal degradation via the E6AP ubiquitin ligase pathway.

### 2. Overexpression of Anti-Apoptotic BCL-2 Family Proteins

Ow et al. (2020) demonstrated that HNSCC cells rely on inhibition of apoptosis via **BCL-xL** and **MCL-1** overexpression. High BCL-2 expression in salivary gland carcinoma relates to poor prognosis and advanced T and N classification (Scott-Brown's Chapter 10). Overexpression of these anti-apoptotic proteins raises the threshold for MOMP, allowing cancer cells to survive despite accumulating pro-apoptotic signals.

### 3. CASP8 Inactivating Mutations

CASP8 mutations are found in 8-10% of HNSCC, predominantly in oral cavity tumours. These mutations disable the extrinsic apoptotic pathway. As noted in Scott-Brown's Chapter 6, CASP8 mutations were often associated with wild-type TP53, suggesting that tumours may evolve to evade apoptosis through either the intrinsic or extrinsic pathway — but rarely both simultaneously.

### 4. PI3K/AKT Pathway Activation

The PI3K/AKT pathway is a major survival signalling pathway. As described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 6, activation of the PI3K/AKT pathway is involved in cellular proliferation and apoptosis resistance via activation of mTOR. Constitutive activation through PIK3CA mutations (common in both HPV+ and HPV− HNSCC) or loss of the opposing phosphatase PTEN promotes cell survival by phosphorylating and inactivating pro-apoptotic proteins BAD and procaspase-9, while activating NF-κB-mediated survival signalling.

### 5. EGFR Overexpression

Harrison notes that activation of EGFR by TGF-α functions to promote cellular proliferation and inhibit apoptosis. EGFR expression positively correlates with increased radioresistance and inversely correlates with radiation-induced apoptosis. Blockade of EGFR with cetuximab (C225) downregulates anti-apoptotic proteins such as STAT3 and thus promotes radiation-induced apoptosis.

### 6. Overexpression of IAPs (Survivin)

Survivin — an inhibitor of apoptosis protein — is frequently overexpressed in HNSCC. Nuclear survivin expression indicates worse prognosis in salivary gland carcinoma (Scott-Brown's Chapter 10). Survivin directly inhibits caspase-3 and caspase-7, preventing the execution phase of apoptosis.

### 7. NF-κB Activation

NF-κB is a transcription factor that promotes expression of anti-apoptotic genes (BCL-2, BCL-xL, cIAP1/2, XIAP). Scott-Brown's Chapter 6 describes how genes such as FADD, TRAF3, PIK3CA, TP53, and MYC impact on the regulation of NF-κB activity, which coordinates cell survival, death, inflammation, and immunity.

## Part D: Therapeutic Targeting of Apoptosis in HNSCC

### Current Approaches

| Strategy | Mechanism | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Radiotherapy | DNA damage → p53-mediated intrinsic apoptosis | Standard fractionation |
| Cisplatin | DNA crosslinks → intrinsic pathway activation | First-line chemotherapy |
| Cetuximab | EGFR blockade → reduced survival signalling → enhanced apoptosis | FDA-approved for HNSCC |
| Immune checkpoint inhibitors | Restore T-cell mediated extrinsic apoptosis via FASL/TRAIL | Pembrolizumab, nivolumab |

### Emerging Approaches

- **BH3 mimetics:** Small molecules that mimic pro-apoptotic BH3-only proteins, directly antagonizing BCL-2/BCL-xL (e.g., venetoclax targets BCL-2; navitoclax targets BCL-xL)
- **IAP inhibitors (SMAC mimetics):** Compounds that mimic SMAC/DIABLO, relieving IAP-mediated caspase inhibition (e.g., birinapant, xevinapant — early-phase trials in HNSCC)
- **TRAIL receptor agonists:** Recombinant TRAIL or agonistic antibodies to DR4/DR5 — activate the extrinsic pathway selectively in tumour cells
- **p53 gene therapy:** Adenoviral delivery of wild-type p53 (Ad-p53/Gendicine) — restores intrinsic apoptosis in p53-mutant tumours
- **MDM2 inhibitors:** Block MDM2-mediated p53 degradation, restoring p53-dependent apoptosis in tumours with wild-type p53

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Apoptosis is genetically programmed, energy-dependent cell death — distinct from necrosis in being orderly, non-inflammatory, and requiring ATP
> 2. Two main pathways: intrinsic (mitochondrial, regulated by BCL-2 family) and extrinsic (death receptor, initiated by FAS/TRAIL/TNF)
> 3. Both pathways converge on executioner caspases (caspase-3, -6, -7) — the final common pathway of cell dismantling
> 4. Evasion of apoptosis is a hallmark of cancer (Hanahan & Weinberg, 2000) and is extremely common in HNSCC
> 5. TP53 mutation (50-80% of HNSCC) is the most common mechanism of apoptosis evasion — disrupts "guardian of the genome" function
> 6. In HPV+ HNSCC, p53 is inactivated by viral E6 oncoprotein (functional inactivation, not mutation)
> 7. CASP8 mutations (oral cavity predominance) disable the extrinsic pathway and are often mutually exclusive with TP53 mutations
> 8. BCL-xL and MCL-1 overexpression are key anti-apoptotic mechanisms in HNSCC (Ow et al., 2020)
> 9. PI3K/AKT pathway activation promotes survival by inactivating pro-apoptotic BAD and procaspase-9
> 10. Cetuximab enhances radiation-induced apoptosis by downregulating STAT3 — the mechanistic basis for bioradiotherapy

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The reason radiotherapy and chemotherapy work in cancer is fundamentally because they induce apoptosis — understanding apoptosis pathways explains both treatment efficacy and resistance
> - p53 mutation confers both radioresistance and chemoresistance through deficient DNA damage-induced apoptosis — this is why p53-mutant HNSCC has worse outcomes
> - The mutual exclusivity of CASP8 and TP53 mutations in HNSCC suggests that tumours only need to disable ONE apoptotic pathway to gain a survival advantage
> - Cetuximab added to radiotherapy works partly by promoting radiation-induced apoptosis through STAT3 downregulation — this is why the Bonner trial showed benefit
> - BH3 mimetics and SMAC mimetics represent the frontier of apoptosis-targeting therapy in HNSCC — xevinapant (IAP inhibitor) is in phase III trials
> - Always distinguish apoptosis (programmed, non-inflammatory) from necrosis (pathological, inflammatory) in exam answers — the key differentiating features are energy dependence, membrane integrity, and inflammatory response
