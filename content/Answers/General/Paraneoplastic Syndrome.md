## Introduction

Paraneoplastic syndromes (PNS) are clinical disorders that accompany malignant disease but are not directly attributable to the physical effects of the primary tumour, its regional or distant metastases, or treatment toxicity. They are caused by substances (hormones, peptides, cytokines, antibodies) produced by the tumour, or by immune responses triggered by tumour antigens that cross-react with normal host tissues. PNS may precede the clinical diagnosis of cancer, occur concurrently, or appear at the time of recurrence — thus serving as important clinical markers.

In head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, the most frequently encountered paraneoplastic syndrome is **humoral hypercalcaemia of malignancy (HHM)**, mediated by tumour-derived parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP). As described in Montgomery's Chapter 10, hypercalcaemia is common in patients with head and neck cancers and on some occasions resistant hypercalcaemia can be a terminal event. Stell and Maran's Chapter 19 notes that aberrant PTHrP secretion may occur as a paraneoplastic phenomenon in some malignant neoplasms, notably squamous cell carcinoma. Montgomery's Chapter 18 describes several paraneoplastic syndromes with nasopharyngeal carcinoma (NPC) including hypertrophic osteoarthropathy, pyrexia of unknown origin, inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion, and a leukemoid peripheral blood picture. Scott-Brown's Chapter 7 notes that olfactory neuroblastoma is a neuroendocrine tumour capable of causing paraneoplastic syndromes by secreting peptides — including Cushing's syndrome, SIADH, and hypertension from vasoactive peptides.

The classification of paraneoplastic syndromes in head and neck cancer follows the system proposed by Ferlito et al. (2007): **endocrine**, **dermatological**, **haematological**, **neurological**, **rheumatological**, and **ocular** manifestations.

## Part A: Endocrine Paraneoplastic Syndromes

### 1. Humoral Hypercalcaemia of Malignancy (HHM)

This is the most common and clinically significant paraneoplastic syndrome in head and neck cancer. It is mediated by tumour secretion of **parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP)**, which shares structural homology with the first 13 amino acids of PTH and binds the same receptor (PTH1R).

**Mechanism:** PTHrP acts systemically to:
- Increase osteoclastic bone resorption (the dominant mechanism)
- Increase renal tubular calcium reabsorption
- Increase phosphate excretion from proximal renal tubules
- Indirectly increase 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D production (though less than PTH)

**Clinical features:**
- Symptoms include thirst, polyuria, constipation, nausea, confusion, and lethargy
- As described in Montgomery Chapter 10, occasionally hypercalcaemia may be picked up as an incidental finding, but the symptoms of thirst, constipation, and confusion are likely indicators
- Severe hypercalcaemia (>3.5 mmol/L) can cause cardiac arrhythmias, renal failure, coma, and death

**Key clinical points:**
- HHM occurs predominantly in advanced-stage HNSCC — it is a late manifestation and an ominous prognostic sign (Sridhar and Hussein, 1990)
- It is most common in SCC of the oral cavity, oropharynx, and hypopharynx
- The serum PTH level is suppressed (distinguishing HHM from primary hyperparathyroidism), while PTHrP is elevated
- It may be the presenting feature, or may herald recurrence

**Management:**
- Aggressive intravenous hydration with normal saline (200-300 ml/hour initially)
- Intravenous bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid 4 mg IV or pamidronate 90 mg IV) — the mainstay of treatment
- Calcitonin (4-8 IU/kg subcutaneously every 12 hours) — provides rapid but transient effect
- Loop diuretics (furosemide) only AFTER adequate hydration
- Denosumab (RANKL inhibitor) for bisphosphonate-refractory cases
- Definitive treatment of the underlying malignancy is essential — successful tumour control usually resolves the hypercalcaemia

### 2. Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone Secretion (SIADH)

Ectopic secretion of ADH (vasopressin) by the tumour leads to water retention, dilutional hyponatraemia, and inappropriately concentrated urine.

**Associations in HNC:**
- Small cell neuroendocrine carcinoma of the larynx — Montgomery Chapter 14 notes this tumour is associated with various paraneoplastic syndromes
- Nasopharyngeal carcinoma — specifically described in Montgomery Chapter 18
- Olfactory neuroblastoma — Scott-Brown's Chapter 7 lists SIADH among the PNS caused by OAN

**Clinical features:** Hyponatraemia, nausea, headache, confusion, seizures in severe cases

**Management:** Fluid restriction (800-1000 ml/day), demeclocycline, tolvaptan (V2 receptor antagonist), and treatment of underlying malignancy

### 3. Ectopic Cushing's Syndrome

Ectopic ACTH production leads to cortisol excess with characteristic features of hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis, hyperglycaemia, proximal myopathy, and rapid-onset cushingoid features.

**Associations in HNC:**
- Olfactory neuroblastoma — the most commonly reported HNC association (Scott-Brown's Chapter 7)
- Small cell neuroendocrine carcinoma of the larynx
- Medullary thyroid carcinoma (ectopic ACTH production)

### 4. Other Ectopic Hormone Production

- **Ectopic PTH secretion:** Rare; distinguished from PTHrP-mediated HHM by elevated intact PTH levels
- **Hypertension from vasoactive peptides:** Described with olfactory neuroblastoma (Scott-Brown's Chapter 7)
- **Ectopic calcitonin:** From medullary thyroid carcinoma (though this is the normal cell of origin, the overproduction has systemic effects)

## Part B: Dermatological Paraneoplastic Syndromes

### 1. Acrokeratosis Paraneoplastica (Bazex Syndrome)

This is the most specific dermatological paraneoplastic syndrome associated with HNSCC. It is characterized by:
- Psoriasiform or violaceous scaly lesions of the acral areas — fingers, toes, nose, ears, and helices
- Nail dystrophy with ridging and onycholysis
- Extends to involve the cheeks, elbows, and knees as the underlying tumour progresses

**Key features:**
- Almost exclusively associated with SCC of the upper aerodigestive tract (pharynx, larynx, oesophagus) or cervical lymph node metastases from an unknown primary
- May precede the diagnosis of the primary tumour by months
- Resolution of the skin lesions correlates with successful treatment of the underlying malignancy
- Recurrence of skin lesions suggests tumour recurrence

### 2. Dermatomyositis

An inflammatory myopathy with characteristic skin findings (heliotrope rash, Gottron's papules, shawl sign) associated with underlying malignancy in 15-30% of adult cases. Nasopharyngeal carcinoma is a well-recognized association, particularly in Asian populations.

### 3. Other Dermatological PNS

| Syndrome | Features | HNC Association |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| Acanthosis nigricans | Velvety hyperpigmentation of flexures | GI malignancy primarily; reported with oral SCC |
| Erythema gyratum repens | Wood-grain pattern erythema | Rarely with HNSCC |
| Sweet syndrome | Acute febrile neutrophilic dermatosis | Haematological malignancy primarily |
| Pemphigus | Mucocutaneous blistering | Thymoma; rarely with oral cancer |

## Part C: Haematological Paraneoplastic Syndromes

### 1. Leukemoid Reaction

As described in Montgomery Chapter 18, metastatic NPC may be accompanied by a leukemoid peripheral blood picture. This is caused by tumour production of granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) or granulocyte-macrophage CSF (GM-CSF), resulting in a markedly elevated white cell count (>50 × 10⁹/L) without underlying leukaemia.

- Reported with SCC of the larynx, oral cavity, and nasopharynx
- Must be distinguished from true leukaemia and infection
- Usually indicates advanced disease and carries a poor prognosis

### 2. Other Haematological PNS

- **Eosinophilia:** Tumour production of IL-5; reported with oral and laryngeal SCC
- **Thrombocytosis:** Reactive, mediated by IL-6 and thrombopoietin; common in advanced HNSCC
- **Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC):** Rare in HNSCC; tumour procoagulant activity
- **Migratory thrombophlebitis (Trousseau's syndrome):** Hypercoagulable state; more common with mucin-secreting adenocarcinomas

## Part D: Neurological and Rheumatological Paraneoplastic Syndromes

### 1. Neurological PNS

These are mediated by **autoantibodies** directed against neuronal antigens shared by tumour and neural tissue (onconeural antibodies).

- **Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome (LEMS):** Anti-VGCC antibodies; primarily associated with small cell lung cancer but reported with neuroendocrine tumours including those in the head and neck
- **Paraneoplastic neurogenic [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|dysphagia]]:** Scott-Brown's Chapter 54 notes that a small number of paraneoplastic syndromes have been associated with neurogenic dysphagia, including those caused by SCC of the skin
- **Subacute cerebellar degeneration:** Anti-Yo, anti-Hu antibodies; very rare with HNSCC
- **Sensory neuropathy:** Anti-Hu antibodies

### 2. Hypertrophic Osteoarthropathy (HOA)

Specifically described with NPC in Montgomery Chapter 18. Characterized by:
- Periostitis of long bones (painful, tender shins)
- Digital clubbing
- Synovial effusions (arthralgia)

The mechanism involves tumour production of [[Answers/General/Tumour Angiogenesis|VEGF]] and platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), which promote periosteal new bone formation and vascular proliferation.

### 3. Pyrexia of Unknown Origin

Montgomery Chapter 18 lists PUO among the paraneoplastic syndromes of NPC. Tumour-related fever is mediated by cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) produced by the tumour or by host immune cells in response to the tumour. It is a diagnosis of exclusion after infection has been ruled out.

## Part E: Diagnosis and Clinical Significance

### Diagnostic Approach

| Investigation | Purpose |
|--------------|---------|
| Serum calcium, phosphate, PTH, PTHrP | Diagnose HHM; distinguish from primary hyperparathyroidism |
| Serum sodium, urine osmolality | Diagnose SIADH |
| Cortisol, ACTH, dexamethasone suppression | Diagnose ectopic Cushing's |
| Full blood count, peripheral smear | Identify leukemoid reaction, eosinophilia |
| Onconeural antibodies (anti-Hu, anti-Yo) | Neurological PNS |
| Skin biopsy | Specific dermatological PNS |

### Clinical Significance of PNS in HNC

1. **Diagnostic clue:** PNS may precede the clinical diagnosis of the primary tumour — Bazex syndrome may present months before oropharyngeal or laryngeal SCC is diagnosed
2. **Tumour marker:** Resolution of PNS with treatment and recurrence with relapse makes PNS a useful clinical marker of disease activity
3. **Prognostic indicator:** Hypercalcaemia in HNSCC is a marker of advanced disease and poor prognosis (Sridhar and Hussein, 1990); resistant hypercalcaemia can be a terminal event (Montgomery)
4. **Treatment implications:** PNS must be managed alongside the primary tumour — some PNS (e.g., severe hypercalcaemia, SIADH) are medical emergencies requiring immediate intervention

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Paraneoplastic syndromes are remote effects of malignancy caused by tumour-derived hormones, peptides, cytokines, or autoimmune cross-reactivity
> 2. Humoral hypercalcaemia of malignancy (HHM) — mediated by PTHrP — is the most common PNS in head and neck SCC
> 3. PTHrP shares the first 13 amino acids with PTH and binds the same receptor — distinguish from primary HPT by suppressed PTH level
> 4. Hypercalcaemia is a late manifestation of advanced HNSCC and an ominous prognostic sign — resistant hypercalcaemia can be a terminal event
> 5. Bazex syndrome (acrokeratosis paraneoplastica) is almost pathognomonic for SCC of the upper aerodigestive tract — may precede diagnosis
> 6. Olfactory neuroblastoma causes Cushing's syndrome, SIADH, and hypertension from ectopic peptide secretion
> 7. NPC is associated with hypertrophic osteoarthropathy, PUO, SIADH, and leukemoid reaction
> 8. Small cell neuroendocrine carcinoma of the larynx is associated with various PNS — always perform a full paraneoplastic work-up
> 9. Only endocrine and dermatological PNS have well-documented associations with oral SCC specifically (Toro et al., 2010)
> 10. PNS may precede, parallel, or follow the primary tumour — making them valuable as diagnostic clues, tumour markers, and prognostic indicators

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - When a patient with known HNSCC develops confusion, constipation, or lethargy — check serum calcium immediately; HHM is the most common PNS and is easily treatable
> - A patient with unexplained acral psoriasiform lesions and nail dystrophy should be investigated for an occult SCC of the pharynx or larynx — Bazex syndrome may precede the cancer diagnosis by months
> - In a patient with olfactory neuroblastoma, always check for ectopic ACTH (Cushing's) and SIADH — these PNS may complicate management
> - Suppressed intact PTH with elevated serum calcium distinguishes HHM (PTHrP-mediated) from primary hyperparathyroidism — always measure both PTH and PTHrP
> - Resolution of the paraneoplastic syndrome after treatment is a good sign; recurrence of the PNS should prompt investigation for tumour recurrence
> - In hypercalcaemia management: hydrate FIRST (aggressive normal saline), THEN bisphosphonate — never give loop diuretics to a dehydrated patient
