# WHO/ECOG Performance Status Scale

**Source:** Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 4 (Assessment of HNC Patient — Table 4.1), Chapter 7 (Treatment Options — Karnofsky Index, Table 7.3), Chapter 9 (Measures of Treatment Outcomes — Comorbidity Indices); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 5 (Pretreatment Evaluation), Chapter 8 ([[Answers/General/Quality of Life in Head and Neck Cancer|quality of life]])

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> Performance status assessment is a cornerstone of oncological decision-making, influencing treatment selection, clinical trial eligibility, and prognostic stratification. Karnofsky and Burchenal (1949) developed the first formal performance status scale — the Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS) — as a standardization tool to quantify the effects of chemotherapeutic agents, using an 11-point scale from 0% (dead) to 100% (normal). Oken et al. (1982) published the formal ECOG Performance Status Scale (also known as the WHO/Zubrod scale), a simpler 6-point scale (0-5) that has become the most widely used performance measure in clinical trials. Miller et al. (1981) established the WHO criteria for reporting results of cancer treatment, endorsing the ECOG scale as the standard. List et al. (1990) developed the Performance Status Scale for Head and Neck Cancer (PSS-HN) — the only performance instrument specifically designed for head and neck cancer patients, measuring understandability of speech, normalcy of diet, and eating in public. Piccirillo (2000) demonstrated that comorbidity assessment (ACE-27) is an essential complement to performance status, being an independent prognostic factor in head and neck cancer.

## Introduction

Performance status is a standardized measure of a patient's overall functional capacity and ability to perform activities of daily living. It is one of the most important factors in oncological decision-making, influencing treatment selection, eligibility for clinical trials, and prognosis.

Stell and Maran's Chapter 4 states: the patient's general condition should **always be classified using one of the methods of measuring performance status** such as the Eastern Co-operative Oncology Group (ECOG) scheme or the Karnofsky status. Stell and Maran's Chapter 9 notes that the first attempt to quantify the performance status of patients with advanced cancer was made by **Karnofsky in 1948**.

---

## Part A: The ECOG/WHO/Zubrod Performance Status Scale

The ECOG Performance Status Scale was published by Oken et al. (1982) and adopted by the World Health Organization (WHO). It is synonymous with the **WHO Performance Status** and the **Zubrod Scale** (after C. Gordon Zubrod who initiated its development in 1960).

### ECOG/WHO Performance Status Scale

| Grade | Description | Karnofsky Equivalent |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| **0** | Fully active, able to carry on all pre-disease activities without restriction | 90-100% |
| **1** | Restricted in physically strenuous activity, but ambulatory and able to carry out work of a light or sedentary nature (e.g., light housework, office work) | 70-80% |
| **2** | Ambulatory and capable of self-care, but unable to carry out any work activities; up and about more than 50% of waking hours | 50-60% |
| **3** | Capable of only limited self-care; confined to bed or chair 50% or more of waking hours | 30-40% |
| **4** | Completely disabled; cannot carry out any self-care; totally confined to bed or chair | 10-20% |
| **5** | Dead | 0% |

Stell and Maran's Chapter 4 (Table 4.1) reproduces this exact scale as the standard for HNC patient assessment.

### Clinical Significance

- **ECOG 0-1:** Eligible for most clinical trials and aggressive treatment regimens (surgery, concurrent chemoradiotherapy)
- **ECOG 2:** May be eligible for modified treatment; considered borderline for concurrent chemoradiotherapy
- **ECOG 3-4:** Generally not candidates for aggressive curative treatment; palliative approaches preferred

Stell and Maran's Chapter 7 emphasizes: one study has shown the **considerable impact of the patient's performance status on prognosis** in a wide variety of tumours. As comorbidities increase, the proportion of patients receiving no treatment increases in parallel.

## Part B: The Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS)

### Scale

| KPS (%) | Description | Category |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **100** | Normal; no complaints; no evidence of disease | Able to carry on normal activity; no special care needed |
| **90** | Able to carry on normal activity; minor signs or symptoms of disease | |
| **80** | Normal activity with effort; some signs or symptoms of disease | |
| **70** | Able to care for self; unable to carry on normal activity or do work | Unable to work; able to live at home and care for most needs |
| **60** | Requires occasional assistance from others; frequent medical care | |
| **50** | Requires considerable assistance from others; frequent medical care | |
| **40** | Disabled; requires special care and assistance | Unable to care for self; requires institutional or hospital care |
| **30** | Severely disabled; hospitalization indicated; death not imminent | |
| **20** | Very sick; hospitalization necessary; active supportive treatment necessary | |
| **10** | Moribund | Terminal |
| **0** | Dead | |

Stell and Maran's Chapter 7 (Table 7.3) reproduces the complete Karnofsky index and states it is **the best known** of the measurement tools of functional status.

### Comparison of KPS and ECOG

| Feature | KPS | ECOG/WHO |
|---------|-----|----------|
| **Year developed** | 1949 | 1960/1982 |
| **Scale** | 0-100% (11 points) | 0-5 (6 points) |
| **Complexity** | More granular | Simpler |
| **Primary use** | Research and clinical | Clinical trials (standard) |
| **Interobserver variability** | Higher (more levels) | Lower (fewer levels) |
| **Most commonly used** | Neuro-oncology, palliative care | Most solid tumour trials |

## Part C: Performance Status Scale for Head and Neck Cancer (PSS-HN)

Stell and Maran's Chapter 10 describes the PSS-HN developed by List et al. (1990) as a **clinician-rated assessment tool** specifically designed for head and neck cancer patients.

### PSS-HN Subscales

| Subscale | What It Measures | Scoring |
|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **Normalcy of Diet** | What the patient can eat (from full diet to tube-feeding only) | 0-100 |
| **Understandability of Speech** | How well the patient can be understood | 0-100 |
| **Eating in Public** | Willingness and ability to eat in social settings | 0-100 |

Harrison's Chapter 8 notes: the Performance Status Scale for Head and Neck (PSS-HN) is a clinician-rated instrument that correlates with patient-rated quality of life on all three QOL scales. Objective performance status on the PSS significantly correlated with patient-rated QOL.

## Part D: Comorbidity Assessment — Complementing Performance Status

### Why Performance Status Alone Is Insufficient

Stell and Maran's Chapter 9 explains: comorbidity refers to disease that coexists with and is unrelated to the index disease and has been shown to play a **major role in the treatment, outcome and prognosis** of head and neck cancer. It is an **independent prognostic indicator** even when age and TNM stage have been controlled for.

### Key Comorbidity Indices

| Index | Description | Use in HNC |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **ACE-27** | Modified KFI; grades 0-3; designed for cancer patients | Most widely validated in HNC; correlates with morbidity, mortality, and major complication rates |
| **Charlson Comorbidity Index (CCI)** | Weighted sum of 19 conditions; weights 1-6 | Simple to use; validated in laryngeal and thyroid cancer; less sensitive than ACE-27 |
| **Kaplan-Feinstein Index (KFI)** | Classifies diseases as mild/moderate/severe | Most successful in survival stratification for HNC |

Stell and Maran's Chapter 9 notes: the ACE-27 has been widely used in the head and neck cancer population and shown that **moderate to severe comorbidity was associated with a much higher major complication rate** (55% compared with 4% in patients with no or mild comorbidity scores) in patients undergoing major oral cavity/oropharynx surgery with microvascular reconstruction.

### Other Fitness Assessment Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **ASA Score** | Anaesthetic risk classification (I-V) |
| **CEPOD Classification** | Urgency of surgery (elective/urgent/emergency) |
| **Pulmonary function tests** | Assess fitness for conservation laryngeal surgery |

## Part E: Clinical Application in Head and Neck Cancer

### Impact on Treatment Selection

| Performance Status | Treatment Implications |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| **ECOG 0-1** | Suitable for radical surgery, concurrent chemoradiotherapy, clinical trials |
| **ECOG 2** | May tolerate modified treatment; consider radiotherapy alone; weigh risks of CRT |
| **ECOG 3** | Palliative radiotherapy, single-agent chemotherapy, immunotherapy, best supportive care |
| **ECOG 4** | Best supportive care; palliative measures only |

### Impact on Prognosis

Harrison's Chapter 5 confirms: KPS/ECOG performance status influences overall survival in HNC. Patients with poor performance status may be more suited to a single agent rather than combination chemotherapy.

Stell and Maran's Chapter 7 notes: the prognostic impact of comorbidities could be due to either the physiological burden of chronic disease itself or the selection of suboptimal treatment regimes. For HNSCC, the former is more likely.

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The ECOG/WHO/Zubrod Performance Status Scale is the standard measure: 0 (fully active) to 5 (dead) — adopted by WHO in 1979, formally published by Oken et al. in 1982
> 2. The Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS) was developed first (1949): 0-100% scale with 11 grades — best known but more complex
> 3. ECOG 0-1 is generally required for clinical trial eligibility and aggressive treatment (concurrent chemoradiotherapy)
> 4. Performance status has a considerable impact on prognosis — as comorbidities increase, the proportion receiving no treatment increases in parallel
> 5. The PSS-HN (List et al., 1990) is the only HNC-specific performance scale: measures normalcy of diet, understandability of speech, and eating in public
> 6. ACE-27 is the most widely validated comorbidity index in HNC — moderate-to-severe comorbidity increases major complication rate from 4% to 55%
> 7. Performance status and comorbidity are INDEPENDENT prognostic factors — both should be assessed in every HNC patient
> 8. Inadequate pulmonary function (assessed independently) precludes conservation laryngeal surgery
> 9. KFI is most successful for survival stratification in HNC; CCI is simpler but less sensitive than ACE-27
> 10. Performance status is distinct from quality of life — PS measures functional capacity while QoL captures the patient's subjective experience

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, know both scales by heart: ECOG 0-5 and KPS 0-100 — and how they map to each other
> - The question asks specifically about the WHO performance scale — this IS the ECOG scale (they are identical; WHO adopted it)
> - Key threshold: ECOG 2 is the critical cut-off — patients with ECOG ≥2 are generally excluded from most phase III chemotherapy trials
> - Remember PSS-HN's three domains (diet, speech, eating in public) — this is frequently tested as a HNC-specific measure
> - ACE-27 grade 3 comorbidity has the same prognostic impact as a T4 tumour or N2 neck — a striking comparison from Piccirillo
> - In HNC patients, comorbidity is second only to lung and colorectal cancer — due to shared risk factors (smoking, alcohol)
