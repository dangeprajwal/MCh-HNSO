## Introduction

A tracheotomy creates a direct opening in the trachea (typically between the 2nd and 3rd or 3rd and 4th tracheal rings), bypassing the upper airway from the nose/mouth to the subglottic region. This bypass has profound effects on multiple respiratory functions normally performed by the upper airway.

Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 states: the creation of a tracheostomy or tracheotomy **significantly increases the patient's vulnerability to postoperative pneumonia** and demands meticulous postoperative stoma care with humidification and regular suctioning of secretions.

---

## Part A: Normal Functions of the Upper Airway

The upper airway (nose, pharynx, larynx, subglottis) performs critical functions that are completely or partially bypassed by tracheotomy:

| Function | Mechanism | Structure Responsible |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Warming** | Inspired air heated to 37°C | Nasal turbinate vasculature |
| **Humidification** | Air humidified to 100% relative humidity (44 mg H₂O/L) | Nasal mucosa, turbinates |
| **Filtration** | Particulate matter trapped | Nasal vibrissae, mucus blanket |
| **Olfaction** | Odour detection | Olfactory epithelium (cribriform) |
| **Phonation** | Voice production | Larynx (vocal cords) |
| **Cough reflex** | Airway protection | Glottis, subglottis, trachea |
| **Airway resistance** | Generates PEEP; maintains FRC | Nasal passages, glottis |
| **Mucociliary clearance** | Transport of mucus cephalad | Ciliated respiratory epithelium |
| **Anatomical dead space** | 150 mL of gas exchange space | Nose → terminal bronchioles |

---

## Part B: Physiological Changes After Tracheotomy

### 1. Loss of Humidification and Warming

This is the **most significant physiological consequence**. The nose normally heats inspired air to 37°C and saturates it to 100% relative humidity by the time it reaches the carina (the "isothermic saturation boundary"). With a tracheotomy:
- Dry, cool room air enters the trachea directly
- The **isothermic saturation boundary shifts distally** into the bronchial tree
- The lower airway mucosa must compensate, leading to:
  - **Desiccation of secretions** → thick, inspissated mucus → plugging
  - **Damage to ciliated epithelium** → impaired mucociliary clearance
  - **Increased risk of mucus plug** → life-threatening tube occlusion

Stell and Maran's emphasizes that **humidification** is essential for all tracheostomy patients.

### 2. Reduction of Anatomical Dead Space

Tracheotomy **reduces anatomical dead space by approximately 50%** (from ~150 mL to ~75 mL) by bypassing the nose, pharynx, and larynx. This has several effects:

| Parameter | Effect | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|--------|----------------------|
| **Dead space** | Reduced by ~50% | Less wasted ventilation per breath |
| **Alveolar ventilation** | Slightly increased for same tidal volume | May improve gas exchange |
| **Work of breathing** | Reduced (less dead space to ventilate) | Beneficial in respiratory failure |

However, this benefit may be offset by increased **tube resistance** (small inner diameter creates turbulent flow) and apparatus dead space from humidification devices.

### 3. Loss of Cough Effectiveness

The normal cough mechanism requires:
1. Deep inspiration (2-3 L)
2. Glottic closure (creates high intrathoracic pressure ~300 cmH₂O)
3. Explosive opening with high-velocity expiratory flow

With a tracheotomy, **glottic closure cannot generate intrathoracic pressure** effectively because air escapes through the stoma. This results in:
- Reduced cough peak flow
- Impaired secretion clearance
- Increased risk of secretion retention and pneumonia
- Need for regular tracheal suctioning

### 4. Loss of Physiological PEEP and Reduced FRC

The glottis normally provides **physiological PEEP** (auto-PEEP) of approximately 3-5 cmH₂O during expiration through partial glottic narrowing. This maintains alveolar recruitment and functional residual capacity (FRC).

| Effect | Mechanism | Consequence |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Loss of physiological PEEP** | Glottis bypassed → no expiratory braking | Alveolar collapse (atelectasis) |
| **Reduced FRC** | Loss of end-expiratory alveolar recruitment | Ventilation-perfusion mismatch |
| **Increased shunt fraction** | Atelectatic areas perfused but not ventilated | Hypoxaemia |

### 5. Loss of Olfaction

Olfaction requires airflow through the nasal cavity past the olfactory epithelium in the cribriform region. Tracheotomy diverts all airflow away from the nose, resulting in:
- **Complete anosmia** (inability to smell)
- Reduced appetite (flavour is 80% olfaction)
- Loss of protective warning (smoke, gas, spoiled food)
- Reduced [[Answers/General/Quality of Life in Head and Neck Cancer|quality of life]]

### 6. Impaired Phonation

With a cuffed, inflated tracheostomy tube, expired air cannot pass through the vocal cords, rendering the patient **completely aphonic**. Phonation can be restored by:
- Deflating the cuff (allows air leak around the tube)
- Using a **fenestrated tube** (allows air to pass through fenestration to glottis)
- Using a **speaking valve** (Passy-Muir valve — one-way valve allowing inspiration through tube but expiration through glottis)
- **Finger occlusion** of the tube during exhalation

### 7. Impaired [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]]

Tracheotomy affects swallowing through multiple mechanisms:
- **Tethering of the larynx** — the tube restricts normal laryngeal elevation during swallowing
- **Reduced subglottic pressure** — normally required to assist swallowing mechanics
- **Cuff compression** of the oesophagus (if over-inflated)
- **Reduced laryngeal sensitivity** — loss of airflow through the larynx reduces sensory feedback

Stell and Maran's notes: impaired postoperative swallowing with aspiration will eventually lead to **bronchopneumonia**. Strict adherence to a "nil by mouth" policy with an alternative nutritional route is necessary.

### 8. Increased Risk of Infection

| Mechanism | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------|
| Loss of nasal filtration | Bacterial colonization of lower airway |
| Impaired mucociliary clearance | Secretion stasis |
| Suctioning-related mucosal trauma | Portal of entry for bacteria |
| Biofilm on tube surface | Chronic colonization |
| Aspiration (impaired swallowing) | Aspiration pneumonia |

Stell and Maran's confirms: tracheostomy **significantly increases the patient's vulnerability to postoperative pneumonia** and increases wound infection rates.

## Part C: Airway Resistance Changes

| Factor | Effect on Resistance |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Bypassing nose** | Reduces total resistance (nose = 50% of total) |
| **Tracheostomy tube** | Increases resistance (smaller diameter than trachea) |
| **Inner cannula** | Further increases resistance |
| **Secretion encrustation** | Progressively increases resistance |
| **Net effect** | Variable — depends on tube size vs nasal resistance |

The tube imposes **additional resistance** due to its smaller internal diameter compared to the native trachea. Resistance is proportional to 1/r⁴ (Poiseuille's law for laminar flow) and 1/r⁵ (for turbulent flow). Therefore, even small reductions in effective tube diameter from secretion encrustation can dramatically increase airway resistance.

---

## Part D: Clinical Implications and Management

| Physiological Change | Management Strategy |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Loss of humidification | **Heat-moisture exchanger (HME)** or heated humidification system |
| Loss of filtration | Swedish nose / stoma filter |
| Secretion retention | Regular suctioning; adequate humidification |
| Loss of PEEP/FRC | CPAP if needed; early cuff deflation |
| Anosmia | Patient education; safety measures (smoke detectors) |
| Aphonia | Speaking valve (Passy-Muir); fenestrated tube |
| Impaired swallowing | SLT assessment; cuff deflation during meals; FEES/VFSS |
| Infection risk | Aseptic suctioning technique; hand hygiene; stoma care |

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The MOST SIGNIFICANT physiological change is loss of humidification/warming — causes mucus desiccation, ciliary damage, and mucus plugging
> 2. Anatomical dead space is reduced by ~50% (150 mL → 75 mL) — improves alveolar ventilation but offset by tube resistance
> 3. Loss of physiological PEEP (3-5 cmH₂O) → alveolar collapse → atelectasis → V/Q mismatch → hypoxaemia
> 4. Cough effectiveness is markedly impaired — glottic closure cannot generate intrathoracic pressure → poor secretion clearance
> 5. Complete ANOSMIA occurs — airflow bypasses olfactory epithelium; affects appetite and safety (Stell and Maran's)
> 6. Tracheostomy significantly increases vulnerability to PNEUMONIA — demands meticulous humidification and suctioning (Stell and Maran's)
> 7. Impaired swallowing through laryngeal tethering, reduced subglottic pressure, and loss of sensory feedback → aspiration risk
> 8. Tube resistance follows Poiseuille's law (∝ 1/r⁴) — secretion encrustation dramatically increases resistance
> 9. A cuffed, inflated tube causes complete aphonia — restored by cuff deflation, fenestrated tube, or Passy-Muir speaking valve
> 10. HME (heat-moisture exchanger) is essential for all tracheostomy patients to compensate for lost upper airway conditioning

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, organize your answer around the EIGHT key physiological changes: humidification, dead space, cough, PEEP/FRC, olfaction, phonation, swallowing, infection
> - The "isothermic saturation boundary" concept is key: normally at the carina (37°C, 100% RH); with tracheostomy it shifts distally → lower airways must compensate
> - Remember: nose provides 50% of total airway resistance — bypassing it reduces resistance BUT the tube adds resistance back (net effect depends on tube size)
> - Loss of physiological PEEP (glottic braking) → atelectasis is the mechanism for V/Q mismatch and hypoxaemia — this is why CPAP may be needed
> - The Passy-Muir speaking valve is a one-way valve: allows inspiration through tube but forces expiration through vocal cords — restores phonation, cough, and PEEP simultaneously
> - Tracheostomy + aspiration → bronchopneumonia is the most common serious complication; strict "nil by mouth" + SLT assessment is essential
