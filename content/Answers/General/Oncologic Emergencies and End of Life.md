## Introduction

Head and neck cancers present unique oncologic emergencies due to the proximity of critical structures — the airway, major vessels, and cranial nerves — within a confined anatomical space. These emergencies include airway obstruction, carotid blowout syndrome, hypercalcaemia, and superior vena cava obstruction. End-of-life care in HNC is particularly challenging because of the visible and disfiguring nature of the disease, progressive loss of the most fundamental human functions (speech, swallowing, breathing), and the complex psychosocial burden on patients and families. Stell and Maran's Chapter 47 reports that **53% of HNC patients are hospitalized in the last month of life**, with the most common reasons being bleeding (17%), pain (9%), breathing difficulties (9%), and swallowing difficulties (9%). Harrison notes that "treatment decisions made near the end of life are prone to propel a dying person in a senseless direction" — emphasizing the need for humaneness and beneficence in palliative management.

## Part A: Oncologic Emergencies in the Head and Neck Region

### 1. Airway Obstruction

Stell and Maran's describes upper airway obstruction as a common emergency with a staging system:

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| I | No symptoms at rest |
| II | Dyspnoea on exertion, tachypnoea |
| III | Stridor/stertor at rest, accessory muscle use |
| IV | Respiratory arrest |

**Causes (Montgomery Ch10):**
- Tumour progression blocking the larynx/trachea/tracheostomy stoma
- Secretions, blood, or mucous plugging
- Post-treatment oedema (post-RT, post-surgical)
- Bronchopneumonia, aspiration

**Management:**
- **Stell's Dictum:** "When you think about a tracheostomy, that's the time to perform it" — elective tracheostomy is always preferred over emergency
- Humidified air/oxygen, helium-oxygen mixture (heliox) for tracheal obstruction
- Consider tracheostomy, surgery, stenting, or laser debulking
- Acute terminal obstruction: sedation per crisis pack protocol (see below)
- Montgomery Table 10.12: Breathlessness — diazepam 2-5 mg + oral morphine 2.5-5 mg; sublingual lorazepam 0.5-1 mg for panic

### 2. Haemorrhage and Carotid Blowout Syndrome (CBS)

**Classification (Stell and Maran's):**

| Type | Definition | Management |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **Threatened CBS** | Exposed carotid artery with risk factors | Debridement + vascular tissue cover to prevent progression |
| **Impending CBS** | Sentinel bleed (minor haemorrhage heralding major event) | Urgent angiography — diagnostic + therapeutic |
| **Acute CBS** | Active life-threatening haemorrhage from carotid | Emergent endovascular intervention or surgical ligation |

**Risk Factors (Harrison Ch17, Powitzky 2010):**
- Prior radiotherapy (89% of CBS patients)
- Nodal metastasis (69%)
- Prior neck dissection (63%)
- Surgically exposed carotid artery, flap necrosis, mucocutaneous fistula, deep neck space infection

**Management Algorithm (Stell and Maran's):**
1. Stabilize airway (cuffed tracheostomy tube)
2. Fluid resuscitation, direct pressure
3. **Diagnostic angiography** → identify site and type
4. **Temporary balloon occlusion** to assess contralateral carotid adequacy
5. If contralateral adequate → **ligation or embolization** (lower CVA risk)
6. If contralateral inadequate → **stenting or arterial bypass**
7. Without interventional radiology → surgical exploration with transfixion suture ligation — mortality **9-100%** (median 40%), neurological complications **9-84%** (median 60%)

**Key Fact:** CBS is not a common cause of death — only 2/102 and 1/38 patients died this way in two series (Montgomery Ch10). It is often heralded by minor **sentinel bleeds**, making anticipation and planning essential.

### 3. Hypercalcaemia

Montgomery Chapter 10 states this is "**common in patients with head and neck cancers**":

- **Mechanism:** Paraneoplastic (PTHrP secretion) or bony metastases
- **Symptoms:** Thirst, polyuria, constipation, confusion, drowsiness, cardiac arrhythmias
- **Management:**
  - Mild: Encourage oral fluids
  - Moderate-severe: IV normal saline rehydration + IV bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid)
  - Resistant hypercalcaemia can be a "**terminal event**"

### 4. Superior Vena Cava Obstruction (SVCO)

- **Causes:** Mediastinal lymphadenopathy, direct tumour invasion (thyroid, oesophageal extension)
- **Symptoms:** Facial oedema, neck vein distension, dyspnoea, cyanosis, headache
- **Management (Montgomery Table 10.12):**
  - Radiotherapy or SVC stenting
  - High-dose dexamethasone (12-16 mg/day) for acute symptom relief

### 5. Other Oncologic Emergencies

| Emergency | Key Features | Management |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Spinal Cord Compression | Cervical spine metastases; neck pain, progressive weakness, sensory level | High-dose dexamethasone + urgent MRI; surgical decompression or RT |
| Tumour Lysis Syndrome | Rare in HNC; risk with bulky, rapidly responsive tumours after chemotherapy | IV hydration, rasburicase, monitoring K+/PO4/uric acid/Ca2+ |
| Sepsis/Overwhelming Infection | Post-RT mucositis, neutropenic sepsis, deep neck space infection | Blood cultures, broad-spectrum antibiotics, source control |
| Aspiration Pneumonia | Impaired laryngeal function, fistulae, tracheostomy | NBM, antibiotics, consider tracheostomy/laryngeal diversion |

## Part B: End-of-Life Issues in Head and Neck Cancer

### Causes of Death in HNC

Stell and Maran's Chapter 47 (Table 47.3) reports the causes of death in 36 hospice HNC patients:

| Cause of Death | Percentage |
|---------------|-----------|
| Progressive disease | 47% |
| Bronchopneumonia | 25% |
| Massive haemorrhage | 8% |
| Airway obstruction | 6% |
| Myocardial infarction | 6% |
| Cardiac failure | 6% |

**Place of Death:** Hospital 62%, hospice 19%, home 16%, nursing home 3%

### Key End-of-Life Issues and Their Management

#### 1. Pain Management

- 40-80% of advanced HNC patients experience significant pain
- WHO analgesic ladder: Non-opioid → Weak opioid → Strong opioid
- Montgomery: Analgesic requirements may **increase** as disease progresses
- Continue analgesia even when all other treatments discontinued

#### 2. Fungating Wounds

Montgomery Chapter 10 describes these as deeply distressing:

- **Impact:** Distressing due to appearance and odour; lead to social isolation
- **Pain:** Immediate-release opioids before dressing changes
- **Odour Management:** Metronidazole gel reduces bacterial colonization; charcoal dressings layered over wound dressings
- **Bleeding:** Adrenaline soaks, alginate dressings, topical/oral tranexamic acid (0.5-1 g QDS)
- **Radiotherapy:** Can control superficial bleeding in selected patients

#### 3. Dysphagia and Nutritional Issues

- Progressive swallowing difficulty is near-universal in advanced HNC
- **Decision Point:** Stell and Maran's states that "artificial hydration and nutrition may become **burdensome and inappropriate** towards the end of life and may **prolong the dying process** unnecessarily"
- Harrison notes that gastrostomy is a "humanely applicable" palliative procedure to diminish misery
- The decision to withdraw nutrition requires sensitive multidisciplinary discussion with patient and family

#### 4. Communication Difficulties

- Speech deteriorates as disease progresses (tumour bulk, tracheostomy, cranial nerve involvement)
- Montgomery: Liaison with speech therapist essential; provide alternative communication methods (writing boards, apps, picture charts)

#### 5. Fistulae and Secretions

- **Orocutaneous Fistulae:** Liquid food and saliva pour through; profoundly distressing
- **Excessive Secretions:** Anticholinergics — hyoscine hydrobromide or glycopyrronium bromide
- **Terminal Secretions ("Death Rattle"):** Glycopyrronium 200 mcg SC every 4-6 hours

#### 6. Lymphoedema

Montgomery Ch10:
- Raise head of bed at night; diuretics may reduce tightness
- High-dose steroids may reduce tumour bulk and associated oedema
- Specialized massage if no infection; compression for lymphorrhoea

#### 7. Psychosocial Distress

- Disfigurement causes profound social isolation
- Loss of speech, ability to eat, and altered appearance impact dignity
- Sesterhenn et al. (2008): HNC described as "one of the most unpleasant and agonizing ways a person may die"
- Depression screening, psychological support, spiritual care essential

### The Dying Patient

Montgomery Chapter 10 (lines 18157-18222) provides detailed guidance:

**General Principles:**
1. Discontinue all unnecessary treatments, drugs, and investigations
2. Continue analgesia and anti-emetics; convert to subcutaneous route
3. Anticipate terminal events — keep medications prescribed and available

**Terminal Agitation:**
- **Exclude reversible causes:** Pain, urinary retention, faecal impaction, drug accumulation
- **Management:** Midazolam 20-30 mg/24h SC continuous infusion, or levomepromazine 12.5-50 mg/24h SC

**Terminal Respiratory Distress:**
- Opioids + benzodiazepines SC
- Stop parenteral fluids (reduces secretions)
- Add hyoscine or glycopyrronium for secretions

**Crisis Pack (Montgomery Table 10.11):**

For patients at risk of catastrophic terminal events (especially haemorrhage):

| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Medications** | IV/IM midazolam 10-20 mg + diamorphine (equivalent to 4-hourly dose) |
| **Equipment** | Dark towels and gloves readily available |
| **Protocol** | Do NOT leave the patient alone; DNAR order in place |
| **Planning** | Multiprofessional plan; open discussion with patient/family |

**Quality of Death (Stell and Maran's Box 47.5):**
- Adequate pain and symptom control
- Clear decisions about management
- Being treated as a whole person
- Making preparations for death
- Achieving a sense of completion

**Place of Death:**
- Most HNC patients request an inpatient unit where they are known
- Respect patient's wishes, religious beliefs, cultural needs
- Allow death with dignity; ongoing support from familiar professionals eases the transition

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The major oncologic emergencies in HNC are: airway obstruction, carotid blowout syndrome, hypercalcaemia (common, can be terminal), SVCO, spinal cord compression, sepsis, and aspiration pneumonia
> 2. Carotid blowout syndrome: classified as threatened/impending/acute; 89% have prior RT; endovascular therapy is treatment of choice; sentinel bleeds herald 50% of cases (Powitzky 2010)
> 3. Stell's dictum for airway: "When you think about a tracheostomy, that's the time to perform it" — elective always preferred over emergency
> 4. Causes of death in HNC: progressive disease (47%), bronchopneumonia (25%), massive haemorrhage (8%), airway obstruction (6%) — Stell Ch47
> 5. The crisis pack (midazolam + diamorphine + dark towels) must be prepared for patients at risk of catastrophic terminal haemorrhage; anticipation and planning are paramount

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Sentinel Bleed = Red Flag:** Any minor bleed in a patient with exposed carotid or prior RT/surgery should trigger urgent vascular assessment — 50% of CBS cases are heralded by sentinel bleeds
> - **Hypercalcaemia Mimics:** Confusion in an HNC patient may be hypercalcaemia (common), not brain metastases — always check corrected calcium before imaging
> - **Artificial Nutrition Decision:** Stell and Maran's explicitly states that artificial hydration/nutrition may "prolong the dying process unnecessarily" — this is a critical exam discussion point
> - **Terminal Agitation Checklist:** Always exclude the "4 Ps" — Pain, Pee (urinary retention), Poo (faecal impaction), Pills (drug accumulation) before sedating
> - **Dark Towels:** A simple but powerful intervention — dark towels at the bedside reduce the visual distress of haemorrhage for patients, families, and staff
