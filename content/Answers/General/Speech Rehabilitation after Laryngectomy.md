## Introduction

Total laryngectomy results in the permanent loss of the vocal cords and normal voice production, along with the formation of a permanent neck stoma and separation of the trachea from the oesophagus into discrete systems. As described in Montgomery's textbook (Chapter 32), the loss of the voice, altered [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]], and a permanent tracheostoma continue to have profound effects on the patient's physical and psychological rehabilitation. For patients who undergo laryngectomy, the speech pathologist is a critical member of the multidisciplinary team because of the loss of laryngeal voice and the inability to produce audible and intelligible speech.

### What is Speech?

Speech is the production of meaningful audible sounds through the coordinated action of three systems:
1. **Initiator (Power source):** Moving column of air from the lungs
2. **Vibrator (Sound source):** Vocal cords — the approximated vocal folds vibrate as air passes through them, producing a fundamental tone
3. **Resonators and articulators:** The pharynx, oral cavity, nasal cavity amplify and shape the sound; the tongue, teeth, lips, and soft palate articulate the sound into recognizable speech

After total laryngectomy, the vibrator (vocal cords) is removed and the initiator (lungs) is disconnected from the vocal tract by the permanent tracheostomy. All methods of alaryngeal speech must therefore provide an alternative vibrator and, in some methods, an alternative initiator.

---

## Part A: Methods of Alaryngeal Speech

Three options are available for voice restoration after total laryngectomy: oesophageal speech, the artificial larynx (electrolarynx), and surgical voice restoration (tracheoesophageal voice prosthesis). As described in Scott-Brown's, SVR has largely replaced the other methods, being easier to learn and producing better quality voice.

### Comparison of Alaryngeal Speech Methods

| Feature | Oesophageal Speech | Electrolarynx | TEP/SVR |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|---------|
| Initiator | Oesophageal air (40-70 ml) | Battery-powered vibrator | Lungs (500 ml) |
| Vibrator | PE segment | Electronic tone | PE segment |
| Hands-free | Yes | No (one hand) | Digital occlusion or ATSV |
| Learning time | Months (prolonged) | Hours-days (rapid) | Days-weeks |
| Success rate | <30% | ~90% | 70-90% |
| Voice quality | Low intensity, interrupted | Mechanical, monotone | Most natural, loud |
| Equipment | None | Electronic device | Voice prosthesis |
| Maintenance | None | Battery replacement | Regular prosthesis changes |

## Part B: Oesophageal Speech

Oesophageal speech occurs as a result of compression of oral air into the oesophagus that is expelled past the pharyngo-oesophageal (PE) segment and vibrates the mucosal walls for sound production. As described in Montgomery, the patient must rapidly impound air into the oesophagus and then expel air into the mouth to produce fluent speech. Resonation occurs in the vocal tract (pharynx, mouth, nose) and articulation of words takes place in the mouth using the lips, teeth, and tongue — similar to normal laryngeal speakers.

As described in Scott-Brown's, oesophageal speech requires air to be taken into the top of the oesophagus either by **injection** (consonant injection or glossopharyngeal press) or **inhalation**, and then forced out again causing the PE segment to vibrate and produce sound.

**Limitations:**
- Learning requires determination and prolonged speech therapy — less than one-third of patients are able to speak using successful intelligible oesophageal speech
- Voice is generally reduced in intensity, poorly sustained, has limited fluency interrupted by the frequent need to inject air
- The listener is often distracted by expiratory noise from the stoma
- Only 40-70 ml of air available (vs. 500 ml with TEP)

---

## Part C: Artificial Larynx (Electrolarynx)

The electrolarynx produces a sound that is then transmitted into the resonating cavities either by direct pressure of the machine against the skin of the upper neck or through a tube directly into the oral cavity. As described in Montgomery, voice acquisition is usually rapid and does not interfere with the subsequent acquisition of oesophageal or TE speech. Early introduction of the artificial larynx often relieves the emotional frustration of not being able to speak during the immediate postoperative period.

**Types:**
- **Transcervical devices:** Vibrating head placed against neck skin (e.g., Servox, Western Electric) — vibrations transmitted through tissues into oral cavity
- **Intra-oral devices:** Vibrations delivered directly into the mouth via a small tube — useful when neck tissues are too fibrotic or irradiated for transcervical transmission

**Advantages:** Rapid acquisition, no surgery required, useful as back-up device
**Limitations:** Mechanical/robotic sound quality, requires one hand to operate, social stigma

Despite the disadvantages, all laryngectomized patients should be encouraged to use an artificial larynx as a back-up in the event that their primary mode of alaryngeal communication fails.

## Part D: Tracheoesophageal Voice Prosthesis (TEP/SVR)

### Mechanism of Voice Production

The technique involves creating a tracheo-oesophageal puncture (TEP) — a fistula through the posterior tracheal wall into the oesophagus. This TEP is stented open with a one-way valve made of medical-grade silicone. As described in Scott-Brown's, air can pass into the oesophagus for voice production when the stomal exit is temporarily covered, but food and fluid are not aspirated.

**Physics of TEP voice production:**
1. Patient occludes the stoma (digitally, or via hands-free adjustable tracheostoma valve/ATSV)
2. Exhaled air from the lungs (up to 500 ml) is diverted through the one-way valve into the upper oesophagus
3. Air passes upward through the PE segment, causing the pharyngeal/oesophageal mucosa to vibrate — producing a fundamental tone
4. This sound is amplified by the resonating cavities (pharynx, nose, mouth) and articulated into speech by the tongue, teeth, lips, and palate

As Montgomery notes, the major difference between TE and oesophageal speech is the volume and capacity of the air reservoirs. For oesophageal speech, only 40-70 ml of air can be trapped in the upper oesophagus, whereas a maximum tidal capacity of 500 ml is available for TE speech — comparable to normal lung-powered laryngeal voice. This means that TE speech is much louder and more sustained than oesophageal voice.

### Primary vs. Secondary TEP

**Primary TEP:** Performed at the time of laryngectomy — preferred approach. As described in Scott-Brown's, the puncture is positioned in the midline about 10-15 mm below the cut edge of the posterior tracheal wall. Curved artery forceps are inserted through the pharyngeal defect into the upper oesophagus, and a scalpel is used to make a horizontal incision through mucosa and muscle onto the tip of the forceps.

**Secondary TEP:** Performed as a separate procedure in patients who have complex resections and reconstructions, those undergoing salvage surgery, or those who have failed oesophageal speech acquisition.

### Types of Voice Prostheses

| Prosthesis | Year | Type |
|-----------|------|------|
| Blom-Singer duckbill | 1979 | Patient-changed (exdwelling) |
| Blom-Singer low pressure | 1983 | Patient-changed |
| Blom-Singer indwelling | 1995 | Clinician-changed |
| Provox | 1988 | Indwelling |
| Provox 2 | 1997 | Indwelling |
| Provox Vega | 2009 | Indwelling (latest generation) |

As Scott-Brown's notes, the Blom-Singer and Provox prostheses range include valves of different diameters, lengths, opening pressures and design as well as custom-made varieties. **Exdwelling** prostheses can be self-changed by the patient; **indwelling** prostheses are changed by the healthcare professional only and can remain in situ over extended periods.

## Part E: Surgical Optimization for Voice

### Cricopharyngeal Myotomy

As described in Scott-Brown's, once the larynx is removed, a myotomy of the upper oesophageal sphincter is carried out. This is important to avoid hypertonicity and spasm of these muscles during attempted phonation, and to allow expansion of the upper oesophagus providing an air reservoir below the PE segment. Hypertonicity or spasm will interrupt the flow of air to a varying degree, restricting or completely stopping voice production. This spasm is a cause of TEP speech failure in 10-12% of patients.

A short posterior midline myotomy is carried out over a distance of 4-5 cm starting from just below the level of the TEP site, extending cranially into the fibres of the thyropharyngeus. The upper two-thirds of the vibratory segment is spared, leaving these muscles tonic for good voice production.

### Pharyngeal Closure Technique

- **Horizontal closure** produces a wider pharynx above the PE segment and may improve resonance for speech
- **Constrictor muscle repair** creates a suitable tonic PE segment for vibration — if muscles are hypotonic (e.g., after total pharyngolaryngectomy with reconstruction), the voice will be weak
- **Suprahyoid muscle reattachment** strengthens the mucosa above the repair, avoiding a pseudoepiglottis and anterior pouch

### Mucosal Width

A transverse, unstretched mucosal width of at least **6 cm** at the narrowest point is necessary to enable adequate swallowing and effortless tracheo-oesophageal speech. Augmentation with a flap is preferable if the residual mucosal strip is less than 6 cm.

## Part F: PE Segment Tonicity and Management of Hypertonicity

### The PE Segment

The PE segment is the source of sound production for both oesophageal and TE speakers. For optimal outcomes, the newly created vibratory segment must dilate on swallow and vibrate for voice — surrounded by tonic muscles. As Scott-Brown's describes:

- **Hypotonicity:** Voice quality is low, weak, whispery and often wet; swallowing is functional. Managed using digital pressure against the outside of the neck
- **Hypertonicity:** Swallowing requires increased pressure; voice is high pitched, effortful, and difficult or impossible to maintain

### Management of Hypertonicity

Montgomery notes that videofluoroscopy shows approximately 60% of patients with poor or failed speech have hypertonic or spasmodic PE segments. Management options:

1. **Prevention at surgery:** Cricopharyngeal myotomy (primary myotomy at time of laryngectomy)
2. **Botulinum toxin injection:** Now preferred over surgical alternatives. As Scott-Brown's describes, toxin is injected unilaterally at three sites along the myotomy line. Dosage is influenced by the degree of hypertonicity, neck fibrosis, and whether a primary myotomy was performed. A single treatment is often sufficient
3. **Pharmacological:** Low opening pressure prosthesis selection for mild hypertonicity
4. **Surgical neurectomy:** Unilateral pharyngeal plexus neurectomy (3-5 branches identified with nerve stimulator and divided) — not widely used
5. **Open myotomy:** Rarely performed since introduction of botulinum toxin, as it may cause hypotonicity and risks perforation

## Part G: Problem-Solving — Prosthesis Complications

### Central Leakage (Through the Prosthesis)

**Candida albicans** is the most common cause. As described in Scott-Brown's, Candida spp. thrive in warm moist environments with a pH near 6.5 (e.g., oesophagus). A voice prosthesis acts as a foreign body, becoming covered by a biofilm of bacteria and yeasts. Candida interferes with the prosthesis valve mechanism, causing leakage and aspiration or increasing airflow resistance.

**Management:**
- Daily brushing and flushing of prosthesis in situ
- Liquid antifungal (nystatin) through prosthesis
- Exdwelling prostheses: regular removal, washing, overnight sterilization in 3% hydrogen peroxide
- Mycological analysis if prosthesis lifetime is too short — targeted antifungal
- Dietary adjustment including probiotics
- Prosthesis replacement when valve function fails

### Peripheral Leakage (Around the Prosthesis)

Occurs when tissue around the TEP no longer beds down snugly onto the prosthesis barrel. As Scott-Brown's notes, reported reasons include:
- Oversized prosthesis pistoning within the TEP
- Thinned or weakened party wall (below 8 mm), associated with wider diameter prosthesis, often in patients with concurrent comorbidity (e.g., diabetes)

**Management:**
- Minimize trauma by avoiding TEP dilation and reducing number of changes
- Successively smaller diameter catheters to shrink the TEP
- Snugly fitting extended flange prosthesis
- Injecting a filler to bulk the party wall
- Purse string suture procedures (reported poor success rates)

### Granulation Tissue

Connective tissue occurring as an acute inflammatory response to interaction between prosthesis, Candida, and patient tissue. Managing the Candida colonization is essential to prevent recurrence.

## Part H: Speech Rehabilitation after Free Flap Reconstruction

When resection extends beyond the larynx to include the pharynx or oesophagus, the pharyngeal mucosa is replaced by skin (cutaneous, myocutaneous, or free flaps) or intestinal mucosa (free jejunum or transposed stomach). As Montgomery describes, this alters the dynamics of the new PE segment and vocal tract, and may significantly influence the quality of the resulting voice.

Key considerations:
- **Fasciocutaneous flaps** (ALT, radial forearm) remain adynamic and passive conduits — patients are more likely to use TE voice for routine conversation than those with jejunal flaps
- **Jejunal flaps** retain peristaltic function not coordinated with native pharynx — more dysphagia; can allow TEP speech but often poor quality
- **Vascularized tissue reconstruction** is preferred in salvage laryngectomy after chemoradiotherapy — reduces pharyngocutaneous fistula by one-third
- **Minimum mucosal width** of 6 cm is critical — below this, augmentation with a flap is necessary

---

## Part I: Heat and Moisture Exchange (HME)

Nasal functions of air humidification, filtration, and warming are lost when air is taken in via the stoma. As described in Scott-Brown's, an HME works as an artificial nose, reducing mucus over-production, warming and filtering the air, reducing coughing and assisting sleep. Several randomized studies have shown clear benefit from HME use in reducing pulmonary symptoms and improving lung function. Ideally, an HME should be provided within a few days of surgery.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Speech requires an initiator (air source), vibrator (sound source), and resonators/articulators
> 2. Three methods of alaryngeal speech: oesophageal speech (<30% success), electrolarynx (~90%), TEP/SVR (70-90%)
> 3. TEP diverts lung air (500 ml) through a one-way valve into the oesophagus — vibrates the PE segment for voice
> 4. Oesophageal speech uses only 40-70 ml of air — TEP uses 500 ml (comparable to normal laryngeal voice)
> 5. Primary TEP at time of laryngectomy is the preferred approach — positioned 10-15 mm below the tracheal cut edge
> 6. Cricopharyngeal myotomy (4-5 cm) is essential to prevent PE spasm — a cause of TEP failure in 10-12%
> 7. Minimum 6 cm unstretched mucosal width is needed for adequate swallowing and effortless TE speech
> 8. PE segment hypertonicity is managed with botulinum toxin injection — preferred over surgical myotomy
> 9. Central leakage is caused by Candida biofilm on the prosthesis; peripheral leakage by TEP tract enlargement
> 10. Fasciocutaneous flaps (ALT, RFFF) produce better TE voice outcomes than jejunal free flaps

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Always offer patients all three speech rehabilitation options preoperatively — but counsel that TEP/SVR is the gold standard with the most natural voice
> - Patients treated with total laryngectomy and TE voice restoration often have better functional outcomes and QOL than those treated with organ preservation protocols that spare but cripple the larynx (Montgomery)
> - The electrolarynx should be introduced immediately postoperatively as a temporary communication aid — it does not interfere with subsequent TE speech acquisition
> - When performing myotomy, spare the upper two-thirds of the vibratory segment for good voice — only the lower third (below the TEP level) should be relaxed
> - If a patient cannot achieve TE voice, always investigate with videofluoroscopy before assuming prosthesis failure — 60% of failures are due to PE segment hypertonicity that is treatable
> - In post-chemoradiotherapy salvage laryngectomy, use vascularized tissue reconstruction even when primary closure is possible — reduces fistula risk by one-third
