# Hyponatremia Management in the Postoperative Setting

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Hyponatremia management in the postoperative setting (PGI 2021)

**Source:** Jatin Shah - Head and Neck Surgery and Oncology, Chapter 7 (Postoperative Care — Skull Base); Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 27 (Pituitary Surgery — SIADH and DI), Chapter 32 (Hypopharyngeal Cancer — [[Answers/General/Paraneoplastic Syndrome|paraneoplastic syndrome]])

## Introduction

Hyponatremia (serum Na⁺ <135 mmol/L) is the most common electrolyte disorder in hospitalized patients and is particularly prevalent after major head and neck surgery. A recent tertiary cancer centre study found postoperative hyponatremia in **67% of HNC surgical patients**, with 32% classified as moderate-to-severe.

In head and neck surgery, hyponatremia most commonly results from:
1. **Excess free water administration** during perioperative fluid management
2. **SIADH** — caused by surgical stress, pain, anaesthetic agents, or ectopic ADH secretion by tumours
3. **Cerebral salt wasting (CSW)** — particularly after skull base surgery
4. **Medications** — opioids, carbamazepine, vincristine, cisplatin

Jatin Shah's Chapter 7 emphasizes the importance of keeping skull base surgery patients on the "dry" side to avoid fluid overload, and Stell and Maran's Chapter 27 describes SIADH as a key postoperative complication causing water retention and fall in plasma sodium levels, with confusion, convulsions, and the need for strict fluid restriction.

## Part A: Classification

### By Severity

| Classification | Serum Na⁺ (mmol/L) | Symptoms |
|---------------|---------------------|----------|
| **Mild** | 130-134 | Often asymptomatic; may have fatigue, nausea |
| **Moderate** | 125-129 | Headache, lethargy, confusion, muscle cramps |
| **Severe** | <125 | Seizures, coma, respiratory arrest, cerebral oedema |

### By Onset

| Onset | Duration | Risk |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Acute** | <48 hours | High risk of cerebral oedema and herniation; requires urgent treatment |
| **Chronic** | >48 hours | Brain has adapted; high risk of ODS if corrected too rapidly |

### By Volume Status

| Volume Status | Mechanism | Common Causes in HNC |
|--------------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Hypovolaemic** | Na⁺ loss > water loss | Surgical blood loss, drains, GI losses, diuretics |
| **Euvolaemic** | Water retention with normal Na⁺ stores | **SIADH** (most common in HNC), hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency |
| **Hypervolaemic** | Total body Na⁺ and water both increased | Heart failure, cirrhosis, renal failure (less common in HNC postop) |

## Part B: Aetiology in Head and Neck Surgery

### 1. SIADH (Most Common)

SIADH accounts for approximately one-third of all hospital-acquired hyponatremia cases. In HNC patients, SIADH may be caused by:

- **Surgical stress and pain** — the most common trigger postoperatively
- **Ectopic ADH production** by tumours (especially small cell carcinoma of larynx, NPC)
- **Medications:** opioid analgesics, carbamazepine, SSRIs, cyclophosphamide, vincristine, cisplatin
- **Anaesthetic agents:** propofol, volatile anaesthetics
- **Positive pressure ventilation** — reduces venous return, stimulates ADH release
- **Nausea and vomiting** — potent stimuli for ADH release

**Diagnostic criteria for SIADH (Bartter and Schwartz):**
1. Serum osmolality <275 mOsm/kg (hypotonic)
2. Urine osmolality >100 mOsm/kg (inappropriately concentrated)
3. Urine sodium >30 mmol/L (euvolaemic)
4. Clinical euvolaemia (no oedema, no dehydration)
5. Normal thyroid and adrenal function
6. No diuretic use

### 2. Cerebral Salt Wasting (CSW)

Particularly relevant after **skull base surgery** (Jatin Shah's Chapter 7). CSW results from:
- Disruption of the hypothalamic-pituitary axis
- Release of brain natriuretic peptide (BNP)
- Renal sodium wasting with consequent volume depletion

**Critical distinction from SIADH:**

| Parameter | SIADH | CSW |
|-----------|-------|-----|
| **Volume status** | Euvolaemic | Hypovolaemic |
| **Urine sodium** | >30 mmol/L | >30 mmol/L |
| **Serum uric acid** | Low | Low |
| **Central venous pressure** | Normal/high | Low |
| **Response to fluid restriction** | Improves | Worsens |
| **Treatment** | Fluid restriction | Volume resuscitation with saline |

This distinction is clinically critical: fluid restriction for CSW will worsen hypovolaemia, while fluid resuscitation for SIADH will worsen hyponatremia.

### 3. Dilutional Hyponatremia

Excessive hypotonic fluid administration perioperatively — a common iatrogenic cause. Risk factors include:
- Large-volume irrigation during surgery
- Prolonged use of 5% dextrose for maintenance fluids
- Excessive free water in enteral feeding

### 4. Other Causes Relevant to HNC

- **Hypothyroidism** — following thyroidectomy or radiation to the thyroid
- **Adrenal insufficiency** — pituitary surgery, metastatic disease
- **Cisplatin-induced nephrotoxicity** — renal salt wasting
- **Diabetes insipidus followed by SIADH** — triphasic response after pituitary/skull base surgery (DI → SIADH → DI)

## Part C: Diagnostic Approach

### Step-by-Step Algorithm

**Step 1:** Confirm true hyponatremia
- Exclude pseudohyponatremia (hyperglycaemia, hyperlipidaemia, hyperproteinaemia)
- Corrected Na⁺ for hyperglycaemia: for every 5.5 mmol/L glucose rise above normal, add 2.4 mmol/L to measured Na⁺

**Step 2:** Assess serum osmolality
- Low (<275 mOsm/kg) → true hypotonic hyponatremia → proceed
- Normal/high → pseudohyponatremia or osmotic shift (mannitol, contrast)

**Step 3:** Assess volume status clinically
- Examine JVP, skin turgor, mucous membranes, blood pressure, heart rate
- Check urine sodium and osmolality

**Step 4:** Urine osmolality and sodium

| Urine Osm | Urine Na⁺ | Interpretation |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| <100 mOsm/kg | Low | Excess water intake (psychogenic polydipsia) |
| >100 mOsm/kg | <30 mmol/L | Hypovolaemic: extrarenal losses (GI, drains) |
| >100 mOsm/kg | >30 mmol/L | SIADH, CSW, adrenal insufficiency, renal losses |

**Step 5:** Additional investigations
- Thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4)
- Morning cortisol / short synacthen test
- Serum uric acid (low in both SIADH and CSW)

## Part D: Management

### Principles

1. **Rate of correction is critical** — avoid osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS)
2. **Acute symptomatic hyponatremia** requires urgent treatment with hypertonic saline
3. **Chronic hyponatremia** must be corrected slowly
4. **Treat the underlying cause** — not just the number

### Rate of Correction (Sterns, 1987; Verbalis, 2013)

| Scenario | Maximum Correction Rate |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Acute (<48 hours), symptomatic** | 1-2 mmol/L/hour initially for first 3-4 hours to relieve symptoms |
| **Chronic (>48 hours)** | ≤8-10 mmol/L in 24 hours; ≤18 mmol/L in 48 hours |
| **High-risk for ODS** (alcoholism, malnutrition, hypokalaemia, Na⁺ <105) | ≤6-8 mmol/L in 24 hours |

**WARNING:** Overcorrection causes **osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS)** — previously called central pontine myelinolysis — presenting with dysarthria, [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|dysphagia]], quadriparesis, and "locked-in" syndrome 2-6 days after correction.

### Treatment by Aetiology

#### SIADH (Euvolaemic Hyponatremia)

As Stell and Maran's Chapter 27 states: treatment is by strict fluid restriction.

| Severity | Management |
|----------|-----------|
| **Mild (130-134)** | Fluid restriction to 800-1000 mL/day; identify and treat cause |
| **Moderate (125-129)** | Strict fluid restriction (500-800 mL/day); consider salt tablets ± furosemide |
| **Severe (<125) with symptoms** | 3% hypertonic saline (100 mL bolus over 10-20 min, repeat x2 if needed); aim for 4-6 mmol/L rise in first 6 hours |
| **Refractory SIADH** | Tolvaptan (15 mg OD, V2 receptor antagonist); urea (15-30 g/day); demeclocycline (600-1200 mg/day) |

**Tolvaptan** — an orally active, selective vasopressin V2-receptor antagonist — is increasingly used for SIADH-related hyponatremia in cancer patients (Castillo et al., 2012). It promotes aquaresis (free water excretion) without sodium loss.

#### Hypovolaemic Hyponatremia (Including CSW)

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | **Volume resuscitation** with 0.9% normal saline |
| 2 | Replace ongoing losses (surgical drains, GI losses) |
| 3 | Monitor serum Na⁺ every 4-6 hours during correction |
| 4 | Once euvolaemic, Na⁺ should improve; if not, reassess diagnosis |

#### Hypervolaemic Hyponatremia

- Fluid and sodium restriction
- Diuretics (furosemide)
- Treat underlying cause (heart failure, cirrhosis)

### 3% Hypertonic Saline Protocol (For Acute Severe Hyponatremia)

1. **Indication:** Seizures, coma, or severe neurological symptoms with Na⁺ <120 mmol/L
2. **Initial bolus:** 100 mL of 3% NaCl over 10-20 minutes
3. **Repeat:** Up to 2 additional 100 mL boluses if symptoms persist (check Na⁺ after each)
4. **Target:** Raise Na⁺ by 4-6 mmol/L in first 6 hours (sufficient to relieve brain oedema)
5. **Do NOT exceed:** 8-10 mmol/L in 24 hours
6. **Monitor:** Serum Na⁺ every 2-4 hours during active correction

### If Overcorrection Occurs

- **Immediately** administer 5% dextrose water to re-lower serum Na⁺
- Consider DDAVP (desmopressin) 2-4 μg IV to prevent further water excretion
- Target: bring Na⁺ back below the safe 24-hour correction limit

## Part E: Head and Neck Surgery-Specific Considerations

### Skull Base Surgery

Jatin Shah's Chapter 7 emphasizes keeping patients on the "dry" side postoperatively to avoid fluid overload. The **triphasic response** after pituitary/skull base surgery is particularly important:

| Phase | Timing | Mechanism | Na⁺ Change |
|-------|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Phase 1: DI** | Days 1-3 | Acute ADH deficiency from stalk injury | Hypernatremia |
| **Phase 2: SIADH** | Days 4-10 | Release of stored ADH from damaged neurons | **Hyponatremia** |
| **Phase 3: DI** | Days 10+ | Permanent ADH deficiency (if stalk severed) | Hypernatremia |

### Major Head and Neck Resection

Risk factors for postoperative hyponatremia after HNC surgery include:
- Surgical reconstruction (OR 2.15)
- Intraoperative blood loss >475 mL (OR 1.54)
- Fluid replacement >1650 mL (OR 1.51)
- High Charlson comorbidity index (OR 1.51)

### Cisplatin-Related

Cisplatin causes renal tubular damage leading to salt-wasting nephropathy and hypomagnesaemia. Aggressive pre-hydration and monitoring of electrolytes are essential during cisplatin-based chemoradiation.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Postoperative hyponatremia occurs in up to 67% of HNC surgical patients; SIADH is the most common cause of euvolaemic hyponatremia
> 2. Classification by severity (mild/moderate/severe), onset (acute/chronic), and volume status (hypo/eu/hypervolaemic) guides management
> 3. SIADH diagnostic criteria (Bartter-Schwartz): low serum osmolality, inappropriately concentrated urine, urine Na⁺ >30, euvolaemia, normal thyroid/adrenal function
> 4. Critical distinction: SIADH (euvolaemic → fluid restrict) vs CSW (hypovolaemic → volume resuscitate) — wrong treatment worsens the condition
> 5. Rate of correction is the most critical principle: ≤8-10 mmol/L/day for chronic hyponatremia; overcorrection causes osmotic demyelination syndrome
> 6. Acute severe hyponatremia with seizures/coma: 100 mL boluses of 3% hypertonic saline; aim for 4-6 mmol/L rise in first 6 hours
> 7. Tolvaptan (V2 receptor antagonist) provides mechanism-based treatment for SIADH — promotes aquaresis without sodium loss
> 8. Triphasic response (DI → SIADH → DI) is characteristic after skull base/pituitary surgery — must monitor Na⁺ closely through all phases
> 9. Risk factors in HNC: surgical reconstruction, high blood loss, excessive fluid replacement, cisplatin nephrotoxicity
> 10. If overcorrection occurs: immediately give 5% dextrose water ± DDAVP to re-lower Na⁺

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The exam question is specifically about the POSTOPERATIVE setting — focus on SIADH, CSW, and iatrogenic dilution rather than chronic causes
> - The most dangerous mistake is confusing SIADH with CSW: fluid restriction in a patient with CSW causes hypovolaemia and worsening hyponatremia
> - Always check volume status FIRST — this is the critical branch point in the diagnostic algorithm
> - For skull base surgery patients, remember the triphasic response and monitor Na⁺ every 4-6 hours during the first 2 weeks
> - Correct Na⁺ too slowly = cerebral oedema persists; correct too fast = osmotic demyelination — the safe window is ≤8-10 mmol/L/day
> - In HNC patients on cisplatin, hyponatremia may be multifactorial: SIADH from cancer + salt wasting from cisplatin + free water from hydration
