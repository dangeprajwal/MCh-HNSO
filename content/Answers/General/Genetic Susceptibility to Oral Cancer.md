## Overview

While tobacco and alcohol are the dominant aetiological agents in oral cancer, individual susceptibility varies significantly — not all heavy smokers develop cancer. This variation is largely attributable to **inherited genetic polymorphisms** affecting carcinogen metabolism, DNA repair capacity, cell cycle control, and immune surveillance. Understanding these genetic determinants helps explain why some individuals develop oral cancer with minimal exposure while others with heavy exposure are spared.

## Categories of Genetic Susceptibility

### 1. Carcinogen-Metabolising Enzyme Polymorphisms

The metabolism of tobacco carcinogens occurs in two phases:
- **Phase I (activation):** Cytochrome P450 enzymes (CYP) convert procarcinogens to reactive intermediates
- **Phase II (detoxification):** Glutathione S-transferases (GST) and N-acetyltransferases (NAT) conjugate and detoxify reactive metabolites

Polymorphisms that **increase Phase I activation** or **decrease Phase II detoxification** result in higher carcinogen exposure to DNA.

| Gene | Polymorphism | Effect | Oral Cancer Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| CYP1A1 | MspI (m1), Ile462Val (m2) | Increased carcinogen activation | OR 1.5–3.0 |
| CYP2E1 | RsaI/PstI | Altered ethanol and nitrosamine metabolism | OR 1.2–2.0 |
| GSTM1 | Null genotype (homozygous deletion) | Loss of detoxification capacity | OR 1.3–2.5 |
| GSTT1 | Null genotype | Loss of detoxification capacity | OR 1.2–2.0 |
| GSTP1 | Ile105Val | Reduced enzyme activity | OR 1.3–1.8 |
| NAT2 | Slow acetylator | Reduced detoxification of aromatic amines | OR 1.2–1.5 |

**GSTM1 null genotype** is the most extensively studied — present in 40–50% of the population and associated with a 1.3–2.5× increased risk, especially in tobacco users.

**Gene-environment interaction:** The risk from GSTM1 null genotype is significantly amplified in tobacco users (multiplicative interaction), demonstrating that genetic susceptibility modifies environmental risk.

### 2. DNA Repair Gene Polymorphisms

DNA repair pathways protect against mutagenesis from carcinogen-induced DNA damage. Polymorphisms in repair genes reduce repair capacity, allowing mutations to accumulate.

| Pathway | Gene | Polymorphism | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nucleotide excision repair (NER) | XPC | PAT+/− | Reduced repair of bulky adducts |
| NER | ERCC2/XPD | Lys751Gln | Reduced repair capacity |
| Base excision repair (BER) | XRCC1 | Arg399Gln | Impaired single-strand break repair |
| Double-strand break repair | XRCC3 | Thr241Met | Reduced homologous recombination |
| Mismatch repair | hMLH1, hMSH2 | Promoter methylation | Microsatellite instability |
| Translesion synthesis | POLH | Various | Reduced bypass of UV/carcinogen lesions |

Individuals with **reduced DNA repair capacity** (measured by mutagen sensitivity assays using bleomycin or BPDE) have a 2–3× increased risk of head and neck cancer.

### 3. Hereditary Cancer Syndromes

| Syndrome | Gene/Defect | Inheritance | HNC Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fanconi anaemia | FANC genes (DNA crosslink repair) | Autosomal recessive | 500–700× increased oral/oropharyngeal SCC risk |
| Li-Fraumeni syndrome | TP53 germline mutation | Autosomal dominant | Increased HNC with minimal tobacco exposure |
| Bloom syndrome | BLM (RecQ helicase) | Autosomal recessive | Increased chromosomal fragility → cancer |
| Ataxia-telangiectasia | ATM | Autosomal recessive | Increased radiosensitivity and cancer risk |
| Dyskeratosis congenita | [[Answers/General/Telomerase and Cancer|telomerase]] maintenance genes | Variable | [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Leukoplakia Management|oral leukoplakia]] → SCC |
| Xeroderma pigmentosum | NER pathway genes | Autosomal recessive | Lip/skin SCC from UV sensitivity |

**Fanconi anaemia** deserves special emphasis — these patients develop oral SCC at a **median age of 31 years** (decades earlier than sporadic cases) with a cumulative incidence of ~14% by age 40. The mechanism involves defective DNA interstrand crosslink repair, and HPV infection may play a synergistic role.

### 4. Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS)

Large-scale GWAS have identified several susceptibility loci:

| Locus | Gene Region | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 4q21 | ADH1B/ADH7 | Alcohol metabolism; strongest association |
| 6p21 | HLA region | Immune regulation |
| 9p21 | CDKN2A/CDKN2B | Cell cycle control |
| 12q24 | ALDH2 | Aldehyde metabolism |
| 2q33 | CASP8 | [[Answers/General/Apoptosis|apoptosis]] regulation |

The **ADH1B** locus (alcohol dehydrogenase) shows the strongest and most consistent association — polymorphisms affecting alcohol metabolism modify oral cancer risk independently of alcohol consumption volume.

### 5. Immunological Factors

- Patients on **chronic immunosuppression** (organ transplant recipients, bone marrow transplant) have increased incidence of oral and skin cancer
- **HIV/AIDS** patients have elevated HNC risk, particularly in the HPV-associated oropharyngeal subsite
- HLA polymorphisms influence immune surveillance against HPV and tumour-associated antigens

## Mutagen Sensitivity Assay

A functional test of genetic susceptibility:
- Peripheral blood lymphocytes are exposed to **bleomycin** (induces DNA breaks)
- Chromatid breaks per cell are counted
- Individuals with **>1 break per cell** are considered "mutagen sensitive"
- Mutagen-sensitive individuals have a **2–3× increased risk** of HNC
- Reflects the aggregate effect of multiple DNA repair gene polymorphisms

## Gene-Environment Interactions

| Genetic Factor | Environmental Factor | Combined Risk |
|---|---|---|
| GSTM1 null | Heavy smoking | Multiplicative (OR 5–8×) |
| CYP1A1 variant | Tobacco + alcohol | Synergistic |
| XRCC1 Arg399Gln | Tobacco | Additive (OR 3–4×) |
| ADH1B polymorphism | Heavy alcohol | Multiplicative |
| Fanconi anaemia | HPV infection | Dramatically increased SCC risk |

The concept of **pharmacogenomics** extends to cancer risk: individuals with high-risk genotypes who are exposed to carcinogens have exponentially greater risk than either factor alone.

## Clinical Implications

### Current Practice
- Genetic susceptibility testing is **not yet standard** in clinical [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Cancer Screening|oral cancer screening]]
- Family history of HNC should raise suspicion for inherited susceptibility
- Young patients (<40 years) with oral cancer and minimal tobacco exposure should be evaluated for hereditary syndromes (especially Fanconi anaemia)

### Future Directions
- **Polygenic risk scores** combining multiple susceptibility loci may identify high-risk individuals for targeted screening
- Integration of genetic data with tobacco/alcohol exposure for personalised risk stratification
- Pharmacogenomic-guided [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Chemoprevention in Oral Cancer|chemoprevention]] strategies

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Genetic susceptibility to oral cancer operates through carcinogen metabolism, DNA repair, cell cycle control, and immune surveillance pathways
> 2. GSTM1 null genotype (40–50% prevalence) is the most studied polymorphism — confers 1.3–2.5× increased risk by reducing carcinogen detoxification
> 3. CYP1A1 polymorphisms increase Phase I carcinogen activation, amplifying DNA damage from tobacco exposure
> 4. DNA repair gene polymorphisms (XRCC1, XPC, ERCC2) reduce repair capacity — mutagen sensitivity assay shows 2–3× increased HNC risk
> 5. Fanconi anaemia confers 500–700× increased oral SCC risk with median onset age 31 — the strongest known genetic predisposition
> 6. Li-Fraumeni syndrome (germline TP53 mutation) increases HNC risk even with minimal tobacco exposure
> 7. GWAS have identified ADH1B (alcohol metabolism) at 4q21 as the strongest common susceptibility locus
> 8. Gene-environment interactions are multiplicative — GSTM1 null + heavy smoking → OR 5–8×
> 9. Mutagen sensitivity assay using bleomycin is a functional test of aggregate DNA repair capacity
> 10. Genetic susceptibility testing is not yet standard clinical practice but polygenic risk scores are under development

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Young oral cancer patient (<40) with no tobacco history** → screen for Fanconi anaemia (chromosomal breakage test) and Li-Fraumeni syndrome (TP53 germline testing)
> 2. For the exam, remember the **two phases**: Phase I (CYP — activation) and Phase II (GST — detoxification); increased Phase I or decreased Phase II = increased risk
> 3. **GSTM1 null + GSTT1 null** double-null genotype has the highest risk among metabolic polymorphisms — seen in ~20% of the population
> 4. The concept of **mutagen sensitivity** elegantly explains why some heavy smokers never develop cancer — their DNA repair machinery is inherently more efficient
> 5. Fanconi anaemia patients who develop oral SCC pose unique treatment challenges — **extreme sensitivity to radiation and cisplatin** due to defective DNA repair
