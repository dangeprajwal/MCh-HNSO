# Monoclonal Antibodies in Head and Neck Cancer

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Monoclonal antibodies in head and neck cancers (AMRITA 2018)

**Source:** Scott-Brown's Otorhinolaryngology Volume 3, Chapter 30 (Biologically Targeted Agents in HNC); Scott-Brown's Volume 3, Chapter 6 (Molecular Biology of HNC); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> Monoclonal antibodies (MABs) have fundamentally changed the treatment landscape of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma. Bonner et al. (2006) published the landmark phase III trial demonstrating that adding cetuximab (an anti-EGFR MAB) to radiotherapy significantly improved locoregional control and overall survival in locoregionally advanced HNSCC — the first targeted therapy to show a survival benefit in this disease. Vermorken et al. (2008) established the EXTREME regimen (cetuximab + platinum/5-FU) as the first-line standard for recurrent/metastatic HNSCC, improving median OS from 7.4 to 10.1 months. Ferris et al. (2016) reported the CheckMate-141 trial — the first phase III RCT of an immune checkpoint inhibitor (nivolumab, anti-PD-1) in HNSCC — demonstrating superior overall survival over investigator's choice in platinum-refractory recurrent/metastatic disease. Burtness et al. (2019) published KEYNOTE-048, which established pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1) ± chemotherapy as the new first-line standard for recurrent/metastatic HNSCC, replacing the EXTREME regimen. Cohen et al. (2019) reported the KEYNOTE-040 trial confirming single-agent pembrolizumab's superiority over standard chemotherapy in second-line recurrent/metastatic HNSCC.

## Introduction

Monoclonal antibodies (MABs) are laboratory-produced molecules engineered to target specific antigens on cancer cells or immune cells. In head and neck cancer, MABs have been developed against two principal targets: (1) the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) — to inhibit tumour growth signalling, and (2) immune checkpoint molecules (PD-1/PD-L1) — to enhance antitumour immune responses.

As described in Scott-Brown's Volume 3 (Chapter 30), huge improvements in our knowledge of the biology of cancer have opened up the possibility of developing novel targeted therapies for head and neck cancers. Three specific themes appear most promising: targeting growth factor independence, developing molecularly targeted radiosensitizers, and enhancing antitumour immune responses using immunomodulatory MABs.

## Part A: Anti-EGFR Monoclonal Antibodies

### EGFR Biology in HNSCC

SCCHN very frequently displays upregulated EGFR signalling. As described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 30, EGFR is a member of the c-erbB family of transmembrane type I receptor tyrosine kinases comprising four members (EGFR/HER1, HER-2, HER-3, HER-4). These receptors consist of a glycosylated extracellular ligand-binding domain, a hydrophobic transmembrane component, and an intracellular domain with tyrosine kinase activity.

Binding of ligand (TGF-α, EGF) to EGFR leads to receptor dimerization, autophosphorylation, and activation of downstream signalling cascades — principally the **RAS-MAPK pathway** (promoting proliferation) and the **[[Answers/General/PI3-Kinase Pathway|PI3K pathway]]-AKT-mTOR pathway** (promoting survival and anti-[[Answers/General/Apoptosis|apoptosis]]). Overexpression of EGFR, without gene amplification, is the dominant process in HNSCC.

As described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 6, cetuximab is a monoclonal antibody that binds to the EGFR, inhibits activation of the EGFR pathway, and recruits antigen-directed cell cytotoxicity (ADCC). It is used clinically for the treatment of metastatic head and neck cancer.

### Cetuximab (C225, Erbitux)

Cetuximab is a human-murine chimeric IgG1 MAB targeting the extracellular domain of EGFR. It works through multiple mechanisms:
- **Competitive inhibition:** Blocks ligand binding → prevents receptor activation
- **Receptor downregulation:** Promotes EGFR internalization and degradation
- **Anti-proliferative effects:** Inhibits downstream RAS-MAPK and PI3K signalling
- **Anti-angiogenic effects:** Reduces [[Answers/General/Tumour Angiogenesis|VEGF]] production
- **Antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC):** Recruits NK cells via Fc receptor binding
- **Radiosensitization:** Inhibits DNA damage repair; blocks radiation-induced EGFR nuclear translocation

### Key Trials with Cetuximab

**1. Cetuximab + RT in Locoregionally Advanced HNSCC (Bonner et al., NEJM 2006)**

As described in Scott-Brown's, in a phase III study of 424 subjects with LA-HNSCC, adding cetuximab to radiotherapy significantly improved:
- Locoregional control: median 24.4 vs 14.9 months (HR 0.68, p=0.005)
- Overall survival: median 49.0 vs 29.3 months (HR 0.74, p=0.03)
- The occurrence of **acneiform skin rash** represented a biomarker of favourable outcome
- Cetuximab was associated with higher incidence of rash and infusion reactions, but otherwise grade ≥3 toxicities were similar

**Important caveat:** As Scott-Brown's notes, the clinical importance of these findings has been questioned since the control arm was radiotherapy alone — not the current standard of chemoradiotherapy with cisplatin. RTOG-0522 subsequently tested RT + cisplatin vs RT + cisplatin + cetuximab in 940 patients and found **no benefit from adding cetuximab to chemoradiotherapy**, with worse grade 3-4 mucosal/skin reactions in the triple combination.

**Current role of cetuximab + RT:** Restricted to patients who cannot tolerate cisplatin-based chemoradiotherapy (elderly, renal impairment, hearing loss, poor performance status).

**2. EXTREME Trial — Cetuximab in R/M HNSCC (Vermorken et al., NEJM 2008)**

In 442 patients with untreated recurrent/metastatic HNSCC:
- Cisplatin/carboplatin + 5-FU ± cetuximab (6 cycles; cetuximab maintenance until progression)
- Cetuximab arm: median OS 10.1 vs 7.4 months (p=0.04)
- PFS: 5.6 vs 3.3 months (p<0.001)
- Response rate: 36% vs 20% (p<0.001)

This established **EXTREME** as the first-line standard for R/M HNSCC until superseded by KEYNOTE-048.

### Other Anti-EGFR MABs

| MAB | Type | Key Trial | Outcome |
|-----|------|-----------|---------|
| Panitumumab | Fully human anti-EGFR | CONCERT-1, CONCERT-2, SPECTRUM | All negative; no benefit over standard; more toxicity |
| Zalutumumab | Fully human anti-EGFR | DAHANCA-19 | No improvement in LRC when added to CRT + nimorazole |

As Scott-Brown's concludes, taken together, these data indicate that in the curative setting, anti-EGFR MAB therapy should be restricted to the use of cetuximab plus radiotherapy.

## Part B: Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (Anti-PD-1 MABs)

### PD-1/PD-L1 Biology

The immune checkpoint pathway PD-1/PD-L1 is a mechanism by which tumour cells evade immune destruction. Tumour cells express PD-L1 on their surface, which binds to PD-1 on T-cells, suppressing T-cell activation and allowing tumour immune escape. Blocking this interaction with anti-PD-1 MABs "releases the brakes" on the immune system, enabling T-cell-mediated tumour killing.

As described in Scott-Brown's Chapter 6, in depth analysis of the immune microenvironment has demonstrated that head and neck cancer is characterized by high levels of immune cell infiltration and immunosuppression, regardless of HPV status. PD-1/PD-L1 interaction inhibitors have shown potential for the treatment of a number of cancers. Following positive outcomes in several clinical trials, nivolumab has been approved by the FDA for the treatment of head and neck cancer.

### Nivolumab (Opdivo) — Anti-PD-1 MAB

**CheckMate-141 (Ferris et al., NEJM 2016):**
- Phase III RCT: 361 patients with platinum-refractory R/M HNSCC
- Nivolumab vs investigator's choice (methotrexate, docetaxel, or cetuximab)
- Median OS: **7.5 months (nivolumab) vs 5.1 months (standard)** — HR 0.70, p=0.01
- 1-year OS: 36% vs 16.6%
- Response rate: 13.3% vs 5.8% — but responses were durable
- Better tolerated than chemotherapy; fewer grade 3-4 adverse events
- Benefit seen regardless of HPV status and PD-L1 expression

**FDA approval:** November 2016 for platinum-refractory R/M HNSCC

### Pembrolizumab (Keytruda) — Anti-PD-1 MAB

**KEYNOTE-040 (Cohen et al., Lancet 2019):**
- Phase III: 495 patients with platinum-refractory R/M HNSCC
- Pembrolizumab vs investigator's choice (methotrexate, docetaxel, cetuximab)
- Median OS: 8.4 vs 6.9 months (HR 0.80, p=0.0161)
- Greater benefit in PD-L1 CPS ≥1 (HR 0.74)

**KEYNOTE-048 (Burtness et al., Lancet 2019) — Practice-Changing:**
- Phase III: 882 patients with untreated R/M HNSCC (first-line)
- Three arms: pembrolizumab alone vs pembrolizumab + platinum/5-FU vs cetuximab + platinum/5-FU (EXTREME)
- **Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy** improved OS vs EXTREME: 13.0 vs 10.7 months (HR 0.77, p=0.0034)
- **Pembrolizumab monotherapy** improved OS in PD-L1 CPS ≥20: 14.9 vs 10.7 months (HR 0.61)
- Pembrolizumab monotherapy also improved OS in CPS ≥1: 12.3 vs 10.3 months (HR 0.78)
- Pembrolizumab was better tolerated than cetuximab-chemotherapy

**KEYNOTE-048 established the new first-line standard:**
- **CPS ≥20:** Pembrolizumab monotherapy OR pembrolizumab + chemotherapy
- **CPS 1-19:** Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy
- **CPS <1:** Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy (or cetuximab + chemotherapy)

## Part D: Toxicity Profiles

### Cetuximab-Specific Toxicities
- **Acneiform rash** (most common; paradoxically a biomarker of response)
- **Infusion reactions** (including anaphylaxis — more common with chimeric MABs)
- **Hypomagnesaemia** (monitor magnesium levels during treatment)
- **Skin dryness, paronychia, fissuring** (dermatological toxicities)

### Checkpoint Inhibitor Toxicities (Immune-Related Adverse Events — irAEs)
- **Dermatitis/rash** (most common)
- **Thyroiditis/hypothyroidism** (immune-mediated endocrinopathy)
- **Pneumonitis** (potentially life-threatening; requires early recognition)
- **Hepatitis, colitis, nephritis** (organ-specific autoimmune inflammation)
- **Fatigue** (very common, usually mild)

Overall, checkpoint inhibitors are better tolerated than cytotoxic chemotherapy — fewer grade 3-4 adverse events. However, irAEs can be severe and require early recognition and management with corticosteroids.

|----------|-----------|--------|

| Ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4) | Enhances T-cell priming; combination with nivolumab | Phase II/III |
| Bi-specific antibodies | Target two antigens simultaneously (e.g., PD-1 + CTLA-4) | Early clinical |
| Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) | MAB linked to cytotoxic payload; targeted drug delivery | Phase I/II |
| Anti-PD-1 + cetuximab combinations | Dual pathway blockade (immune + EGFR) | Phase II (promising) |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Three MABs are FDA-approved for HNSCC: cetuximab (anti-EGFR), nivolumab (anti-PD-1), pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1)
> 2. Cetuximab + RT improved OS from 29.3 to 49.0 months in LA-HNSCC (Bonner 2006) — but this was vs RT alone, not CRT
> 3. Adding cetuximab to standard cisplatin-CRT showed no benefit (RTOG-0522) — cetuximab + RT is for cisplatin-unfit patients only
> 4. EXTREME regimen (cetuximab + platinum/5-FU) was the first-line R/M standard until KEYNOTE-048
> 5. CheckMate-141: nivolumab improved OS vs standard therapy in platinum-refractory R/M HNSCC (7.5 vs 5.1 months)
> 6. KEYNOTE-048: pembrolizumab ± chemo replaced EXTREME as first-line standard for R/M HNSCC
> 7. PD-L1 CPS score determines first-line treatment choice: CPS ≥20 → pembrolizumab alone acceptable
> 8. Cetuximab works through EGFR blockade + ADCC + radiosensitization; checkpoint inhibitors restore T-cell function
> 9. Acneiform rash with cetuximab is a biomarker of response — more rash = better outcome
> 10. Immune-related adverse events (irAEs) are the characteristic toxicity of checkpoint inhibitors — require early recognition

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Cetuximab + RT is NOT equivalent to cisplatin-CRT — it is only for patients who truly cannot receive cisplatin (don't default to it for convenience)
> - The acneiform rash from cetuximab is not an allergy — it is an on-target effect; patients with grade ≥2 rash have significantly better survival
> - Always check PD-L1 CPS score before first-line treatment decisions in R/M HNSCC — it determines whether pembrolizumab monotherapy is appropriate
> - Checkpoint inhibitor responses can be slow (weeks to months) but are often durable — "pseudo-progression" (initial enlargement before response) is recognized
> - Hypothyroidism from checkpoint inhibitors may be permanent — check TFTs regularly during and after treatment
> - The paradigm shift from EXTREME to KEYNOTE-048 occurred in 2019 — this is high-yield for exams
