# Tumour Angiogenesis in Head and Neck Cancer

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Tumour angiogenesis (KIDWAI 2020)
> - Anti-angiogenesis drugs — mechanism of action (KIDWAI 2020)

**Source:** Scott-Brown's Otorhinolaryngology Volume 3, Chapter 6 (Molecular Biology of Head and Neck Cancer — Sustained Angiogenesis); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 5 (Angiogenesis-Related Markers), Chapter 7 (Anti-angiogenic Therapy); Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 3 (Molecular Biology — Angiogenesis)

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> Tumour angiogenesis — the process by which tumours recruit new blood vessels to sustain growth — was first proposed as a therapeutic target by Folkman in 1971. Folkman (1971) published the seminal hypothesis in the New England Journal of Medicine that tumour growth is angiogenesis-dependent and that anti-angiogenic therapy could arrest tumour progression — a concept that took two decades to validate but revolutionized cancer therapeutics. Ferrara and Henzel (1989) isolated and characterized vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) from bovine pituitary follicular cells and coined the term "VEGF" — identifying the central mediator of tumour angiogenesis. Hanahan and Weinberg (2000) codified "sustained angiogenesis" as one of the six hallmarks of cancer in their landmark Cell review, establishing the conceptual framework that all cancers must acquire angiogenic capability. Argiris et al. (2019) published the pivotal phase III E1305 trial of chemotherapy with or without bevacizumab in recurrent/metastatic HNSCC — the largest anti-angiogenic trial in head and neck cancer, showing improved PFS but no OS benefit. Dong et al. (2020) published the definitive meta-analysis of microvessel density (MVD) as a prognostic marker in HNSCC, confirming that high MVD is significantly associated with poor overall survival and progression-free survival.

## Introduction

Angiogenesis — the formation of new blood vessels from pre-existing vasculature — is a fundamental requirement for tumour growth beyond a critical size. Stell and Maran's Chapter 3 states: tumour cells are unable to grow beyond a mass of approximately **5-10 mm** without the development of tumour vasculature. This process, known as the **angiogenic switch**, marks a critical transition from avascular dormancy to vascular-dependent progressive growth.

Scott-Brown's Chapter 6 identifies sustained angiogenesis as one of the **six hallmarks of cancer** (Hanahan and Weinberg, 2000), noting that the ability to induce and sustain new blood vessel formation is essential for tumour expansion, invasion, and metastasis.

Harrison's Chapter 5 confirms: angiogenesis-related markers, particularly VEGF and microvessel density, have emerged as **important prognostic factors** in HNSCC and as targets for novel therapeutics.

---

## Part A: The Angiogenic Switch and Its Regulation

### The Angiogenic Balance

Stell and Maran's Chapter 3 explains that the angiogenic switch is governed by a balance between **pro-angiogenic factors** (stimulators) and **anti-angiogenic factors** (inhibitors). In normal tissues, this balance favours quiescence. In tumours, the balance tips towards stimulation.

| Pro-Angiogenic Factors | Anti-Angiogenic Factors |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| **VEGF-A** (most potent) | Thrombospondin-1 (THBS1) |
| VEGF-B, C, D, E | Angiostatin |
| FGF (fibroblast growth factor) | Endostatin |
| IL-8 (interleukin-8) | Interferons |
| PDGF | Tissue inhibitors of MMPs (TIMPs) |
| Angiopoietins (Ang-1, Ang-2) | Tumstatin |

### Triggers for the Angiogenic Switch

Scott-Brown's Chapter 6 provides the molecular mechanisms:

**1. Hypoxia and HIF-1α:** The most important trigger. Under hypoxic conditions, **HIF-1α (hypoxia-inducible factor 1-alpha)** accumulates and activates transcription of VEGF and other angiogenic genes. In normoxia, HIF-1α is rapidly degraded by the VHL (von Hippel-Lindau) ubiquitin ligase complex.

**2. p53 loss:** Scott-Brown's notes that wild-type p53 promotes expression of **thrombospondin-1 (THBS1)**, an anti-angiogenic factor, and regulates HIF-1α degradation. Loss of p53 — the most common genetic alteration in HNSCC — removes this anti-angiogenic brake.

**3. NF-κB pathway:** Scott-Brown's states that NF-κB target genes affect angiogenesis, linking inflammatory signalling to neovascularization.

**4. Oncogene activation:** Activation of RAS, [[Answers/General/PI3-Kinase Pathway|PI3K/AKT pathway]]/mTOR, and EGFR pathways all converge on VEGF upregulation.

## Part B: VEGF Family and Receptors

### The VEGF Family

Stell and Maran's and Harrison's Chapter 5 describe the VEGF family:

| Member | Primary Function | Receptor |
|--------|-----------------|----------|
| **VEGF-A** | Tumour angiogenesis (most potent) | VEGFR-1 (Flt-1), VEGFR-2 (KDR/Flk-1) |
| **VEGF-B** | Embryonic angiogenesis | VEGFR-1 |
| **VEGF-C** | Lymphangiogenesis | VEGFR-2, VEGFR-3 (Flt-4) |
| **VEGF-D** | Lymphangiogenesis | VEGFR-2, VEGFR-3 |
| **VEGF-E** | Viral angiogenesis | VEGFR-2 |

Harrison's Chapter 5 emphasizes: **VEGF-A** is a 45-kDa homodimeric glycoprotein and the principal mediator of tumour angiogenesis. It exerts its effects primarily through **VEGFR-2 (KDR)**, which is the major signalling receptor on vascular endothelial cells.

### VEGF-C/VEGFR-3 and Lymphangiogenesis

Harrison's notes a critical distinction: while VEGF-A drives blood vessel angiogenesis, **VEGF-C and VEGF-D** act through **VEGFR-3** to promote **lymphangiogenesis** — the formation of new lymphatic vessels. This has direct relevance to lymph node metastasis in HNSCC.

### Effects of VEGF Signalling

VEGF binding to its receptors triggers multiple downstream events:
- **Endothelial cell proliferation** — mitogenic effect
- **Endothelial cell migration** — chemotactic effect
- **Vascular permeability** — increases vessel leakiness (hence the original name: Vascular Permeability Factor)
- **Endothelial cell survival** — anti-[[Answers/General/Apoptosis|apoptosis]] signalling via PI3K/AKT
- **Extracellular matrix remodelling** — MMP activation for vessel sprouting

## Part C: Microvessel Density as a Prognostic Marker

### Assessment of Tumour Angiogenesis

Harrison's Chapter 5 describes the method for quantifying tumour angiogenesis:

**Microvessel density (MVD)** is measured by immunohistochemical staining using endothelial markers:

| Marker | Specificity |
|--------|-------------|
| **Factor VIII** (von Willebrand) | Traditional marker |
| **CD-31 (PECAM-1)** | Pan-endothelial; most commonly used |
| **CD-34** | Vascular endothelium |
| **CD-105 (Endoglin)** | Activated/proliferating endothelium — most specific for tumour neovascularization |

### Prognostic Significance in HNSCC

Harrison's Chapter 5 cites a meta-analysis of **12 studies encompassing 1002 patients** which demonstrated that elevated VEGF expression was associated with a **1.88-fold increased 2-year mortality risk** in HNSCC.

Stell and Maran's confirms: studies in HNSCC have shown that **increased microvessel density correlates with tumour progression** — from normal mucosa through dysplasia to invasive carcinoma.

Dong et al. (2020) meta-analysis of 14 studies confirmed:
- High MVD = poor overall survival (HR 1.66, p = 0.001)
- High MVD = poor progression-free survival (HR 2.07, p = 0.003)

## Part D: Anti-Angiogenic Therapy in HNSCC

### Mechanism of Anti-Angiogenic Drugs

Anti-angiogenic agents work by interrupting the VEGF signalling pathway at multiple levels:

| Strategy | Agent | Mechanism |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| **VEGF ligand neutralization** | Bevacizumab (Avastin) | Humanized monoclonal antibody binds VEGF-A |
| **VEGFR tyrosine kinase inhibition** | Sorafenib, sunitinib, lenvatinib | Small molecule multi-kinase inhibitors |
| **Soluble decoy receptor** | Aflibercept (VEGF Trap) | Fusion protein binds VEGF-A, VEGF-B, PlGF |
| **mTOR inhibition** | Temsirolimus, everolimus | Indirect: reduces HIF-1α → decreased VEGF production |

### Bevacizumab in HNSCC

Harrison's Chapter 7 describes the clinical evidence:

**Phase I/II trial (bevacizumab + erlotinib):**
- Patients with recurrent/metastatic HNSCC
- **15% response rate**
- **Median overall survival: 7.1 months**

**Phase III E1305 trial (Argiris et al., 2019):**
- Largest anti-angiogenic trial in HNSCC (403 patients)
- Chemotherapy (platinum + 5-FU or docetaxel) ± bevacizumab
- **Improved PFS:** 6.0 months vs 4.3 months (HR 0.70, p = 0.0014)
- **No OS improvement:** 12.6 months vs 11.0 months (HR 0.87, p = 0.22)
- Increased toxicity with bevacizumab (bleeding, hypertension)
- Bevacizumab is NOT approved for HNSCC

### Other Anti-Angiogenic Agents Investigated in HNSCC

| Agent | Class | HNSCC Evidence |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| **Sorafenib** | Multi-kinase (VEGFR, RAF, PDGFR) | Phase II: modest activity |
| **Sunitinib** | Multi-kinase (VEGFR, PDGFR, KIT) | Phase II: limited efficacy |
| **Lenvatinib** | Multi-kinase (VEGFR, FGFR, RET) | Approved for thyroid cancer; trials in HNSCC |
| **Vandetanib** | VEGFR-2, EGFR, RET | Phase II trials |

### Why Anti-Angiogenic Monotherapy Has Limited Efficacy in HNSCC

Several mechanisms of resistance have been identified:
- **Alternative angiogenic pathways** — FGF, PDGF, IL-8 bypass VEGF blockade
- **Vessel co-option** — tumours grow along existing vessels without neoangiogenesis
- **Recruitment of bone marrow-derived proangiogenic cells** — VEGF-independent
- **Increased invasiveness** — anti-angiogenic therapy may promote a more invasive phenotype
- **Hypoxia-mediated resistance** — VEGF inhibition increases tumour hypoxia, which paradoxically upregulates other survival pathways

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Tumours cannot grow beyond 5-10 mm without neovascularization — the "angiogenic switch" marks the transition to vascular-dependent growth (Stell and Maran's)
> 2. Sustained angiogenesis is one of the SIX HALLMARKS of cancer (Hanahan and Weinberg, 2000)
> 3. VEGF-A is the most potent pro-angiogenic factor; acts primarily through VEGFR-2 (KDR) on endothelial cells
> 4. HIF-1α is the master regulator: accumulates under hypoxia → activates VEGF transcription; degraded in normoxia by VHL complex
> 5. p53 loss removes anti-angiogenic brake — wild-type p53 promotes thrombospondin-1 (THBS1) and degrades HIF-1α (Scott-Brown's)
> 6. VEGF-C/VEGF-D act through VEGFR-3 to drive LYMPHANGIOGENESIS — directly relevant to lymph node metastasis
> 7. Elevated VEGF expression = 1.88-fold increased 2-year mortality risk in HNSCC (meta-analysis of 12 studies/1002 patients — Harrison)
> 8. Bevacizumab (anti-VEGF antibody) improved PFS but NOT OS in R/M HNSCC in the phase III E1305 trial (Argiris et al., 2019)
> 9. MVD is assessed by CD-31 (most common), CD-34, Factor VIII, or CD-105 (most specific for proliferating endothelium)
> 10. Anti-angiogenic monotherapy has limited efficacy in HNSCC due to alternative pathways, vessel co-option, and hypoxia-mediated resistance

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, draw the pathway: Hypoxia → HIF-1α accumulation → VEGF transcription → VEGFR-2 activation → endothelial proliferation/migration/survival → neovascularization
> - Know the VEGF family: VEGF-A (blood vessel angiogenesis via VEGFR-2) vs VEGF-C/D (lymphangiogenesis via VEGFR-3) — this distinction is clinically important
> - The "angiogenic balance" concept is high-yield: pro-angiogenic (VEGF, FGF, IL-8) vs anti-angiogenic (thrombospondin-1, angiostatin, endostatin) — tumour tips this balance
> - Bevacizumab is NOT approved for HNSCC despite improved PFS — this is a key exam fact
> - CD-105 (endoglin) is the most specific marker for tumour neovascularization — it stains only actively proliferating endothelium, unlike CD-31 which stains all vessels
> - Remember Folkman's 1971 hypothesis — it took 33 years for the first anti-angiogenic drug (bevacizumab) to gain FDA approval (2004, for colorectal cancer)
