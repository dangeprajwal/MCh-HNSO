## Introduction

Tumour angiogenesis — the formation of new blood vessels from pre-existing vasculature — is a hallmark of cancer and a prerequisite for solid tumour growth beyond 1-2 mm in diameter. Without the ability to induce a blood supply, tumour cells cannot obtain sufficient oxygen and nutrients and remain in a dormant, avascular state. This concept was first proposed by Judah Folkman in his seminal 1971 New England Journal of Medicine paper, where he hypothesized that solid tumour growth is angiogenesis-dependent and that blocking angiogenesis could represent a novel anticancer strategy. This landmark proposition, initially met with scepticism, launched a field that has since produced over 35,000 publications and led to the development of anti-angiogenic drugs now used in clinical oncology.

In head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC), the relationship between angiogenesis, tumour hypoxia, and treatment resistance is particularly important. Tumour hypoxia — resulting from an imbalance between oxygen supply and consumption in rapidly growing tumours — is an independent predictor of poor prognosis and is directly linked to radioresistance. Understanding the biology of angiogenesis is therefore essential not only for developing targeted therapies, but also for understanding why some tumours respond poorly to conventional treatment.

The process of tumour neovascularization involves a complex cascade of events. First, the tumour releases pro-angiogenic signals that activate endothelial cells in nearby blood vessels. These activated endothelial cells then degrade the basement membrane, migrate toward the tumour, proliferate to form new vessel sprouts, and eventually organize into functional capillary tubes. This new vasculature, however, is characteristically abnormal — tortuous, leaky, and disorganized — contributing to heterogeneous blood flow, areas of hypoxia, and elevated interstitial pressure within the tumour.

## Part A: The Angiogenic Switch

In normal tissues, the vasculature is quiescent, maintained in a resting state by a tightly regulated balance between pro-angiogenic and anti-angiogenic factors. Endothelial cells in normal adult tissues have an extraordinarily long turnover time — measured in years — reflecting this balance. In cancer, this equilibrium is disrupted at a critical point known as the **angiogenic switch**.

The concept of the angiogenic switch was formalized by Hanahan and Folkman in their 1996 Cell paper. They described how early-stage tumours (including carcinoma in situ) can exist in an avascular state, held dormant because the anti-angiogenic signals predominate. At some point during tumour progression, a genetic or epigenetic event tips the balance �� the tumour cell begins producing excess pro-angiogenic factors and/or loses expression of angiogenic inhibitors. This moment is the angiogenic switch, and it represents a key transition in the multistep progression from a pre-invasive to an invasive and ultimately metastatic malignancy.

**Triggers of the angiogenic switch include:**

- **Hypoxia:** As tumour cell clusters outgrow their oxygen diffusion distance (approximately 100-200 micrometres from the nearest blood vessel), hypoxia activates hypoxia-inducible factor 1-alpha (HIF-1α), which transcriptionally upregulates VEGF and other pro-angiogenic genes. This is considered the most important physiological trigger.
- **Oncogene activation:** Mutations in RAS, MYC, and PIK3CA directly upregulate VEGF expression and other angiogenic pathways, even in the absence of hypoxia. Amplification of MYC, which occurs in 10-15% of HNSCC, has been shown to drive angiogenic gene expression programs.
- **Tumour suppressor loss:** Wild-type p53 normally suppresses angiogenesis by upregulating the expression of thrombospondin-1 (a potent endogenous anti-angiogenic protein) and by promoting the degradation of HIF-1α. Loss of p53 function ��� which occurs in 60-80% of HPV-negative HNSCC — therefore removes two critical brakes on angiogenesis simultaneously.
- **Inflammatory mediators:** Tumour-associated macrophages and other inflammatory cells secrete VEGF, FGF, IL-8, and matrix metalloproteinases that further promote neovascularization. This represents one of the connections between the hallmarks of cancer — inflammation promoting angiogenesis.

Once the angiogenic switch is activated, new blood vessels rapidly invade the tumour mass, providing the nutrients and oxygen required for exponential growth. Importantly, the new tumour vasculature also provides the route for haematogenous metastasis �� cancer cells intravasate through the leaky, disorganized tumour vessels to reach distant sites.

## Part B: Pro-Angiogenic Factors

### 1. VEGF (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor) — The Central Mediator

Vascular endothelial growth factor is the single most important pro-angiogenic molecule in cancer biology. It was first identified by Napoleone Ferrara and colleagues at Genentech in the late 1980s, and the comprehensive characterization of VEGF biology by Ferrara et al. (2003) in Nature Medicine laid the foundation for the development of bevacizumab — the first anti-angiogenic drug approved for cancer.

The VEGF family comprises five members: **VEGF-A** (the most important for tumour angiogenesis), VEGF-B, VEGF-C (important for lymphangiogenesis), VEGF-D, and placental growth factor (PlGF). VEGF-A itself exists in multiple splice variants (VEGF121, VEGF165, VEGF189, VEGF206), with VEGF165 being the predominant isoform in most cancers. These splice variants differ in their ability to bind heparan sulphate proteoglycans on the cell surface, which affects their bioavailability and signalling characteristics.

VEGF signals through three tyrosine kinase receptors: **VEGFR-1** (Flt-1), **VEGFR-2** (KDR/Flk-1), and **VEGFR-3** (Flt-4). Of these, VEGFR-2 is the primary receptor mediating the pro-angiogenic effects of VEGF-A — it triggers endothelial cell proliferation, migration, survival, and vascular permeability through downstream activation of the RAS/MAPK, PI3K/AKT, and PLCγ pathways. VEGFR-3 is primarily involved in lymphangiogenesis and is activated by VEGF-C and VEGF-D — this pathway is relevant to lymph node metastasis in HNSCC.

The expression of VEGF-A is potently upregulated by hypoxia through the **HIF-1α** transcription factor. Under normoxic conditions, HIF-1α is rapidly degraded by the von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) ubiquitin ligase system. Under hypoxic conditions, HIF-1α is stabilized, dimerizes with HIF-1β, and translocates to the nucleus where it binds to hypoxia-response elements (HREs) in the VEGF promoter, activating transcription. This hypoxia-VEGF axis is the principal driver of tumour angiogenesis and is the reason why rapidly growing, poorly oxygenated tumours become the most vascularized.

In addition to hypoxia, VEGF expression can be upregulated by growth factors (EGF, PDGF), cytokines (IL-6, IL-8), and oncogenic signalling (RAS, PI3K pathway). This means that even well-oxygenated tumour regions can produce VEGF through oncogene-driven mechanisms — a concept known as "oncogene-dependent angiogenesis."

## Part C: Anti-Angiogenic Factors

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Thrombospondin-1 (TSP-1) | Regulated by p53; inhibits endothelial migration; suppresses VEGF |
| Endostatin | Fragment of collagen XVIII; inhibits endothelial proliferation |
| Angiostatin | Fragment of plasminogen; inhibits endothelial cell growth |
| Tumstatin | Fragment of collagen IV; induces endothelial apoptosis |
| Interferons (IFN-α, IFN-β) | Suppress FGF and VEGF expression |
| Tissue inhibitors of MMPs (TIMPs) | Block matrix degradation and VEGF release |

**p53 is a critical regulator of angiogenesis:** Wild-type p53 upregulates thrombospondin-1 and promotes HIF-1α degradation, suppressing neoangiogenesis. TP53 mutation (60-80% of HNSCC) therefore leads to both loss of cell cycle control AND enhanced angiogenesis.

### The Process of Tumour Neovascularization

Tumour angiogenesis proceeds through a series of defined steps. First, in response to VEGF and other pro-angiogenic signals, endothelial cells in nearby existing blood vessels become activated. These activated endothelial cells secrete matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) that degrade the vascular basement membrane and the surrounding extracellular matrix (ECM), creating space for vascular sprouting. The endothelial cells then migrate toward the angiogenic stimulus (the VEGF concentration gradient) and proliferate to form new capillary sprouts. These sprouts elongate, form lumens, and eventually anastomose with other sprouts to create functional capillary loops. Pericytes and smooth muscle cells are then recruited (primarily through PDGF signalling) to stabilize the new vessels.

However, tumour blood vessels are characteristically abnormal compared to normal vasculature. They are irregular in calibre, excessively branched, tortuous, and have a discontinuous basement membrane with gaps between endothelial cells. They lack normal smooth muscle and pericyte coverage, which makes them leaky and poorly functional. This abnormal vascular architecture results in heterogeneous blood flow, areas of hyperperfusion alternating with areas of stagnation, elevated interstitial fluid pressure within the tumour (which impedes drug delivery), and paradoxically, persistent hypoxia despite increased vessel density. The hypoxia-driven VEGF upregulation creates a self-perpetuating cycle: inadequate tumour oxygenation drives further angiogenesis, but the new vessels are dysfunctional and fail to adequately resolve the hypoxia.

## Part D: Angiogenesis in Head and Neck Cancer

### Angiogenesis as a Requirement for HNSCC Progression

As stated by Stell and Maran's Textbook: tumours cannot grow to sizes beyond 5-10 mm without access to the circulatory system for oxygen and nutrients and release of their metabolic waste products. As a consequence, neoangiogenesis is required for HNSCC progression. Tumours secrete multiple soluble factors — including VEGF, acidic and basic fibroblast growth factors (FGF1/2), and interleukin-8 (IL-8) — to promote vascular ingrowth. In addition, adhesion molecules mediating cell-cell and cell-matrix interactions, such as integrins and cadherins, also contribute to proangiogenic signals. Several studies have linked neoangiogenesis to the development and progression of HNSCC, and histopathological studies consistently show that increased microvessel density accompanies tumour progression. Upregulation of VEGF family members — VEGF-A, VEGF-B, VEGF-C, VEGF-D, and VEGF-E — along with FGF proteins, and downregulation of the anti-angiogenic factor thrombospondin-1, have been demonstrated in a high percentage of HNSCC. Some of these factors, particularly VEGF, have also been detected in the serum of HNSCC patients, raising the possibility of their use as circulating biomarkers.

The expression of multiple key molecules involved in HNSCC invasion, angiogenesis, and metastasis — including MMPs and VEGFs — is a common feature in both experimental and clinical models of HNSCC. Importantly, VEGF expression appears to be an independent predictor of surgical and chemotherapy outcome, suggesting that resistance to these treatments may be conferred, at least in part, through activation of neoangiogenesis. This is supported by the finding that EGFR activation — the most commonly overexpressed receptor in HNSCC (present in 90%) — can directly upregulate VEGF expression, creating a direct link between growth factor receptor signalling and tumour angiogenesis.

### Tumour Hypoxia and Radioresistance

Tumour hypoxia is a well-recognized phenomenon in head and neck cancer that arises as a direct consequence of the imbalance between increasing tumour metabolic demand and the inadequate, disorganized blood supply that characterizes tumour neovascularization. Unlike normal tissue vasculature — which is hierarchically organized with arterioles, capillaries, and venules — tumour blood vessels are tortuous, leaky, blind-ended, and lack normal smooth muscle and pericyte coverage. This structural abnormality results in heterogeneous blood flow with areas of acute (fluctuating) and chronic (diffusion-limited) hypoxia within the tumour.

The clinical consequences of tumour hypoxia in HNSCC are profound. Hypoxic cells are **2.5-3 times more resistant to ionizing radiation** than well-oxygenated cells. This is because the oxygen fixation hypothesis states that molecular oxygen must be present at the time of radiation exposure to "fix" (make permanent) the DNA free radical damage induced by radiation. In the absence of oxygen, DNA radicals can be repaired by endogenous sulphydryl compounds, reducing radiation efficacy. This biological reality is the reason why bulky, poorly oxygenated head and neck tumours respond poorly to radiotherapy alone, and why concurrent chemotherapy (which acts as a radiosensitizer independent of oxygen status) improves outcomes.

Tumour hypoxia has been confirmed as an **independent predictor of poor prognosis** in HNSCC through multiple clinical studies using direct oxygen electrode measurements (Eppendorf probes), as well as indirect markers. Tumours adapt to hypoxia by activating HIF-1α transcriptional programs that upregulate a suite of genes essential for survival in low-oxygen conditions, including VEGF (promoting angiogenesis), glucose transporters GLUT-1 (enhancing glycolysis), carbonic anhydrase IX (CA-IX, regulating pH), and erythropoietin (stimulating red cell production). These hypoxia markers — particularly GLUT-1 and CA-IX detectable on immunohistochemistry — are being investigated as prognostic biomarkers, though they are not yet part of routine clinical practice.

### Microvessel Density (MVD) as a Prognostic Marker

Microvessel density, measured by immunohistochemical staining for endothelial markers (CD31, CD34, or Factor VIII-related antigen), quantifies the degree of tumour vascularization. Higher MVD has been correlated with increased risk of regional and distant metastasis in several tumour types. In HNSCC, the evidence is mixed — some studies report that higher MVD correlates with worse survival, while others suggest the opposite (that highly vascularized tumours may be better oxygenated and more radiosensitive). This discrepancy likely reflects the difference between vessel number (quantity) and vessel function (quality of perfusion).

### Genetic Drivers of Angiogenesis in HNSCC

Analyses of head and neck cancer genomes might at first appear to reveal limited direct genetic disruption of angiogenic regulatory processes. Unlike some cancers (e.g., clear cell renal carcinoma with VHL mutations directly activating HIF/VEGF), HNSCC does not commonly harbour mutations in canonical angiogenic genes. However, the most frequently mutated genes in HNSCC have significant indirect effects on angiogenesis:

- **TP53 mutations** (60-80% of HPV-negative HNSCC): Wild-type p53 normally suppresses angiogenesis through two mechanisms — it upregulates the expression of the angiogenesis suppressor thrombospondin-1 (THBS1) and promotes the degradation of HIF-1α. Loss of p53 function therefore simultaneously removes two critical anti-angiogenic controls, permitting sustained VEGF-driven neovascularization.
- **PIK3CA mutations/amplification** (20-30%): Activates the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway, which in turn activates NF-κB signalling. NF-κB is a master transcription factor that upregulates multiple pro-angiogenic genes including VEGF, IL-8, and COX-2. PIK3CA-driven NF-κB activation represents a hypoxia-independent mechanism for sustained angiogenesis.
- **MYC amplification** (10-15%): The MYC oncogene directly drives expression of angiogenic gene programs, and its effects span multiple hallmarks of cancer — including sustained angiogenesis, self-sufficiency in growth signals, and limitless replicative potential.
- **FADD, TRAF3, CASP8 mutations**: These genes, which are part of the NF-κB signalling network, are recurrently mutated in HNSCC. Their alteration contributes to dysregulated NF-κB activity, which in turn affects inflammation and angiogenesis. Thus, some of the most common mutations in head and neck cancer likely have a significant impact on tumour vascularization through indirect NF-κB-mediated mechanisms.

### Hypoxia Modification Strategies

The clinical significance of tumour hypoxia has driven the development of several therapeutic strategies aimed at overcoming hypoxia-mediated radioresistance. According to Montgomery's Principles and Practice:

**Modification of haemoglobin level:** Haemoglobin concentration is an important prognostic factor for response to radiotherapy in head and neck cancer. It is standard practice in many centres to monitor haemoglobin weekly during RT and transfuse to maintain levels above 12 g/dL. However, the use of erythropoietin (EPO) to maintain haemoglobin has been disappointing — a large randomized trial (351 patients) showed that EPO actually worsened locoregional control, possibly because HNSCC cells may express EPO receptors that stimulate anti-apoptotic signalling.

**Hypoxic cell radiosensitizers (nitroimidazoles):** These compounds mimic the biological effects of oxygen by diffusing to hypoxic cells and "fixing" radiation-induced DNA damage. The DAHANCA-5 trial demonstrated a highly significant benefit with nimorazole added to conventional RT for pharyngeal and supraglottic cancers: improved locoregional control (49% vs. 33% at 5 years) and disease-specific survival (52% vs. 41%). Despite these results, nimorazole is not standard treatment outside Denmark due to logistical constraints.

**ARCON (Accelerated Radiotherapy with Carbogen and Nicotinamide):** This combined strategy aims to combat both accelerated tumour repopulation and hypoxia using accelerated RT, hyperoxic gas inhalation (carbogen: 95% O2 + 5% CO2), and the vasoactive drug nicotinamide that decreases perfusion-limited hypoxia.

**Bioreductive drugs (tirapazamine):** These prodrugs are selectively cytotoxic to hypoxic cells. Tirapazamine is reduced to an active free radical in hypoxic conditions, producing DNA strand breaks, but remains inactive in normoxic tissues.

**Meta-analysis of hypoxia modification:** A meta-analysis of 27 trials in 4,250 head and neck cancer patients demonstrated a 7% absolute benefit in locoregional control with hypoxia modification plus radiation versus RT alone (46% vs. 39%), translating to an odds ratio of 1.35 (95% CI 1.20-1.53) in favour of hypoxia modification.

### VEGF as a Prognostic Marker in HNSCC

Kyzas et al. (2005) conducted the largest meta-analysis of VEGF expression as a prognostic factor in head and neck cancer. Their analysis demonstrated that VEGF overexpression is significantly associated with worse overall survival in HNSCC. VEGF expression also correlates with higher microvessel density and a more aggressive tumour phenotype. Despite this evidence, VEGF assessment has not entered routine clinical practice for HNSCC, partly because VEGF levels are influenced by numerous factors (hypoxia, inflammation, treatment effects) and partly because targeted anti-VEGF therapy has not yet shown transformative benefit in this tumour type. Nevertheless, VEGF remains one of the most extensively studied prognostic biomarkers in HNSCC and continues to be investigated in the context of combination immunotherapy-anti-angiogenic regimens.

## Part E: Anti-Angiogenic Therapy

### Approved Anti-Angiogenic Agents

| Agent | Mechanism | Status in HNSCC |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Bevacizumab | Anti-VEGF monoclonal antibody | Phase II studies in HNSCC; not FDA-approved for HNC |
| Sorafenib | Multi-kinase inhibitor (VEGFR, PDGFR, RAF) | Limited activity as single agent in HNSCC |
| Sunitinib | Multi-kinase inhibitor (VEGFR, PDGFR, KIT) | Phase II trials in R/M HNSCC |
| Lenvatinib | Multi-kinase inhibitor (VEGFR, FGFR) | Approved for thyroid cancer; trials in HNSCC |
| Ramucirumab | Anti-VEGFR2 antibody | Under investigation |

### The Concept of Vessel Normalization

An important concept in anti-angiogenic therapy, proposed by Rakesh Jain, is that anti-angiogenic agents do not simply destroy tumour vasculature. Instead, they can transiently "normalize" the abnormal tumour vasculature — pruning excessive, immature vessels and restoring a more organized vascular architecture. This normalization window — typically lasting days to weeks — paradoxically improves tumour oxygenation and drug delivery, potentially enhancing the efficacy of concurrent chemotherapy and radiotherapy. The concept of a "normalization window" has driven interest in scheduling anti-angiogenic agents before or concurrently with chemoradiation, rather than as standalone therapy.

### Limitations of Anti-Angiogenic Therapy in HNSCC

Despite the strong biological rationale, anti-angiogenic therapy has had limited clinical success in HNSCC compared to other tumour types:

- **Bleeding risk:** This is a particularly serious concern in head and neck cancer patients because of the proximity of major vessels (carotid artery, internal jugular vein) to the tumour and surgical field. Fatal haemorrhage has been reported with bevacizumab in patients with tumour encasing the carotid artery, and this risk has limited the enthusiasm for anti-VEGF therapy in this population.
- **Wound healing impairment:** Anti-angiogenic agents inhibit the neovascularization required for normal wound healing. In a surgical specialty where complex flap reconstruction is frequently performed, this is a significant barrier. Anti-angiogenic therapy is typically held for 4-6 weeks before and after surgery.
- **Modest single-agent activity:** Anti-angiogenic monotherapy in recurrent/metastatic HNSCC has shown limited response rates. Tumours adapt by upregulating alternative pro-angiogenic pathways (FGF, PDGF, Ang-2), a phenomenon known as evasive resistance.
- **Resistance mechanisms:** Beyond evasive resistance, tumours can co-opt existing host blood vessels (vessel co-option) instead of inducing new ones, rendering anti-angiogenic therapy ineffective. Tumours can also recruit bone marrow-derived endothelial progenitor cells to sustain neovascularization.
- **No FDA approval:** As of 2025, no anti-angiogenic agent is approved as monotherapy for HNSCC. Lenvatinib is approved for differentiated thyroid cancer — the one success of anti-angiogenic targeting in head and neck oncology.

### Future Directions

Current research focuses on combining anti-angiogenic agents with immune checkpoint inhibitors. The rationale is that tumour vasculature abnormalities create an immunosuppressive microenvironment by limiting T-cell infiltration and promoting regulatory T-cell and myeloid-derived suppressor cell accumulation. Vascular normalization with anti-VEGF agents may improve immune cell trafficking into the tumour, potentially synergizing with PD-1/PD-L1 blockade. Early-phase trials combining lenvatinib or bevacizumab with pembrolizumab in recurrent/metastatic HNSCC are ongoing and represent one of the most promising directions in anti-angiogenic therapy for this disease.
