## Introduction

Perineural invasion (PNI) is defined as the presence of tumour cells within, around, or along the sheaths of peripheral nerves on histopathological examination. It represents a distinct route of tumour propagation in head and neck cancer and is one of the most important adverse pathological features influencing prognosis and treatment planning. PNI is distinct from **perineural tumour spread (PNTS)**, which refers to macroscopic extension of tumour along named nerves (e.g., facial nerve, trigeminal nerve divisions) that is detectable on imaging — typically MRI showing nerve enlargement, enhancement, or obliteration of the perineural fat planes.

PNI is commonly observed across all HNSCC subsites but is particularly prevalent in certain tumour types: adenoid cystic carcinoma of the salivary glands (up to 80% PNI rate), squamous cell carcinoma of the oral cavity (30-60%), and cutaneous SCC with neurotropism. As described in Jatin Shah's textbook, microscopic perineural invasion does not portend the same poor prognosis as invasion of named nerves such as the trigeminal and facial nerves. However, both forms of nerve involvement carry prognostic significance and influence treatment decisions.

---

## Part A: Definition and Histopathological Features

PNI is diagnosed on routine histopathological examination (H&E staining) when tumour cells are identified in close association with neural structures. Three patterns are recognized:

1. **Intraneural invasion:** Tumour cells within the nerve sheath, between nerve fibres (endoneurium)
2. **Perineural invasion:** Tumour cells surrounding ≥33% of the nerve circumference within the perineurial space — the most commonly used diagnostic criterion
3. **Epineural invasion:** Tumour cells in the loose connective tissue surrounding the nerve bundle

The pathological reporting of PNI varies significantly between institutions, contributing to inconsistency in the literature. Variables that should be reported include: presence/absence of PNI, number of nerves involved, size of the largest nerve involved (small nerve <1 mm vs. large nerve ≥1 mm), and whether the involved nerve is a named nerve.

**Clinical significance of nerve size:** Invasion of large-calibre named nerves (≥1 mm diameter) — particularly branches of the trigeminal, facial, glossopharyngeal, vagus, and hypoglossal nerves — carries worse prognostic implications than invasion of small, unnamed nerves. Large-nerve PNI is more likely to result in clinically significant perineural tumour spread toward the skull base, potentially causing devastating cranial nerve palsies and intracranial extension.

---

## Part B: Mechanism of Perineural Invasion

PNI is not merely passive tumour spread along a path of least resistance. Current evidence, as reviewed by Amit et al. (2016), demonstrates that PNI is an **active, bidirectional process** involving reciprocal signalling between tumour cells and neural components. This tumour-nerve "dialogue" involves multiple molecular pathways:

### Neurotrophic Signalling
- **Nerve Growth Factor (NGF):** Secreted by tumour cells; binds to TrkA receptors on nerves, promoting axonal growth toward the tumour
- **Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF):** Tumour-derived BDNF attracts nerve fibres and promotes neural survival
- **Glial Cell Line-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (GDNF):** Promotes cancer cell migration along nerves
- The result is **neuritogenesis** — tumour cells actively recruit nerve fibres into the tumour microenvironment, which in turn provide growth factors and signalling molecules that promote tumour survival and proliferation

### Chemokine Signalling
- **CXCL12/CXCR4 axis:** Schwann cells and perineural fibroblasts express CXCL12, which attracts CXCR4-expressing tumour cells toward nerves
- **CCL2/CCR2:** Promotes macrophage infiltration at the tumour-nerve interface, facilitating invasion

### Adhesion and Invasion Molecules
- **Neural cell adhesion molecule (NCAM):** Mediates tumour cell adhesion to neural structures
- **L1CAM:** Cell adhesion molecule overexpressed in HNSCC with PNI
- **Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs):** Degrade the perineurium (the nerve's protective sheath), allowing tumour cell entry
- **Mucin 1 (MUC1):** Overexpressed in PNI-positive tumours

### The Schwann Cell Role
Schwann cells — the myelinating cells of peripheral nerves — play an active role in PNI. Upon encountering tumour cells, Schwann cells dedifferentiate, migrate toward the tumour, and create "bridges" that facilitate tumour cell migration along nerve tracks. This process is analogous to Schwann cell behaviour during nerve regeneration after injury, and has been termed "Schwann cell-assisted invasion."

## Part C: Clinical and Prognostic Significance

### Impact on Outcomes

A systematic review and meta-analysis (Chen et al., 2019) confirmed that PNI is an independent prognostic factor in HNSCC:

| Outcome | Hazard Ratio (PNI+ vs PNI−) |
|---------|------------------------------|
| Overall survival | HR 1.72 (95% CI 1.47-2.01) |
| Disease-specific survival | HR 1.79 (95% CI 1.55-2.07) |
| Local recurrence | Significantly increased |
| Regional recurrence | Significantly increased |
| Distant metastasis | Significantly increased |

### PNI by Subsite

| Subsite | PNI Prevalence | Clinical Significance |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| Oral cavity SCC | 30-60% | Predicts local recurrence, nodal metastasis; influences decision for [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Adjuvant Radiation Therapy in Oral Cancer|adjuvant RT]] |
| Salivary gland (ACC) | Up to 80% | Hallmark feature of adenoid cystic carcinoma; skip lesions along nerve |
| Cutaneous SCC | 2-14% | High-risk feature; predicts nodal metastasis and local recurrence |
| Oropharyngeal SCC | 20-40% | [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-positive]] tumours may have lower PNI rates |
| Laryngeal SCC | Variable | Associated with extralaryngeal spread |

### PNI and the [[Answers/Oral Cavity/AJCC 8th Edition Oral Cavity Staging|AJCC staging]] System

Despite its prognostic importance, PNI has not been incorporated into the AJCC 8th edition TNM staging for most HNC subsites (unlike [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Depth of Invasion in Oral Cancer|depth of invasion]] and [[Answers/General/Extranodal Extension|extranodal extension]], which were added). However, PNI is recognized as a **high-risk pathological feature** that influences the decision for adjuvant treatment:

- **Adjuvant radiotherapy:** PNI is listed by NCCN as an adverse feature that, in combination with other risk factors, favours the addition of adjuvant RT after surgery
- **Adjuvant chemoradiation:** The combination of PNI with positive margins or extranodal extension may warrant concurrent cisplatin + RT rather than RT alone (as per EORTC 22931 and RTOG 9501 criteria)

### Clinical Signs of Perineural Tumour Spread

Patients with macroscopic PNTS along named nerves may present with:
- **Pain:** Often the earliest symptom — described as burning, shooting, or electric in character
- **Paresthesia/numbness:** Along the distribution of the involved nerve (e.g., V2 or V3 numbness in oral/sinonasal cancer)
- **Motor deficits:** Facial nerve palsy (parotid/skin cancer), tongue deviation (hypoglossal), vocal cord palsy (vagus/RLN)
- **[[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]]:** Involvement of motor branches of V3 to pterygoid muscles

## Part D: Imaging Assessment

MRI is the imaging modality of choice for detecting perineural tumour spread:

| Finding | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Nerve enlargement | Thickening of the affected nerve on T1-weighted images |
| Nerve enhancement | Abnormal contrast enhancement along the nerve course (normally nerves do not enhance) |
| Obliteration of perineural fat | Loss of the normal fat signal in the neural foramina (foramen ovale, foramen rotundum, stylomastoid foramen) |
| Denervation muscle changes | Fatty atrophy or oedema of muscles supplied by the involved nerve (e.g., muscles of mastication in V3 involvement) |
| Widening of neural foramina | Expansion of bony foramina by tumour — visible on CT |

**Skip lesions** are a characteristic feature of PNI, particularly in adenoid cystic carcinoma — tumour may be present along a nerve with intervening segments of normal nerve, making complete surgical clearance extremely difficult.

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. PNI = tumour cells within/around peripheral nerve sheaths; distinct from PNTS (macroscopic spread along named nerves)
> 2. PNI is an active, bidirectional process — not passive spread — driven by tumour-nerve molecular dialogue
> 3. NGF, BDNF, GDNF, CXCL12/CXCR4, and Schwann cells all facilitate perineural invasion
> 4. PNI independently predicts worse OS (HR 1.72), DSS (HR 1.79), and increased local/regional/distant recurrence
> 5. PNI prevalence: oral cavity SCC 30-60%, adenoid cystic carcinoma up to 80%, cutaneous SCC 2-14%
> 6. Large-nerve PNI (≥1 mm, named nerves) carries worse prognosis than small-nerve PNI
> 7. PNI is a high-risk feature warranting adjuvant RT; combined with other adverse features, it may warrant adjuvant CRT
> 8. MRI is the gold standard for detecting PNTS — look for nerve enlargement, enhancement, and perineural fat obliteration
> 9. Skip lesions (characteristic of ACC) make complete surgical clearance of PNI extremely difficult
> 10. Despite prognostic importance, PNI has not been added to AJCC 8th edition TNM staging for most HNC subsites

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Always ask the pathologist specifically about PNI in the histology report — it is not always routinely commented on unless requested
> - Pain or numbness preceding the diagnosis of HNSCC should raise suspicion for perineural tumour spread — investigate with MRI
> - In adenoid cystic carcinoma, assume PNI is present until proven otherwise — plan the radiation field to cover the nerve pathway to the skull base
> - Facial nerve palsy in a parotid mass is a red flag for high-grade malignancy with PNI — this is a relative contraindication to nerve preservation
> - When PNI involves the trigeminal nerve, trace the involved division back to the Gasserian ganglion on MRI — failure to treat the full extent leads to skull base recurrence
> - PNI-positive oral cavity SCC should receive adjuvant RT even if margins are clear — PNI alone increases local recurrence risk significantly
