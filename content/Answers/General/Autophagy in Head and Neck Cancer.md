## Introduction

Autophagy is a fundamental, evolutionarily conserved catabolic process in which cells degrade and recycle their own cytoplasmic components — including damaged organelles, misfolded proteins, and intracellular pathogens — through lysosomal degradation. The process was first described morphologically in the 1960s by Christian de Duve (who also coined the term "autophagy"), but the molecular machinery was elucidated by Yoshinori Ohsumi through his identification of autophagy-related (ATG) genes in yeast, for which he received the **2016 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine**.

Harrison's Chapter 6 notes that p53 is involved in the regulation of diverse cellular processes including cell cycle, apoptosis, **autophagy**, DNA repair, and metabolism — highlighting that autophagy is now recognized as a core component of the cellular response to stress, alongside apoptosis.

In cancer, autophagy plays a **paradoxical dual role**: it acts as a tumour suppressor in early carcinogenesis by eliminating damaged organelles and preventing genomic instability, but once a tumour is established, cancer cells co-opt autophagy as a survival mechanism to withstand metabolic stress, nutrient deprivation, hypoxia, and the cytotoxic effects of radiotherapy and chemotherapy. This "double-edged sword" makes autophagy both an attractive and a challenging therapeutic target in HNSCC.

## Part A: Mechanisms of Autophagy

### Types of Autophagy

| Type | Mechanism | Selectivity |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Macroautophagy** | Double-membrane autophagosome engulfs cytoplasm → fuses with lysosome | Non-selective or selective |
| Microautophagy | Lysosomal membrane directly invaginates to engulf cytoplasm | Non-selective |
| Chaperone-mediated autophagy (CMA) | Chaperone (HSC70) targets specific proteins with KFERQ motif to lysosomal LAMP-2A receptor | Highly selective |

**Macroautophagy** (hereafter referred to as "autophagy") is the most relevant form in cancer biology.

### Molecular Machinery — The ATG System

The process of macroautophagy proceeds through four sequential steps, each regulated by specific ATG protein complexes:

**1. Initiation**
- Under nutrient-replete conditions, **mTORC1** (mammalian target of rapamycin complex 1) phosphorylates and inhibits the ULK1 complex (ULK1-ATG13-FIP200-ATG101)
- Under stress — nutrient deprivation, hypoxia, DNA damage — mTORC1 is inhibited (via AMPK activation or [[Answers/General/PI3-Kinase Pathway|PI3K/AKT pathway]] downregulation)
- The ULK1 complex is released and activated, initiating autophagosome formation

**2. Nucleation**
- ULK1 activates the **Beclin-1/VPS34/PI3K class III complex**, which generates phosphatidylinositol 3-phosphate (PI3P) at the endoplasmic reticulum membrane
- PI3P recruits downstream effectors (WIPI proteins, ATG16L1) to the isolation membrane (phagophore)
- **Beclin-1** is a key tumour suppressor — it is monoallelically deleted in many cancers

**3. Elongation and Closure**
- Two ubiquitin-like conjugation systems drive autophagosome membrane expansion:
  - **ATG12-ATG5-ATG16L1 complex:** Functions as an E3-like enzyme
  - **LC3 (MAP1LC3) lipidation:** Pro-LC3 is cleaved by ATG4 to LC3-I, which is then conjugated to phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) by ATG7 and ATG3 to form **LC3-II** — the lipidated form that is inserted into the autophagosomal membrane
- **LC3-II is the gold-standard marker of autophagosome formation** — detected by Western blot or immunohistochemistry

**4. Fusion and Degradation**
- The completed autophagosome fuses with a lysosome to form an **autolysosome**
- Lysosomal hydrolases (proteases, lipases, nucleases) degrade the sequestered contents
- Degradation products (amino acids, fatty acids, nucleotides) are released back to the cytoplasm for reuse

### Key Regulatory Pathways

| Pathway | Effect on Autophagy | Mechanism |
|---------|---------------------|-----------|
| **mTORC1** | Inhibits | Phosphorylates and inactivates ULK1 complex |
| **AMPK** | Activates | Inhibits mTORC1; directly activates ULK1 |
| **p53** | Dual role | Nuclear p53 → activates (via DRAM, SESN1/2); cytoplasmic p53 → inhibits (via AMPK) |
| **PI3K/AKT** | Inhibits | Activates mTORC1 |
| **Beclin-1** | Activates | Essential for autophagosome nucleation; inhibited by BCL-2 binding |
| **HIF-1α** | Activates | Hypoxia → BNIP3/BNIP3L expression → displaces BCL-2 from Beclin-1 |

## Part B: The Dual Role of Autophagy in Cancer

### Tumour Suppression (Early Carcinogenesis)

In normal cells and during the early stages of carcinogenesis, autophagy functions as a tumour-suppressive mechanism by:

1. **Removing damaged organelles:** Especially damaged mitochondria (mitophagy) — prevents accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) that cause DNA mutations
2. **Maintaining genomic stability:** By clearing damaged proteins and preventing the accumulation of p62/SQSTM1 aggregates that promote NF-κB signalling and chronic inflammation
3. **Eliminating oncogenic proteins:** Selective autophagy targets specific oncoproteins for degradation
4. **Preventing chronic inflammation:** By removing damaged-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs)

**Evidence for tumour suppression:**
- **Beclin-1** (BECN1) is monoallelically deleted in 40-75% of breast, ovarian, and prostate cancers — establishing it as a haploinsufficient tumour suppressor
- Beclin-1 heterozygous knockout mice develop spontaneous tumours
- ATG5 and ATG7 knockouts show increased susceptibility to liver tumours

### Tumour Promotion (Established Cancer)

Once a tumour is established, cancer cells exploit autophagy as a critical survival mechanism:

1. **Metabolic stress survival:** Autophagy provides nutrients through self-digestion when the tumour outgrows its blood supply
2. **Hypoxia tolerance:** Hypoxic tumour cells (a hallmark of HNSCC) activate autophagy via HIF-1α/BNIP3 to survive oxygen deprivation
3. **Therapeutic resistance:** Autophagy protects cancer cells from the cytotoxic effects of radiotherapy, cisplatin, [[Answers/General/Monoclonal Antibodies in Head and Neck Cancer|cetuximab]], and other therapeutic agents — by recycling damaged cellular components and preventing apoptosis
4. **Anoikis resistance:** Autophagy helps detached cells survive during metastasis
5. **Dormancy maintenance:** Tumour cells in a dormant state rely on autophagy for survival

## Part C: Autophagy in HNSCC — Specific Evidence

### Autophagy and Radioresistance

Autophagy has been identified as a major driver of radioresistance in HNSCC, particularly under hypoxic conditions. Radiation-induced DNA damage activates autophagy through p53-dependent and p53-independent pathways. The cytoprotective autophagy that follows irradiation helps cancer cells:
- Clear damaged organelles and avoid apoptosis
- Maintain energy homeostasis during radiation-induced metabolic stress
- Survive the oxidative stress generated by ionizing radiation

Targeting BECLIN1 and BNIP3/BNIP3L with siRNA sensitizes HNSCC cells to irradiation, highlighting the importance of autophagy modulation to enhance radiotherapy effectiveness.

### Autophagy and Cisplatin Resistance

Cisplatin — the backbone of HNSCC chemotherapy — induces autophagy in tumour cells as a survival response. Inhibition of autophagy with chloroquine or hydroxychloroquine enhances cisplatin-induced apoptosis in HNSCC cell lines. This has provided the rationale for clinical trials combining autophagy inhibitors with standard chemotherapy.

### HPV and Autophagy

The HPV16 E7 oncoprotein is involved in the degradation of AMBRA1 (a key autophagy regulator) and STING (stimulator of interferon genes), suggesting that HPV modulates autophagy to evade innate immune detection. HPV may dampen bulk autophagic flux via AMBRA1 degradation but promote selective autophagic degradation of STING and mitochondria — creating a permissive environment for viral persistence and carcinogenesis.

### Autophagy Biomarkers in HNSCC

| Marker | Finding | Clinical Significance |
|--------|---------|----------------------|
| LC3B (high) | Increased autophagosome formation | Poor overall survival in oropharyngeal SCC |
| Beclin-1 (low) | Reduced autophagy initiation | Associated with aggressive tumour behaviour |
| p62/SQSTM1 (high) | Impaired autophagic flux | Worse prognosis; NF-κB activation |
| ATG5 (variable) | Essential for autophagosome elongation | Prognostic value in autophagy-related gene signatures |

## Part D: Therapeutic Targeting of Autophagy in HNSCC

### Autophagy Inhibition (Most Studied Strategy)

Since autophagy predominantly promotes tumour survival in established HNSCC, inhibiting autophagy to sensitize tumours to radiotherapy and chemotherapy is the primary therapeutic strategy being investigated.

| Agent | Mechanism | Status |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| **Chloroquine (CQ)** | Inhibits autophagosome-lysosome fusion (raises lysosomal pH) | Repurposed antimalarial; clinical trials in combination with RT/CT |
| **Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ)** | Same mechanism as CQ; better tolerated | Phase I/II trials in HNSCC combined with standard therapy |
| **Lys05** | More potent lysosomal inhibitor than HCQ | Preclinical |
| **ULK1 inhibitors** | Block autophagy initiation | Preclinical |
| **VPS34 inhibitors** | Block Beclin-1/PI3K III nucleation complex | Preclinical |

**Combination strategies:**
- **CDK4/6 inhibitors + autophagy inhibitors:** CDK4/6 inhibitor abemaciclib activates pro-survival autophagy via ROS generation in HNSCC; co-inhibition of CDK4/6 and autophagy synergistically decreases cell viability
- **Cetuximab + HCQ:** Cetuximab-induced autophagy may limit its therapeutic efficacy; combining with autophagy inhibition may overcome resistance
- **Cisplatin + CQ/HCQ:** Enhanced cisplatin-induced apoptosis in HNSCC preclinical models

### Autophagy Induction (Context-Dependent)

In specific contexts — particularly early carcinogenesis or when the goal is to induce autophagic cell death (type II [[Answers/General/Apoptosis|programmed cell death]]) — promoting excessive autophagy may be therapeutic:
- **mTOR inhibitors** (rapamycin, everolimus): Activate autophagy; may have a role in HNSCC when p53 is wild-type
- **Metformin:** AMPK activation → mTORC1 inhibition → autophagy induction; epidemiological data suggest reduced cancer risk

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Autophagy is a conserved catabolic process: cytoplasmic contents → autophagosome → autolysosome → degradation and recycling
> 2. Yoshinori Ohsumi identified ATG genes in yeast — 2016 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine
> 3. Key regulatory axis: mTORC1 (inhibits) vs AMPK (activates) — nutrient-sensing pathways control autophagy
> 4. Beclin-1 is a key autophagy regulator and haploinsufficient tumour suppressor — monoallelically deleted in many cancers
> 5. LC3-II (lipidated LC3) is the gold-standard marker of autophagosome formation
> 6. Dual role in cancer: tumour suppression early (prevents genomic instability) vs tumour promotion later (survival under stress)
> 7. In established HNSCC, autophagy drives resistance to radiotherapy, cisplatin, and cetuximab — by preventing apoptosis
> 8. HPV E7 modulates autophagy by degrading AMBRA1 and STING — aiding immune evasion
> 9. Hydroxychloroquine (autophagy inhibitor) combined with standard therapy is the most advanced clinical strategy
> 10. High LC3B expression correlates with poor overall survival in oropharyngeal HNSCC

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Think of autophagy as the cell's "recycling programme" — it breaks down damaged parts to reuse the building blocks, keeping the cell alive under stress
> - The dual role of autophagy explains why simply "turning it off" or "turning it on" is insufficient — the therapeutic strategy depends on the stage of carcinogenesis and treatment context
> - Autophagy inhibition (HCQ) + cisplatin/RT is the most promising combination — but results from clinical trials have been mixed, partly because HCQ is not a very potent autophagy inhibitor
> - The connection between autophagy and the mTOR pathway links this topic directly to [[Answers/General/Cell Cycle and Cancer|cell cycle regulation]] and [[Answers/General/Tumour Angiogenesis|angiogenesis]] — mTOR is a central hub
> - For exam purposes, remember the four steps: Initiation (ULK1) → Nucleation (Beclin-1/VPS34) → Elongation (LC3 lipidation) → Fusion/Degradation (autolysosome)
> - p53 has a dual role in autophagy: nuclear p53 activates it (via DRAM), cytoplasmic p53 inhibits it — this parallels p53's complex roles in other cellular processes
