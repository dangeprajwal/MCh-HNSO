# Prevention and Management of Trismus after Head and Neck Cancer Treatment

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Surgical and nonsurgical steps to prevent trismus after HNC treatment (AIIMS 2020)

**Source:** Jatin Shah - Head and Neck Surgery and Oncology, Chapter 7 (Postoperative Care), Chapter 9 (Maxillary Reconstruction), Chapter 18 (Oncologic Dentistry and Prosthetics), Chapter 19 (Late Toxicity of Radiation); Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 11 (Late Effects of Radiotherapy), Chapter 15 (Larynx — Late Effects)

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> Trismus, defined as restricted mouth opening ≤35 mm, affects 25-38% of head and neck cancer patients following treatment and has a profound impact on [[Answers/General/Quality of Life in Head and Neck Cancer|quality of life]], nutrition, oral hygiene, speech, and cancer surveillance. Dijkstra et al. (2004) published the first comprehensive systematic review of trismus in head and neck oncology, establishing the ≤35 mm diagnostic criterion and demonstrating that radiotherapy involving the TMJ and/or pterygoid muscles reduces mouth opening by 18%. Buchbinder et al. (1993) conducted the seminal comparative study of jaw mobilization techniques, showing that the TheraBite device produced significantly greater mouth opening improvement (13.6 mm) than tongue depressors (4.4 mm) or unassisted exercise (6.0 mm). Bensadoun et al. (2010) in their MASCC/ISOO systematic review found that IMRT significantly reduced trismus prevalence (5%) compared to conventional RT (25.4%). Kamstra et al. (2017) systematically reviewed exercise therapy for trismus, finding that while exercises can improve established trismus, they may not prevent its development. Shao et al. (2020) provided the most comprehensive meta-analysis of 13 RCTs confirming that exercise therapy improves maximal interincisal opening (MIO) in established trismus.

## Introduction

Trismus is defined as restricted mouth opening, with a maximal interincisal opening (MIO) of **≤35 mm** (Dijkstra et al., 2004). Normal MIO is 40-60 mm (average ~45 mm). In head and neck cancer patients, trismus results from both the disease itself (tumour invasion of [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Masticator Space|masticator space]]) and its treatment (surgery and/or radiation therapy).

Jatin Shah's Chapter 19 states unequivocally: **no effective treatment exists to correct established trismus. Therefore the management of trismus is prevention**, with jaw-stretching active and passive exercises instituted soon after the completion of therapy and continued until satisfactory jaw opening is achieved.

The prevalence of trismus varies by treatment modality:
- **Conventional RT involving masticator space:** 25.4% (Bensadoun et al., 2010)
- **IMRT:** 5% (significantly reduced)
- **Surgery involving masticator space/TMJ:** Variable, but common after retromolar/pterygoid resections
- **Combined surgery + RT/CRT:** Up to 38%

## Part A: Aetiology and Pathophysiology

### Causes of Trismus in HNC

| Cause | Mechanism | Timing |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| **Tumour invasion** | Direct infiltration of pterygoid muscles, masticator space, TMJ | Pre-treatment |
| **Surgical trauma** | Disruption of muscles of mastication, TMJ, coronoid process | Immediate postoperative |
| **Radiation fibrosis** | Progressive fibrosis of muscles of mastication, TMJ capsule, periarticular tissues | Late (months to years) |
| **Scarring/contracture** | Fibrosis of surgical site, wound contracture in oral cavity/oropharynx | Weeks to months |
| **Pain/spasm** | Reflex spasm of masticatory muscles from postoperative pain | Early postoperative |
| **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Submucous Fibrosis|oral submucous fibrosis]]** | Betel nut/areca nut-related fibrosis of buccal mucosa | Pre-existing (endemic populations) |

### Pathophysiology of Radiation-Induced Trismus

Jatin Shah's Chapter 18 explains: irradiation of the muscles of mastication can produce fibrosis that leads to trismus over time. The process involves:

1. **Acute phase (during RT):** Radiation-induced oedema and inflammation of the masticatory muscles → pain and reactive spasm
2. **Subacute phase (weeks-months):** Inflammatory cell infiltration and early fibroblast activation
3. **Chronic phase (months-years):** Progressive, irreversible fibrosis of the masseter, medial and lateral pterygoid muscles, and TMJ capsule — characterized by excessive collagen deposition, loss of muscle elasticity, and replacement of muscle fibres with fibrotic tissue

Jatin Shah's Chapter 7 provides the surgical mechanism: when the surgical intervention involves the masticator space or temporomandibular joint, trismus develops initially because of spasm of the muscles of mastication resulting from postoperative pain and discomfort, and later occurs as a result of fibrosis around the TMJ and the masticator group of muscles.

### Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| **Radiation dose to pterygoid muscles** | >50 Gy significantly increases risk |
| **Radiation dose to TMJ** | >50 Gy to TMJ region |
| **Field involving masticator space** | Direct radiation to masseter/pterygoids |
| **Surgical site** | Retromolar trigone, buccal mucosa, maxillary sinus, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Infratemporal Fossa Spread|infratemporal fossa]] |
| **Combined modality** | Surgery + RT higher risk than either alone |
| **Conventional RT vs IMRT** | IMRT 5% vs conventional RT 25.4% |
| **Pre-existing conditions** | Submucous fibrosis, TMJ disorders, poor dentition |

## Part B: Prevention Strategies

### Non-Surgical (Radiation-Related) Prevention

**1. IMRT (Intensity-Modulated Radiation Therapy)**

The most effective strategy for preventing radiation-induced trismus is to minimize dose to the muscles of mastication. Bensadoun et al. (2010) showed that IMRT reduced trismus prevalence from 25.4% (conventional RT) to 5%. Stell and Maran's Chapter 15 notes the benefit of conformal radiotherapy techniques in avoiding morbidity of trismus.

**Key dose constraints:**
- Mean dose to ipsilateral pterygoid muscles: aim for **<40 Gy**
- Mean dose to TMJ: aim for **<50 Gy**
- Delineate muscles of mastication as organs-at-risk (OAR) in treatment planning

**2. Pre-Treatment Exercise Programs**

Jatin Shah's Chapter 18 is emphatic: patients should begin to perform exercises **before radiation therapy commences**, and if the exercises are stopped during treatment, they must be resumed as soon as the acute side effects of radiation therapy have subsided.

**3. Jaw Exercise Therapy During and After Treatment**

Exercises should be performed in multiple cycles throughout every day:

| Device/Technique | Description | Evidence |
|-----------------|-------------|----------|
| **TheraBite** | Passive jaw motion rehabilitation device using repetitive passive motion and sustained stretch | Buchbinder 1993: 13.6 mm gain (best results) |
| **Tongue depressors** | Stacked and taped tongue blades inserted between teeth, progressively increased | Buchbinder 1993: 4.4 mm gain |
| **Acrylic resin corkscrew** | Custom-made screw device for progressive jaw opening | Jatin Shah recommends |
| **Dynasplint** | Spring-loaded trismus device providing low-load prolonged stretch | Used in chronic trismus |
| **Digital stretching** | Patient uses thumb and fingers to forcefully open mouth to point of tolerance | Simple, no equipment needed |

Jatin Shah's Chapter 18 describes the available devices: tongue blades taped together in a stack, an acrylic resin "corkscrew," a TheraBite, and a Dynasplint.

**Exercise protocol (recommended):**
- Begin **before** RT commences (baseline MIO measurement)
- Continue during RT as tolerated
- Resume immediately after acute side effects subside
- **Frequency:** Multiple sessions daily (3-5 sets per day)
- **Duration:** Each set: 5-10 repetitions of maximum opening, hold for 5-10 seconds
- **Indefinite continuation:** Exercises must be continued long-term; stopping allows fibrosis to progress

**4. Pharmacological Prevention**

| Agent | Mechanism | Evidence |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Pentoxifylline** | Anti-fibrotic; improves microcirculation; inhibits TGF-β | Some evidence for radiation fibrosis, limited for trismus specifically |
| **Pentoxifylline + Vitamin E** | Synergistic anti-fibrotic effect | Used in PENTOCLO regimen for radiation fibrosis |
| **Botulinum toxin** | Temporary muscle relaxation; may reduce pain-related spasm | Case reports; not standard of care |

### Surgical Prevention

**1. Surgical Technique Modifications**

- **Avoid unnecessary disruption of masticatory muscles:** Careful dissection preserving pterygoid muscle attachments when oncologically safe
- **Coronoid process preservation:** Avoid coronoidectomy unless required for oncological clearance
- **TMJ preservation:** Avoid TMJ violation during surgery when possible
- **Reconstruction:** Adequate soft tissue reconstruction to prevent wound contracture

**2. Mandibular Reconstruction**

Jatin Shah's Chapter 9 describes the importance of prosthetic devices in preventing trismus after maxillectomy: fabrication of the dental [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Obturator vs Flap Reconstruction|obturator reconstruction]] should take into consideration prevention of trismus. Early mouth opening exercises to prevent trismus are essential — jaw exercises are initiated to prevent fibrosis.

**3. Flap Reconstruction**

- Use of well-vascularized soft tissue flaps in the retromolar/pterygoid area reduces scar contracture
- Free flap reconstruction of mandibular defects restores mandibular continuity and prevents lateral deviation/dysfunction

## Part C: Management of Established Trismus

Stell and Maran's Chapter 11 states: where soft tissue fibrosis occurs in the region of the mandible, the functional result may be significant trismus. These changes should be anticipated or identified as early as possible to limit progression. Patient interventions include mandibular stretching exercises and the use of prosthetic aids.

### Non-Surgical Management

**1. Passive stretching devices:**
- TheraBite (most studied, best evidence)
- Dynasplint (low-load prolonged duration stretch)
- Stacked tongue depressors (simple, accessible)
- Digital stretching with fingers

**2. Physical therapy:**
- Supervised jaw exercises with a physiotherapist
- Biofeedback techniques
- Combination of active and passive exercises

**3. Pharmacological:**
- Pentoxifylline (400 mg TDS) + Vitamin E (1000 IU OD) — PENTOCLO regimen for radiation fibrosis
- NSAIDs for pain-related spasm component

### Surgical Management of Established Trismus

When conservative measures fail and trismus is severe (<20 mm):

| Procedure | Indication | Detail |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Coronoidectomy** | Fibrosis involving coronoid/temporalis insertion | Removal of coronoid process releases temporalis traction |
| **Release of fibrotic bands** | Scar bands in buccal/retromolar region | Division of fibrotic tissue; may require flap coverage |
| **TMJ surgery** | Ankylosis or severe periarticular fibrosis | Gap arthroplasty, interpositional arthroplasty |
| **Free flap interposition** | Recurrent contracture after release | Vascularized tissue prevents re-fibrosis |
| **Mandibular reconstruction** | [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Types of Mandibulectomy|segmental mandibulectomy]] with deviation | Restoration of mandibular continuity and alignment |

Jatin Shah's Chapter 18 notes the importance of mandibular guidance appliances after segmental mandibulectomy: to decrease scar contracture and trismus and improve the maxillomandibular relationship, with early mandibular exercises.

## Part D: Monitoring and Long-Term Follow-Up

Jatin Shah's emphatically states: it is crucial for the clinician to reinforce the importance of preventing trismus at every opportunity because difficulty in opening the mouth not only affects quality of life but also **interferes with adequate posttreatment monitoring for recurrent or new cancer**.

### Monitoring Protocol

1. **Baseline MIO measurement** before any treatment
2. **Regular MIO measurement** at each follow-up visit (using a ruler or Willis gauge)
3. **Grading:**
   - Mild: MIO 30-35 mm
   - Moderate: MIO 20-30 mm
   - Severe: MIO <20 mm
4. **Early intervention** when any decline is detected — do not wait for established trismus
5. **Compliance monitoring** — patients often discontinue exercises; regular reinforcement is essential

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Trismus = MIO ≤35 mm; affects 25-38% of HNC patients after treatment; prevention is the primary management strategy
> 2. No effective treatment exists for established trismus — Jatin Shah emphasizes prevention with early jaw exercises
> 3. IMRT is the most effective preventive measure: reduces trismus from 25.4% (conventional RT) to 5% (Bensadoun et al., 2010)
> 4. Dose constraints: aim for <40 Gy to pterygoid muscles and <50 Gy to TMJ
> 5. TheraBite produces the greatest MIO improvement (13.6 mm) compared to tongue depressors (4.4 mm) and unassisted exercise (6.0 mm)
> 6. Exercises must begin BEFORE radiotherapy and continue indefinitely — stopping allows fibrosis to progress
> 7. Radiation fibrosis is the primary mechanism: progressive, irreversible fibrosis of masticatory muscles and TMJ capsule
> 8. Surgical prevention: preserve masticatory muscles, reconstruct mandibular defects, use well-vascularized flaps to prevent contracture
> 9. Pentoxifylline + Vitamin E (PENTOCLO regimen) has some evidence for treating radiation fibrosis
> 10. Trismus impairs not only quality of life but also post-treatment cancer surveillance — a critical clinical implication

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The exam question specifically asks for BOTH surgical AND nonsurgical steps — structure your answer accordingly
> - Key non-surgical steps: (1) IMRT with dose constraints, (2) pre-RT baseline exercises, (3) TheraBite/device-assisted exercises during and after RT, (4) pharmacological agents (pentoxifylline + Vit E)
> - Key surgical steps: (1) technique modifications to preserve masticatory muscles, (2) coronoidectomy for severe cases, (3) flap reconstruction to prevent contracture, (4) mandibular guidance appliances after segmental mandibulectomy
> - The single most important message: START EXERCISES EARLY and NEVER STOP — this is emphasized repeatedly in every textbook
> - For established severe trismus (<20 mm), surgical options include coronoidectomy, release of fibrotic bands with free flap interposition, and gap arthroplasty
