## Introduction

Infrared therapy encompasses the use of near-infrared (NIR) and infrared (IR) wavelengths of light for therapeutic purposes. In head and neck oncology, this is predominantly applied as **photobiomodulation (PBM) therapy** — previously known as low-level laser therapy (LLLT). PBM uses low-energy laser or LED light in the red (630-700 nm) to near-infrared (700-1000 nm) spectrum to stimulate cellular processes, reduce inflammation, and promote tissue healing. It has become an evidence-based intervention for oral mucositis prevention and is now recommended in international guidelines.

## Physics and Mechanism of Action

### Electromagnetic Spectrum

| Region | Wavelength | Therapeutic Application |
|---|---|---|
| Red light | 630-700 nm | Superficial tissue (mucosa, skin) |
| **Near-infrared (NIR)** | 700-1000 nm | Deeper tissue penetration (muscles, lymphatics) |
| Mid-infrared | 1000 nm - 10 μm | CO2 laser (10.6 μm) — ablative, not PBM |
| Far-infrared | 10-100 μm | Thermal therapy only |

### Photobiomodulation Mechanism

1. **Photon absorption**: chromophore **cytochrome c oxidase** (Complex IV of mitochondrial respiratory chain) absorbs red/NIR photons
2. **Increased ATP production**: enhanced mitochondrial electron transport → increased ATP synthesis
3. **Reactive oxygen species (ROS) modulation**: brief controlled ROS generation → activation of redox-sensitive transcription factors (NF-κB, AP-1)
4. **Anti-inflammatory effects**: reduced pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6); increased anti-inflammatory mediators
5. **Cellular proliferation**: enhanced fibroblast, keratinocyte, and endothelial cell proliferation
6. **Angiogenesis**: VEGF upregulation → improved tissue perfusion and healing
7. **Neural effects**: modulation of nerve conduction → analgesic effect; reduced neuropathic pain

### Key Principle: Biphasic Dose Response (Arndt-Schulz Law)

- **Low doses** stimulate cellular function (therapeutic window)
- **High doses** inhibit or damage tissue
- Optimal energy density: **1-4 J/cm²** for mucosal applications
- Optimal power output: 10-150 mW (low-level; non-thermal)

## Therapeutic Parameters

| Parameter | Recommended Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Wavelength** | 632-970 nm (most evidence at 660 nm and 808-830 nm) | Red for mucosa; NIR for deeper tissue |
| **Power output** | 10-150 mW | Low-level (non-thermal) |
| **Energy density** | 1-4 J/cm² | Per treatment point |
| **Spot size** | 0.01-1 cm² | Depends on device |
| **Application time** | 10-60 seconds per point | Multiple points per session |
| **Frequency** | Daily during RT/CRT | Preventive protocol |
| **Device** | Diode laser or LED | Both effective; LED cheaper and larger field |

## Clinical Applications in Head and Neck Cancer

### A. Oral Mucositis Prevention and Treatment

**Primary evidence-based indication — MASCC/ISOO Level II recommendation**

#### Mechanism in Mucositis

- Radiation/chemotherapy damages basal epithelial cells and activates NF-κB → inflammatory cascade → mucosal breakdown
- PBM at molecular level: reduces NF-κB activation, decreases TNF-α/IL-1β, promotes epithelial cell proliferation, accelerates mucosal healing

#### Evidence

**Antunes et al. 2013 (RCT, n=94):**
- HNC patients receiving CRT randomised to LLLT (660 nm, 40 mW, 10s/point, 4 J/cm²) vs sham laser
- Severe mucositis (Grade 3-4): LLLT **7.6%** vs sham **40%** (P<0.001)
- Significant reduction in opioid use, weight loss, and feeding tube requirement

**Bjordal et al. 2011 (Meta-analysis):**
- 11 RCTs, 415 patients
- LLLT significantly reduced risk of severe mucositis (RR 2.03, 95% CI 1.11-3.69)
- Reduced mucositis duration by a mean of 4.7 days

**MASCC/ISOO Guidelines (Lalla et al. 2014):**
- **Recommendation**: PBM for prevention of oral mucositis in patients receiving RT for HNC, with or without chemotherapy
- Evidence level: II (well-designed cohort studies and RCTs)
- Recommended wavelength: 632.8-660 nm, energy density 1-6 J/cm²

#### Protocol

- **Timing**: begin on Day 1 of RT/CRT; continue daily throughout treatment
- **Sites**: bilateral buccal mucosa, lateral tongue, floor of mouth, soft palate, oropharynx — 6-10 points per session
- **Duration**: 10-60 seconds per point (varies by device power)
- **Total treatment time**: 10-15 minutes per session

### B. Head and Neck Lymphoedema

- Post-treatment lymphoedema affects **75% of HNC patients** — causes facial/neck swelling, fibrosis, dysphagia
- **Ridner et al. 2013**: LLLT (905 nm, pulsed) applied to affected head/neck regions → significant reduction in tissue fibrosis score and patient-reported symptoms
- Mechanism: stimulates lymphatic endothelial cell proliferation, improves lymphatic drainage, reduces fibrosis
- Applied as adjunct to complete decongestive therapy (CDT)

### C. Wound Healing

- PBM accelerates wound healing in post-surgical and post-radiation tissue
- Promotes fibroblast proliferation, collagen synthesis, and angiogenesis
- Emerging evidence for ORN wound management and post-surgical flap viability
- **Caution**: avoid direct application over known residual or recurrent tumour

### D. Pain Management

| Application | Mechanism | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| Mucositis pain | Reduced inflammation, neural modulation | Strong (RCTs) |
| Post-surgical pain | Anti-inflammatory, endorphin release | Moderate |
| Trismus/fibrosis | Tissue remodelling, reduced myofascial pain | Emerging |
| Neuropathic pain | Nerve regeneration, conduction modulation | Preliminary |

### E. Xerostomia

- Emerging evidence that PBM applied over major salivary glands during RT may preserve salivary function
- Mechanism: reduced radiation-induced apoptosis of acinar cells
- Early clinical trials show improved saliva flow rates — requires further validation

## Safety Considerations

### Safety in Cancer Patients

| Concern | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Tumour stimulation** | No clinical evidence that PBM promotes tumour growth at therapeutic doses |
| **In vitro studies** | Mixed results — some show stimulation at high doses; most show no effect at therapeutic parameters |
| **Clinical safety** | No increased local recurrence in any clinical trial of PBM in HNC patients |
| **MASCC/ISOO position** | Considered safe for use in cancer patients at recommended parameters |
| **Precaution** | Avoid direct application over known residual or recurrent tumour |

### Contraindications

- Direct application over active tumour site
- Photosensitising drug use (porfimer, ALA) — risk of enhanced photosensitivity
- Direct ocular exposure (use protective eyewear)
- Pregnancy (precautionary — limited data)

## Comparison: PBM vs Other Mucositis Interventions

| Intervention | Mechanism | Evidence Level | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PBM/LLLT** | Photobiomodulation | MASCC recommended | Equipment cost, training |
| Benzydamine rinse | Anti-inflammatory | MASCC recommended (moderate dose RT) | Ineffective for severe mucositis |
| Palifermin (KGF) | Epithelial cell growth factor | Approved for HSCT; off-label for HNC | Cost, IV administration |
| Cryotherapy | Vasoconstriction | MASCC recommended (bolus 5-FU) | Not applicable for RT |
| Oral hygiene protocol | Infection prevention | Essential supportive care | Adjunctive only |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> - Infrared/photobiomodulation therapy uses red (630-700 nm) and NIR (700-1000 nm) light at low energy to stimulate cellular healing
> - Mechanism: cytochrome c oxidase absorbs photons → increased ATP → reduced inflammation → enhanced proliferation and healing
> - **MASCC/ISOO recommends PBM** for prevention of oral mucositis in HNC patients receiving RT/CRT
> - Antunes RCT: PBM reduced severe mucositis from 40% to 7.6% during CRT; Bjordal meta-analysis confirmed significant benefit
> - Additional applications: head and neck lymphoedema, wound healing, pain management, and emerging role in xerostomia prevention

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - PBM must be started on **Day 1 of RT** and continued daily throughout treatment — it is **preventive**, not just therapeutic; starting after mucositis develops is less effective
> - The optimal wavelength for oral mucositis is **660 nm** (red) at **1-4 J/cm²** — NIR (808-830 nm) is better for deeper tissue applications like lymphoedema
> - PBM is **non-thermal** — if the patient feels heat, the power is too high; reduce intensity or increase spot size
> - There is **no clinical evidence** that PBM at therapeutic doses promotes tumour growth — it is safe to use during and after cancer treatment
> - LED devices are cheaper and cover larger areas than laser diodes — equally effective for mucositis prevention and increasingly used in clinical practice
