## Introduction

The balloon occlusion test (BOT), also referred to as the balloon test occlusion (BTO), is a preoperative neurointerventional procedure designed to assess the risk of cerebral ischaemia if the internal carotid artery (ICA) must be sacrificed during surgery. As described in Harrison's Chapter 35, approximately 15% of patients are at risk for a delayed stroke due to watershed areas of ischaemia in the distribution of the middle cerebral artery following ICA sacrifice, even when four-vessel angiography demonstrates good collateral cross-fill.

Without any form of preoperative occlusion testing, the incidence of stroke after permanent carotid artery occlusion ranges from **17-30%** (ASITN, 2001). The BOT, by identifying patients with inadequate collateral circulation, allows surgeons to stratify patients into risk categories and plan accordingly — either proceeding with ICA sacrifice, performing vascular bypass/reconstruction, or modifying the surgical approach.

As Jatin Shah's Chapter 6 emphasizes, balloon occlusion testing may be required in patients for whom circulation of one hemisphere is at risk because of the tumour or surgery. Stell and Maran's Chapter 34 states unequivocally: the plan to resect the carotid should always be a preoperative decision and almost never should a situation arise where this is contemplated for the first time peri-operatively.

---

## Part A: Indications

### In Head and Neck Oncology

| Indication | Clinical Scenario |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Carotid artery invasion by tumour** | Advanced or recurrent HNSCC encasing/invading the ICA |
| **Skull base surgery** | Tumours requiring potential ICA sacrifice (sinonasal, NPC, temporal bone) |
| **Paragangliomas** | Large carotid body tumours, glomus jugulare/vagale encasing the ICA |
| **Carotid blowout syndrome** | Pre-intervention assessment before endovascular sacrifice |
| **Parapharyngeal space tumours** | Tumours involving the ICA in the parapharyngeal space |

**Specific textbook references:**

Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 states that temporary balloon occlusion testing, to assess cerebral perfusion, may be indicated in certain large carotid paragangliomas or in order to determine whether the patient will tolerate common carotid clamping to enable tumour removal. This test can also evaluate whether or not a patient will tolerate internal carotid artery sacrifice.

Harrison's Chapter 12 states that a balloon occlusion study is often performed preoperatively to determine the patient's ability to temporarily tolerate complete obstruction of blood flow through the carotid artery. If the patient is able to tolerate a BTO, they are considered a candidate for carotid artery resection. If the patient is not able to tolerate the BTO, then carotid artery resection is no longer a viable surgical alternative.

Scott-Brown's Chapter 12 notes that therapeutic interventions such as pre-operative embolization or balloon occlusion studies can be performed at the same time as diagnostic angiography for parapharyngeal space tumours.

## Part B: Technique

### Standard Protocol

The BTO is performed in a neuroangiographic suite under continuous neurological monitoring:

**1. Vascular access and catheterization**
- Femoral artery puncture (Seldinger technique)
- Guide catheter advanced to the common carotid artery
- A non-detachable balloon catheter is navigated into the ICA, typically at the level of the petrous or cavernous segment

**2. Balloon inflation and clinical testing**
- The balloon is inflated to occlude the ICA completely
- Standard clinical BTO lasts **15-30 minutes** (Mathis et al. used 15 minutes in their 500-case series)
- During occlusion, the patient is assessed for:
  - Level of consciousness
  - Speech and language function
  - Contralateral motor strength (hand grip, arm drift)
  - Visual field assessment
  - Presence of neurological symptoms (headache, confusion)
- The patient must remain **awake and cooperative** throughout — general anaesthesia is NOT used during clinical testing

**3. Adjunctive cerebral perfusion assessment**

Clinical testing alone has a **false-negative rate of up to 10%** — patients may pass the clinical test but still develop delayed stroke. Therefore, quantitative cerebral blood flow (CBF) measurement is strongly recommended:

| Method | Principle | Advantage |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Xenon-enhanced CT CBF** | Stable xenon inhalation during BTO; measures regional CBF | Most validated; CBF <30 mL/100g/min = high risk (Linskey) |
| **99mTc-HMPAO SPECT** | Radiotracer injection during BTO; measures cerebral perfusion | Widely available; detects asymmetry |
| **CT perfusion** | Dynamic contrast bolus during BTO | Fast; readily available |
| **Transcranial Doppler** | Measures MCA flow velocity during occlusion | Non-invasive; real-time monitoring |
| **Stump pressure** | Measures back-pressure distal to balloon | Simple; <50 mmHg suggests inadequate collateral |

Jatin Shah's Chapter 6 specifically recommends balloon occlusion of the ipsilateral carotid artery with 99mTc-hexamethyl-propyleneamine oxime (HMPAO) brain SPECT as having the best sensitivity and specificity.

**4. Hypotensive challenge (optional)**

Some centres add a hypotensive challenge during BTO — reducing systolic BP by 20-30% with pharmacological agents — to unmask borderline collateral circulation that might fail under the haemodynamic stress of surgery. This increases the sensitivity of the test.

## Part C: Interpretation and Risk Stratification

### Risk Categories

| Result | CBF Finding | Stroke Risk | Management |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Pass clinical + normal CBF** | CBF ≥30 mL/100g/min bilaterally | Low (~3-5%) | ICA sacrifice without reconstruction may be considered |
| **Pass clinical + reduced CBF** | Asymmetric CBF reduction | Intermediate (~10-15%) | ICA sacrifice with vascular reconstruction recommended |
| **Fail clinical** | N/A (test terminated) | High (>25%) | ICA sacrifice contraindicated; consider bypass or alternative approach |

As Linskey et al. (1994) demonstrated, even patients who pass clinical BTO may still suffer stroke if CBF is reduced below 30 mL/100g/min — the combined test is therefore superior to clinical monitoring alone.

### What "Passing" and "Failing" Means

- **Passing the BTO** does NOT guarantee absence of stroke risk — Jatin Shah's notes that even this test is not entirely reliable because it does not take into account the adverse impact of intraoperative hypotension, hypoxia, or involvement of other collateral vessels not assessed by BTO, and thromboembolism may develop after ligation
- **Failing the BTO** indicates that ICA sacrifice without reconstruction is contraindicated, but it does not necessarily mean that surgery is impossible — vascular bypass or reconstruction may be an option

## Part D: Management Based on BTO Results

### If BTO is Passed (Adequate Collateral)

Stell and Maran's Chapter 34 describes the approach: balloon occlusion of the artery with SPECT is performed. If the test shows adequate crossflow and does not cause symptoms, permanent occlusion can be performed with coils preoperatively, with resection being performed 2 weeks later.

Scott-Brown's Chapter 18 describes a similar approach, noting that if the test shows adequate crossflow and does not cause symptoms, there is a lesser need to shunt the artery during resection. However, in all cases, reconstruction of the artery using an appropriate graft is recommended to reduce chances of neurological complications.

### If BTO is Failed (Inadequate Collateral)

Options include:

1. **Vascular bypass/reconstruction** — saphenous vein graft is most commonly used (Stell and Maran's)
2. **Extracranial-intracranial (EC-IC) bypass** — superficial temporal artery to MCA anastomosis
3. **Modification of surgical approach** — subadventitial dissection to preserve ICA integrity
4. **Non-surgical treatment** — radiation or chemoradiation if surgery is not feasible
5. **Stenting** — endovascular stent to maintain ICA patency during or after carotid manipulation

### Carotid Blowout Syndrome

Stell and Maran's Chapter 23 describes the role of BTO in carotid blowout: adequacy of contralateral carotid to the circle of Willis can be assessed by temporary balloon occlusion, while monitoring neurological status and measuring backflow pressure. If judged adequate, ligation or embolization is associated with a lower risk of cerebrovascular accident. If collateral supply is insufficient, the options include stenting or arterial bypass surgery.

## Part E: Complications and Limitations

### Complications (Mathis et al., 500 cases)

| Complication | Rate |
|-------------|------|
| **Overall complications** | 3.2% (16/500) |
| **Asymptomatic complications** | 1.6% (8/500) |
| **Transient neurological deficit** | 1.2% (6/500) |
| **Permanent neurological deficit** | 0.4% (2/500) |
| **Death** | 0% |

### Limitations

1. **False-negative rate:** Even with combined clinical + CBF testing, 3-5% of patients who pass BTO will develop stroke after permanent ICA occlusion — the test is not 100% reliable
2. **Does not account for intraoperative variables:** Hypotension, hypoxia, blood loss, and manipulation of other collateral vessels during surgery may compromise cerebral perfusion even in patients with adequate collateral
3. **Thromboembolism risk:** Delayed thrombus formation at the ICA stump is not predicted by BTO
4. **Requires specialized infrastructure:** As Jatin Shah's notes, to perform these invasive angiographic procedures safely, a multidisciplinary infrastructure is essential, and the tests themselves carry a risk of neurological complications
5. **Static vs dynamic assessment:** BTO assesses collateral at one point in time under controlled conditions — does not predict long-term adequacy

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The balloon occlusion test (BTO) assesses cerebral collateral adequacy before planned ICA sacrifice — performed in a neuroangiographic suite with the patient awake
> 2. Without preoperative testing, stroke risk after permanent ICA occlusion is 17-30%; BTO reduces this by identifying high-risk patients
> 3. Standard protocol: balloon inflation in ICA for 15-30 minutes with continuous neurological monitoring (speech, motor, consciousness)
> 4. Clinical testing alone has a 10% false-negative rate — adjunctive CBF measurement (xenon CT, SPECT, CT perfusion) is essential
> 5. CBF <30 mL/100g/min during BTO = high stroke risk (Linskey et al., 1994) — ICA sacrifice without reconstruction is contraindicated
> 6. BTO + 99mTc-HMPAO SPECT has the best sensitivity and specificity for predicting cerebral ischaemia (Jatin Shah)
> 7. Passing the BTO does NOT guarantee absence of stroke — intraoperative hypotension, thromboembolism, and other collateral compromise are not predicted
> 8. Complications of BTO itself are low: 0.4% permanent deficit, 0% mortality in the 500-case series (Mathis et al., 1995)
> 9. The plan to resect the carotid should ALWAYS be a preoperative decision — BTO must be performed before, never contemplated intraoperatively for the first time
> 10. If BTO is failed: options include vascular bypass (saphenous vein graft), EC-IC bypass, subadventitial dissection, stenting, or non-surgical treatment

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The key principle: BTO determines IF the carotid can be sacrificed safely — it is a gatekeeper test for carotid resection in HNC
> - Three main indications in HNC: (1) advanced/recurrent SCC invading the carotid, (2) large paragangliomas, (3) carotid blowout syndrome
> - For exam purposes, remember the risk stratification: Pass clinical + normal CBF = low risk (3-5%); Pass clinical + reduced CBF = intermediate (10-15%); Fail clinical = high risk (>25%)
> - A hypotensive challenge during BTO increases sensitivity by unmasking borderline collateral — some centres consider this mandatory
> - In carotid blowout, BTO guides whether endovascular sacrifice (embolization) is safe or whether stenting/bypass is needed
> - Stell and Maran's approach for passed BTO: permanent coil occlusion first, then surgical resection 2 weeks later — this staged approach reduces intraoperative haemodynamic instability
