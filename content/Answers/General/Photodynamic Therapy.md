## Introduction

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) is a minimally invasive treatment modality that uses the interaction between a photosensitizing agent, light of a specific wavelength, and molecular oxygen to produce selective tumour destruction. In head and neck oncology, PDT has emerged as a valuable tool for the management of premalignant lesions, early-stage squamous cell carcinomas (CIS, T1, T2), recurrent superficial tumours, and field cancerization. The key advantage of PDT is its ability to provide wide-field mucosal ablation while preserving underlying tissue architecture, making it particularly attractive for sites where functional preservation is critical — such as the oral cavity and larynx.

PDT works through a cold photochemical process that produces [[Answers/General/Apoptosis|apoptosis]] and vascular shutdown, resulting in less scarring compared to surgery or radiotherapy. As described in Harrison's Head and Neck Cancer (Chapter 16A), photosensitizers may be preferentially concentrated in both tumour cells and in the extratumoral compartment, especially the tumour vasculature. Photoactivation occurs with exposure to wavelength-specific light, typically administered via laser, generating localized oxidative stress through singlet oxygen production. For many of the photosensitizers evaluated clinically, this process is oxygen-dependent — which is an important consideration in hypoxic tumour microenvironments.

The main attraction of PDT as a therapeutic modality, as emphasized in Harrison, is the ability to offer wide-field superficial mucosal ablative therapy given the challenges that often exist in defining the peripheral extent of lesions in the oral cavity. As noted in Stell and Maran's Textbook (Chapter 29), PDT lacks "tissue memory" — meaning that unlike radiotherapy, this treatment can be given on a number of occasions without cumulative dose limitation. This is a critical advantage in the context of field cancerization, where patients may develop multiple sequential mucosal lesions over their lifetime requiring repeated treatment.

## Part A: Principles and Mechanism of PDT

### The Three Essential Components

PDT requires the simultaneous presence of three elements:

1. **Photosensitizer (PS):** A light-sensitive drug administered systemically (IV) or topically
2. **Light:** Wavelength-specific light, typically delivered via laser (630-652 nm range)
3. **Molecular oxygen:** Required for the photochemical reaction

### Mechanism of Action

The photosensitizer is administered and preferentially accumulates in tumour tissue due to the relative absence of lymphatic drainage in malignant tissues. After an appropriate drug-light interval (to maximize tumour-to-normal tissue ratio), the lesion is illuminated with wavelength-specific light, typically from a laser source.

The photochemical reaction proceeds through two pathways:

- **Type I reaction:** Direct electron transfer from the excited photosensitizer to biomolecules, generating free radicals
- **Type II reaction:** Energy transfer to molecular oxygen, producing highly reactive singlet oxygen — this is the predominant mechanism of tumour destruction

### Tumour Destruction Occurs Through Three Mechanisms

1. **Direct cellular damage:** Singlet oxygen causes oxidative damage to cellular organelles, particularly mitochondria and cell membranes, leading to apoptosis and necrosis
2. **Vascular shutdown:** Damage to tumour vasculature endothelium causes thrombosis and ischaemic necrosis of the tumour
3. **Immune activation:** PDT triggers an inflammatory response that can generate anti-tumour immunity

### Key Photosensitizers Used in Head and Neck PDT

| Photosensitizer | Wavelength | Drug-Light Interval | Key Feature |
|----------------|-----------|-------------------|------------|
| Porfimer sodium (Photofrin) | 630 nm | 48-72 hours | Most studied; prolonged skin photosensitivity (4-6 weeks) |
| mTHPC (Foscan/Temoporfin) | 652 nm | 96 hours | EU approved for HNC; deeper penetration |
| 5-ALA | 635 nm | 4-6 hours (topical) | Topical application; short photosensitivity |
| Methyl-ALA (Metvix) | 635 nm | 3 hours (topical) | Enhanced tissue penetration over ALA |

### Depth of Treatment

The depth of clinical activity is typically limited to several millimeters with surface illumination. However, the application of brachytherapy techniques to administer interstitial PDT implants allows treatment of larger tumour volumes.

## Part B: Role of PDT in Oral Cancer Management

### Indications in Oral Cancer

1. **Premalignant lesions:** [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Leukoplakia Management|oral leukoplakia]], erythroplakia, oral dysplasia
2. **Early invasive cancer:** CIS, T1, and select T2 oral SCC
3. **Field cancerization:** Wide-field mucosal ablation for multifocal disease
4. **Recurrent superficial tumours:** After previous surgery or radiation
5. **Positive/close [[Surgical Margins|margins]]:** Adjuvant intraoperative PDT after resection
6. **Salvage therapy:** For patients unfit for surgery or re-irradiation

### Clinical Evidence in Oral Cancer

A series of 38 patients with oral lesions (tongue, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]], buccal regions) of stage T1/T2 N0 were treated with PDT. The majority (26/38, 68%) achieved a complete response, with a 5-year survival of 84.2%. The morbidity of PDT treatment was reported to be less than surgery, radiation, or chemoradiotherapy.

In another European report, 170 patients treated with PDT showed an overall response rate of 90.7% with a complete response rate of 70.8%.

Biel's landmark series of 29 patients with CIS and T1 recurrent oral SCC demonstrated 100% complete response after a single PDT treatment, with 5 patients developing local recurrence at follow-up to 70 months, yielding an 80% cure rate.

### PDT for Oral Premalignant Lesions

Complete response rates exceeding 80% have been reported for oral dysplasia with various photosensitizers. However, two important limitations exist:

- Long-term control rates remain a significant concern
- The ability of PDT to influence the risk of malignant transformation remains to be established

### Advantages of PDT in Oral Cancer

- Wide-field superficial mucosal ablation — addresses the challenge of defining peripheral extent of oral lesions
- Repeatable without cumulative toxicity (unlike radiotherapy)
- Preservation of tissue architecture and function
- Minimal scarring compared to surgery
- Can be used in previously irradiated fields

## Part C: Role of PDT in Laryngeal Cancer

### Evidence in Laryngeal Cancer

Biel's series of 25 patients with CIS and T1 SCC of the true vocal cord demonstrated:

- 100% complete response after single PDT treatment
- Only 1 recurrence at follow-up to 79 months
- **95% cure rate** — comparable to radiotherapy and transoral laser surgery

In a larger pooled analysis of 518 patients treated with PDT for CIS, T1, or T2 cancers of the head and neck, 462 (89.1%) obtained a complete clinical response after a single treatment. Laryngeal cancers specifically (171 patients) achieved a durable complete response rate of 89% with up to 16-year follow-up.

### Advantages in Laryngeal Cancer

- Voice preservation with minimal mucosal scarring
- Can be repeated for recurrence
- No radiation dose to surrounding tissues
- Suitable for patients who have failed previous RT (salvage setting)

## Part D: Other Applications in Head and Neck Oncology

### Lip Cancer

PDT with Photofrin administered intravenously followed by single non-thermal illumination (20 J/cm, irradiance 100 mW/cm2) yields complete response rates comparable to surgery or radiotherapy. Less scarring occurs compared to surgery or radiotherapy due to the cold photochemical process.

### Skin Cancer (Head and Neck Region)

- **Actinic keratoses:** 71-100% response rate for facial lesions
- **Bowen disease:** 89-100% clearance with systemic photosensitizers
- **Superficial BCC:** 79-100% complete response with topical ALA
- **Nodular BCC:** Less reliable; not recommended for morpheaform lesions

### Adjuvant Intraoperative PDT

For advanced (stage III/IV) head and neck tumours, PDT has been explored as adjuvant intraoperative therapy following surgical resection — particularly for close or positive margins where re-excision or re-irradiation carries unacceptable risk.

## Part E: Limitations and Complications

### Limitations

- Depth of penetration limited to a few millimeters (surface illumination)
- Not suitable for bulky or deeply invasive tumours
- Prolonged skin photosensitivity (4-6 weeks with Porfimer sodium)
- Pain during and after treatment
- Long-term control rates for dysplasia remain uncertain
- Limited availability and expertise

### Complications

- **Photosensitivity:** Most common; patients must avoid sunlight for 4-6 weeks (Photofrin) or 2 weeks (Foscan)
- **Pain:** Local pain during and after treatment
- **Oedema:** Swelling in the treatment area — may require precautionary tracheostomy for laryngeal PDT
- **Scarring:** Less than surgery/RT but can occur with larger lesions
- No permanent long-term sequelae reported in major series

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. PDT requires three components: photosensitizer, wavelength-specific light, and molecular oxygen
> 2. Tumour destruction occurs via singlet oxygen generation (Type II reaction), causing direct cell kill, vascular shutdown, and immune activation
> 3. Depth of treatment is limited to a few millimeters with surface illumination
> 4. For CIS/T1 oral SCC, PDT achieves 80-90% complete response rates
> 5. For CIS/T1 laryngeal SCC, PDT achieves 89-95% cure rates comparable to RT and TLM
> 6. Key advantage: repeatable without cumulative toxicity (unlike RT), preserves tissue architecture
> 7. PDT is particularly valuable for field cancerization, multifocal disease, and post-RT recurrences
> 8. Main photosensitizers: Porfimer sodium (Photofrin), mTHPC (Foscan), 5-ALA
> 9. Skin photosensitivity is the most common complication (4-6 weeks with Photofrin)
> 10. PDT is NOT suitable for bulky, deeply invasive tumours — it is a superficial mucosal treatment

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - PDT lacks "tissue memory" — unlike radiotherapy, it can be given repeatedly without cumulative dose limitation
> - The gold standard for early oral cavity cancer remains surgery; PDT proves its value in field cancerization, superficial recurrence, and salvage settings
> - For T1 glottic cancer, PDT cure rates (95%) are comparable to RT and TLM, with excellent voice outcomes
> - Interstitial PDT using brachytherapy-style implants can treat larger volumes than surface illumination
> - Always warn patients about photosensitivity: avoid sunlight exposure for 4-6 weeks after Photofrin
> - mTHPC (Foscan) has deeper tissue penetration than Photofrin and shorter photosensitivity period
