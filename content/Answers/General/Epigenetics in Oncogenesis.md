The Cancer Genome Atlas Network (2015) provided the definitive genomic landscape of HNSCC, revealing that the histone methyltransferase MLL2 is mutated in 18% of head and neck cancers — establishing epigenetic regulators as major targets in HNSCC.

## Introduction

Epigenetics refers to heritable changes in gene expression that occur **without alterations in the DNA sequence itself**. These changes regulate which genes are active or silenced in any given cell and are essential for normal development and differentiation. In cancer, epigenetic mechanisms are profoundly dysregulated, leading to inappropriate silencing of tumour suppressor genes and activation of oncogenes.

Scott-Brown's Chapter 6 states: epigenetic modification of gene expression and function is another major mechanism that promotes cancer in the head and neck. It has been proposed by some as an **additional emerging hallmark in cancer development** (Jones and Baylin, 2002; Dawson and Kouzarides, 2012).

Harrison's Chapter 16 confirms: it is increasingly recognized that tumour-specific genetic and **epigenetic alterations** affect key physiologic pathways that control functions such as cell proliferation, DNA repair, neoangiogenesis, motility, and metastasis.

## Part A: Mechanisms of Epigenetic Regulation

### 1. DNA Methylation

Scott-Brown's Chapter 6 provides a detailed explanation: the most common epigenetic modification of DNA is the **methylation of cytosine residues to 5-methylcytosine**. This occurs predominantly in residues that are 5' to a guanine base, i.e. a **CpG site**. In many genes, the concentration of CpGs is higher in the non-coding promoter regions — the **CpG islands**.

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **CpG sites** | Cytosine-guanine dinucleotides; targets of methylation |
| **CpG islands** | Clusters of CpGs in gene promoter regions |
| **Hypermethylation** | Increased methylation → gene silencing (TSG inactivation) |
| **Hypomethylation** | Decreased methylation → genomic instability, oncogene activation |
| **DNMTs** | DNA methyltransferases catalyse the addition of methyl groups |

Scott-Brown's explains: typically, increased methylation of cytosines in CpG islands results in **reduced gene transcription** — epigenetic silencing.

**In HNSCC:**
- **CDKN2A (p16) hypermethylation** — the most frequent epigenetic alteration; observed in 10-20% of cases (Stell and Maran's), associated with reduced overall survival (Scott-Brown's)
- **PTEN** promoter methylation — loss of [[Answers/General/PI3-Kinase Pathway|PI3K pathway]] pathway regulation
- **DCC (Deleted in Colon Carcinoma)** — promoter hypermethylation frequent in HNSCC, rarely mutated (Harrison)
- **MDM2, Interferon-alpha, RAR-beta** — commonly hypermethylated (Harrison)

### 2. Histone Modifications

Histones are proteins around which DNA is wound to form chromatin. Post-translational modifications of histone tails regulate chromatin structure and gene accessibility:

| Modification | Effect on Chromatin | Gene Expression |
|-------------|--------------------| ----------------|
| **Acetylation** (by HATs) | Relaxes chromatin (euchromatin) | Activates transcription |
| **Deacetylation** (by HDACs) | Condenses chromatin (heterochromatin) | Silences transcription |
| **Methylation** (H3K4) | Open chromatin | Activates transcription |
| **Methylation** (H3K9, H3K27) | Closed chromatin | Silences transcription |
| **Phosphorylation** | Variable | Context-dependent |
| **Ubiquitination** | Variable | Context-dependent |

Scott-Brown's Chapter 6 notes: the TCGA network found mutations in the **histone H3 methyltransferase gene MLL2 in 18%** of head and neck cancers studied — establishing that mutations in epigenetic regulators themselves are a major mechanism in HNSCC.

### 3. Non-coding RNAs

Harrison's Chapter 16 describes: **microRNA (miRNA)** — short 22-base sequences that can selectively block gene transcription — promises to provide yet another substantial mechanism for epigenetic tumour-specific alteration.

Scott-Brown's Chapter 6 provides a specific example: **let-7c** is a microRNA that regulates a number of target genes and has been found to be **down-regulated/deleted in approximately 40% of HPV-negative** head and neck cancers, associated with increased CDK6 expression and [[Answers/General/Cell Cycle and Cancer|cell cycle]] progression.

### 4. Chromatin Remodelling

Chromatin remodelling complexes (SWI/SNF family) use ATP hydrolysis to alter nucleosome positioning, affecting gene accessibility. Mutations in chromatin remodelling genes are increasingly recognized in HNSCC.

## Part B: Epigenetic Dysregulation in HNSCC

### The Dual Pattern: Hypermethylation and Hypomethylation

Jones and Baylin (2002) established that cancers exhibit a characteristic dual pattern:

| Pattern | Mechanism | Consequence in HNSCC |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Promoter hypermethylation** | CpG island methylation of TSG promoters | Silences p16, PTEN, DCC, RAR-beta |
| **Global hypomethylation** | Loss of methylation in repetitive sequences | Genomic instability, chromosomal rearrangements |

### Key Genes Silenced by Epigenetic Mechanisms in HNSCC

| Gene | Function | Mechanism of Silencing | Frequency |
|------|----------|----------------------|-----------|
| **CDKN2A (p16)** | Cell cycle regulation (G1-S) | Promoter hypermethylation | 10-20% |
| **PTEN** | PI3K pathway inhibition | Methylation/deletion | ~30% |
| **DCC** | Tumour suppressor | Promoter hypermethylation | Frequent |
| **RAR-beta** | Retinoic acid receptor | Hypermethylation | Common |
| **E-cadherin** | Cell adhesion | Promoter methylation | Variable |
| **MGMT** | DNA repair | Hypermethylation | Variable |

Stell and Maran's Chapter 3 provides a critical insight: p16 is inactivated through three mechanisms — somatic mutations (5-15%), homozygous deletions (30-60%), and **epigenetic silencing by hypermethylation (10-20%)**. As a result, immunohistochemical analysis demonstrates that **p16 absence is present in at least 80% of HNSCC**.

### [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-positive]] vs HPV-Negative Epigenetic Profiles

Stell and Maran's notes: HPV positivity and intrinsic p16 silencing are **mutually exclusive events**, suggesting they are functionally redundant. In HPV+ tumours, the E7 oncoprotein degrades pRB directly, while in HPV- tumours, p16 hypermethylation achieves the same functional effect of cell cycle deregulation.

## Part C: Epigenetics as a Biomarker in HNSCC

### Molecular Detection and Staging

Stell and Maran's Chapter 3 describes the clinical utility: studies have included [[Answers/General/PCR and Microarray Analysis|PCR]]-based analysis of **promoter hypermethylation events** and mitochondrial DNA mutations, suggesting improved sensitivity and specificity over traditional LOH analysis.

Critically: tumour-specific methylation events can be detected in the **saliva and serum** of patients with HNSCC, foreseeing development of a **non-invasive routine screening test** for smokers and drinkers.

Harrison's Chapter 16 notes: examination of a spectrum of histologic lesions from atypia through dysplasia to invasive cancer shows a general pattern of **accumulated epigenetic alterations correlated with progressive phenotypic change** — some alterations (like p16 loss) occur early in atypia/dysplasia, while others appear only in invasive SCC.

### Dietary Factors and Epigenetics

Scott-Brown's provides an important link: dietary **folate** may influence methylation patterns, since it ultimately provides the single carbon (methyl) units. However, the links between folate levels in diet and gene methylation are complex and often counter-intuitive.

## Part D: Epigenetic Therapy

### Epigenetic Drugs in Cancer

| Class | Mechanism | Examples | Status |
|-------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **DNMT inhibitors** | Block DNA methyltransferases → reactivate silenced TSGs | Azacitidine, decitabine | Approved for MDS/AML; trials in HNSCC |
| **HDAC inhibitors** | Block histone deacetylases → open chromatin → restore gene expression | Vorinostat, romidepsin | Approved for lymphoma; investigated in HNSCC |
| **BET inhibitors** | Block bromodomain proteins reading acetyl marks | JQ1, OTX-015 | Preclinical/Phase I |
| **EZH2 inhibitors** | Block H3K27 methyltransferase | Tazemetostat | Approved for epithelioid sarcoma; trials in solid tumours |

Dawson and Kouzarides (2012) emphasize that the **reversibility** of epigenetic changes — unlike genetic mutations — makes them particularly attractive therapeutic targets. Unlike gene mutations, methylation can be pharmacologically reversed, potentially reactivating silenced tumour suppressor genes.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Epigenetics = heritable changes in gene expression WITHOUT changes in DNA sequence — includes DNA methylation, histone modifications, non-coding RNAs, and chromatin remodelling
> 2. CpG island HYPERMETHYLATION of tumour suppressor gene promoters is the most common epigenetic alteration in cancer — silences p16, PTEN, DCC, RAR-beta in HNSCC
> 3. Global HYPOMETHYLATION occurs simultaneously — causes genomic instability and chromosomal rearrangements
> 4. CDKN2A (p16) is inactivated in 80% of HNSCC: by mutation (5-15%), deletion (30-60%), or HYPERMETHYLATION (10-20%) — three routes, one outcome
> 5. MLL2 (histone H3 methyltransferase) is mutated in 18% of HNSCC — mutations in epigenetic regulators themselves drive cancer (TCGA, 2015)
> 6. let-7c microRNA is down-regulated in ~40% of HPV-negative HNSCC — leads to increased CDK6 expression and cell cycle progression (Scott-Brown's)
> 7. HPV positivity and p16 methylation are MUTUALLY EXCLUSIVE — both achieve pRB pathway inactivation by different mechanisms (Stell and Maran's)
> 8. Tumour-specific methylation can be detected in SALIVA and SERUM — potential non-invasive screening biomarker for HNSCC
> 9. Epigenetic changes are REVERSIBLE — unlike genetic mutations — making them attractive therapeutic targets (DNMT inhibitors, HDAC inhibitors)
> 10. Histone acetylation = open chromatin = gene activation; histone deacetylation = closed chromatin = gene silencing

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, know the FOUR main epigenetic mechanisms: (1) DNA methylation, (2) histone modifications, (3) non-coding RNAs (miRNA), (4) chromatin remodelling
> - The key concept: CpG island hypermethylation SILENCES tumour suppressor genes — this is an ALTERNATIVE to genetic mutation for inactivating TSGs
> - p16 is the highest-yield example: 80% of HNSCC lack p16 function, but only 5-15% have mutations — the rest are silenced by deletion or METHYLATION
> - Remember the dual pattern: promoter HYPERmethylation (silences genes) + global HYPOmethylation (causes instability) — both occur simultaneously
> - The reversibility of epigenetic changes is the key therapeutic concept — DNMT inhibitors (azacitidine, decitabine) can reactivate silenced TSGs
> - Methylation-based liquid biopsy (saliva/serum) is an emerging clinical application for HNSCC screening — high-yield for the exam
