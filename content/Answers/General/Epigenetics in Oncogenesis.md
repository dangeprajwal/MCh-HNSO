## Introduction

Epigenetics refers to heritable changes in gene expression that occur without alteration of the underlying DNA sequence. Unlike genetic mutations — which permanently change the nucleotide sequence of DNA — epigenetic modifications regulate whether genes are "turned on" or "turned off" through chemical modifications to the DNA itself or to the histone proteins around which DNA is wrapped. These modifications are reversible, heritable through cell division, and critically influenced by environmental factors such as diet, tobacco exposure, and aging. In oncogenesis, epigenetic alterations function as an alternative pathway to genetic mutation for silencing tumour suppressor genes, and their reversibility makes them attractive therapeutic targets.

As described in Scott-Brown's Volume 3 (Chapter 6), epigenetic modification of gene expression and function is a major mechanism that promotes cancer in the head and neck. Epigenetic regulation has been proposed by some as an additional emerging hallmark in cancer development, alongside the six classical hallmarks defined by Hanahan and Weinberg. Considerable evidence points to epigenetic alterations playing an important role in the development of head and neck cancers, supported by data from large-scale genomic studies including the TCGA consortium, which found mutations in the histone H3 methyltransferase gene MLL2 in 18% of head and neck cancers — confirming that genes controlling epigenetic processes are themselves frequently disrupted in HNSCC.

## Part A: Types of Epigenetic Modifications

### 1. DNA Methylation

DNA methylation is the most extensively studied epigenetic modification. It involves the addition of a methyl group (-CH3) to the 5-carbon position of cytosine residues, converting them to 5-methylcytosine. This occurs predominantly at cytosines that are immediately 5' to a guanine base — termed **CpG dinucleotides**. In many genes, the concentration of CpG sites is higher in the non-coding promoter regions, forming structures known as **CpG islands**. The enzymes that catalyze this reaction are the DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs): DNMT1 (maintenance methyltransferase, copies methylation pattern during replication), DNMT3a and DNMT3b (de novo methyltransferases that establish new methylation patterns).

**Effect on gene expression:** Methylation of CpG islands in the promoter region of a gene typically results in **transcriptional silencing** — the gene is "turned off." This occurs through two mechanisms: (1) methylated CpGs directly block the binding of transcription factors to the promoter, and (2) methylated DNA recruits methyl-binding domain (MBD) proteins that in turn recruit histone deacetylases (HDACs) and other chromatin-modifying enzymes, leading to a closed, transcriptionally inactive chromatin structure known as heterochromatin.

**In cancer:** Two opposite patterns of aberrant methylation coexist:
- **Promoter hypermethylation:** Excessive methylation of CpG islands in the promoter regions of tumour suppressor genes leads to their transcriptional silencing. This is functionally equivalent to gene deletion or loss-of-function mutation — the TSG product is no longer produced. As noted in Scott-Brown's, the most commonly observed epigenetic modification with strong links to cancer is the methylation of cytosine residues at CpG islands in the promoter regions of tumour suppressor genes. For example, CDKN2A (p16) hypermethylation has been observed to be associated with reduced overall survival in population-based studies of head and neck cancer.
- **Global hypomethylation:** Decreased methylation across repetitive DNA sequences and satellite DNA leads to chromosomal instability, reactivation of transposable elements, and genomic instability — promoting the acquisition of further mutations.

### Commonly Hypermethylated Genes in HNSCC

| Gene | Function | Consequence of Silencing |
|------|----------|------------------------|
| CDKN2A (p16) | CDK4/6 inhibitor; cell cycle control | Loss of G1-S checkpoint; uncontrolled proliferation |
| DAPK1 | Death-associated protein kinase; apoptosis | Evasion of apoptosis |
| MGMT | DNA repair (O6-methylguanine) | Impaired DNA repair; resistance to alkylating agents |
| RASSF1A | RAS association domain family; growth suppression | Loss of growth inhibition |
| CDH1 (E-cadherin) | Cell adhesion | Loss of cell-cell adhesion; promotes invasion and metastasis |
| TIMP3 | Tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases | Enhanced MMP activity; promotes invasion |
| RARβ2 | Retinoic acid receptor beta | Loss of retinoid-mediated growth control |
| p14ARF | MDM2 inhibitor; stabilizes p53 | Loss of p53 stabilization |

As described in Stell and Maran's Textbook (Chapter 3), CDKN2A/p16 can be inactivated in HNSCC by three mechanisms: homozygous deletion, somatic mutation, or epigenetic silencing. Epigenetic silencing by promoter hypermethylation accounts for approximately 20-30% of p16 inactivation events in HNSCC, making it the most common epigenetic event in this disease. Furthermore, tumour-specific methylation events can be detected in the saliva and serum of HNSCC patients, raising the possibility of using methylation-based liquid biopsy for early cancer detection and surveillance.

### 2. Histone Modifications

DNA in the nucleus is wrapped around octameric histone protein complexes (H2A, H2B, H3, H4) to form nucleosomes — the basic structural units of chromatin. The N-terminal "tails" of histone proteins protrude from the nucleosome core and can be chemically modified by a variety of enzymes. These modifications collectively determine whether chromatin is in an "open" (euchromatin, transcriptionally active) or "closed" (heterochromatin, transcriptionally silent) state. This is referred to as the **histone code**.

**Key histone modifications:**

| Modification | Effect | Enzymes |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Acetylation (H3K9ac, H3K27ac) | Opens chromatin → gene activation | HATs (histone acetyltransferases) add; HDACs remove |
| Methylation (H3K4me3) | Gene activation | HMTs (histone methyltransferases, e.g., MLL2) |
| Methylation (H3K9me3, H3K27me3) | Gene silencing | EZH2 (Polycomb group); SUV39H1 |
| Phosphorylation (H3S10) | Chromatin condensation during mitosis | Aurora kinases |
| Ubiquitination (H2AK119ub) | Gene silencing | Polycomb repressive complex 1 (PRC1) |

**In HNSCC:** The TCGA found that MLL2 (a histone H3 lysine 4 methyltransferase responsible for activating gene expression) is mutated in 18% of head and neck cancers. Loss of MLL2 function reduces H3K4 trimethylation at gene promoters, leading to reduced transcription of genes involved in differentiation and tumour suppression. Additionally, overexpression of EZH2 (the catalytic subunit of PRC2, which adds the repressive H3K27me3 mark) has been reported in HNSCC and is associated with aggressive disease.

### 3. Non-Coding RNAs

Non-coding RNAs — particularly **microRNAs (miRNAs)** and **long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs)** — are increasingly recognized as key epigenetic regulators in cancer:

- **MicroRNAs (miRNAs):** Small (21-25 nucleotide) non-coding RNAs that bind to complementary sequences in the 3'-UTR of target mRNAs, causing translational repression or mRNA degradation. In HNSCC, specific miRNAs can function as either oncogenes (onco-miRs, e.g., miR-21, miR-155) or tumour suppressors (e.g., miR-34a, miR-200 family). miR-21 is consistently overexpressed in HNSCC and promotes proliferation by targeting PTEN and PDCD4.
- **Long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs):** RNA molecules >200 nucleotides that do not encode protein but regulate gene expression through chromatin remodelling, transcriptional regulation, and post-transcriptional processing. HOTAIR overexpression has been linked to metastasis in HNSCC.

## Part B: Role of Epigenetics in Head and Neck Oncogenesis

### The Epigenetic Model of Cancer

Epigenetic modifications and genetic mutations are not mutually exclusive — they cooperate in a stepwise fashion during carcinogenesis. The current model proposes that epigenetic silencing of TSGs can function as one of the "two hits" in Knudson's two-hit hypothesis. For example, p16/CDKN2A may be inactivated in HNSCC by genetic deletion on one allele and promoter hypermethylation on the other — achieving biallelic inactivation through a combination of genetic and epigenetic mechanisms.

### Epigenetics and Field Cancerization

Epigenetic changes have been detected in histologically normal mucosa adjacent to HNSCC tumours — representing the molecular basis of field cancerization. Promoter hypermethylation of p14, p15, and p16 has been detected in precancerous lesions from betel-quid chewers in Sri Lanka, and these changes precede the development of invasive cancer. This suggests that epigenetic profiling of the "at-risk" mucosa may identify patients at highest risk of malignant transformation.

### Environmental Factors Influencing Epigenetics

As noted in Scott-Brown's, dietary folate may influence epigenetic regulation since it ultimately provides the methyl groups required for DNA methylation, but the links between folate levels in diet and gene methylation are complex and often counter-intuitive. Tobacco carcinogens have been shown to induce aberrant DNA methylation patterns, contributing to the epigenetic dysregulation observed in tobacco-related HNSCC. Alcohol's effects on folate metabolism may further amplify methylation abnormalities, providing a potential mechanism for the synergistic carcinogenic effect of tobacco and alcohol.

## Part C: Clinical Applications of Epigenetics

### Epigenetic Biomarkers

- **Promoter methylation in saliva:** Tumour-specific methylation of genes like DAPK, MGMT, p16, and TIMP3 can be detected in saliva rinses of HNSCC patients — offering potential for non-invasive screening
- **Serum/plasma methylation markers:** Cell-free DNA in blood shows tumour-specific methylation patterns that can be used for disease monitoring
- **Prognostic value:** CDKN2A hypermethylation is associated with reduced overall survival; MGMT methylation predicts response to alkylating agents

### Epigenetic Therapy (Epi-drugs)

The reversibility of epigenetic modifications makes them attractive therapeutic targets:

| Drug Class | Mechanism | Examples | Status in HNSCC |
|-----------|-----------|---------|----------------|
| DNMT inhibitors | Demethylate DNA → reactivate silenced TSGs | 5-azacytidine, decitabine | Clinical trials |
| HDAC inhibitors | Increase histone acetylation → open chromatin → gene reactivation | Vorinostat, romidepsin | Phase I/II trials in HNSCC |
| EZH2 inhibitors | Reduce H3K27me3 → derepress TSGs | Tazemetostat | Under investigation |
| BET inhibitors | Block bromodomain recognition of acetylated histones | JQ1 | Preclinical |

Coombes et al. (2003) demonstrated that treatment of HNSCC cells with the DNMT inhibitor 5-aza-2'-deoxycytidine (5-aza-dC) increased CDKN2A expression and histone H3 acetylation. Synergistic reactivation of p16 was observed upon sequential treatment with both 5-aza-dC and the HDAC inhibitor TSA — establishing the principle that combined epigenetic therapy targeting both DNA methylation and histone deacetylation can effectively "reset" the epigenetic code at silenced tumour suppressor loci.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Epigenetics = heritable changes in gene expression without DNA sequence alteration — methylation, histone modification, non-coding RNAs
> 2. DNA methylation at CpG islands in TSG promoters causes transcriptional silencing — functionally equivalent to gene deletion
> 3. CDKN2A (p16) promoter hypermethylation is the most frequent epigenetic event in HNSCC — associated with reduced OS
> 4. Global DNA hypomethylation coexists with promoter hypermethylation — causing chromosomal instability
> 5. Histone modifications form the "histone code": acetylation = activation, H3K27me3 = silencing
> 6. MLL2 histone methyltransferase is mutated in 18% of HNSCC (TCGA) — confirming epigenetic regulators as cancer driver genes
> 7. miR-21 is the most consistently overexpressed miRNA in HNSCC — targets PTEN and PDCD4
> 8. Tumour-specific methylation is detectable in saliva and serum — potential for non-invasive screening
> 9. Epigenetic therapy (DNMT inhibitors, HDAC inhibitors) can reactivate silenced TSGs — reversible unlike genetic mutations
> 10. Epigenetic changes in "normal" mucosa adjacent to tumours represent the molecular basis of field cancerization

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Epigenetic silencing is the "third mechanism" of TSG inactivation (alongside deletion and mutation) — often overlooked in exam answers
> - p16 can be inactivated by deletion, mutation, OR methylation — but p16 is overexpressed in HPV+ disease (E7 inactivates Rb → feedback upregulation) — don't confuse these two situations
> - Saliva-based methylation testing (DAPK, MGMT, p16) is one of the most promising non-invasive screening approaches for oral cancer — watch this space
> - Dietary folate provides methyl groups for DNA methylation — folate deficiency may contribute to epigenetic dysregulation in malnourished HNC patients
> - HDAC inhibitors (vorinostat) have shown activity in combination with radiotherapy in HNSCC clinical trials — a translational application of epigenetic biology
> - When writing about the molecular biology of cancer, always include epigenetics alongside genetic mutations — examiners expect both
