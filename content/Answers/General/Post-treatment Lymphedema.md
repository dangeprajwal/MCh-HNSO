## Introduction

Head and neck lymphedema (HNL) is the accumulation of protein-rich fluid in the soft tissues of the head and neck region due to impaired lymphatic drainage following cancer treatment. It is one of the most common yet **under-recognized** complications of head and neck cancer therapy, affecting both visible external structures (face, neck, submentum) and internal mucosal structures (larynx, pharynx, tongue).

Harrison's Chapter 22 lists lymphedema as a **delayed complication** of oropharyngeal cancer treatment alongside fistula, flap loss, trismus, and shoulder dysfunction. Harrison's Chapter 25 identifies lymphedema as both an **acute and long-term toxicity** of external beam radiation for nasopharyngeal carcinoma.

Ridner et al. (2016) demonstrated that **>90% of HNC patients** experience some form of internal, external, or combined lymphedema within 18 months of treatment, with over half developing fibrosis — establishing the concept of a **lymphedema-fibrosis continuum**.

---

## Part A: Pathophysiology

### Normal Lymphatic Drainage of the Head and Neck

The head and neck has a rich lymphatic network draining into cervical lymph node groups (levels I-VII). Lymphatic fluid is propelled by smooth muscle contraction in lymphatic vessel walls, skeletal muscle movement, and respiratory motion.

### Mechanisms of HNL Development

| Mechanism | Cause | Effect |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| **Surgical disruption** | Neck dissection, primary tumour resection | Direct damage to lymphatic channels and nodes |
| **Radiation fibrosis** | External beam RT to lymphatic drainage areas | Progressive fibrotic obliteration of lymphatic vessels |
| **Inflammatory damage** | Chemoradiation-induced inflammation | Endothelial injury and lymphatic dysfunction |
| **Venous obstruction** | Post-surgical scarring, tumour compression | Elevated venous pressure impedes lymphatic drainage |

Harrison's Chapter 22 specifically notes that neck dissection was performed 1-3 weeks after TORS to **minimize lymphedema** in the oropharynx — highlighting the direct relationship between surgical timing and lymphedema risk.

### Types of Head and Neck Lymphedema

| Type | Location | Features |
|------|----------|----------|
| **External** | Face, neck, submentum, periorbital | Visible swelling; measured by tape/perometry |
| **Internal** | Larynx, pharynx, tongue, oral cavity | Mucosal swelling; assessed by endoscopy |
| **Combined** | Both external and internal | Most common pattern |

Deng et al. (2012) found that **75.3%** of HNC patients had external lymphedema, while internal lymphedema was present in **9.1%** (likely underestimated due to assessment methods).

## Part B: Risk Factors

### Treatment-Related Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Mechanism |
|------------|-----------|
| **Neck dissection** | Most significant surgical risk factor; OR 1.96 |
| **Bilateral neck dissection** | Greater lymphatic disruption |
| **Number of lymph nodes removed** | Directly proportional to lymphedema risk |
| **Radiation dose** | Higher doses = more fibrosis; doses >60 Gy significant |
| **Combined modality treatment** | Surgery + RT > RT alone > surgery alone |
| **Chemotherapy (cisplatin)** | Inflammatory tissue damage |
| **Extent of primary resection** | Larger resections disrupt more lymphatic channels |

### Patient-Related Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Evidence |
|------------|----------|
| **Advanced nodal disease** | Higher nodal stage = greater lymphedema risk |
| **Tumour subsite** | Oropharyngeal and oral cavity sites higher risk |
| **Smoking** | Impaired wound healing and lymphatic function |
| **Obesity** | Increased tissue oedema |
| **Pre-existing lymphatic dysfunction** | Baseline lymphatic compromise |

### Temporal Pattern

Ridner et al. (2016) showed that external HNL develops most rapidly between **2 and 6 months** following treatment:
- 60 days: 28.9% cumulative incidence
- 180 days: 62.8% cumulative incidence
- 360 days: ~70% (stabilizes)

## Part C: Clinical Presentation and Assessment

### Clinical Features

**External lymphedema:**
- Soft, pitting oedema of the face, neck, and submentum (early)
- Firm, non-pitting fibrosis (late — the "fibrosis continuum")
- Asymmetric facial swelling
- Skin changes: thickening, induration

**Internal lymphedema:**
Harrison's Chapter 27 describes: there is often minimal lymphedema of the larynx for 1-2 months after RT, which usually subsides or stabilizes. An **increase in edema, particularly if associated with hoarseness or pain, suggests recurrence** even if there is no obvious tumour. This clinical pearl is critical for post-treatment surveillance.

- Pharyngeal/laryngeal mucosal swelling → dysphagia, dysphonia
- Tongue swelling → speech difficulty, airway compromise
- Arytenoid oedema → hoarseness, stridor

### Assessment Methods

| Method | What It Measures |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Tape measurement** | Circumference of face/neck at defined landmarks |
| **Patterson scale** | Clinician-rated 0-3 scale for HNL severity |
| **Endoscopic assessment** | Internal lymphedema of larynx/pharynx |
| **VHNSS** (Vanderbilt Head and Neck Symptom Survey) | Patient-reported outcome; validated lymphedema subscale |
| **Bioimpedance spectroscopy** | Measures extracellular fluid; emerging tool |
| **MRI/CT** | Soft tissue oedema and fibrosis assessment |

### Grading

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **0** | No lymphedema |
| **1 (Mild)** | Soft, pitting oedema; reversible with elevation |
| **2 (Moderate)** | Non-pitting oedema; fibrotic changes; partially reversible |
| **3 (Severe)** | Irreversible fibrosis; skin changes; functional impairment |

## Part D: Management

### Complete Decongestive Therapy (CDT)

Smith and Lewin (2010) established CDT as the **evidence-based standard of care** for HNL. CDT consists of four components:

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **1. Manual lymphatic drainage (MLD)** | Gentle massage following lymphatic pathways to redirect fluid to functioning lymphatic territories |
| **2. Compression therapy** | Compression garments/bandaging to maintain reduced volume |
| **3. Exercise** | Active range of motion and stretching to promote lymphatic flow |
| **4. Skin care** | Meticulous hygiene to prevent infection (cellulitis) |

CDT is delivered in two phases:
- **Phase 1 (Intensive/Reduction):** Daily MLD + bandaging + exercises for 2-4 weeks
- **Phase 2 (Maintenance):** Self-MLD + compression garments + exercises (lifelong)

### Additional Therapeutic Options

| Treatment | Evidence | Indication |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Low-level laser therapy** | Reduces fibrosis and swelling | Fibrotic HNL |
| **Kinesio taping** | Supports lymphatic flow | Adjunct to CDT |
| **Pneumatic compression** | Intermittent sequential compression | Moderate-severe HNL |
| **Selenium supplementation** | Anti-inflammatory; reduces radiation-induced lymphedema | During/after RT |
| **Surgical options** | Lymphovenous anastomosis; vascularized lymph node transfer | Refractory cases |

### Specific Considerations in HNC

**Internal lymphedema management:**
- Endoscopic monitoring for airway compromise
- Speech-language pathology for swallowing rehabilitation
- Steroids (dexamethasone) for acute mucosal oedema
- Tracheostomy if severe airway obstruction

Harrison's Chapter 25 notes: it is **unusual for patients to require a tracheotomy** before RT unless severe lymphedema develops at the time of direct laryngoscopy and biopsy.

## Part E: Prevention

| Strategy | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Selective neck dissection** | Preserves uninvolved levels; less lymphatic disruption |
| **IMRT** | Reduces radiation dose to non-target lymphatic structures |
| **Baseline assessment** | Document pre-treatment measurements for comparison |
| **Early physiotherapy** | Begin range-of-motion exercises within 2 weeks of surgery |
| **Patient education** | Self-monitoring for early signs; skin care; infection prevention |
| **Avoid tight clothing** | Restrictive garments impede lymphatic return |
| **Weight management** | Obesity worsens lymphedema |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Head and neck lymphedema affects >90% of HNC patients within 18 months of treatment — it is under-recognized and under-treated (Ridner et al., 2016)
> 2. HNL exists as a CONTINUUM from lymphedema to fibrosis — >50% of patients develop fibrosis, which is partially irreversible
> 3. External lymphedema (face, neck) and INTERNAL lymphedema (larynx, pharynx) both occur — internal lymphedema affects swallowing and airway
> 4. Neck dissection is the most significant surgical risk factor; combined modality treatment (surgery + RT + chemo) carries highest risk
> 5. Complete Decongestive Therapy (CDT) is the standard of care: manual lymphatic drainage + compression + exercise + skin care (Smith and Lewin, 2010)
> 6. External HNL develops most rapidly between 2-6 months post-treatment, stabilizing by ~12 months
> 7. Increasing laryngeal oedema post-RT, especially with hoarseness or pain, should raise suspicion of RECURRENCE — not just persistent lymphedema (Harrison)
> 8. 75.3% of HNC patients develop external lymphedema; risk factors include bilateral ND, higher RT dose, advanced nodal disease, and smoking
> 9. IMRT and selective neck dissection are key preventive strategies — minimizing lymphatic structure damage
> 10. CDT is delivered in two phases: intensive reduction (daily MLD for 2-4 weeks) then lifelong maintenance (self-MLD + compression)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, know the FOUR components of CDT: manual lymphatic drainage, compression, exercise, skin care — this is the standard treatment
> - Internal lymphedema is the most dangerous form — can cause airway obstruction, dysphagia, and aspiration; may require tracheostomy
> - The critical clinical pearl from Harrison: post-RT laryngeal oedema that INCREASES or is associated with hoarseness/pain = suspect RECURRENCE, not just lymphedema
> - Risk factors to remember: bilateral neck dissection > unilateral, combined treatment > single modality, higher RT dose, more nodes removed
> - The lymphedema-fibrosis continuum concept (Ridner 2016) is important: early lymphedema is soft and reversible; late fibrosis is firm and partially irreversible — early intervention is key
> - HNL is the "forgotten" complication — far less attention than extremity lymphedema despite being more common in HNC survivors
