## Introduction

Tumour suppressor genes (TSGs) are normal cellular genes whose protein products inhibit cell proliferation, promote [[Answers/General/Apoptosis|apoptosis]], and maintain genomic stability. In contrast to oncogenes — where a single mutated copy can promote cancer through gain-of-function (phenotypic dominance) — TSGs require inactivation of both alleles to lose their protective function. As described in Scott-Brown's Volume 3 (Chapter 6), the functions of two classes of genes — oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes — are central to understanding cancer biology. In contrast to oncogenes, tumour suppressor genes are normal cellular genes whose function inhibits cell proliferation and survival; the function of both copies of a TSG must be lost in order to promote cancer (so-called phenotypic recessiveness). Mutated TSGs are responsible for the majority of inherited cancer syndromes, although inherited TSG mutations are not a significant cause of head and neck cancers — the majority of TSG inactivation in HNSCC occurs through somatic events.

TSGs were first recognized through study of inherited cancers. Montgomery's textbook (Chapter 2) describes how TSGs were first recognized in the etiology of the inherited forms of retinoblastomas and Wilm's tumours. It is now evident that mutation in the p53 TSG occurs in a wide variety of human cancers. Li-Fraumeni families, who have a germline p53 mutation, can develop cancer at multiple sites, emphasizing the role of p53 as the "guardian of the genome."

The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) analysis of 279 HNSCCs confirmed that loss of TSG function is the most common molecular event in this disease — far more frequent than oncogene activation. As described in Scott-Brown's, the most common pathway affected in both [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-positive]] and HPV-negative disease is loss of normal cell cycle control, which occurs in 90-100% of both types of cancer.

## Part A: The Two-Hit Hypothesis and Mechanisms of TSG Inactivation

### Knudson's Two-Hit Hypothesis

Alfred Knudson (1971) proposed, based on statistical analysis of retinoblastoma cases, that cancer development requires two mutational events ("hits") to inactivate both alleles of a critical gene:

- **Hereditary cancers:** One hit is inherited (germline mutation), the second is somatic — hence earlier onset, bilateral tumours, autosomal dominant inheritance pattern
- **Sporadic cancers:** Both hits must occur somatically in the same cell — hence later onset, unilateral tumours

This hypothesis was subsequently confirmed by the cloning of the RB1 gene and has become the foundational model for understanding all TSGs. As noted in Scott-Brown's, RB was the first TSG to be identified, and any treatise on the RB gene will usually include mention of Alfred Knudsen, retinoblastoma, and the two-hit hypothesis.

### Mechanisms of TSG Inactivation

TSGs can be inactivated through multiple mechanisms, often with different mechanisms affecting the two alleles:

| Mechanism | Description | Example in HNSCC |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Point mutation | Missense or nonsense mutation in coding sequence | TP53 (60-80% of HPV− HNSCC) |
| Deletion | Homozygous deletion of the gene locus | CDKN2A at 9p21 |
| Loss of heterozygosity (LOH) | Loss of one allele + mutation/methylation of the other | FHIT at 3p14 (66-68%) |
| Promoter hypermethylation | [[Answers/General/Epigenetics in Oncogenesis|epigenetics]] silencing without DNA sequence change | CDKN2A (20-30% of HNSCC) |
| Viral oncoprotein binding | Functional inactivation of TSG protein | HPV E6 degrades p53; E7 inactivates Rb |

As described in Montgomery's textbook, CDKN2A/p16 can be inactivated in HNSCC by three mechanisms: homozygous deletion, somatic mutation (10-15% of HNSCCs), or transcriptional silencing through promoter methylation — and these major inactivation mechanisms are not mutually exclusive. Loss of p16 is present in 52-82% of tumours from HNSCC patients.

The concept of **haploinsufficiency** — where loss of a single allele of a TSG can produce a tumour-prone phenotype — has expanded beyond the classical two-hit model. Montgomery notes that classically, both alleles need to be inactivated, such as deletion of one allele along with a mutation in the other; however, some TSGs display haploinsufficiency, allowing hemizygous TSG to show a tumour-prone phenotype when challenged with carcinogens.

## Part B: Major Tumour Suppressor Genes in HNSCC

### 1. TP53 — The Guardian of the Genome

TP53 is the single most commonly mutated gene in HNSCC. As described in Scott-Brown's, TP53 has been found to be mutated in approximately 50-60% of all head and neck cancers. TCGA network analysis showed that this varied from approximately 80% in HPV-negative cancers to 0% in HPV-positive cancers — this difference is explained by the fact that HPV is oncogenic precisely because it can inactivate the p53 protein through E6-mediated degradation rather than through genetic mutation.

**Functions of p53:**
- Sequence-specific DNA binding protein that regulates expression of hundreds of target genes
- Potent inducer of apoptosis — under normal circumstances, MDM2 and MDM4 maintain p53 at low levels in an inactive form
- Following genotoxic stress, p53-MDM2 interaction is prevented → p53 levels rise → upregulation of p53 target genes mediating cell cycle arrest (p21) or apoptosis (BAX, PUMA)
- Activated by DNA damage, oncogene activation, hypoxia, and other cellular stresses

**Prognostic significance:** Loss of p53 function has been found to be associated with patient outcomes, with patients harbouring **disruptive mutations** (non-conservative amino acid substitutions in critical parts of the DNA binding domain) displaying reduced overall survival compared to those with wild-type or non-disruptive mutations. Montgomery adds that mutant p53 protein overexpression is associated with tumour recurrence, reduced disease-free and overall survival rates, and decreased rates of local control in HNSCC. Furthermore, p53 overexpression in regional lymph nodes was found to be an independent predictor of regional failure and poor prognosis.

**Role as molecular staging marker:** The findings of p53 mutations in histologically negative margins of completely resected tumours and the distinct p53 genotypes in synchronous primary tumours suggest that p53 can be used as a marker for molecular staging and fingerprinting in HNSCC. A higher incidence of p53 mutation has been found in the tumours of smokers and drinkers than in those who developed HNSCC without exposure to these agents, and p53 mutation appears to be an early event in HNSCC carcinogenesis as it has been observed in premalignant lesions.

### 2. CDKN2A (p16INK4a) — Cell Cycle Gatekeeper

The most commonly deleted chromosome site in HNSCC is located at 9p21-p24, which is the locus for the CDKN2A/MTS1/INK4A TSG. The p16INK4A gene product is an inhibitor of the cyclin/[[Answers/General/Cell Cycle and Cancer|cyclin-dependent kinases]] complex. As described in Scott-Brown's, p16 functions as a negative regulator of G1-S phase progression that acts by binding to and inhibiting CDK4 and CDK6 — preventing Rb phosphorylation and blocking cell cycle progression.

**Key points from Montgomery:**
- LOH of 9p21-22 has been reported with high frequency in dysplasia, CIS, and HNSCC — suggesting this genetic alteration is involved in early developmental stages of HNSCC, whereas deregulation of p53 and cyclin D1 occur later
- Loss of p16 is present in 52-82% of HNSCC patients
- Using [[Answers/General/FISH in Head and Neck Cancer|FISH]], p16 deletion was detected in 52% of patients with HNSCC and was significantly associated with development of distant metastases
- A prospective study of locally advanced laryngeal SCC identified p16 mutation as an independent predictive factor for disease relapse and death

**The CDKN2A locus encodes two proteins** with tumour suppressor activities: p16 (p16INK4a, inhibiting CDK4/6) and p14 (p14ARF, which stabilizes p53 by inhibiting MDM2). Therefore, loss of the CDKN2A locus simultaneously disrupts both the Rb and p53 pathways.

**In HPV-positive disease:** HPV E7 inactivates Rb, leading to loss of negative feedback on p16 expression → p16 accumulates and is overexpressed. This is why p16 immunohistochemistry serves as a surrogate marker of HPV infection — but p16 overexpression results from Rb inactivation, not directly from HPV.

### 3. RB1 — The First Tumour Suppressor

RB was the first TSG to be identified. Its protein product (Rb) regulates the G1-S phase transition of the cell cycle by binding to and inhibiting the activity of E2F transcription factors. As described in Scott-Brown's, phosphorylation of RB by Cyclin D/CDK4/6 complexes leads to inactivation of RB — it no longer binds to E2F, and as a result, E2Fs promote transcription of genes involved in DNA synthesis (S-phase). RB not only regulates the cell cycle but also regulates the cell cycle arrest that is a requirement for terminal differentiation.

In HNSCC, direct RB1 mutation is relatively uncommon (~5% of HPV-negative tumours) because RB is more commonly inactivated indirectly — through CDKN2A loss (removing the brake on CDK4/6) or Cyclin D1 amplification (constitutively phosphorylating Rb). In HPV-positive HNSCC, E7 oncoprotein directly binds and inactivates Rb.

### 4. NOTCH1 — A Novel Tumour Suppressor in HNSCC

The TCGA study identified NOTCH1 as a frequently mutated gene in HNSCC, with predominantly loss-of-function mutations — establishing it as a TSG rather than an oncogene in this context (in contrast to its oncogenic role in T-cell leukaemia). NOTCH1 mutations are found in approximately 15-19% of HNSCC and are associated with an oral cavity subgroup that has favourable clinical outcomes (infrequent copy number alterations with activating mutations of HRAS or PIK3CA, coupled with inactivating mutations of CASP8 and NOTCH1).

## Part C: The Genetic Progression Model

Califano et al. (1996) established the first genetic progression model for HNSCC, demonstrating that sequential loss of TSG function accompanies the histological progression from normal mucosa to invasive cancer:

| Histological Stage | Genetic Events |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Normal mucosa → Hyperplasia | LOH at 9p21 (CDKN2A/p16) |
| Hyperplasia → Dysplasia | LOH at 3p (FHIT) and 17p (TP53) |
| Dysplasia → CIS | LOH at 11q13, 13q, 14q |
| CIS → Invasive carcinoma | LOH at 6p, 8p, 4q; Cyclin D1 amplification |

This model demonstrated three critical principles:
1. **Sequential accumulation:** Each histopathological step is accompanied by additional genetic alterations — the spectrum of chromosomal loss progressively increases
2. **Field cancerization:** Adjacent areas of tissue with different histological appearance share common genetic changes (arising from a single progenitor clone), but more advanced areas exhibit additional genetic alterations
3. **Early vs. late events:** Loss of 9p21 (p16) is the earliest detectable TSG loss, whereas TP53 mutation occurs at a later stage — confirming the order of TSG inactivation in HNSCC

## Part D: HPV-Mediated TSG Inactivation

In HPV-positive HNSCC, viral oncoproteins directly inactivate the two central TSG pathways without requiring genetic mutation:

- **E6 oncoprotein:** Binds p53 via E6AP (E6-associated protein) → promotes p53 degradation via the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway → loss of G1 checkpoint and apoptosis
- **E7 oncoprotein:** Binds and inactivates Rb → releases E2F → forces S-phase entry

As described in Scott-Brown's, together these viral oncogenes inactivate two TSGs, TP53 and RB. One consequence is that HPV-positive tumours have far fewer somatic mutations than HPV-negative tumours (because the critical TSGs are already inactivated in all of these cells). Another consequence of HPV-mediated TSG inactivation is that loss of Rb function leads to upregulation of p16/CDKN2A expression — providing the basis for using p16 immunohistochemistry as a surrogate marker for HPV status.

## Part E: Clinical Implications

### TSG Status and Treatment Response

- **TP53 mutation:** Associated with reduced response to radiotherapy and chemotherapy (loss of apoptotic pathway), worse OS, increased recurrence
- **CDKN2A loss:** Associated with distant metastasis and disease relapse; p16 overexpression (HPV+) paradoxically indicates better prognosis
- **FHIT loss:** Although an adverse prognostic factor, FHIT-negative high-risk tumours experienced locoregional recurrence less often (18%) than FHIT-positive tumours (33%), suggesting FHIT-negative tumours may be more sensitive to postoperative radiotherapy

### Therapeutic Targeting of TSG Loss

Rescuing loss of TSG function is inherently more difficult than inhibiting overactive oncogenes. As noted in Scott-Brown's, this remains a significant challenge. Current approaches include:
- **CDK4/6 inhibitors** (palbociclib, ribociclib): Mimic the effect of lost p16 function by directly inhibiting CDK4/6
- **MDM2 inhibitors:** Restore p53 function in tumours with wild-type p53 that is suppressed by MDM2 overexpression
- **Synthetic lethality:** Exploiting dependencies created by specific TSG losses (e.g., PTEN-deficient tumours may be sensitive to PI3K/mTOR inhibitors)
- **Gene therapy:** Adenoviral delivery of wild-type p53 (Ad-p53/Advexin) has been tested in clinical trials in HNSCC

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. TSGs are normal cellular genes whose loss of function promotes cancer — both alleles must be inactivated (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis)
> 2. TSG inactivation can occur by mutation, deletion, LOH, promoter hypermethylation, or viral oncoprotein binding
> 3. TP53 is the most commonly mutated gene in HNSCC: ~80% HPV-negative, 0% HPV-positive (E6 degrades p53 instead)
> 4. CDKN2A/p16 loss at 9p21 is the earliest detectable TSG event in HNSCC — present in 52-82% of tumours
> 5. The CDKN2A locus encodes two TSGs: p16 (inhibits CDK4/6) and p14ARF (stabilizes p53 via MDM2 inhibition)
> 6. Disruptive TP53 mutations predict reduced overall survival; p53 in histologically negative margins predicts recurrence
> 7. Califano's genetic progression model: sequential LOH at 9p→3p→17p accompanies hyperplasia→dysplasia→carcinoma
> 8. TCGA identified novel TSGs in HNSCC: NOTCH1 (15-19%), FAT1, NSD1, AJUBA
> 9. HPV E6 and E7 inactivate p53 and Rb respectively — explaining why HPV+ tumours have fewer somatic mutations
> 10. Rescuing TSG function is harder than inhibiting oncogenes — CDK4/6 inhibitors and MDM2 inhibitors are under investigation

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - p16 immunohistochemistry is used as a surrogate marker for HPV status — but remember p16 overexpression results from Rb inactivation by E7, not directly from HPV
> - p53 mutation is a late event in the Califano model; p16 loss at 9p is early — this is why p16 LOH is detectable in premalignant lesions
> - The CDKN2A locus is a "double hit" target: losing it knocks out both the p16/Rb pathway AND the p14ARF/p53 pathway simultaneously
> - Finding p53 mutations in histologically clear [[Answers/General/Surgical Margins in Head and Neck Cancer|surgical margins]] is a molecular indicator of residual disease — molecular staging may be more accurate than histological staging
> - FHIT-negative tumours paradoxically respond better to postoperative RT — a potential molecular predictor of radiation sensitivity
> - When writing about TSGs in exams, always contrast with oncogenes: TSGs are recessive (need two hits), oncogenes are dominant (one hit); TSGs are lost, oncogenes are gained
