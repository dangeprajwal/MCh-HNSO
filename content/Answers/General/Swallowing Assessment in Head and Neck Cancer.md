## Introduction

Dysphagia is one of the most debilitating consequences of advanced laryngeal cancer and its treatment. Up to 50% of advanced HNC survivors experience chronic dysphagia, with aspiration rates of 30-62% after RT/CRT and silent aspiration in at least one-third of aspirators. The paradigm has shifted from post-treatment rehabilitation to pretreatment prevention — fibrosis, once established, is nearly impossible to reverse. Comprehensive swallowing assessment guides treatment selection, identifies patients at risk, and directs rehabilitation strategies.

## Physiology of Swallowing

### Normal Swallowing Phases

| Phase | Duration | Key Events | Neural Control |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oral preparatory | Variable | Mastication, bolus formation | Voluntary (CN V, VII, XII) |
| Oral propulsive | ~1 second | Tongue drives bolus posteriorly | Voluntary (CN XII) |
| Pharyngeal | ~1 second | Velopharyngeal closure, laryngeal elevation, epiglottic tilt, UES opening | Involuntary (CN IX, X, XII) |
| Oesophageal | 8-20 seconds | Peristalsis through oesophagus | Autonomic (CN X) |

### Critical Swallowing Structures

- **Pharyngeal constrictors** (superior, middle, inferior): generate pharyngeal stripping wave
- **Suprahyoid muscles** (mylohyoid, geniohyoid, digastric): hyolaryngeal excursion for UES opening
- **Laryngeal sphincter**: three-tier closure — true cords, false cords, epiglottis
- **Base of tongue (BOT)**: drives bolus into pharynx; BOT retraction is critical for pharyngeal clearance

## Methods of Swallowing Assessment

### A. Clinical/Bedside Assessment

- **Water swallow test**: 50 mL water — observe for cough, wet voice, oxygen desaturation
- **Evans blue dye test**: for tracheostomised patients — blue-coloured food/liquid, observe for blue-tinged tracheal aspirate
- **Cranial nerve examination**: CN V (mastication), VII (lip seal), IX/X (gag, palatal elevation, vocal cord mobility), XII (tongue movement)
- **Cervical auscultation**: stethoscope over larynx to detect swallowing sounds and aspiration
- **Limitations**: misses silent aspiration in up to 40% of cases

### B. Videofluoroscopic Swallowing Study (VFSS/Modified Barium Swallow)

**Gold standard for swallowing physiology assessment**

- **Procedure**: lateral and AP fluoroscopy during swallowing of graded consistencies (thin liquid → thick liquid → puree → solid) coated with barium or water-soluble contrast
- **Parameters assessed**: tongue movement, soft palate elevation, epiglottic tilt, laryngeal closure, cricopharyngeal opening, pharyngeal peristalsis, aspiration/penetration
- Use **water-soluble contrast** when aspiration is suspected (barium aspiration can cause chemical pneumonitis)
- **Penetration-Aspiration Scale (PAS)**: 8-point scale — 1 (no penetration) to 8 (silent aspiration)
- Quantitative measures: pharyngeal transit time, residue severity, UES opening diameter

### C. Fibreoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing (FEES)

- **Langmore et al. (1988)**: first described procedure using flexible nasopharyngoscope
- **Advantages over VFSS**: portable/bedside, no radiation, repeatable, directly visualises mucosa and secretions, evaluates sensation
- **Procedure**: scope positioned in oropharynx above epiglottis → coloured food/liquid boluses given → observe pooling, penetration, aspiration, residue
- **FEESST** (with sensory testing): air pulse to aryepiglottic fold to elicit laryngeal adductor reflex — assesses sensory threshold
- **White-out period**: moment of swallowing when pharyngeal walls collapse around scope — cannot directly visualise aspiration during swallow (limitation)
- Complementary to VFSS — FEES better for secretion management, VFSS better for pharyngeal physiology

### D. Patient-Reported Outcome Measures

| Instrument | Items | Domains | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| MDADI (Chen 2001) | 20 items, 1-5 scale | Global, emotional, functional, physical | Dysphagia-specific QOL, validated for HNC |
| EAT-10 | 10 items | Single score | Quick screening tool |
| SWAL-QOL | 44 items | 10 domains | Most comprehensive |
| PSS-HN (List) | 3 scales | Eating, diet, speech | Clinician-rated |
| UW-QoL | 12 items | Multiple | HNC-specific QOL |

### E. Other Assessment Methods

- **High-resolution manometry (HRM)**: pharyngeal and UES pressure profiles
- **Pharyngeal EMG**: assessment of pharyngeal constrictor function
- **Ultrasound**: tongue movement assessment, non-invasive
- **Scintigraphy**: quantifies aspiration volume and pharyngeal residue

## Swallowing Impairment by Treatment Modality

### After Surgery

| Surgery | Swallowing Impact | Key Deficit |
|---|---|---|
| Supraglottic laryngectomy | Moderate-severe aspiration | Loss of supraglottic sphincter |
| Vertical hemilaryngectomy | Mild | Minimal impact |
| Supracricoid (CHP/CHEP) | Moderate, temporary | Reduced laryngeal closure |
| Total laryngectomy | No aspiration risk | Pharyngeal stenosis, stricture |
| BOT resection | Moderate-severe | Impaired bolus propulsion |
| TORS for OPSCC | Mild-moderate (96% swallow without G-tube) | Better than open surgery |

- **Jatin Shah**: "Almost every patient aspirates to a varying degree after a supraglottic partial laryngectomy" — most tolerate this well
- Patients with poor pulmonary function, advanced emphysema, or advanced age are poor candidates for conservation laryngeal surgery

### After Radiotherapy/Chemoradiation

- **Acute effects** (during treatment): mucositis, oedema, pain → dysphagia in 60-80%
- **Subacute effects** (1-6 months): resolving mucositis, early fibrosis
- **Late effects** (>6 months): pharyngeal constrictor fibrosis, cranial neuropathy (CN IX, X, XII), [[Answers/General/Post-treatment Lymphedema|lymphoedema]], [[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|trismus]], [[Answers/General/Xerostomia Management|xerostomia]]
- **Radiation-associated dysphagia (RAD)**: progressive fibrosis of pharyngeal constrictors and supraglottic larynx
- **Late dysphagia** (Hutcheson 2012): may develop or progress **years** after treatment — 86% had OPC; 48% cranial neuropathy, 38% trismus; 66% became G-tube dependent
- Aspiration rates 30-62% after definitive CRT; silent aspiration in ≥33% of aspirators

## Prevention Strategies

### A. Pretreatment Swallowing Exercises (Prehabilitation)

**Paradigm shift: from rehabilitation to prevention**

- **Kulbersh et al. (2006)**: patients performing pretreatment exercises showed significantly improved MDADI scores (P=0.0002) vs controls
- Fibrosis once established is **nearly impossible to reverse** — early concentrated intervention is critical
- **Exercise regimen** (start before treatment):
  - **Effortful swallow**: increases BOT retraction and pharyngeal pressure
  - **Mendelsohn manoeuvre**: prolongs laryngeal elevation and UES opening
  - **Shaker exercise** (head-lift): strengthens suprahyoid muscles, improves UES opening
  - **Tongue strengthening**: tongue press against palate with resistance (IOPI device)
  - **Masako manoeuvre** (tongue-hold): enhances pharyngeal constrictor contraction

### B. Dysphagia-Optimised IMRT (DO-IMRT)

- **Eisbruch et al. (2004)**: identified dysphagia-aspiration related structures (DARS):
  - Superior pharyngeal constrictor
  - Middle pharyngeal constrictor
  - Inferior pharyngeal constrictor
  - Glottic and supraglottic larynx
- IMRT dose constraints: pharyngeal constrictors mean dose <50 Gy; glottic larynx mean dose <40 Gy
- Sparing DARS reduces long-term dysphagia and aspiration rates without compromising tumour control

### C. Compensatory Strategies During/After Treatment

| Strategy | Mechanism | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Chin tuck | Narrows airway entrance | Delayed pharyngeal swallow |
| Head rotation (to weak side) | Directs bolus to stronger side | Unilateral pharyngeal weakness |
| Supraglottic swallow | Voluntary breath-hold → swallow → cough | Reduced laryngeal closure |
| Super-supraglottic swallow | Effortful breath-hold → swallow → cough | Severe aspiration risk |
| Diet modification | Altered consistencies (thickened liquids) | Thin liquid aspiration |
| Thermal-tactile stimulation | Cold stimulus to anterior faucial pillars | Delayed swallow trigger |

### D. Enteral Feeding

- **Prophylactic PEG/RIG**: considered for advanced-stage patients undergoing CRT — avoids weight loss >10% during treatment
- **Reactive NG tube**: placed when dysphagia develops during treatment
- Debate: prophylactic vs reactive — prophylactic may lead to "disuse dysphagia" if patients do not continue oral intake
- **Goal**: maintain oral swallowing effort even with supplemental tube feeding to prevent deconditioning

## Assessment Algorithm for Advanced Laryngeal Cancer

1. **Pretreatment**: MDADI + baseline VFSS/FEES → identify at-risk patients → initiate prehabilitation exercises
2. **During treatment**: weekly clinical swallowing assessment → FEES if aspiration suspected → diet modification + compensatory strategies
3. **Post-treatment (6-8 weeks)**: VFSS/FEES → assess residual function → intensive rehabilitation if needed
4. **Long-term surveillance**: MDADI every 3-6 months → FEES/VFSS if symptoms change → watch for late-onset dysphagia for ≥5 years

> [!abstract] Key Points
> - VFSS is the gold standard for swallowing physiology; FEES is the gold standard for bedside/portable assessment
> - MDADI is the validated dysphagia-specific QOL instrument for HNC (20 items; global, emotional, functional, physical subscales)
> - **Pretreatment swallowing exercises (prehabilitation)** significantly improve outcomes — fibrosis once established is irreversible
> - Dysphagia-optimised IMRT sparing pharyngeal constrictors and supraglottic larynx reduces long-term dysphagia
> - Late dysphagia may develop or progress years after RT/CRT — long-term surveillance with serial FEES/MDADI is essential

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **Silent aspiration** occurs in ≥33% of post-CRT aspirators — clinical exam alone misses these; instrumental assessment (VFSS/FEES) is mandatory
> - Use **water-soluble contrast** (not barium) for VFSS when aspiration is suspected — barium aspiration can cause fatal chemical pneumonitis
> - After supraglottic laryngectomy, aspiration is **universal** initially — patient selection (adequate pulmonary reserve, motivation, age <65) is critical
> - Even when PEG-dependent, patients must continue **oral exercises and trial swallows** to prevent disuse atrophy — "use it or lose it" principle
> - The **Mendelsohn manoeuvre** is the single most effective exercise for improving pharyngeal swallowing efficiency and UES opening
