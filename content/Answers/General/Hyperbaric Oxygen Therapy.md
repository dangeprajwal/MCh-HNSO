> [!cite] Landmark Articles

> Marx et al. (1985) published the seminal RCT establishing the "Marx protocol" — prophylactic HBO before dental extractions in irradiated mandibles reduced ORN incidence to 5.4% versus 29.9% with penicillin alone, becoming the foundation for HBO use in head and neck oncology. Annane et al. (2004) conducted the ORN96 trial, a double-blind placebo-controlled RCT that challenged HBO's role in treating established ORN — the trial was stopped early because HBO showed worse outcomes than placebo (19% vs 32% resolution), fundamentally changing clinical practice. Bennett et al. (2016) published the definitive Cochrane systematic review of 14 trials (753 patients) finding moderate-quality evidence that HBO improves mucosal coverage in ORN (NNT = 5) but insufficient evidence for routine prophylactic use. Shaw et al. (2019) published the HOPON trial, a phase III RCT that found no benefit of prophylactic HBO — ORN incidence was 6.4% with HBO versus 5.7% with controls after dental extractions, effectively refuting the Marx protocol in the modern radiotherapy era. Henk et al. (1977) conducted the first controlled trial of HBO as a radiosensitiser, demonstrating improved local tumour control but no survival difference, a concept that was subsequently abandoned due to impracticality and toxicity.

## Definition and Mechanism

Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) therapy involves breathing **100% oxygen at pressures above 1 atmosphere absolute (ATA)**, typically 2.0–2.5 ATA, in a pressurised chamber. In head and neck cancer, HBO has been used primarily for managing late radiation tissue injury (LRTI), particularly [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Osteoradionecrosis of the Mandible|osteoradionecrosis of the mandible]].

### Mechanisms of Action

| Mechanism | Effect |
|---|---|
| Hyperoxygenation | Increases dissolved plasma oxygen 10–15×; supplies hypoxic tissues |
| Neoangiogenesis | Stimulates capillary budding in irradiated tissue (8–9× increase in vessel density) |
| Osteogenesis | Promotes bone matrix production and mineralisation |
| Bactericidal effect | Directly toxic to anaerobes; enhances macrophage peroxidase activity |
| Vasoconstriction | Reduces oedema without compromising O₂ delivery |
| Fibroblast stimulation | Enhances collagen synthesis and wound healing |

### The Marx Protocol (Standard Regimen)

- **20 preoperative dives** + **10 postoperative dives**
- Each session: 90 minutes breathing 100% O₂ at 2.2–2.5 ATA
- Total course: 30 sessions over 6–8 weeks

## Current Indications in Head and Neck Cancer

### 1. Osteoradionecrosis (ORN)

**For Prevention (Prophylactic HBO):**
- Marx (1985) established the protocol: HBO before dental extractions in irradiated mandibles reduced ORN from 29.9% to 5.4%
- **However, HOPON trial (Shaw 2019) found NO benefit** — ORN rates were 6.4% (HBO) vs 5.7% (control) in the modern RT era
- Current consensus: prophylactic HBO is **NOT routinely recommended** before dental extractions; good dental care and surgical technique are more important

**For Treatment of Established ORN:**
- Annane ORN96 trial (2004): HBO showed **worse outcomes** than placebo (19% vs 32% resolution) — trial stopped early
- HBO **cannot revitalise dead, necrotic bone** — sequestrectomy is the only definitive remedy
- May be useful in **early ORN with unexposed bone** (Marx stage I–II) as adjunct to conservative management
- Limited role in advanced ORN (Marx stage III) — surgical debridement ± free flap reconstruction is required

### 2. Dental Implant Placement in Irradiated Bone

- HBO (20 pre + 10 post dives) before implant placement in irradiated jaws may improve osseointegration
- Failure rates reduced from ~50% to <10% with HBO in early series
- **However, modern implant design and techniques have improved success rates** — routine HBO for implants is no longer universally advocated
- Recent meta-analysis: overall mean implant survival 83% with mean follow-up ≥5 years, with improvements attributed to implant design rather than HBO

### 3. Soft Tissue Radionecrosis

- Refractory radiation-induced soft tissue ulceration may benefit from HBO
- Bennett Cochrane review: moderate evidence for improved mucosal coverage (NNT = 5)
- Chondroradionecrosis of larynx — HBO may avoid the need for total laryngectomy in selected cases

### 4. HBO as Radiosensitiser (Historical — Abandoned)

- Henk (1977): first RCT showed improved local tumour control with HBO-enhanced RT
- Cochrane review: reduced mortality (RR 0.82, p = 0.03) but used impractical hypofractionated regimens
- Abandoned due to: oxygen-toxic seizures, severe radiation tissue injury, over 50% of patients medically unfit, impracticality of delivering HBO during RT

## Contraindications

| Absolute | Relative |
|---|---|
| Untreated pneumothorax | COPD (compromised respiratory drive) |
| Active/residual neoplasia | Middle ear disease (may need grommets) |
| Certain chemotherapy agents (bleomycin, doxorubicin — enhanced toxicity) | Claustrophobia |

**Critical safety concern:** Whether HBO-mediated reoxygenation can activate dormant cancer cells. No systematic study has addressed this, but anecdotal reports of tumour reactivation exist. HBO should be used with **extreme caution** in patients with uncertain disease-free status.

## Complications of HBO

| Complication | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Middle ear barotrauma | 15–20% (most common) |
| Serous otitis media | 5–10% |
| Oxygen-toxic seizures | 1–2% |
| Reversible myopia | 10–15% |
| Claustrophobia/anxiety | 5–10% |
| Pneumothorax | Rare |

## Current Evidence Summary

| Indication | Evidence Level | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| ORN prevention (pre-extraction) | Level I (HOPON) | NOT recommended routinely |
| ORN treatment (established) | Level I (ORN96) | NOT beneficial; may be harmful |
| Early ORN (unexposed bone) | Level III–IV | May be beneficial as adjunct |
| Implant placement in irradiated bone | Level III | Consider case-by-case |
| Soft tissue radionecrosis | Level II (Cochrane) | Moderate benefit (NNT = 5) |
| Radiosensitisation | Level I (historical) | Abandoned — impractical and toxic |
| Chondroradionecrosis | Level IV | May avoid laryngectomy in selected cases |

## Practical Considerations

- HBO is **time-consuming** (30 sessions over 6–8 weeks) and **expensive**
- Must be performed at an accredited wound care centre — limited availability
- Patient must travel daily for treatment, which may be impractical
- For most ORN cases, **conservative management** (Water Pik irrigation, debridement, long-term antibiotics) or **surgical resection with free flap reconstruction** provides better outcomes than HBO

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. HBO delivers 100% oxygen at 2.0–2.5 ATA, promoting neoangiogenesis, osteogenesis, and bactericidal effects in irradiated tissue
> 2. The Marx protocol (20 pre + 10 post dives) was the standard but has been challenged by modern evidence
> 3. HOPON trial (2019): prophylactic HBO before dental extractions in irradiated mandibles showed NO benefit over controls — refutes the Marx protocol
> 4. Annane ORN96 trial (2004): HBO for established ORN showed WORSE outcomes than placebo — trial stopped early
> 5. HBO cannot revitalise dead necrotic bone — sequestrectomy remains the definitive treatment for established ORN
> 6. Bennett Cochrane review: moderate evidence for HBO improving mucosal coverage in soft tissue radionecrosis (NNT = 5)
> 7. HBO as radiosensitiser was abandoned due to impracticality, toxicity, and patient unsuitability
> 8. Concern exists about HBO reactivating dormant cancer cells — use with caution in patients with uncertain disease-free status
> 9. Modern implant designs have reduced the need for routine HBO before implant placement in irradiated bone
> 10. Current role of HBO in HNC is very limited — conservative care and surgical reconstruction have largely replaced it

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Two landmark trials killed HBO in ORN** — Annane 2004 (treatment doesn't work) and Shaw 2019 HOPON (prevention doesn't work either)
> 2. The only remaining role for HBO is in **early ORN with intact mucosa** (Marx stage I–II) where it may promote healing as an adjunct to conservative care
> 3. For the AIIMS exam, know the **Marx protocol numbers** (20+10 dives, 2.2–2.5 ATA, 90 min) and the **two trials that refuted it**
> 4. If asked about HBO for ORN in the exam, the answer should emphasise that **surgical management with free flap reconstruction** is the definitive treatment for advanced ORN, not HBO
> 5. HBO remains more popular in dental/maxillofacial literature than in head and neck surgical oncology — surgical oncologists have largely moved away from it
