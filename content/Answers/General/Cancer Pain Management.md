> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> The WHO analgesic ladder, introduced in 1986, is the foundational framework for cancer pain management worldwide. Zech et al. (1995) provided the most robust validation in a landmark 10-year prospective study of 2,118 patients, demonstrating good pain relief in 76% using the stepwise approach. ## Introduction

Pain is one of the most feared consequences of cancer and is experienced by up to 80% of patients with head and neck carcinoma at some point during their disease course. The prevalence is even higher in advanced disease, where pain rates approach 85-90%. Despite this burden, cancer pain remains significantly undertreated — studies have estimated the prevalence of insufficient pain management at approximately 70% in some settings, reflecting a combination of clinician reluctance to prescribe adequate opioid analgesia, patient reluctance to report pain or take opioids, and systemic barriers to opioid availability. Yet cancer pain can be effectively managed in up to 90% of patients by following certain established guidelines of pain management.

The World Health Organization (WHO) analgesic ladder, introduced in 1986 as part of the WHO Cancer Pain and Palliative Care Program, remains the foundational framework for stepwise cancer pain management. This three-step approach was validated in the landmark 10-year prospective study by Zech et al. (1995), which demonstrated good pain relief in 76% of 2,118 patients treated according to the WHO guidelines, and has been specifically validated for head and neck cancer pain by Grond et al. (1993).

Pain in the head and neck cancer patient is complex and multifactorial. It can be **disease-related** (tumour invasion of bone, nerve, or soft tissue), **treatment-related** (post-surgical pain, radiation-induced mucositis, chemotherapy-induced neuropathy), or a combination of both. Nearly all patients receiving radiotherapy to the head and neck experience significant pain at some point during treatment, typically commencing in the second to third week as mucositis develops. The pain frequently has both nociceptive and neuropathic components, owing to the dense innervation of the head and neck region and the propensity of HNSCC to invade cranial nerves (particularly the trigeminal, glossopharyngeal, and vagus nerves). [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Referred Otalgia in Oral Cancer|referred otalgia]] — pain perceived in the ear but originating from tumours of the tongue base, tonsil, pyriform fossa, or larynx — is a classic example of the complexity of head and neck cancer pain and is mediated through the auricular branches of cranial nerves V, IX, and X. Effective pain management requires a thorough understanding of these mechanisms and a multimodal approach that addresses both the nociceptive and neuropathic components.

## Part A: The WHO Analgesic Ladder

### The Three-Step Approach

The WHO analgesic ladder provides a simple, systematic framework for escalating analgesic therapy based on pain severity:

**Step 1: Mild Pain (NRS 1-3)**
- Non-opioid analgesics
- Paracetamol (acetaminophen) and/or NSAIDs
- Plus adjuvant medications as needed

**Step 2: Moderate Pain (NRS 4-6)**
- Weak opioids (codeine, tramadol, dihydrocodeine)
- Plus non-opioid analgesics (Step 1)
- Plus adjuvant medications as needed

**Step 3: Severe Pain (NRS 7-10)**
- Strong opioids (morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl, methadone, hydromorphone)
- Plus non-opioid analgesics (Step 1)
- Plus adjuvant medications as needed

### Key Principles of the WHO Ladder

1. **By the mouth:** Oral administration preferred whenever possible
2. **By the clock:** Around-the-clock (ATC) dosing — not PRN/on-demand; ATC dosing improves adherence and provides more consistent pain control
3. **By the ladder:** Stepwise escalation based on pain intensity
4. **For the individual:** Dosing individualized to the patient
5. **Attention to detail:** Regular reassessment and dose titration

### Adjuvant Medications (Used at Any Step)

| Adjuvant | Indication | Examples |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Gabapentinoids | Neuropathic pain | Gabapentin, pregabalin |
| Corticosteroids | Nerve compression, inflammation | Dexamethasone, prednisone |
| Antidepressants | Neuropathic pain, mood | Amitriptyline, duloxetine |
| Bisphosphonates | Bone pain | Zoledronic acid |
| Muscle relaxants | Muscle spasm | Baclofen |

## Part B: Pain Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer

### Comprehensive Pain Assessment Should Include

- **Location, intensity, quality** — using validated scales (NRS 0-10, Visual Analogue Scale)
- **Temporal features** — onset, duration, daily fluctuations
- **Aggravating and alleviating factors**
- **Cause identification** — disease-related vs. treatment-related
- **Neuropathic component** — burning, shooting, tingling suggests nerve involvement → guides use of adjuvant medications
- **Functional impact** — effect on eating, speaking, sleeping, QOL
- **Psychosocial factors** — meaning of pain for the patient, cultural and spiritual dimensions, anxiety, depression
- **Substance use history** — opioid-naive vs. opioid-tolerant (management differs significantly)
- **Prior analgesic use and effectiveness**

### Types of Pain in Head and Neck Cancer

- **Nociceptive (somatic):** Dull, aching — from tissue invasion, post-surgical
- **Nociceptive (visceral):** Deep, cramping — from organ involvement
- **Neuropathic:** Burning, shooting, electric — from nerve infiltration by tumour or treatment-induced (post-RT, post-surgical)
- **Mixed pain:** Most common in advanced HNC — has both nociceptive and neuropathic components

## Part C: Specific Analgesic Considerations in HNC

### Non-Opioid Analgesics (Step 1)

- **Paracetamol:** Safe first-line; 1g QDS (max 4g/day); caution with hepatic impairment
- **NSAIDs (naproxen, celecoxib):** Effective for inflammatory and bone pain; use cautiously due to GI and cardiovascular risks; avoid in patients with mucositis or post-operative bleeding risk

### Weak Opioids (Step 2)

- **Codeine:** 30-60 mg 4-6 hourly; ceiling dose; 10% of population are poor metabolizers (no effect)
- **Tramadol:** 50-100 mg QDS; dual mechanism (opioid + monoamine); risk of seizures and serotonin syndrome

### Strong Opioids (Step 3)

- **Oral morphine:** Gold standard; start 5-10 mg 4-hourly (opioid-naive); titrate to effect; convert to modified-release once stable dose found
- **Oxycodone:** Alternative to morphine; available in immediate and modified-release; less histamine release
- **Fentanyl:** Transdermal patches for stable pain; buccal/sublingual for breakthrough pain; useful when oral route unavailable (post-surgical HNC patients)
- **Methadone:** As described in Harrison's Head and Neck Cancer (Chapter 10), methadone is a long-acting opiate that can treat both the incident and neuropathic components of cancer pain — a dual mechanism that makes it particularly valuable in HNC where mixed pain is common. It has a long half-life averaging about 24 hours, can be dosed in 6-to-8-hour intervals, and compared with other opiates is relatively inexpensive. Due to the lack of active metabolites, it may be preferable in the setting of renal impairment. However, in view of its prolonged and variable half-life, as well as individual variation in metabolism, familiarity with prescribing is essential — full effects of the medication may be delayed for days and up to a week or more, creating a risk of delayed respiratory depression if doses are escalated too quickly. Patient and family education is essential to reinforce that methadone is not only for maintenance or withdrawal from heroin but is a legitimate and effective analgesic for cancer pain

### Postoperative Pain in HNC

As described in Harrison's Chapter 10, postoperative pain in head and neck cancer patients is initially controlled with parenteral narcotics, administered by continuous intravenous route, patient-controlled analgesia (PCA), or by subcutaneous injection. These are then converted to an oral analgesic via the nasogastric tube or by mouth when feasible. For patients who have been treated with opioid analgesics prior to surgery, informing the acute pain management team prior to surgery is critically important, as these opioid-tolerant patients may need substantially more than usual amounts of postoperative analgesics — failure to account for their baseline opioid requirement will result in inadequate pain control and significant patient distress.

Mild postoperative pain should be treated with acetaminophen-based combinations. For moderate-to-severe postoperative pain, opioids in pill or liquid form (morphine solution, oxycodone) are used. The transition from IV to oral analgesics should be planned carefully, particularly in patients who cannot swallow (post-glossectomy, post-pharyngectomy) — in these patients, fentanyl transdermal patches, subcutaneous morphine infusions, or analgesics administered via nasogastric or PEG tube may be required.

Nonopioid adjuvants are frequently needed in the postoperative period. Gabapentinoids (gabapentin or pregabalin) are essential for managing neuropathic pain, which is common after head and neck surgery involving cranial nerve resection or manipulation. Glucocorticoids (dexamethasone) are used for their anti-inflammatory and anti-oedema effects, and bisphosphonates may be added for bone pain from mandibular involvement or bony metastases.

## Part D: The Fourth Step and Interventional Approaches

When pharmacological management is inadequate, a fourth step is recognized:

- **Nerve blocks:** Stellate ganglion, superior laryngeal nerve, glossopharyngeal nerve blocks
- **Neurolysis:** Chemical or radiofrequency ablation of pain pathways
- **Intrathecal drug delivery** — for refractory pain
- **Radiation therapy** — for painful bone metastases (palliative single fraction 8 Gy)
- **Cordotomy** — rarely used; for unilateral cancer pain

### Integrative Approaches

- **Acupuncture:** Studied as complementary therapy for cancer pain; evidence supports reduction of analgesic requirements
- **Cognitive behavioural therapy** — addresses psychological components
- **Physical therapy** — gentle stretching, mobilization

## Part E: Barriers to Effective Pain Management

- **Patient barriers:** Fear of addiction, belief that pain is inevitable, reluctance to report pain, stoicism
- **Clinician barriers:** Under-prescribing of opioids, inadequate pain assessment, insufficient knowledge
- **System barriers:** Opioid availability restrictions, lack of palliative care services
- **Cultural barriers:** Attitudes toward narcotics vary significantly across populations

**Education of patients and families** regarding pain management is essential — around-the-clock dosing is more effective than PRN, and opioids used for cancer pain rarely lead to true addiction.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The WHO analgesic ladder provides a 3-step framework: non-opioids → weak opioids → strong opioids
> 2. Key principles: by the mouth, by the clock (ATC dosing), by the ladder, for the individual
> 3. Cancer pain can be effectively managed in up to 90% of patients following the WHO ladder
> 4. Zech et al. validated the ladder in 2,118 patients over 10 years — 76% achieved good pain relief
> 5. Adjuvant medications (gabapentin, corticosteroids) are essential for neuropathic pain at any step
> 6. Methadone treats both nociceptive and neuropathic pain and is preferred in renal impairment
> 7. Fentanyl transdermal patches are valuable when the oral route is unavailable (common in HNC)
> 8. A fourth step (interventional/neurolytic) is recognized for refractory pain
> 9. The second step (weak opioids) is increasingly questioned — recent RCT showed no benefit over direct strong opioid use
> 10. Nearly all HNC patients receiving RT experience pain — institute analgesics early in treatment

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Always assess for neuropathic pain (burning, shooting) — it requires specific adjuvant therapy, not just escalating opioids
> - In post-laryngectomy patients unable to swallow, fentanyl patches or subcutaneous infusions are practical alternatives to oral morphine
> - Methadone's long and variable half-life means full effects may take up to a week — titrate cautiously
> - Around-the-clock dosing is significantly more effective than PRN — always prescribe background analgesia with breakthrough doses
> - For opioid-tolerant patients undergoing surgery, inform the anaesthetic team preoperatively — they will need significantly higher post-operative doses
> - Pain assessment should include psychosocial dimensions — depression and anxiety amplify pain perception and are common in HNC (up to 40%)
