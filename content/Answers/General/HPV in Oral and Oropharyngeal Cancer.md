## Overview

Human papillomavirus (HPV) is now established as the **primary aetiological agent** in a rising epidemic of oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma (OPSCC). The role of HPV in oral cavity cancer is far less clear and remains controversial. This distinction between HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer and HPV-negative tobacco/alcohol-related cancer represents one of the most important paradigm shifts in head and neck oncology.

## HPV Biology

### HPV Types and Oncogenic Potential

| Category | Types | Cancer Association |
|---|---|---|
| High-risk (oncogenic) | 16, 18, 31, 33, 35, 45, 52, 58 | Cervical, oropharyngeal, anogenital |
| Low-risk | 6, 11, 42, 43 | Benign warts, laryngeal papillomatosis |

**HPV-16** accounts for **>90%** of HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancers. HPV-18 is a minor contributor (~5%).

### Oncogenic Mechanisms

| Viral Protein | Target | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| E6 | p53 → ubiquitin-mediated degradation | Loss of cell cycle arrest and apoptosis |
| E7 | Rb → functional inactivation | Uncontrolled G1→S transition |
| E7 | p16 (CDKN2A) → overexpression (paradoxical) | p16 used as surrogate marker for HPV |
| E6 | Telomerase activation | Cellular immortalisation |
| E5 | EGFR amplification | Proliferative signalling |

**Key concept:** HPV E6 degrades p53 and E7 inactivates Rb — these are the same tumour suppressors disrupted by somatic mutations in HPV-negative cancers, but the mechanism differs (viral protein vs gene mutation).

### p16 as Surrogate Marker

- Rb inactivation by E7 → loss of negative feedback → **p16 (CDKN2A) overexpression**
- p16 immunohistochemistry (IHC) is the clinical standard for HPV detection in oropharyngeal cancer
- **p16-positive:** diffuse strong nuclear and cytoplasmic staining in ≥70% of tumour cells
- p16 IHC has ~90% sensitivity and ~80% specificity for HPV status in oropharyngeal SCC

## HPV in Oropharyngeal Cancer

### Epidemiology

| Parameter | HPV-Positive OPSCC | HPV-Negative OPSCC |
|---|---|---|
| Incidence trend | Rapidly increasing (epidemic) | Declining (tobacco control) |
| Proportion of OPSCC | 60–80% (Western countries) | 20–40% |
| Predominant site | Palatine tonsil, tongue base (lymphoid tissue) | Variable |
| Age | Younger (40–60 years) | Older (55–75 years) |
| Gender | Male predominance (M:F 3:1) | M:F 2–3:1 |
| Risk factors | Sexual behaviour (oral sex), number of partners | Tobacco, alcohol |
| Tobacco/alcohol | Often absent or minimal | Usually present |

### Distinct Clinical Profile

| Feature | HPV-Positive | HPV-Negative |
|---|---|---|
| T-stage at presentation | Often small (T1–T2) | Often advanced (T3–T4) |
| N-stage at presentation | Often advanced (cystic N2–N3) | Variable |
| Histology | Non-keratinising, basaloid | Keratinising |
| p53 status | Wild-type (degraded by E6) | Mutated |
| p16 IHC | Positive (≥70% staining) | Negative |
| Prognosis | Dramatically better (70–90% 5-yr OS) | Poor (30–50% 5-yr OS) |

### AJCC 8th Edition: Separate Staging

The AJCC 8th Edition created a **separate staging system** for p16-positive oropharyngeal cancer, reflecting its distinct biology:

**Clinical staging (p16-positive OPSCC):**

| Stage | T | N |
|---|---|---|
| I | T0–T2 | N0–N1 |
| II | T0–T2 | N2; OR T3 N0–N2 |
| III | T4 or N3 | Any |
| IV | M1 | Any |

**Key difference:** There is no stage IVA or IVB for p16-positive OPSCC — the worst locoregional stage is III, reflecting the good prognosis even with advanced nodal disease. [[Answers/General/Extranodal Extension|ENE]] is not incorporated into clinical staging of HPV-positive disease.

## HPV in Oral Cavity Cancer

### The Controversy

The role of HPV in oral cavity SCC is **far less established** than in oropharyngeal cancer:

| Evidence | Finding |
|---|---|
| IARC multicentre study | HPV DNA in only **3.9%** of 766 oral cavity cancers |
| Other studies | HPV prevalence 0–60% (wide variation due to methodology) |
| Biological plausibility | Oral cavity lacks lymphoid tissue (crypts) that facilitate HPV entry in oropharynx |
| p16 positivity | Present in some oral cavity cancers but may be HPV-independent (ink, senescence) |
| Clinical behaviour | HPV-positive oral cavity cancers do NOT consistently show better prognosis |

**Current consensus:** HPV is NOT considered a significant aetiological factor in oral cavity SCC. The standard risk factors (tobacco, alcohol, betel quid) remain dominant. p16 testing in oral cavity cancer is not routinely recommended.

## De-Escalation Strategies in HPV-Positive OPSCC

The excellent prognosis of HPV-positive OPSCC has motivated **treatment de-escalation** to reduce treatment-related morbidity:

| Strategy | Approach | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Reduced RT dose | 50–56 Gy instead of 70 Gy | Ongoing trials (ECOG 3311, ADEPT) |
| Replace cisplatin with cetuximab | Less toxic regimen | **FAILED** — De-ESCALaTE and RTOG 1016 showed cetuximab inferior |
| TORS + de-escalated adjuvant | Surgery → reduced/omit adjuvant RT | ECOG 3311 Phase II promising |
| Immunotherapy-based | Checkpoint inhibitor combinations | Under investigation |
| Omit chemotherapy | RT alone for good responders | Under investigation |

**Critical lesson:** De-ESCALaTE (Mehanna 2019) and RTOG 1016 (Gillison 2019) both demonstrated that cetuximab is **inferior** to cisplatin in HPV-positive OPSCC — cisplatin remains the standard concurrent agent.

## Detection Methods

| Method | What It Detects | Sensitivity | Specificity | Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| p16 IHC | Surrogate marker (CDKN2A overexpression) | ~90% | ~80% | Clinical standard for OPC |
| HPV ISH | HPV DNA in tissue | 85–95% | 95–98% | Confirmatory |
| HPV PCR | HPV DNA (type-specific) | 95–98% | 90–95% | Research; may detect incidental HPV |
| E6/E7 mRNA | Transcriptionally active HPV | 95–98% | 98–99% | Gold standard (research) |
| Serology (E6 antibodies) | Prior HPV exposure | 90% | 95% | Epidemiological studies |

**For clinical practice:** p16 IHC alone is sufficient for oropharyngeal SCC staging per AJCC 8th Edition.

## HPV Vaccination and Head and Neck Cancer

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Vaccines available | Gardasil 9 (9-valent: HPV 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58) |
| Target population | Males and females aged 9–26 years |
| Efficacy against HPV-16 | >95% for incident infection |
| Impact on OPSCC | Expected decline in 20–30 years (given latency period) |
| Current evidence | Modelling studies predict 60–80% reduction in HPV-positive OPSCC |

HPV vaccination represents the **primary prevention** strategy for HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. HPV-16 causes >90% of HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancers — the fastest rising HNC subtype in Western countries
> 2. Oncogenic mechanism: E6 degrades p53; E7 inactivates Rb → p16 overexpression (used as surrogate marker)
> 3. HPV-positive OPSCC has dramatically better prognosis (70–90% 5-yr OS) than HPV-negative (30–50%)
> 4. AJCC 8th Edition created separate staging for p16-positive OPC — no stage IVA/IVB; worst locoregional stage is III
> 5. HPV role in oral cavity SCC is NOT established — prevalence only ~4% in large studies; tobacco/alcohol remain dominant
> 6. p16 IHC is the clinical standard for HPV detection in oropharyngeal cancer but is NOT routinely tested in oral cavity SCC
> 7. De-ESCALaTE and RTOG 1016 showed cetuximab is INFERIOR to cisplatin in HPV-positive OPSCC — cisplatin remains standard
> 8. HPV vaccination (Gardasil 9) is expected to reduce HPV-positive OPSCC incidence by 60–80% with a 20–30 year latency
> 9. Characteristic clinical profile of HPV+ OPSCC: younger, non-smoker, small primary with large cystic nodes, non-keratinising
> 10. E6/E7 mRNA is the gold standard for detecting transcriptionally active HPV; p16 IHC is the practical clinical standard

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **"Small tonsil primary + big cystic node + young non-smoker = HPV-positive until proven otherwise"** — this clinical triad should trigger p16 testing
> 2. **p16 positivity in oral cavity cancer does NOT equal HPV** — p16 can be positive due to HPV-independent mechanisms; do not apply the favourable HPV-positive prognosis to oral cavity SCC
> 3. The **cystic metastatic node** in HPV-positive OPSCC mimics a branchial cleft cyst — always get p16/HPV testing on any cystic neck mass in an adult
> 4. For the exam, know the **two trials that killed cetuximab**: De-ESCALaTE (Mehanna 2019) and RTOG 1016 (Gillison 2019) — both non-inferiority trials that FAILED
> 5. HPV vaccination advocacy is now part of head and neck surgical oncology practice — surgeons should recommend vaccination for patients' children
