# Epidemiology of Head and Neck Cancer in India

## Introduction

India bears one of the highest burdens of head and neck cancer in the world. Stell and Maran's Chapter 1 states: in the **Indian subcontinent, HNC accounts for 45% of all malignancies**, with oral cancer being the most common type, accounting for **one-third of all cancers**.

Montgomery's Chapter 12 confirms: in Sri Lanka, India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh, oral cancer is the **most common cancer in men** and accounts for **30% of all new cancers**. India has one of the highest incidence rates of oral/oropharyngeal cancer: **12.5 per 100,000 per year**.

Bagal et al. (2023) analysed 37 population-based cancer registries and found: HNC ASR = **25.9/100,000 (males)** and **8.0/100,000 (females)**. The **lifetime risk** of developing HNC is 1 in 33 for Indian males and 1 in 107 for females.

## Part A: Incidence by Region and Site

### Regional Variation Across India

Bagal et al. (2023) provided the definitive regional data from 37 PBCRs:

| Region | Male ASR (/100,000) | Leading HNC Site |
|--------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Northeast** | **31.7** (highest) | Hypopharynx |
| **North** | 28.5 | Mouth |
| **Central** | 28.3 | Mouth |
| **West** | 24.4 | Mouth |
| **South** | 23.9 | Mouth |
| **East** | 18.3 (lowest) | Mouth |

**Key observation:** Mouth is the leading HNC site in **all regions except the northeast**, where hypopharyngeal cancer predominates — reflecting regional differences in tobacco habits.

### Site Distribution

Jatin Shah notes: in the Western world, tongue and [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Floor of Mouth Carcinoma|floor of mouth carcinoma]] are the most common oral cancer sites. However, in regions where chewing tobacco/betel quid is common, **retromolar trigone and buccal mucosa** are the most common sites.

Montgomery describes the "**cancer-prone crescent**" concept: saliva pooling in the floor of mouth exposes mucosa to dissolved carcinogens. In developing countries, site distribution differs due to specific habits — betel quid chewing causes high buccal cancer rates in the Indian subcontinent.

### Site-Specific Associations with Tobacco Habits

| Tobacco Habit | Cancer Site | Region |
|--------------|-------------|--------|
| **Bidi smoking** | Oral commissure, tongue, base of tongue | Pan-India |
| **Reverse smoking (Chutta)** | Hard palate, palatine arch | Andhra Pradesh, coastal India |
| **Paan chewing (betel quid)** | Alveolobuccal cancer | Pan-India, especially eastern states |
| **Khaini (tobacco + lime)** | Inferior gingivobuccal sulcus | Bihar, Jharkhand, northeast |
| **Gutka/Paan masala** | Oral cavity (buccal mucosa) | Pan-India (youth) |
| **Toombak** | Oral cavity (very high nitrosamines) | Sudan/imported to India |

## Part B: Etiology and Risk Factors

### 1. Tobacco (Most Important Risk Factor)

Stell and Maran's states: tobacco and alcohol are the **two most important factors**; their interaction is **supermultiplicative for mouth cancer** and **additive for laryngeal cancer**. Combined tobacco and alcohol use increases risk **10-20 fold** (Jatin Shah).

Montgomery notes: **34,000 of 56,000** estimated annual oral cancer cases in India are tobacco-induced — **potentially avoidable**.

**Smokeless tobacco in India:**
Gupta and Johnson (2014) meta-analysis confirmed:
- Chewing tobacco: **OR 7.46** (95% CI 5.86-9.50)
- Betel quid without tobacco: **OR 2.82** (95% CI 2.35-3.40)
- Both are strong **independent** risk factors

Montgomery describes tobacco-specific nitrosamines **NNN** and **NNK** as independently inducing oral cancers.

### 2. Alcohol

Jatin Shah: alcohol increases risk **2-3 fold** in a dose-dependent manner. The synergistic interaction with tobacco makes combined users the highest-risk group.

### 3. Betel Quid and Areca Nut

Stell and Maran's notes: betel quid with tobacco is strongly associated with **leukoplakia, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Submucous Fibrosis|oral submucous fibrosis]]**, and [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Lichen Planus|oral lichen planus]]. Montgomery adds: paan masala and gutka contain **over 2,000 chemicals**, many carcinogenic.

**Oral submucous fibrosis (OSMF):**
- Caused by chewing areca nut and betel quid
- Confined to South-East Asia
- Annual malignant transformation rate: approximately **0.5%** (Montgomery)
- A premalignant condition requiring lifelong surveillance

### 4. HPV and [[Answers/General/EBV Testing in Head and Neck Cancer|EBV]]

- HPV (particularly type 16) associated with **oropharyngeal cancer** — rising incidence in urban India
- EBV associated with **nasopharyngeal cancer** — higher incidence in northeast India (Mongoloid population)

### 5. Other Factors

| Factor | Associated Cancer |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Nutritional deficiency** (iron, vitamins) | Plummer-Vinson syndrome → postcricoid cancer |
| **UV radiation** | Lip cancer |
| **Occupational exposure** (wood dust, nickel) | Sinonasal cancers |
| **Poor oral hygiene** | Oral cavity cancer |
| **Ionizing radiation** | Thyroid, salivary gland cancers |

## Part C: Factors Influencing Incidence

### Why India Has High Incidence

| Factor | Contribution |
|--------|-------------|
| **Widespread tobacco use** | >28% adults use tobacco (GATS India) |
| **Diverse tobacco products** | Bidi, paan, khaini, gutka, chutta — each associated with specific subsites |
| **Areca nut/betel quid** | Independent carcinogen; causes OSMF |
| **Low socioeconomic status** | Poor nutrition, limited healthcare access |
| **Late presentation** | 66.6% diagnosed at locally advanced stage (Mathur et al., 2020) |
| **Limited screening** | No organized population-based screening programme |
| **High alcohol use** | Synergistic with tobacco |
| **Male predominance** | Cultural: tobacco use more common in males (4:1 ratio) |

### Why Northeast India Has Highest Incidence

- Highest prevalence of smokeless tobacco use
- Fermented betel nut (Tamul) and tobacco (Sadha) unique to the region
- Hypopharyngeal cancer predominance (unlike rest of India)
- Mongoloid population — [[Answers/General/Genetic Susceptibility to Oral Cancer|genetic susceptibility]] factors may contribute

## Part D: Preventive Measures and Implementation

### Primary Prevention

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Tobacco cessation** | National Tobacco Control Programme (NTCP); tobacco cessation centres at district hospitals; Quitline services |
| **Legislative measures** | COTPA (Cigarettes and Other Tobacco Products Act, 2003): ban on gutka/paan masala in many states; pictorial warnings on packages; ban on tobacco advertising |
| **Taxation** | Higher taxes on tobacco products; GST increases |
| **Youth education** | School-based anti-tobacco education; TAPS (Tobacco Advertising, Promotion, Sponsorship) ban |
| **Alcohol control** | Awareness campaigns; regulation of availability |

### Secondary Prevention (Screening)

Sankaranarayanan et al. (2005) — the **Trivandrum Oral Cancer Screening Study** — is the landmark evidence:
- Cluster-randomized trial in Kerala: 7 intervention vs 6 control clusters
- Three rounds of oral visual inspection by **trained health workers** at 3-year intervals (1996-2004)
- **Significant reduction in oral cancer mortality** among tobacco/alcohol users
- This is the **only RCT worldwide** demonstrating efficacy of visual oral cancer screening

**Implementation strategy:**
- Train primary health workers (ASHA, ANMs) in visual oral examination
- Target high-risk populations (tobacco/alcohol users, >40 years)
- Integrate into existing primary healthcare infrastructure
- Referral pathway to district hospitals for suspicious lesions

### Tertiary Prevention

| Strategy | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Management of premalignant lesions** | Leukoplakia, [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Oral Leukoplakia Management|erythroplakia]], OSMF — biopsy, excision, surveillance |
| **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Chemoprevention in Oral Cancer|chemoprevention]]** | Isotretinoin (13-cis-retinoic acid) for second primary prevention; limited by side effects (Stell and Maran's) |
| **Diet modification** | 5+ servings of fruit/vegetables daily; antioxidants (vitamins A, C, E) protective (Stell and Maran's) |
| **[[Answers/Oral Cavity/Field Cancerization|field cancerization]] surveillance** | Even complete removal of premalignant lesions does not eliminate cancer risk — lifelong follow-up required |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. India: HNC accounts for 45% of all malignancies; oral cancer is #1 cancer in Indian males (1/3 of all cancers) — Stell and Maran's
> 2. HNC ASR: 25.9/100,000 males; lifetime risk 1 in 33 males, 1 in 107 females (Bagal et al., 2023)
> 3. Northeast India has HIGHEST HNC incidence (31.7/100,000) with hypopharynx as leading site; mouth leads in all other regions
> 4. 34,000 of 56,000 annual oral cancers in India are tobacco-induced — POTENTIALLY AVOIDABLE (Montgomery)
> 5. Smokeless tobacco OR 7.46; betel quid without tobacco OR 2.82 — both INDEPENDENT risk factors (Gupta & Johnson meta-analysis)
> 6. Site-specific habits: bidi → commissure/tongue; reverse smoking → hard palate; paan → alveolobuccal; khaini → gingivobuccal sulcus
> 7. 66.6% of HNC in India diagnosed at LOCALLY ADVANCED stage — screening and early detection are critical (NCRP, 2020)
> 8. Trivandrum RCT (Sankaranarayanan, 2005): ONLY RCT worldwide showing visual screening reduces oral cancer mortality in tobacco users
> 9. OSMF from areca nut has 0.5% annual malignant transformation rate — a unique Indian premalignant condition
> 10. COTPA (2003) is the key legislative measure: bans gutka, mandates pictorial warnings, prohibits tobacco advertising

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, know the REGIONAL VARIATION: northeast highest (31.7), east lowest (18.3) — and the reason (different tobacco habits)
> - The SITE-HABIT correlation is high-yield: reverse smoking → hard palate is the classic association; khaini → gingivobuccal sulcus is India-specific
> - Tobacco + alcohol interaction is SUPERMULTIPLICATIVE for mouth cancer but only ADDITIVE for laryngeal cancer — this distinction matters
> - The Trivandrum trial is the MOST IMPORTANT cancer screening RCT from India — know it in detail: visual inspection, trained health workers, 3-year intervals
> - OSMF is a uniquely South Asian premalignant condition — caused by areca nut, not tobacco; 0.5% annual transformation; no reversal once established
> - Remember the "cancer-prone crescent" concept for explaining why floor of mouth and ventral tongue are common sites in Western populations
