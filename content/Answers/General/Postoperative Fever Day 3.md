# Postoperative Fever on Day 3: Differential Diagnosis and Approach

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Differential diagnosis and approach to a case with fever on postoperative day 3 (AMRITA 2008)

**Source:** Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 14 (Complications and Their Management — Pneumonia, DVT, Sepsis, Wound Complications); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 22 (Postoperative Complications)

(2012) published the National Tracheostomy Safety Project emergency guidelines, relevant because tracheostomy-related infections are a significant cause of postoperative fever in HNC patients.

## Introduction

Postoperative fever is defined as a temperature **≥38.0°C (100.4°F)** in the postoperative period. It is one of the most common clinical problems encountered after major head and neck surgery. The timing, pattern, and associated clinical features guide the differential diagnosis.

**Day 3 is a critical transition point:** early inflammatory fever (days 1-2) has typically resolved, and infection-related causes become increasingly likely. In head and neck surgery, the risk is particularly high due to contaminated surgical fields (oral/pharyngeal mucosa), prolonged operative times, and the frequent use of indwelling catheters and drains.

---

## Part A: The "5 Ws" — Systematic Differential Diagnosis

The classic mnemonic for postoperative fever differentials is the **"5 Ws"**, organized by temporal pattern:

| Day | "W" | Cause | Mechanism in HNC Surgery |
|-----|-----|-------|--------------------------|
| **Day 1-2** | **Wind** (Lungs) | Atelectasis, pneumonia | Intubation, impaired cough post-tracheostomy, aspiration |
| **Day 3-5** | **Water** (UTI) | Urinary tract infection | Indwelling catheter during prolonged surgery |
| **Day 5-7** | **Wound** (SSI) | Surgical site infection | Contaminated field (UADT), salivary leak, flap complications |
| **Day 5+** | **Walking** (DVT/PE) | Deep vein thrombosis | Prolonged immobility, malignancy, dehydration |
| **Any day** | **Wonder drugs** | Drug fever | Antibiotics (beta-lactams), blood products, anaesthetic agents |

### Additional Causes Specific to Head and Neck Surgery

| Cause | Timing | Features |
|-------|--------|----------|
| **Pharyngocutaneous fistula** | Day 5-10 | Salivary leak through wound; wound erythema |
| **Flap necrosis/infection** | Day 3-7 | Flap discolouration, oedema, purulent drainage |
| **Parotitis** | Day 3-5 | Painful parotid swelling; dehydration + poor oral hygiene |
| **Tracheostomy site infection** | Day 3-7 | Peristomal erythema, purulent secretions |
| **Haematoma infection** | Day 3-5 | Expanding neck mass, warmth, fluctuance |
| **Alcohol withdrawal** | Day 2-4 | Tremor, agitation, tachycardia, diaphoresis |
| **Transfusion reaction** | Hours | Rigors, urticaria, haemolysis |

## Part B: Causes Most Likely on Day 3

On postoperative **day 3**, the differential narrows to:

### 1. Respiratory Causes (Wind)

Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 states: the creation of a tracheostomy or tracheotomy **significantly increases the patient's vulnerability to postoperative pneumonia** and demands meticulous postoperative stoma care with humidification and regular suctioning.

**Atelectasis:** Most common cause of fever in first 48 hours, may persist to day 3. Due to:
- Shallow breathing from pain
- Inadequate cough (especially with tracheostomy)
- Prolonged intubation
- General anaesthesia effects on mucociliary function

**Pneumonia:** Stell and Maran's Box 11.3 lists symptoms and signs: pleuritic chest pain, cough, sputum production, rigors, **fever >38°C**, decreased air entry, bronchial breathing, crackles.

**Aspiration pneumonia:** Stell and Maran's emphasizes: impaired postoperative [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]] with aspiration will eventually lead to **bronchopneumonia**. Strict "nil by mouth" policy with alternative nutritional route is necessary.

### 2. Urinary Tract Infection (Water)

All major HNC procedures involve indwelling urinary catheter. By day 3, catheter-associated UTI becomes a significant risk. Risk increases with duration of catheterization.

### 3. Early Wound Infection

Robbins et al. (1990) found **19.75% wound infection rate** in HNC surgery. Penel et al. (2001) reported even higher rates (**41.8%**) when the upper aerodigestive tract mucosa was opened — as in most oral cavity and oropharyngeal procedures. Risk factors from Stell and Maran's include:
- Tracheostomy (increases wound infection rates)
- Contaminated surgical field (oral, pharyngeal)
- Prolonged surgery
- Malnutrition
- Prior radiotherapy

### 4. Line/Catheter Infection

Central venous catheters, peripheral IV lines, and arterial lines can all become sources of infection by day 3. Catheter-related bloodstream infection (CRBSI) should be considered.

### 5. Alcohol Withdrawal

Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 recognizes this as a significant concern in HNC patients (who have high rates of alcohol consumption). Delirium tremens typically presents on **days 2-4** with fever, tachycardia, hypertension, tremor, agitation, hallucinations, and seizures.

## Part C: Systematic Evaluation

### Clinical Assessment

| Step | Assessment | Key Findings |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **1. History** | Timing, pattern of fever, associated symptoms | Cough (pneumonia), dysuria (UTI), wound pain (SSI) |
| **2. Vital signs** | Temperature, HR, BP, RR, SpO₂ | Tachycardia + fever = sepsis screen |
| **3. Wound inspection** | Erythema, warmth, swelling, discharge | Purulent drainage = SSI |
| **4. Chest auscultation** | Air entry, crackles, bronchial breathing | Focal signs = pneumonia |
| **5. Catheter sites** | IV sites, central lines, urinary catheter | Erythema, phlebitis |
| **6. Calves** | Tenderness, swelling, warmth | Unilateral = DVT |
| **7. Tracheostomy site** | Peristomal skin, secretion character | Purulent secretions |
| **8. Flap assessment** | Colour, turgor, Doppler | Necrosis = infection source |
| **9. Drug chart review** | Recent medications, blood products | Drug fever |
| **10. Alcohol history** | Quantity, last drink | Withdrawal syndrome |

### Investigations

| Investigation | Purpose |
|--------------|---------|
| **FBC with differential** | Leucocytosis with left shift (infection); eosinophilia (drug reaction) |
| **CRP/Procalcitonin** | CRP elevated in infection; procalcitonin more specific for bacterial infection |
| **Blood cultures** (×2 sets) | Identify bacteraemia; essential before antibiotics |
| **Urine microscopy and culture** | UTI diagnosis |
| **Chest X-ray** | Pneumonia, atelectasis, pleural effusion |
| **Wound swab** | Identify wound pathogen and sensitivities |
| **Sputum culture** | If productive cough or tracheal aspirate |
| **D-dimer/Duplex USS** | If DVT suspected |
| **ABG** | Assess oxygenation; sepsis screening |

## Part D: Management

### General Principles

1. **Identify and treat the source** — targeted antibiotics based on suspected/confirmed source
2. **Source control** — drain collections, remove infected lines, debride wounds
3. **Supportive care** — IV fluids, antipyretics, oxygen if needed

### Source-Specific Management

| Cause | Management |
|-------|-----------|
| **Atelectasis** | Chest physiotherapy, incentive spirometry, mobilization, adequate analgesia |
| **Pneumonia** | Empiric antibiotics (co-amoxiclav or piperacillin-tazobactam); adjust per culture |
| **UTI** | Remove catheter if possible; antibiotics per urine culture |
| **SSI** | Wound opening, drainage, debridement; antibiotics covering Staph, Strep, anaerobes |
| **Pharyngocutaneous fistula** | NPO, wound care, pressure dressing; may need surgical repair |
| **Flap infection/necrosis** | Urgent surgical review; debridement; salvage if possible |
| **DVT/PE** | Anticoagulation (LMWH → warfarin/DOAC); IVC filter if contraindicated |
| **Drug fever** | Discontinue offending agent; fever resolves within 48-72 hours |
| **Alcohol withdrawal** | Benzodiazepines (chlordiazepoxide/diazepam); thiamine; monitoring |
| **Line infection** | Remove catheter; send tip for culture; systemic antibiotics |
| **Sepsis/septic shock** | Sepsis 6 bundle: oxygen, cultures, antibiotics, fluids, lactate, urine output |

### Microbiology of HNC Wound Infections

Head and neck wound infections are typically **polymicrobial** due to contamination with oral flora:

| Organism Type | Common Pathogens |
|--------------|------------------|
| **Gram-positive** | Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA), Streptococcus spp. |
| **Gram-negative** | Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Klebsiella, E. coli |
| **Anaerobes** | Bacteroides, Prevotella, Peptostreptococcus |
| **Mixed** | Most common pattern in UADT contaminated wounds |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Postoperative fever on day 3 requires systematic evaluation — use the "5 Ws": Wind, Water, Wound, Walking, Wonder drugs
> 2. Day 3 is the TRANSITION POINT: early inflammatory fever (days 1-2) should have resolved; persistent/new fever suggests infection
> 3. Wound infection rates in HNC surgery are HIGH: 19.75% overall (Robbins, 1990); up to 41.8% with UADT mucosal breach (Penel, 2001)
> 4. Tracheostomy significantly increases vulnerability to postoperative PNEUMONIA — requires humidification and regular suctioning (Stell and Maran's)
> 5. Aspiration pneumonia from impaired swallowing is a major cause of fever in HNC patients — strict "nil by mouth" policy
> 6. ALCOHOL WITHDRAWAL must be considered in HNC patients — presents days 2-4 with fever, tachycardia, tremor, agitation
> 7. HNC wound infections are typically POLYMICROBIAL (oral flora) — antibiotic coverage must include Gram-positive, Gram-negative, AND anaerobes
> 8. Pharyngocutaneous fistula presents day 5-10 with salivary leak and wound infection — a cause specific to HNC surgery
> 9. Initial workup: FBC, CRP, blood cultures (×2), urine M/C/S, chest X-ray, wound swab — before starting empiric antibiotics
> 10. Sepsis 6 bundle for suspected sepsis: oxygen, blood cultures, antibiotics within 1 hour, IV fluids, lactate, urine output monitoring

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, ALWAYS mention the "5 Ws" mnemonic and add HNC-specific causes: pharyngocutaneous fistula, flap necrosis, tracheostomy infection, alcohol withdrawal
> - Day 3 differential is dominated by: persistent atelectasis/early pneumonia, UTI (catheter), line infection, and alcohol withdrawal — wound infection usually presents later (day 5-7)
> - The MOST IMPORTANT early step: BLOOD CULTURES before antibiotics — this simple step is frequently omitted and costs valuable diagnostic information
> - Alcohol withdrawal is an exam favourite — HNC patients have high alcohol consumption rates; delirium tremens on day 2-4 is life-threatening
> - Drug fever should be a diagnosis of EXCLUSION — characteristically: fever without toxicity, eosinophilia, resolves within 48-72 hours of stopping the drug
> - For empiric antibiotics in HNC wound infection: piperacillin-tazobactam or co-amoxiclav + metronidazole covers the typical polymicrobial oral flora
