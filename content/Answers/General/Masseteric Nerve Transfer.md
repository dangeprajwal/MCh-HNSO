# Masseteric Nerve Transfer

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Masseteric Nerve transfer (AIIMS 2020)

**Source:** Stell and Maran's Textbook of Head and Neck Surgery, Chapter 50 (Facial Nerve — Acute Palsy), Chapter 56 (Facial Reanimation Surgery); Scott-Brown's Otorhinolaryngology Volume 3, Chapter 95 (Facial Reanimation Surgery); Harrison - Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 35 (Skull Base — Facial Reanimation)

> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> The masseteric nerve transfer has emerged as one of the most significant advances in facial reanimation surgery over the past two decades. Spira (1978) first described the concept of anastomosing the masseteric nerve to the lower division of the facial nerve for correction of lower facial paralysis — the foundational work upon which all subsequent developments were built. Manktelow et al. (2006) published the landmark study demonstrating the effectiveness of free gracilis muscle transfer innervated by the masseter motor nerve in adults, with 96% patient satisfaction and evidence of cerebral adaptation enabling spontaneous smiling. In the same year, Bae, Zuker, and Manktelow (2006) provided the critical comparative data showing that masseter nerve-innervated gracilis produced greater commissure excursion than cross-facial nerve graft innervation. Borschel et al. (2012) established the anatomical and histomorphometric basis for the masseteric nerve's superiority — demonstrating a 3:1 axonal ratio advantage over cross-facial nerve grafts. Biglioli et al. (2012) then popularized the direct masseteric-facial nerve anastomosis technique for early facial reanimation, avoiding the need for free muscle transfer entirely.

## Introduction

Facial paralysis is a devastating complication of head and neck surgery, particularly following parotidectomy, skull base surgery, and excision of tumours involving the facial nerve. As Harrison's Chapter 35 states, when the branches of the facial nerve are sacrificed with the resection, facial reanimation techniques may be employed.

The masseteric nerve (motor branch of the trigeminal nerve V3 to the masseter muscle) has emerged as the **preferred donor nerve** for facial reanimation, largely replacing the hypoglossal nerve which was previously the standard. As Stell and Maran's Chapter 56 states: "the masseter nerve is gaining in popularity as the first choice of donor nerve" because donor morbidity is minimal and some patients seem to regain spontaneous muscle movement.

The masseteric nerve can be used in two principal ways:
1. **Direct masseteric-to-facial nerve neurorrhaphy** — for early reanimation when the distal facial nerve stump is intact
2. **Powering a free functioning muscle transfer** (usually gracilis) — for chronic facial palsy when the native facial musculature has atrophied

---

## Part A: Anatomy of the Masseteric Nerve

### Course and Relations

- **Origin:** Motor root of the trigeminal nerve (V3, mandibular division) — arises from the anterior trunk along with the deep temporal nerves and lateral pterygoid nerve
- **Course:** Passes laterally above the lateral pterygoid muscle, through the mandibular notch (sigmoid notch) between the coronoid process and the condyle
- **Termination:** Enters the deep surface of the masseter muscle
- **Key surgical landmark:** The nerve is found approximately **3 cm anterior to the tragus and 1 cm below the zygomatic arch**, deep to the parotid-masseteric fascia

### Why the Masseteric Nerve is an Ideal Donor

| Property | Masseteric Nerve | Hypoglossal Nerve | Cross-Facial Nerve Graft |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Axon count** | ~2800 myelinated fibres | ~5000+ | ~100-900 (single buccal branch) |
| **Axon:recipient ratio** | 3:1 favourable | Excessive (requires partial section) | <1:1 (insufficient) |
| **Proximity to facial nerve** | Adjacent (same operative field) | Requires dissection in neck | Requires tunnelling across face |
| **Donor morbidity** | Minimal (slight masticatory weakness) | Hemitongue atrophy, speech/swallow difficulty | Contralateral facial weakness |
| **Reinnervation time** | 2-3 months | 4-6 months | 6-9 months (two-stage) |
| **Number of stages** | Single | Single | Two stages |
| **Spontaneous smile** | 60-80% with rehabilitation | Uncommon | Natural but weak |

Borschel et al. (2012) established that the masseteric nerve contains approximately **2800 myelinated axons** — providing a **3:1 axon ratio** when coapted to a facial nerve branch. This is far superior to the cross-facial nerve graft, which delivers only 100-900 axons after regeneration through a long graft, and avoids the excessive axon load of the hypoglossal nerve that causes synkinesis.

---

## Part B: Indications and Timing

### Indications

**1. Early facial paralysis (0-18 months) — Direct Masseteric-Facial Neurorrhaphy**
- Facial nerve transection during parotidectomy, skull base surgery, or tumour excision
- When proximal facial nerve stump is unavailable but distal branches are intact
- Bell's palsy or traumatic facial palsy with no recovery by 12 months
- "Babysitter" procedure to maintain motor end plates while awaiting cross-facial nerve graft maturation

**2. Chronic facial paralysis (>18-24 months) — Free Muscle Transfer**
- Long-standing facial palsy where native facial muscles have undergone irreversible atrophy
- Failed prior reanimation procedures
- Congenital facial palsy (e.g., Möbius syndrome)

As Stell and Maran's Chapter 50 states: the most important predictor of long-term function was repair prior to 12 months of injury. If a nerve stump cannot be identified proximally, consideration should be made to a hypoglossal nerve or a trigeminal nerve (motor nerve to masseter) to facial nerve graft.

### Timing Considerations

| Time from Injury | Native Muscles | Recommended Procedure |
|-----------------|---------------|----------------------|
| **0-3 days** | Intact | Direct nerve repair or cable graft (if proximal stump available) |
| **2 weeks-12 months** | Intact (degenerating) | Masseteric-facial neurorrhaphy |
| **12-18 months** | Borderline viability | Masseteric-facial neurorrhaphy (may still work) |
| **>18-24 months** | Atrophied/fibrosed | Free functioning muscle transfer (gracilis) with masseteric nerve |

## Part C: Surgical Techniques

### Technique 1: Direct Masseteric-Facial Nerve Neurorrhaphy

**Approach:**
1. Modified Blair incision (as for parotidectomy)
2. Identify the main trunk or branches of the facial nerve distally
3. Locate the masseteric nerve by palpating the mandibular notch — the nerve is found at the deep surface of the masseter, approximately 3 cm anterior to the tragus
4. The masseter muscle is split in the direction of its fibres to expose the nerve
5. The masseteric nerve is divided as distally as possible to maximize length
6. Tension-free coaptation to the facial nerve trunk or buccal branch using microsurgical technique (9-0 or 10-0 nylon)
7. If a gap exists, a short interposition nerve graft (e.g., great auricular nerve) may be used

**Advantages:** Single-stage, rapid reinnervation (2-3 months), minimal donor morbidity

### Technique 2: Free Gracilis Muscle Transfer with Masseteric Nerve

This is the procedure described in detail in Scott-Brown's Chapter 95 and Stell and Maran's Chapter 56 for chronic facial palsy:

1. **Muscle harvest:** A segment of gracilis muscle is harvested from the medial thigh with its neurovascular pedicle ([[Answers/Oral Cavity/Obturator vs Flap Reconstruction|obturator reconstruction]] nerve branch, medial circumflex femoral vessels)
2. **Inset:** The muscle is tunnelled subcutaneously in the cheek and secured to the modiolus/oral commissure distally and the zygomatic arch/temporal fascia proximally
3. **Vascular anastomosis:** The facial or superficial temporal vessels are used for microvascular anastomosis
4. **Neural coaptation:** The obturator nerve branch of the gracilis is coapted to the masseteric nerve using microsurgical technique
5. Reinnervation occurs in 2-3 months; functional smile typically by 4-6 months

Scott-Brown's Chapter 95 notes: the use of the masseteric branch of the Vth cranial nerve to power muscle transplants in a single operative stage has recently become used. Although initially thought that only a cross-facial nerve graft could produce spontaneous facial activity, spontaneous activity has been observed in a significant proportion of transplants innervated by the nerve to masseter — ascribed to cortical plasticity and the observation that the normal masseteric nerve is active during smiling on EMG studies.

### Technique 3: Dual Innervation (Babysitter + Cross-Facial Nerve Graft)

Stell and Maran's describes a dual innervation approach where the muscle (e.g., latissimus dorsi) is innervated from both the contralateral facial nerve and the ipsilateral masseter, in the hope of gaining the benefits of both — the power of the masseteric nerve and the spontaneity of the cross-facial nerve graft.

## Part D: Outcomes and Rehabilitation

### Outcomes

Manktelow et al. (2006) — the definitive study:
- **27 patients, 45 muscle transfers** innervated by the masseteric nerve
- Average commissure movement: **13.0 ± 4.7 mm** (normal ~15 mm)
- **96% patient satisfaction** with smile
- **80% could smile without biting** (i.e., without conscious clenching)
- **60% achieved spontaneous smiling** with biofeedback therapy
- Age did not affect the amount of movement

Stell and Maran's Chapter 56 corroborates: in a recent study, 80% of patients were able to smile without biting and 60% were able to smile spontaneously with the aid of therapy and biofeedback.

Bae et al. (2006) — comparative data:
- 166 free gracilis transfers in 121 children
- Masseteric nerve innervation (94 procedures) produced **greater commissure excursion** than cross-facial nerve graft (70 procedures)
- Masseteric nerve innervation is a "very reproducible operation"

### Rehabilitation

Stell and Maran's describes the rehabilitation process in detail:

- With the **masseter nerve** as a donor, reinnervation of the muscle takes only **2-3 months** (vs 4-6 months for cross-facial nerve graft)
- However, rehabilitation is more demanding: the patient must first learn to control the smile **by clenching of the teeth**
- The strength of the smile will be proportionate to the **strength of the bite**, which the patient can learn to control
- In time, the majority of patients can learn to smile **without biting** and, in some cases, **without conscious thought**
- Physical therapy including **biofeedback and facial expression exercises** using a mirror should begin early
- Cortical plasticity enables gradual transition from voluntary (bite-triggered) to spontaneous smiling

## Part E: Comparison with Other Donor Nerves

### Masseteric vs Hypoglossal Nerve

Stell and Maran's Chapter 56 provides a clear comparison:

| Parameter | Masseteric Nerve | Hypoglossal Nerve |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Donor morbidity** | Minimal (slight masticatory weakness) | Hemitongue atrophy, speech difficulty, [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|swallowing assessment]] difficulty, synkinesis |
| **Contraindications** | Few | Young children (developing speech), patients with pre-existing swallowing difficulty |
| **Reinnervation speed** | 2-3 months | 4-6 months |
| **Spontaneous smile** | 60-80% with rehabilitation | Uncommon |
| **Excursion** | Greater | Less |
| **Popularity trend** | Increasing — now first choice | Decreasing — being replaced |

The textbook states: tongue atrophy and associated difficulty with mastication, speech and swallowing are known complications of hypoglossal nerve transfer. For this reason, the use of the hypoglossal nerve is discouraged in young children whose speech is yet to develop and may be contraindicated in patients with pre-existing difficulties with swallowing.

### Masseteric vs Cross-Facial Nerve Graft

| Parameter | Masseteric Nerve | Cross-Facial Nerve Graft |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Stages** | Single stage | Two stages (6-9 months apart) |
| **Axon delivery** | ~2800 (abundant) | ~100-900 (limited after regeneration) |
| **Excursion strength** | Strong (13 mm) | Weaker |
| **Spontaneity** | Initially voluntary; 60-80% become spontaneous | Natural spontaneity |
| **Best indication** | Patients needing strong smile; older patients; heavy face | Younger patients; where spontaneity is priority |

Stell and Maran's notes that masseteric nerve use may be indicated in patients whose predisposition or occupation requires them to smile frequently, those with a heavy face, and older patients in whom a cross-facial nerve graft is known to be less successful.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The masseteric nerve (V3 motor branch to masseter) is now the **first-choice donor nerve** for facial reanimation, replacing the hypoglossal nerve
> 2. Spira (1978) first described the masseteric-to-facial nerve anastomosis; Manktelow/Zuker (2006) established its effectiveness with free gracilis transfer
> 3. The masseteric nerve contains ~2800 myelinated axons with a 3:1 axon ratio advantage over cross-facial nerve graft (Borschel et al., 2012)
> 4. Two main techniques: (1) direct masseteric-facial neurorrhaphy for early palsy (<18 months), (2) free gracilis transfer for chronic palsy (>18 months)
> 5. Reinnervation occurs in 2-3 months — faster than hypoglossal (4-6 months) or cross-facial nerve graft (6-9 months)
> 6. 96% patient satisfaction; average commissure excursion 13 mm; 80% smile without biting; 60% achieve spontaneous smile
> 7. Minimal donor morbidity (slight masticatory weakness) — major advantage over hypoglossal nerve (hemitongue atrophy, dysarthria, dysphagia)
> 8. Cortical plasticity enables transition from voluntary (bite-triggered) to spontaneous smiling
> 9. Dual innervation (masseteric + cross-facial) combines the power of the masseteric nerve with the spontaneity of the cross-facial graft
> 10. The masseteric nerve is found 3 cm anterior to tragus, 1 cm below zygomatic arch — key surgical landmark

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - For the exam, know the THREE donor nerve options: masseteric (V3), hypoglossal (XII), and cross-facial nerve graft (contralateral VII) — and why masseteric is now preferred
> - The critical time window: facial muscles undergo irreversible atrophy after 18-24 months of denervation — direct neurorrhaphy must be done before this; after this, only free muscle transfer works
> - The "babysitter" concept: masseteric nerve transfer maintains motor end plates while a cross-facial nerve graft regenerates — combining the best of both approaches
> - Masseteric nerve is ideal for head and neck cancer patients specifically because: (1) it is in the same operative field as parotidectomy/skull base surgery, (2) it avoids hypoglossal sacrifice which would worsen swallowing in patients already at risk, (3) single-stage procedure reduces the burden on cancer patients
> - Remember: 80% smile without biting and 60% spontaneous smile — these are the key outcome numbers from the Manktelow/Zuker series
