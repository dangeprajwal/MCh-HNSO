## Introduction

Transoral laser microsurgery (TLM) is a minimally invasive surgical approach to functional organ preservation for cancer of the upper aerodigestive tract. It involves tumour resection using specialized endoscopic instruments, operating microscopes, and lasers, with the wound bed often left to heal by secondary intention. TLM differs fundamentally from traditional surgical oncology by departing from the classic Halstedian principle of en bloc resection. Instead, the tumour is divided with the laser and the margin followed under the microscope — allowing piecemeal resection with frozen-section control via the endoscope. In contrast to radiation-based protocols, TLM places no limit on future treatment options and avoids the long-term sequelae of definitive radiotherapy.

## Part A: Principles of Laser Physics

### What is a Laser?

**LASER** = Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. A laser consists of three key components:

1. **Lasing medium** (e.g., CO2 gas, KTP crystal, Nd:YAG crystal)
2. **Excitation source** (electrical current or flashlamp)
3. **Resonant chamber** with mirrors for optical feedback

### Properties of Laser Light

- **Monochromatic:** Single wavelength (determined by lasing medium)
- **Collimated:** Parallel, non-divergent beam
- **Coherent:** Photons oscillate in phase — temporally and spatially

### Laser-Tissue Interaction

The effect of laser light on tissue depends on its wavelength, power, spot size, and exposure time:

| Wavelength | Tissue Effect |
|-----------|---------------|
| 415 nm (blue) | Absorbed by haemoglobin — coagulation of vessels |
| 532 nm (green/KTP) | Absorbed by haemoglobin — selective vascular coagulation |
| 1064 nm (Nd:YAG) | Deep penetration (3-5 mm) — coagulation and deep tissue heating |
| 2013 nm (Thulium) | Absorbed by water — cutting and coagulation |
| 10,600 nm (CO2) | Highly absorbed by water — precise cutting with minimal thermal spread |

## Part B: Types of Lasers in Head and Neck Surgery

### 1. CO2 Laser (10,600 nm) — The Workhorse of TLM

- Wavelength: 10.6 µm (mid-infrared, invisible — requires He-Ne aiming beam)
- High absorption by water → absorbed by all water-containing tissues independent of tissue colour
- Provides excellent cutting with minimal thermal damage (0.1-0.5 mm)
- Can be coupled to micromanipulator and operating microscope
- Operating modes: Continuous Wave (CW), SuperPulse, UltraPulse (< 1 ms pulses — reduced thermal spread)
- **Gold standard for TLM** — most experience, best tissue cutting properties

### 2. KTP (Potassium Titanyl Phosphate) Laser (532 nm)

- Frequency-doubled Nd:YAG (green light, visible)
- Selectively absorbed by haemoglobin — excellent for vascular lesions
- Can be delivered through flexible fibres — usable in office-based settings
- Used for early glottic cancer (T1) — local control 96% for T1 (Zeitels 2014)
- Advantage: fibre-based delivery through flexible laryngoscope

### 3. Nd:YAG Laser (1064 nm)

- Deep penetration (3-5 mm) — useful for bulky debulking
- Greater thermal damage than CO2 — less suitable for precise excision
- Used for debulking obstructing airway tumours
- Can be delivered through flexible fibres

### 4. Thulium Laser (2013 nm)

- Absorbed by water (similar to CO2 but different wavelength)
- Can be delivered through flexible fibres (unlike CO2)
- Emerging use in transoral laser and robotic surgery
- Cutting properties approach CO2 with advantage of fibre delivery

### 5. Diode Lasers (various wavelengths)

- Compact, portable, relatively inexpensive
- Used in office-based procedures and palliative debulking
- Less precise than CO2 for oncological resection

## Part C: CO2 Laser in Laryngopharyngeal Cancer (TLM)

### Indications

- **Glottic cancer:** Tis, T1, T2 — primary treatment option; selected T3
- **Supraglottic cancer:** T1, T2 — selected cases
- **Hypopharyngeal cancer:** Selected T1, T2 cases
- **Oropharyngeal cancer:** Selected cases (increasingly with TORS)
- **Recurrent/salvage:** Post-RT recurrence with superficial disease
- **Diagnostic and staging:** Precise mapping of tumour extent under microscope

### Oncological Outcomes

| Site/Stage | Local Control | Laryngectomy-Free Survival |
|-----------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Tis glottic | 95-100% | >99% |
| T1a glottic | 85-95% | 95-98% |
| T1b glottic | 80-90% | 90-95% |
| T2 glottic | 70-85% | 80-90% |
| T1-T2 supraglottic | 75-90% | 80-90% |

### ELS Cordectomy Classification (Types I-VI)

| Type | Name | Depth of Resection |
|------|------|-------------------|
| I | Subepithelial | Through superficial lamina propria |
| II | Subligamental | Through vocal ligament |
| III | Transmuscular | Through vocalis muscle |
| IV | Total cordectomy | Entire vocal fold to thyroid perichondrium |
| Va-Vd | Extended | + contralateral cord/arytenoid/subglottis/ventricle |
| VI | Anterior commissurectomy | AC + bilateral anterior cord |

### Advantages of TLM

- **Organ preservation:** Maintains laryngeal function — voice, swallowing, airway
- **No limit on future treatment:** Unlike RT, TLM does not preclude further options if recurrence occurs
- **Avoids RT sequelae:** No xerostomia, fibrosis, dysphagia from radiation
- **Diagnostic precision:** Microsurgical examination defines true tumour extent
- **Reduced morbidity:** No external incision; faster recovery; shorter hospital stay
- **Repeatable:** Can be performed multiple times without cumulative toxicity

### Limitations of TLM

- Requires adequate endoscopic exposure — patients with trismus, limited neck extension, or bulky tumours may not be accessible
- Learning curve — requires microsurgical expertise
- Piecemeal resection complicates margin assessment
- Not suitable for deeply invasive tumours (T3-T4 with cartilage invasion)
- Subglottic extension beyond 10 mm is a relative contraindication

## Part D: Laser Safety in the Operating Room

- **Fire risk:** Laser-safe endotracheal tubes must be used; wet pledgets around the operative field
- **Eye protection:** Wavelength-specific goggles for all OR personnel
- **Plume evacuation:** Laser smoke contains viral DNA and potentially viable particles — smoke evacuator mandatory
- **Signage:** Laser warning signs on OR doors; restricted access during laser use

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The CO2 laser (10,600 nm) is the gold standard for TLM — highly absorbed by water, precise cutting, minimal thermal spread
> 2. TLM represents a paradigm shift from Halstedian en bloc resection to microsurgical piecemeal resection with frozen-section control
> 3. Steiner's 1993 landmark: 100% adjusted 5-year survival in early glottic cancer; 6% local recurrence
> 4. ELS classification (Types I-VI) standardizes cordectomy procedures for reproducibility and outcome reporting
> 5. Local control for T1a glottic cancer: 85-95% with TLM — comparable to radiotherapy
> 6. Key advantage over RT: TLM preserves all future treatment options in case of recurrence
> 7. KTP laser (532 nm) is emerging for office-based treatment of T1 glottic cancer via flexible fibre delivery
> 8. Thulium laser combines CO2-like cutting properties with fibre-based delivery — promising for TLM and TORS
> 9. Adequate endoscopic exposure is the most important factor determining TLM feasibility
> 10. Laser safety: laser-safe ETT, wavelength-specific goggles, smoke evacuation, and OR signage are mandatory

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - TLM is not just "burning away" tumour — it is precision microsurgery under magnification with frozen-section margin control
> - The biggest contraindication to TLM is inadequate exposure, not tumour size — always assess access before committing to TLM
> - Voice outcomes after TLM depend on the depth and extent of cordectomy — Type I-II have excellent voice, Type IV-V have poor voice
> - Unlike RT, TLM can be repeated without cumulative toxicity — this is a major advantage for field cancerization and second primaries
> - In post-RT recurrence, TLM is often the only organ-preserving surgical option — total laryngectomy is the alternative
> - The CO2 laser smoke plume can contain HPV DNA — always use a smoke evacuator, especially when treating papillomatosis
