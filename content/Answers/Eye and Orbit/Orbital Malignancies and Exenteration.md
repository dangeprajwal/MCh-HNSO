## Part A: Anatomy of the Orbit in Skull Base Surgery

### Orbital Walls

The orbit is a four-walled bony pyramid with its apex pointing posteriorly. Volume = ~30 mL.

| Wall | Bones | Surgical Relevance |
|------|-------|--------------------|
| **Roof** | Orbital plate of frontal bone (ant 2/3) + lesser wing of sphenoid (post 1/3) | Borders anterior cranial fossa; CFR specimens traverse here; lacrimal fossa in anterolateral roof |
| **Floor** | Orbital plate of maxilla (largest) + zygomatic bone + palatine bone | Infraorbital canal (V2); blow-out fracture site; roof of maxillary sinus |
| **Medial** | Lamina papyracea (ethmoid, very thin), lacrimal bone, frontal process of maxilla | Thinnest wall; separates orbit from ethmoid sinuses; first to erode in PNS cancer |
| **Lateral** | Zygomatic bone (ant) + greater wing of sphenoid (post) | Strongest wall; lateral orbitotomy approach |

### Key Openings

| Opening | Contents | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Superior orbital fissure | CN III, IV, V1, VI; sympathetic fibres; superior ophthalmic vein | SOF syndrome: ophthalmoplegia + V1 loss + proptosis |
| Inferior orbital fissure | V2 (infraorbital nerve); inferior ophthalmic vein; pterygoid venous plexus | Communicates with PTF and ITF |
| Optic canal | Optic nerve + ophthalmic artery | Within lesser wing of sphenoid |
| Infraorbital canal | Infraorbital nerve (V2) | Runs in orbital floor → infraorbital foramen |

### Orbital Periosteum (Periorbita)

- **Key oncological structure:** Periorbita is a tough fascial barrier between the bony orbit and orbital fat/soft tissue
- **Intact periorbita** = tumour confined to bone/periosteum → **orbital preservation** possible
- **Breached periorbita** = tumour in orbital fat/muscles → **exenteration** required
- This distinction is the basis of **T3 vs T4** classification in PNS cancer

## Part B: Progressive Proptosis in a 2-Year-Old Child

### Differential Diagnosis

| Category | Diagnosis | Age | Notes |
|---------|----------|-----|-------|
| **Malignant** | **Rhabdomyosarcoma** | 5–7 years | Most common **malignant** primary orbital tumour in children; rapid progression |
| **Malignant** | Retinoblastoma (orbital extension) | <5 years | Leukocoria + proptosis; extraocular extension = advanced |
| **Malignant** | Neuroblastoma metastasis (metastatic) | <2 years | Periorbital ecchymosis ("raccoon eyes") + proptosis; primary in adrenal/sympathetic chain |
| **Malignant** | Leukaemia / lymphoma | Any childhood | Bilateral proptosis, adenopathy |
| **Malignant** | Langerhans cell histiocytosis | 1–5 years | Orbital soft tissue; skin lesions; lytic skull defects |
| **Benign** | Capillary haemangioma | <1 year | Most common **benign** orbital tumour; involutes spontaneously by age 7; strawberry appearance |
| **Benign** | Dermoid cyst | Any | Slowly progressive; superotemporal quadrant; dumbbell (trans-septal) variants |
| **Benign** | Optic nerve glioma (ONG) | 2–6 years | Axial proptosis + vision loss; NF1 association (50%); pilocytic astrocytoma |
| **Benign** | Orbital cellulitis | Any | Acute; painful; fever; sinusitis |

### Investigation Approach

1. **CT orbit** (no radiation concern if rapid/malignant progression is suspected): bone involvement, orbital walls, sinus disease
2. **MRI orbit + brain** (preferred for soft tissue): optic nerve involvement, intracranial extension, muscle/fat invasion
3. **Urinary catecholamines** (VMA/HVA) → neuroblastoma
4. **Bone marrow biopsy** if haematological malignancy suspected
5. **Incisional biopsy** → histology and IHC

## Part C: Uveal Melanoma

### Classification

Uveal melanoma arises from melanocytes of the uveal tract (iris + ciliary body + choroid):

| Site | Frequency | Prognosis |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Choroid** | 85% | Worst (closest to rich choroidal vasculature → haematogenous spread to liver) |
| **Ciliary body** | 7% | Intermediate |
| **Iris** | 8% | Best (diagnosed early as visible pigmented lesion) |

### AJCC Staging (8th Edition)

- Based on: largest basal diameter (LBD) and tumour thickness (Callender classification deprecated)
- T1: LBD ≤12 mm; T2: >12–18 mm; T3: >18 mm or ciliary body/extrascleral; T4: extrascleral extension
- **GEP (Gene Expression Profiling):** Class 1A (low metastatic risk), 1B (intermediate), 2 (high metastatic risk — 50% 5-yr metastasis)

### Management

| Tumour Size | Treatment | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| **Very small** (<3 mm thick, <12 mm LBD) | Observation (active surveillance with USS q6m) | Low growth rate; may never require treatment |
| **Small–medium** (2.5–10 mm thick, ≤16 mm LBD) | **Iodine-125 plaque brachytherapy** (standard — COMS) | Ru-106 plaque also used; globe-preserving |
| **Medium** (3–8 mm thick) | Proton beam RT (60–70 Gy equivalents) | Excellent local control; less available |
| **Large** (>8 mm thick, >16 mm LBD) | **Enucleation** (removal of globe) | Standard for large; pre-enucleation RT (COMS: no survival benefit) |
| **Extra-scleral extension** | Enucleation ± orbital exenteration if orbital involvement | Combined approach |

**COMS Trial (Report No. 28, 2006):** Iodine-125 brachytherapy vs enucleation for medium choroidal melanoma → **no significant difference in 12-year all-cause mortality** (BCVA preserved in 43% of brachytherapy patients at 3 years); established plaque brachytherapy as standard for globe preservation.

### Metastatic Disease

- Liver metastases in ~50% at 10 years (haematogenous spread)
- Mutations: **GNAQ/GNA11** (most common; ~90%) — not targeted by BRAF inhibitors (unlike cutaneous melanoma)
- Systemic options: pembrolizumab (limited response), liver-directed therapy (TACE, isolated hepatic perfusion), MEK inhibitors (binimetinib — modest activity in GNAQ-mutant)

## Part D: Topical Mitomycin C for Ocular Surface Malignancies

### Indications

Topical mitomycin C (MMC) is used for:
1. **Ocular surface squamous neoplasia (OSSN)** — including conjunctival intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) and conjunctival SCC
2. **Primary pterygium** (adjuvant to prevent recurrence — lower dose)
3. **Primary acquired melanosis (PAM)** with atypia (controversial)
4. **Adjuvant after surgical excision** of OSSN to clear margins

### Mechanism of Action

- **Alkylating agent:** Mitomycin C is bioreductively activated to produce bifunctional alkylating species
- **DNA cross-linking:** Inhibits DNA synthesis by cross-linking complementary strands → apoptosis in rapidly dividing cells
- **Antiproliferative:** Selective for tumour cells at mucosal surface; poor penetration beyond conjunctival epithelium → limits systemic toxicity

### Regimen (Shields 2003)

- Concentration: **0.02% (mild, well-tolerated)** or **0.04% (aggressive, more side effects)**
- Application: **4 drops per day × 1 week ON, 1 week OFF** (cycles)
- Typical course: 4 cycles (4 weeks total of active treatment over 8 weeks)
- **Punctal occlusion** (digital pressure on lacrimal puncta during instillation) to minimise systemic absorption

### Side Effects

| Side Effect | Frequency | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Corneal epitheliopathy | Common | Punctate keratitis; usually resolves after treatment |
| Punctal stenosis | 5–10% | May cause epiphora |
| Conjunctival scarring | Occasional | Subconjunctival fibrosis |
| Cataract | Rare | Particularly with high doses/prolonged use |
| Corneal stromal melt | Rare | Serious; dose-dependent |
| Scleral thinning | Rare | Requires discontinuation |

### Outcomes

- Complete response rate for CIN/OSSN: **76–94%** (superior to surgery alone in high-risk/multicentric disease)
- Recurrence rate: ~10% (lower than excision alone for extensive lesions)
- **Advantage:** Avoids extensive conjunctival excision → preserves ocular surface; useful for diffuse/multifocal OSSN

## Part E: Orbital Rhabdomyosarcoma

### Epidemiology and Pathology

- Most common **primary malignant orbital tumour in children**
- Peak age: **5–7 years** (orbital RMS tends to present at younger age than overall RMS)
- Histology:
  - **Embryonal** (most common in orbit — ~85%): best prognosis; spindle cells in myxoid stroma; loss of heterozygosity 11p15
  - **Alveolar:** PAX3-FOXO1 or PAX7-FOXO1 fusion; worst prognosis; rare in orbit
  - **Botryoid:** Variant of embryonal; grape-like polypoid growth in hollow viscera
  - **Pleomorphic:** Rare; adults

### Presentation

- Rapid onset proptosis (over weeks — differentiates from dermoid cyst)
- Periorbital oedema, erythema
- Diplopia, chemosis
- No pain initially (unlike cellulitis)
- Usually superotemporal quadrant; can be anywhere

### Staging (IRS/COG)

| Group | Definition |
|-------|-----------|
| Group I | Localised, completely resected |
| Group II | Localised, microscopic residual or regional disease |
| Group III | **Localised, gross residual** (most orbital RMS at diagnosis) |
| Group IV | Distant metastases |

### Management (Do NOT excise primarily — biopsy only)

**Principle: Chemotherapy first; RT if needed; surgery only for biopsy and salvage**

1. **Biopsy** (incisional; anterior orbitotomy): Confirm diagnosis, IHC (desmin, myogenin, MyoD1 positive), cytogenetics (PAX-FOXO1 FISH for alveolar)
2. **Systemic staging:** CT chest/abdomen/pelvis; bone scan; bone marrow biopsy (for alveolar/metastatic)
3. **VAC chemotherapy** (Vincristine + Actinomycin D + Cyclophosphamide) — first-line; 12–24 weeks
4. **Radiotherapy** (50.4 Gy, conformal/IMRT): For Group III (gross residual) and all alveolar; given after induction chemo (at week 12)
5. **Response assessment:** MRI orbit at 12 weeks; complete response → continue VAC; partial/no response → RT ± re-biopsy
6. **Surgery:** Reserved for biopsy failure or salvage; exenteration avoided if at all possible

**Prognosis:**
- Orbital RMS has the **best prognosis** of all RMS sites due to:
  - Usually embryonal histology
  - No lymphatics in the orbit (rare nodal spread)
  - Excellent local control with chemo + RT
- 3-year EFS: ~85% for orbital Group III embryonal RMS

## Part F: Solitary Fibrous Tumour of the Orbit

### Pathology

- Rare mesenchymal tumour; previously called haemangiopericytoma (now reclassified)
- **Hallmark:** NAB2-STAT6 gene fusion → STAT6 nuclear expression (diagnostic IHC marker)
- WHO Grade 1 (low-grade, benign) to Grade 3 (malignant)
- Mostly benign but 10–15% behave aggressively (malignant SFT)

### Clinical Features

- Middle-aged adults
- Slowly progressive, painless proptosis (months to years)
- Well-circumscribed, lobulated mass; usually extraconal
- Imaging: well-defined, intensely enhancing mass on CT/MRI; "flow voids" (vascular tumour)

### Management

- **Complete surgical excision** (lateral orbitotomy for posteriorly placed tumours)
- STAT6 IHC (nuclear) confirms diagnosis
- Incomplete excision → high risk of local recurrence
- Malignant SFT: adjuvant RT; sunitinib/trabectedin for metastatic/unresectable
- **Follow-up:** Long-term (10+ years); late recurrences documented

## Part G: Orbital Exenteration

### Definition and Types

**Orbital exenteration:** Surgical removal of all orbital contents (globe, extraocular muscles, lacrimal gland, orbital fat) with varying amounts of eyelid and bone.

| Type | What is Removed | Eyelids | Bone |
|------|----------------|---------|------|
| **Total exenteration** | All orbital contents + periorbita | Sacrificed | Orbital rims preserved |
| **Modified (subtotal) exenteration** | All orbital contents + periorbita | Lids preserved (skin flaps) | Orbital rims preserved |
| **Extended exenteration** | Orbital contents + adjacent bone/structures | Sacrificed | Orbital walls, maxilla, or skull base removed |

### Indications

**Absolute:**
- PNS malignancy invading orbital fat or muscles (periorbita breached) — T4 disease
- Primary orbital malignancy with diffuse orbital fat/muscle invasion (adenoid cystic carcinoma, SCC, melanoma)
- Conjunctival/eyelid malignancy with full-thickness orbital invasion
- Orbital RMS failing chemotherapy + RT

**Relative (controversial — consider individual patient):**
- Extensive periorbital invasion where margin control impossible without exenteration
- Recurrent disease after prior orbital preservation

**NOT indicated (orbital preservation preferred):**
- Periorbita intact on imaging + intraoperative assessment
- Periorbital invasion only (stripping of periorbita sufficient with adequate margin)

### Orbital Preservation in T3 PNS Cancer (Perry 1988 Principle)

T3 PNS cancer = periorbital involvement. **Periorbita is the key oncological landmark:**

- **Periorbita intact:** Periosteal stripping (periorbita removed with tumour, extraocular muscles and fat preserved) = **orbital preservation** — equivalent local control to exenteration
- **Periorbita breached (fat/muscle invaded):** Exenteration required

**Intraoperative assessment:** Once periorbita is entered, palpate/visualise orbital fat; if fat clearance possible with negative margins → preserve eye; if not → exenterate.

**Visual preservation after orbital stripping:** Maintained in ~60–70% long-term; vision may be compromised by RT-induced retinopathy/cataract

### Surgical Technique of Total Exenteration

1. **Incision:** Lid margin incision or modified langer's lines incision across eyelids; some preserve lid skin for modified exenteration
2. **Periosteal incision:** At orbital rim; periostea raised from orbital walls
3. **Sequential contents mobilisation:** Extraocular muscles cut from insertions; lacrimal gland excised
4. **Optic nerve transection:** At orbital apex (as posterior as possible)
5. **Specimen delivery:** En bloc
6. **Haemostasis:** Bone wax for bony bleeders; bipolar for soft tissue
7. **Reconstruction** (see below)

### Reconstruction After Orbital Exenteration

| Technique | Indication | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Split-thickness skin graft (STSG)** | Dry socket; simple/small defects | Granulation tissue base over bone; economical; socket lining; allows direct inspection for recurrence |
| **Temporalis muscle flap** | Medium defects; posterior orbit | Transposed through zygomatic arch; provides bulk; lined with STSG |
| **Anterolateral thigh (ALT) free flap** | Large defects; extended exenteration | Simultaneous maxillectomy/skull base reconstruction; provides bulk and skin |
| **Rectus abdominis free flap** | Very large defects | High volume dead space obliteration |
| **Wound left open (granulation)** | Historical; poor cosmesis | Largely abandoned |
| **Prosthetic rehabilitation** | After socket maturation | Osseointegrated implants in orbital rim for prosthesis retention; excellent cosmesis |

**Reconstructive goals:**
1. Watertight closure (if dura exposed)
2. Obliteration of dead space
3. Socket lining for prosthesis fitting
4. Aesthetic rehabilitation

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Most common malignant orbital tumour in children: rhabdomyosarcoma (embryonal histology; treat with biopsy + VAC chemotherapy + RT; DO NOT primarily excise)
> 2. Most common benign orbital tumour in children: capillary haemangioma (involutes by age 7; propranolol for vision-threatening cases)
> 3. Progressive proptosis in 2-year-old: always rule out neuroblastoma (raccoon eyes, urinary catecholamines), RMS, and retinoblastoma
> 4. Uveal melanoma: COMS trial (2006) established I-125 plaque brachytherapy equivalent to enucleation for medium choroidal melanoma; GNAQ/GNA11 mutations (not BRAF); liver is primary metastatic site
> 5. Topical mitomycin C 0.02–0.04% for OSSN/CIN: 4 cycles of 1-week-on/1-week-off; 76–94% complete response; side effects include punctal stenosis and corneal epitheliopathy
> 6. Solitary fibrous tumour: NAB2-STAT6 fusion → STAT6 nuclear IHC; complete excision mandatory; low-grade but late recurrence possible
> 7. Orbital exenteration types: total (all contents + periorbita, lids sacrificed), modified (lids preserved), extended (bone included)
> 8. Periorbita intact = orbital preservation possible; periorbita breached + orbital fat/muscle invaded = exenteration required (Perry 1988 principle)
> 9. Reconstruction after exenteration: STSG (simple, allows recurrence monitoring), temporalis flap + STSG (medium defects), ALT free flap (large/extended defects), osseointegrated prosthesis (final rehabilitation)
> 10. Orbital RMS prognosis: best of all RMS sites (no orbital lymphatics; usually embryonal; ~85% 3-year EFS for localised disease)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - "Biopsy only, no primary excision" for orbital rhabdomyosarcoma — this is the cardinal rule; VAC chemo is curative in most cases without disfiguring surgery
> - Raccoon eyes (bilateral periorbital ecchymosis) + proptosis in a child under 2 = neuroblastoma metastases until proven otherwise
> - For uveal melanoma MCQs: COMS = iodine-125 = globe-preserving = equivalent survival to enucleation for medium tumours
> - Topical mitomycin for OSSN: memorise as "4 cycles, 1 week on / 1 week off, 0.02–0.04%" and the side effects (punctal stenosis most commonly tested)
> - STAT6 nuclear staining (IHC) = solitary fibrous tumour — distinguishes it from other orbital spindle cell tumours
> - The periorbita is the "magic line" in PNS cancer orbital invasion: intact → preserve; breached → exenterate (Perry 1988)
> - After exenteration, STSG directly applied to orbital bone allows easy monitoring for recurrence through the translucent graft — an important oncological advantage
> - Orbital RMS IHC: desmin + myogenin + MyoD1 positive; alveolar confirmed by PAX3/7-FOXO1 FISH
