> Brandwein et al. (2001) published the landmark clinicopathologic grading scheme for mucoepidermoid carcinoma, studying 80 patients with rigorous histological criteria including cystic proportion, neural invasion, necrosis, mitoses, and anaplasia, demonstrating that their modified scoring system better stratified risk of death and recurrence than prior AFIP criteria — it remains the most widely cited grading system in clinical practice. Auclair et al. (1992) established the AFIP grading system for intraoral mucoepidermoid carcinoma using a point-scored system based on five histopathological features applied to 143 cases, and this system continues to be used alongside the Brandwein scheme in pathological reporting. Seethala and Stenman (2017) provided the WHO 2022 precursor classification framework for salivary gland tumours, updating histological categories and incorporating molecular diagnostics including CRTC1/3-MAML2 fusions in MEC and introducing new entities such as secretory carcinoma and microsecretory adenocarcinoma. Cooper et al. (RTOG 9501, 2004) demonstrated that postoperative concurrent cisplatin-based chemoradiation improved locoregional control and disease-free survival over radiation alone in high-risk head and neck cancers — the risk factors identified (positive margins, ENE) are directly applied to high-grade salivary gland malignancies. Bernier and Cooper (2005) published the comparative analysis of RTOG 9501 and EORTC 22931, defining the absolute indications for postoperative chemoradiation: positive margins and extranodal extension, with high-grade histology, perineural invasion, and LVI constituting relative indications for adjuvant radiation alone.

## Classification and Management of Malignant Parotid Tumors

### WHO 2022 Classification of Salivary Gland Tumours

The WHO 2022 Classification (5th edition, WHO Classification of Head and Neck Tumours) reorganised salivary gland neoplasms incorporating molecular characterisation. Key entities are listed below.

#### Malignant Epithelial Tumours

| Tumour Type | Key Features | Molecular Marker |
|---|---|---|
| **Mucoepidermoid carcinoma (MEC)** | Most common malignant salivary gland tumour overall | CRTC1/3-MAML2 fusion (t11;19) |
| **Adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC)** | Most common malignant minor salivary gland tumour | MYB-NFIB fusion |
| **Acinic cell carcinoma** | Second most common parotid malignancy; low-grade predominantly | NR4A3 rearrangements |
| **Polymorphous adenocarcinoma (PAC)** | Minor salivary glands; palate; formerly PLGA | PRKD1 hotspot mutation (E710D) |
| **Adenocarcinoma NOS** | Diagnosis of exclusion | Variable |
| **Salivary duct carcinoma (SDC)** | Aggressive; HER2-amplified; resembles ductal carcinoma of breast | HER2/neu amplification, AR expression |
| **Carcinoma ex pleomorphic adenoma (CXPA)** | Malignant transformation of pre-existing PA | Loss of HMGA2, TP53, CDKN2A |
| **Secretory carcinoma** | Formerly mammary analogue secretory carcinoma (MASC) | ETV6-NTRK3 fusion |
| **Epithelial-myoepithelial carcinoma** | Biphasic; low-grade | HRAS mutations |
| **Clear cell carcinoma** | Minor salivary; painless; predilection for palate | EWSR1-ATF1 fusion |
| **Myoepithelial carcinoma** | Rare; variable grade | EWSR1 fusions |
| **Basal cell adenocarcinoma** | Low-grade; resembles basal cell adenoma | — |
| **Mucinous adenocarcinoma** | New WHO 2022 entity | — |
| **Microsecretory adenocarcinoma** | New WHO 2022 entity; intercalated duct origin | MEF2C-SS18 fusion |
| **Sclerosing microcystic adenocarcinoma** | New WHO 2022 entity | — |
| **Sebaceous adenocarcinoma** | Rare; parotid | — |
| **Lymphoepithelial carcinoma** | EBV-associated; parotid; Asian populations | EBV |
| **Squamous cell carcinoma** | Primary (rare) vs metastatic (common) | — |
| **Undifferentiated carcinoma** | Worst prognosis | — |
| **Carcinosarcoma** | Rare; both malignant epithelial + mesenchymal | — |

#### Benign Epithelial Tumours

| Tumour | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| Pleomorphic adenoma (PA) | Most common salivary tumour; 65% of all parotid tumours; PLAG1/HMGA2 |
| Warthin tumour | Second most common; parotid; bilateral 10%; smoking association |
| Basal cell adenoma | Parotid; solid/trabecular/tubular/membranous subtypes |
| Oncocytoma | Parotid; oncocytic cells |
| Lymphadenoma | Parotid; sebaceous/non-sebaceous |
| Canalicular adenoma | Minor salivary; lip |
| Myoepithelioma | Parotid or minor |
| Ductal papillomas | Minor salivary; inverted ductal papilloma, sialadenoma papilliferum |
| Cystadenoma | Minor salivary |

### Mucoepidermoid Carcinoma (MEC): Detailed Account

MEC is the **most common malignant salivary gland tumour overall** (35–40% of malignant parotid tumours). It arises predominantly in the parotid (45%), followed by minor salivary glands (especially palate), and submandibular gland.

#### Histological Composition
MEC is composed of three cell types in varying proportions:
1. **Mucous cells** (goblet-like; PAS-positive; mucin-secreting)
2. **Epidermoid (squamoid) cells** (non-keratinising; sheet-like)
3. **Intermediate cells** (between mucous and epidermoid; considered the proliferating compartment)
4. **Clear cells** (glycogen-rich; present in some tumours)
5. **Columnar cells** (lining cystic spaces)

The relative proportions and architectural pattern determine grade.

#### Histological Grading Systems

**Two main systems are used:**

##### 1. AFIP (Auclair 1992) Point Scoring System

| Feature | Points |
|---|---|
| Intracystic component <25% | +2 |
| Neural invasion | +3 |
| Necrosis | +3 |
| Mitoses ≥4/10 HPF | +3 |
| Anaplasia | +4 |

- **Low grade:** 0–4 points
- **Intermediate grade:** 5–6 points
- **High grade:** ≥7 points

##### 2. Brandwein (2001) Point Scoring System

| Feature | Points |
|---|---|
| Intracystic component ≤25% | +2 |
| Neural invasion | +2 |
| Necrosis | +3 |
| 4+ mitoses/10 HPF | +3 |
| Anaplasia | +4 |
| Bony invasion | +3 |
| Lymphovascular invasion | +3 |

- **Low grade:** 0 points (all low-risk features)
- **Intermediate grade:** 2–3 points
- **High grade:** ≥4 points

> The Brandwein system **downgrades fewer tumours** and has better prognostic discrimination than AFIP in most studies. Both are reported in current pathological practice.

#### Molecular Pathology of MEC

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **CRTC1-MAML2 fusion** | t(11;19)(q21;p13) — present in ~55–80% of all MEC; significantly more common in low/intermediate grade |
| **CRTC3-MAML2 fusion** | Minority (~5%); seen in some high-grade tumours |
| **Clinical significance** | MAML2 translocation-positive tumours have significantly better prognosis, lower recurrence, and improved survival across multiple studies |
| **High-grade MEC** | More likely MAML2-fusion-negative; TP53 mutations; EGFR amplification |
| **Diagnostic use** | MAML2 break-apart FISH — used as diagnostic biomarker for MEC |

#### Management by Grade

| Grade | Surgery | Neck | Adjuvant |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low** | Parotidectomy (superficial if confined); negative margins paramount | cN0: observation or sentinel node; elective ND controversial | None if R0, small, no high-risk features |
| **Intermediate** | Total parotidectomy; nerve preservation if functional | Elective ND levels II–IV | RT if close/positive margins, PNI, T3 |
| **High** | Total/radical parotidectomy; facial nerve sacrifice if involved | Elective ND levels II–V mandatory | Adjuvant RT (≥60 Gy) ± cisplatin (if ENE, positive margins) |

### High-Grade MEC with Temporal Bone Involvement

This represents the most challenging scenario in parotid malignancy management.

#### Anatomical Considerations
- The parotid gland lies adjacent to the **external auditory canal (EAC)** and cartilaginous EAC
- Direct extension routes: via EAC (anteriorly), via the fissures of Santorini in the cartilaginous EAC, via the stylomastoid foramen (facial nerve)
- Intratemporal spread may involve: EAC, middle ear, mastoid, facial nerve canal (petrous), jugular fossa, carotid canal
- **The facial nerve exits the stylomastoid foramen and is invested within the parotid**: involvement requires nerve resection

#### Classification of Temporal Bone Involvement
- **Limited:** EAC only (soft tissue or superficial bony)
- **Moderate:** EAC with middle ear or mastoid involvement
- **Extensive:** Involvement of petrous apex, internal auditory canal, carotid canal, or jugular bulb

#### Surgical Management: Temporal Bone Resection

**Sleeve resection (lateral temporal bone resection, LTBR):**
- Resection of the lateral EAC skin and cartilaginous/bony EAC as a sleeve
- Indicated for limited EAC involvement without tympanic membrane involvement

**Lateral temporal bone resection (LTBR) proper:**
- En bloc resection of the EAC (bony and cartilaginous), tympanic membrane, ossicular chain, and lateral mastoid cortex
- Lateral to the otic capsule
- Indicated for involvement of tympanic membrane or middle ear without otic capsule or facial nerve canal involvement

**Subtotal temporal bone resection (STBR):**
- Adds resection of the petrous apex, medial to the otic capsule
- Facial nerve sacrifice invariably required
- Requires identification and preservation/management of carotid artery and jugular vein

**Combined approach:**
- Total parotidectomy + neck dissection + temporal bone resection
- Single-stage procedure for high-grade MEC with temporal bone involvement
- Reconstruction: temporalis muscle flap, free flap (ALT or rectus) for dead-space obliteration and skin cover

#### Neck Management in High-Grade MEC with Temporal Bone Involvement
- **Levels at risk for parotid primary:** IIA, IIB, III, IV, V (posterior triangle)
- **Selective neck dissection** (levels I–V) recommended for cN0 high-grade disease
- If cN+ (clinical/radiological), therapeutic modified radical or radical neck dissection
- **Retropharyngeal and parapharyngeal nodes** at risk if deep lobe parotid involvement

#### Adjuvant Treatment
- **Postoperative radiotherapy is mandatory** for high-grade MEC with temporal bone involvement
- Target: Primary site (parotid bed + temporal bone) + ipsilateral neck (levels II–V)
- Dose: 60–66 Gy to tumour bed; 50–54 Gy elective neck
- **IMRT** preferred to reduce toxicity to contralateral structures and cochlea
- **Concurrent cisplatin** if ENE present, positive margins, or perineural spread to named nerves at skull base

### AJCC 8th Edition TNM Staging for Major Salivary Glands

#### T Classification

| Stage | Definition |
|---|---|
| **T1** | Tumour ≤2 cm; no extraparenchymal extension |
| **T2** | Tumour >2 cm but ≤4 cm; no extraparenchymal extension |
| **T3** | Tumour >4 cm and/or tumour with extraparenchymal extension |
| **T4a** | Tumour invades skin, mandible, ear canal, and/or facial nerve |
| **T4b** | Tumour invades skull base and/or pterygoid plates, and/or encases carotid artery |

> **Key 8th edition change:** Extranodal extension (ENE) now included in N staging.

#### N Classification (Regional Lymph Nodes)

| Stage | Definition |
|---|---|
| **N0** | No regional lymph node metastasis |
| **N1** | Single ipsilateral node ≤3 cm; ENE(−) |
| **N2a** | Single ipsilateral node >3 cm but ≤6 cm; ENE(−) |
| **N2b** | Multiple ipsilateral nodes ≤6 cm; ENE(−) |
| **N2c** | Bilateral or contralateral nodes ≤6 cm; ENE(−) |
| **N3a** | Node(s) >6 cm; ENE(−) |
| **N3b** | Any node with ENE(+) |

#### M Classification
- **M0:** No distant metastasis
- **M1:** Distant metastasis present

#### Overall Stage Groups

| Stage | T | N | M |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | T1 | N0 | M0 |
| II | T2 | N0 | M0 |
| III | T3 | N0 | M0; or T1–T3 N1 M0 |
| IVA | T4a N0–N2; or T1–T3 N2 | M0 |
| IVB | T4b any N; or any T N3 | M0 |
| IVC | Any T | Any N | M1 |

### Types of Parotidectomy and Oncological Efficacy

#### Extracapsular Dissection (ECD)
- Limited resection excising the tumour with a thin rim of normal parotid tissue
- **Only acceptable for small (<4 cm) mobile benign tumours** (PA in selected hands)
- No oncological role in malignancy
- Recurrence rate for PA with ECD is higher than formal superficial parotidectomy

#### Superficial Parotidectomy
- Removal of the superficial lobe (lateral to the facial nerve plane)
- Requires formal facial nerve dissection from main trunk to terminal branches
- **Indication in malignancy:** Low-grade tumours confined to the superficial lobe with no clinical/radiological evidence of deep lobe involvement or facial nerve involvement
- Adequate margin for T1 low-grade MEC and acinic cell carcinoma

#### Total Parotidectomy (Nerve-Sparing)
- Removal of both superficial and deep lobes with preservation of the facial nerve
- **Standard operation for malignancy in the parotid**
- Nerve preservation is appropriate if:
  - Facial nerve is functional preoperatively
  - Nerve is not directly involved by tumour on imaging or intraoperative assessment
  - Adequate margin can be achieved without nerve sacrifice

#### Total Parotidectomy with Facial Nerve Sacrifice (Radical Parotidectomy)
- **Absolute indications for facial nerve resection:**
  - Preoperative facial nerve palsy attributable to tumour
  - Direct gross tumour involvement of the nerve at surgery (nerve encased, invaded by tumour)
  - Inadequate oncological margin without sacrifice
- **Relative indications:**
  - High-grade malignancy with nerve in close proximity (<1 mm on MRI)
  - Intraoperative loss of nerve monitoring signal with no anatomical cause

> **Key principle (Shah):** A functioning facial nerve should be preserved if it can be done with adequate tumour clearance. An involved or non-functional nerve must be sacrificed. There is no role for "shaving" tumour off an anatomically involved nerve.

#### Modified Radical Parotidectomy
- Total parotidectomy + nerve sacrifice + resection of adjacent structures (skin, masseter, mandible, temporal bone) based on extent of tumour
- Reserved for T4a/T4b disease

#### Neck Dissection — Integration with Parotidectomy

| Neck Status | Procedure |
|---|---|
| cN0, low-grade tumour | Observation or sentinel node biopsy (limited evidence) |
| cN0, high-grade / T3–T4 / ACC | Elective selective neck dissection levels II–IV (or II–V) |
| cN+ | Therapeutic comprehensive neck dissection levels I–V |
| Facial nerve sacrifice with high-grade | Elective ND mandatory (levels IIa, IIb, III, IV, Va) |

**Sentinel node biopsy** in parotid malignancy has limited evidence; not routinely recommended outside clinical trials.

### Indications for Adjuvant Therapy in Salivary Gland Malignancies

#### Absolute Indications for Postoperative Chemoradiation
(Based on RTOG 9501/EORTC 22931 criteria extrapolated to salivary histology)
1. **Positive margins (R1/R2 resection)** — most important
2. **Extranodal extension (ENE)** at lymph nodes

#### Indications for Postoperative Radiation Alone
1. pT3–T4 disease
2. High-grade histology (MEC intermediate/high, SDC, CXPA, high-grade transformation)
3. Perineural invasion (PNI) — especially named nerve involvement
4. Lymphovascular invasion (LVI)
5. Multiple positive lymph nodes
6. Close surgical margins (<5 mm)

#### Technical Points
- IMRT is the standard technique
- Primary site dose: 60–66 Gy (2 Gy/fraction) for positive margin/gross residual; 54–60 Gy for R0 with high-risk features
- Elective neck dose: 50–54 Gy
- Treatment starts within 6 weeks of surgery
- **Concurrent cisplatin** 100 mg/m² every 3 weeks for ENE or R+ margins

### Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma — Specific Management Principles

*(Cross-reference: see [[Answers/Salivary Gland/Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma and Perineural Invasion|ACC and Perineural Invasion]])*

- Wide radical resection is the cornerstone; margins must be generous given skip perineural spread
- **Elective neck dissection is controversial** — ACC has a low rate of nodal metastasis (10–15%); however, for high-grade (solid pattern) T3–T4 disease, elective ND is recommended
- Adjuvant RT: high-dose (≥66 Gy) to the primary site tracking named nerves; reduce local recurrence
- Long-term follow-up mandatory: late pulmonary metastases occur >10 years after primary treatment

---

### Carcinoma Ex Pleomorphic Adenoma (CXPA)

- Malignant transformation in a pre-existing PA (incidence: 3–5% of PAs; higher with recurrent PA and long history)
- **Three subtypes:**
  - **Intracapsular** — carcinoma confined within the capsule of the PA
  - **Minimally invasive** — carcinoma extends ≤1.5 mm beyond the capsule
  - **Widely invasive** — carcinoma extends >1.5 mm beyond capsule (this is the aggressive form)
- Clinical clue: rapid growth in a previously stable parotid mass; facial nerve involvement; pain
- Treatment: total parotidectomy ± nerve sacrifice; neck dissection; adjuvant RT

---

### Salivary Duct Carcinoma (SDC)

- Aggressive malignancy resembling high-grade ductal carcinoma of the breast
- Predominantly parotid; typically males >60 years
- **Molecular:** HER2 amplification (25–40%), AR positivity (>70%), TP53, PTEN loss
- **Targeted therapy:** Trastuzumab/pertuzumab for HER2+; androgen deprivation (enzalutamide, bicalutamide) for AR+ metastatic disease
- Prognosis: 5-year survival ~30%; early distant metastasis (lung, bone, liver)
- Treatment: Total parotidectomy + comprehensive neck dissection + adjuvant CRT

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Malignant parotid tumours are classified per WHO 2022 into a broad range of entities with MEC being the most common overall and ACC the most common in minor salivary glands. MEC grade — determined by either the AFIP or Brandwein scoring system — drives surgical extent, neck management, and adjuvant decisions. High-grade MEC with temporal bone involvement requires en bloc temporal bone resection combined with total parotidectomy, comprehensive neck dissection, and mandatory postoperative chemoradiation. The AJCC 8th edition TNM staging now incorporates extranodal extension into N staging, which is the most important driver of the decision to add concurrent chemotherapy to adjuvant radiation. Facial nerve resection is mandated by preoperative palsy, direct tumour invasion, or inability to achieve adequate clearance without sacrifice — a functioning nerve adjacent to but not invaded by tumour should be preserved.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. MEC is the most common malignant salivary gland tumour (all glands combined); Warthin tumour is the most common bilateral salivary tumour.
> 2. CRTC1-MAML2 translocation in MEC is associated with better prognosis; its absence in high-grade MEC predicts aggressive behaviour.
> 3. The Brandwein system includes bony invasion and LVI as point-scored features, while the AFIP system does not — the Brandwein system therefore classifies more tumours as high-grade.
> 4. T4b disease (skull base/pterygoid/carotid encasement) is unresectable by definition and represents a contraindication to radical surgery without demonstrable benefit.
> 5. Elective neck dissection is mandatory for high-grade MEC, SDC, CXPA, and T3–T4 tumours regardless of clinical N status; nodal occult metastasis rates exceed 20% in these categories.
> 6. Facial nerve sacrifice is absolutely indicated for preoperative palsy attributable to the primary tumour — intraoperative nerve monitoring does not change this decision.
> 7. Lateral temporal bone resection removes the EAC as a sleeve and is the minimum surgery for parotid tumour with EAC involvement; inadequate temporal bone resection is the most common cause of local failure.
> 8. RTOG 9501/EORTC 22931 criteria (positive margins, ENE) for concurrent cisplatin are universally extrapolated to high-grade salivary gland malignancies despite these trials being designed for squamous cell carcinoma.
> 9. Salivary duct carcinoma (SDC) is HER2-amplified in ~35% of cases and AR-positive in >70% — immunohistochemistry for HER2 and AR should be performed on all SDC specimens to guide systemic therapy in the metastatic setting.
> 10. CXPA outcome depends on invasiveness: intracapsular and minimally invasive variants behave nearly identically to benign PA; widely invasive CXPA has the prognosis of a high-grade carcinoma.
