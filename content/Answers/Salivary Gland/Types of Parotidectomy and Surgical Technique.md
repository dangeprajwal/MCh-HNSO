> [!cite] Landmark Articles
> McGurk, Thomas, and Renehan (2003) published the landmark comparative series of 821 patients treated over 40 years in the British Journal of Cancer, demonstrating that extracapsular dissection (ECD) for clinically benign parotid lumps carries equivalent oncological safety to superficial parotidectomy, with the specific finding that even among the small subset of cases unexpectedly found to harbour occult malignancy, morbidity from ECD was substantially lower without compromising cure rates. This study effectively shifted the debate from "which operation preserves the nerve best?" to "which operation achieves margin-negative resection with least morbidity?" and underpins current evidence for tailoring the extent of parotidectomy to the tumour rather than applying the same operation to all patients.
>
> Guntinas-Lichius et al. (2006) analysed 963 parotidectomies at a university teaching hospital in Laryngoscope, reporting permanent facial nerve dysfunction in 6% after total parotidectomy versus significantly lower rates after lateral parotidectomy. In malignant disease, the same group (2004) described outcomes of primary parotid malignoma surgery in patients with preoperatively normal facial nerve function, demonstrating that standardised total parotidectomy with microsurgical nerve preservation achieved acceptable long-term nerve function even in high-grade histology — establishing the principle that nerve preservation should be attempted whenever the nerve is macroscopically uninvolved. Sood et al. (2015) published a systematic review and meta-analysis of facial nerve monitoring during parotidectomy in Otolaryngology–Head and Neck Surgery, confirming that continuous intraoperative EMG monitoring significantly reduces both temporary and permanent facial palsy rates.

## Types of Parotidectomy and Oncological Efficacy

### Classification of Parotidectomy

The European Salivary Gland Society (ESGS) recommends classifying parotidectomy by **anatomical extent** rather than arbitrary designations, as the same label ("superficial parotidectomy") can describe very different operations depending on the extent of nerve dissection:

| Type | Definition | Facial Nerve | Indication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Extracapsular dissection (ECD)** | Tumour removed with a thin rim of normal parotid, no formal nerve dissection | Preserved (not formally dissected) | Benign tumours <3 cm, superficial location, experienced hands |
| **Partial superficial parotidectomy** | Removal of parotid tissue deep to tumour in one lobe, limited nerve branch dissection | Relevant branches identified and preserved | Benign tumours confined to one segment of superficial lobe |
| **Superficial (lateral) parotidectomy** | All parotid tissue lateral to facial nerve plane; formal nerve dissection from trunk to all branches | All branches preserved | Benign superficial lobe tumours, low-grade malignancy confined to superficial lobe |
| **Total conservative parotidectomy (TCP)** | All parotid tissue including deep lobe with formal nerve dissection; nerve preserved | All branches preserved | Deep lobe tumours, low/intermediate-grade malignancy involving both lobes, tumours abutting but not invading nerve |
| **Total parotidectomy with nerve sacrifice** | As above but nerve sacrificed — may be selective (one branch) or total | Nerve or branch sacrificed | High-grade malignancy with gross nerve invasion, PNI, preoperative palsy |
| **Radical parotidectomy** | Total parotidectomy + skin ± bone ± total nerve sacrifice ± neck dissection | Sacrificed entirely | T4 disease, skin invasion, bone involvement |

**Oncological Principle (Shah, 5th ed.):** The goal of parotid surgery for malignancy is **negative margins**. The type of parotidectomy is a consequence of where the tumour is and what is required to achieve clear margins — not a primary determinant of oncological outcome independent of margins.

### Oncological Efficacy Data

#### Extent of Surgery and Margins

| Parameter | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Margin status | Most important determinant of local control and survival; positive margins significantly worsen outcomes regardless of operation type |
| Superficial vs total parotidectomy for malignancy | No randomised trial exists; retrospective data (Spiro, Rapidly, 1986) demonstrate equivalent local control when margins are clear regardless of parotidectomy type |
| Deep lobe involvement | Tumours extending to deep lobe require total conservative parotidectomy; omitting deep lobe removal when involved leads to higher recurrence |
| ECD for benign disease | Recurrence rate of pleomorphic adenoma after ECD: ~2–3% (McGurk 2003), equivalent to superficial parotidectomy (~2–5%) when performed by experienced surgeons with adequate rim of tissue |

#### Mucoepidermoid Carcinoma (MEC): Adequate Parotidectomy

Low/intermediate-grade MEC (AJCC Grade 1–2) represents the most common primary salivary malignancy:

| Grade | Surgery of Primary | Nodal Management | Adjuvant Therapy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low-grade MEC (Grade 1)** | Superficial parotidectomy with clear margins if superficial lobe; total conservative parotidectomy if deep lobe involved | Elective neck dissection (Levels I–III) debated for cN0 T1-T2; most centres perform selective ND Level IIa–III | Generally not required if margins clear, pN0, no adverse features |
| **Intermediate-grade MEC (Grade 2)** | Total conservative parotidectomy (nerve preservation if uninvolved grossly) | Ipsilateral selective neck dissection Levels I–IV | Adjuvant RT recommended for T3–T4, close/positive margins, PNI, LVI, nodal disease |
| **High-grade MEC (Grade 3)** | Total parotidectomy ± nerve sacrifice if invaded; radical if required | Comprehensive neck dissection Levels I–V | Adjuvant RT ± concurrent cisplatin |

**Key principle for MCh exam:** For low/intermediate-grade MEC, **total conservative parotidectomy is the adequate operation** if the tumour is in or approaches the deep lobe or if there is uncertainty about margins with superficial parotidectomy alone. The facial nerve is preserved unless it is macroscopically invaded or encased.

### Operative Procedure: Total Conservative Parotidectomy

#### Patient Preparation and Anaesthesia
- General anaesthesia, nasoendotracheal intubation (keep orotracheal tube away from operative field)
- Facial nerve monitoring electrodes placed in orbicularis oculi, orbicularis oris, and mentalis prior to draping
- Head ring, 15–20° head-up position, slight neck extension and contralateral rotation
- Skin preparation: ear canal packed with cotton; operative field including ipsilateral face and neck
- Neuromuscular blockade avoided after induction (permits nerve monitoring response)

#### Incision
**Modified Blair incision** (standard):
1. Begins in the pre-auricular crease at the level of the superior helix
2. Curves around the lobule in the post-auricular sulcus
3. Runs posteroinferiorly along or just below the hairline behind the ear
4. Turns downward anterior to the sternocleidomastoid, ending 2–3 cm below the mandibular angle

**Modifications:**
- **Modified facelift (rhytidectomy) incision:** Pre-auricular crease hidden within hair-bearing scalp; superior to standard incision cosmetically; allows excellent exposure; increasingly preferred
- **Lazy-S or Y-incision:** Less favoured; poorer cosmesis
- Cervical component can be extended for simultaneous neck dissection

#### Skin Flap Elevation
- Anterior flap raised in a plane just deep to the subcutaneous fat, superficial to the parotid fascia
- Posteriorly, flap raised off the cartilaginous EAC and mastoid periosteum
- The **greater auricular nerve** (C2,3) crosses the posterior surface of SCM approximately 6.5 cm below the tragus (McKinney's point) — sacrifice is almost inevitable with standard incision; posterior branch may sometimes be preserved; counsel patients preoperatively regarding sensory loss over lower pinna and pre-auricular cheek
- Flap is secured with skin hooks; haemostasis with bipolar forceps

#### Identification of the Facial Nerve
Antegrade approach (trunk-first) is preferred in primary cases:

1. **Tragal pointer:** Dissect posteroinferiorly along the anterior border of the cartilaginous EAC. The pointer — the most inferior projection of the tragal cartilage — points toward the main trunk lying 1–1.5 cm deep and inferiorly
2. **Tympanomastoid suture:** Elevate periosteum along the anterior surface of the mastoid; the suture lies at the junction of tympanic and mastoid bones; the nerve exits 6–8 mm deep to its inferior end
3. **Posterior belly of digastric:** Identified by following the SCM to the mastoid; nerve lies just superior to the superior border of the digastric at its mastoid insertion
4. Nerve stimulator (Prass probe at 0.3–0.5 mA): Confirm the structure is the nerve before incision or traction
5. Formal incision of parotid tissue directly over the identified nerve trunk

#### Superficial Lobe Removal
- Once the main trunk is identified, dissection proceeds **antegrade** — following the nerve forward to its bifurcation and then each major branch
- Parotid tissue is separated from the nerve using blunt dissection (mosquito forceps, fine scissors, nerve hook) and gentle bipolar haemostasis
- The nerve is followed to all five peripheral branches (temporal, zygomatic, buccal, marginal mandibular, cervical)
- Superficial lobe delivered with the tumour as a single specimen
- Ensure Stensen's duct is ligated anteriorly

#### Deep Lobe Removal (Total Conservative Parotidectomy)
After superficial lobe removal, the facial nerve branches are now dissected free and retracted upward/anteriorly:

1. The **stylomandibular ligament** is identified and divided — this opens the stylomandibular tunnel and allows access to the deep lobe
2. The deep lobe is delivered medially and anteriorly with careful blunt dissection around the retromandibular vein and external carotid artery
3. The ECA may need to be ligated below the gland if the deep lobe is large
4. Deep lobe PA characteristically "pops" into the wound once the stylomandibular tunnel is opened
5. For deep lobe tumours extending into PPS: combined transcervical-transparotid approach may be required; a cervical skin crease incision is added to allow inferior access and ICA control

#### Wound Closure
- Meticulous haemostasis; suction drain (Redivac or Jackson-Pratt) placed in parotid bed
- SMAS layer closed over the defect to reduce Frey's syndrome risk and improve cosmesis
- SCM flap rotation or dermis-fat graft may be interposed to prevent Frey's and fill retromandibular depression
- Skin closed in layers (deep dermal absorbable + subcuticular or interrupted nylon)
- Pressure dressing applied for 24–48 hours

#### Post-Operative Care
- Drain removed when output <20–30 mL/24h (typically day 2)
- Document House-Brackmann grade daily in immediate post-operative period
- Low-residue diet for 48h to minimise salivary stimulation
- Physiotherapy for eye care if temporary facial nerve weakness
- Histopathological review at 2 weeks to guide adjuvant therapy decision

### Indications for Facial Nerve Resection

The facial nerve should be **preserved** whenever technically possible, even in malignant disease, unless one or more of the following absolute indications is met:

#### Absolute Indications for Sacrifice
| Indication | Clinical Scenario |
|---|---|
| **Preoperative facial nerve palsy** | Establishes nerve is already invaded; sacrifice and reconstruction are planned |
| **Gross nerve invasion at surgery** | Nerve passes through tumour; macroscopic perineural infiltration visible |
| **Nerve inseparable from tumour** | Dense adhesion with no dissection plane — not merely close proximity |
| **Histologically confirmed intrapendicular invasion** | Frozen section of nerve margin confirms cancer |

#### Relative Indications (Discussed Preoperatively)
| Situation | Approach |
|---|---|
| High-grade malignancy (undifferentiated carcinoma, high-grade MEC, salivary duct carcinoma) closely applied to nerve | Attempt nerve-sparing; harvest nerve margins for frozen section; sacrifice if positive |
| Extensive perineural invasion on imaging or clinical features | Nerve-sparing with wide clearance and postoperative RT |
| Recurrent parotid malignancy | Higher threshold for sacrifice; requires multidisciplinary discussion |

#### Reconstruction After Nerve Sacrifice
| Defect | Reconstruction |
|---|---|
| Single branch | Direct repair or cable graft (sural, greater auricular) |
| Main trunk to one division | Sural nerve cable graft (interpositional) |
| Total nerve sacrifice | Hypoglossal-facial anastomosis; masseteric nerve transfer; free gracilis dynamic reconstruction |
| Static support | Gold weight (upper lid), canthoplasty, lower lid shortening, brow lift, fascia lata sling |

**Hypoglossal-facial jump anastomosis** (May's modification): anastomoses a 30% partial division of CN XII to the main trunk of CN VII — preserves tongue function while providing reinnervation; preferred when proximal CN VII stump is unavailable.

### Nerve Monitoring

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Modality | Continuous EMG recording from orbicularis oculi, orbicularis oris, mentalis, and frontalis |
| Stimulation threshold | 0.3–0.5 mA confirms nerve identity; >2 mA threshold at end of case is concerning for significant traction injury |
| Prognostic value | Electromyographic silence at end-of-case correlates with higher rates of temporary palsy; preserved responses correlate with return of function |
| Evidence base | Sood et al. (2015) meta-analysis: monitoring significantly reduced temporary palsy (OR 0.44) and was associated with trend toward reduced permanent palsy |
| Limitations | Does not prevent injury from sharp dissection; cannot prevent thermal injury from electrosurgery; false negative if muscle relaxants not reversed |

### Adjuvant Therapy Indications Post-Parotidectomy

Adjuvant radiotherapy (PORT) is recommended when one or more of the following is present:

| Indication | Evidence Level |
|---|---|
| T3 or T4 primary disease | Standard (Grade A) |
| High-grade histology (undifferentiated, high-grade MEC, salivary duct carcinoma, carcinoma ex PA) | Standard (Grade A) |
| Close margins (<5 mm) or positive margins | Standard (Grade A) |
| Perineural invasion (PNI) | Standard (Grade B) — widen fields to cover entire nerve course to skull base |
| Lymphovascular invasion (LVI) | Standard (Grade B) |
| Extranodal extension (ENE) | Standard (Grade A) — consider concurrent systemic therapy |
| Node-positive disease (pN+) | PORT to bilateral neck or at least ipsilateral neck + drainage basin |
| Bone or skin invasion | PORT to resection bed ± adjacent structures |

**Concurrent chemotherapy** is added for:
- Positive/close margins not amenable to re-resection
- ENE
- High-grade histology with nodal disease

**Radiation technique:**
- IMRT preferred to spare contralateral major salivary glands and cochlea
- Dose: 60–66 Gy in 2 Gy/fraction to resection bed; 54–60 Gy to regional nodes
- PNI: fields include the entire affected nerve to the skull base entry point

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Parotidectomy is classified by anatomical extent, not arbitrary designation: extracapsular dissection, partial superficial, superficial (lateral), total conservative, total with nerve sacrifice, and radical. The oncological principle is negative margins — the type of operation is determined by what achieves this with least morbidity. For low/intermediate-grade MEC, total conservative parotidectomy with facial nerve preservation is adequate provided margins are clear. Facial nerve sacrifice is justified only for preoperative palsy, gross macroscopic invasion, or a nerve inseparable from tumour — not merely for high-grade histology alone when the nerve is grossly uninvolved. Adjuvant radiotherapy is indicated for T3–T4 disease, high-grade histology, close/positive margins, PNI, LVI, and ENE, with concurrent chemotherapy added for ENE or unresectable positive margins.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The European Salivary Gland Society classification defines parotidectomy by anatomical extent (number of lobes and nerve dissection status) — this is more clinically meaningful than traditional "superficial/total" labels.
> 2. Margin-negative resection is the single most important determinant of local control in parotid malignancy, overriding the type of parotidectomy performed.
> 3. Total conservative parotidectomy with facial nerve preservation is the operation of choice for deep lobe tumours and low/intermediate-grade malignancies — it achieves total gland removal without sacrificing an uninvolved nerve.
> 4. Avoid neuromuscular blockade after induction — it abolishes the EMG monitoring signal; relay this to the anaesthesiologist during pre-operative briefing.
> 5. The modified facelift incision gives equivalent exposure to the Blair incision with superior cosmetic results — increasingly preferred at high-volume centres.
> 6. The stylomandibular ligament must be divided to deliver the deep lobe — failure to divide it is the commonest cause of tumour rupture during deep lobe parotidectomy.
> 7. Facial nerve sacrifice is mandated by preoperative palsy (already invaded), gross nerve passage through tumour, or inseparable adhesion — not by histology or grade alone.
> 8. After total nerve sacrifice, the masseteric nerve transfer (CN V3 to CN VII main trunk) is increasingly preferred for immediate reinnervation — shorter distance, reliable motor input, faster recovery.
> 9. Adjuvant radiotherapy fields for PNI must cover the entire nerve course to skull base — treating only the parotid bed results in marginal failure along the nerve.
> 10. A post-parotidectomy EMG stimulation threshold of >2 mA at the end of the case (versus 0.3 mA at identification) signals significant traction neuropraxia — counsel patient about temporary weakness and arrange early physiotherapy.
