> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Perzin et al. (1978) published the foundational clinicopathologic study of 80 adenoid cystic carcinomas arising in salivary glands, correlating histological pattern (cribriform, tubular, solid) with clinical course, and demonstrating that the solid pattern was associated with significantly worse survival and earlier distant metastasis — this grading scheme remains the standard Perzin/Szanto grading used in clinical practice today. Persson et al. (2009) made the pivotal discovery of the recurrent MYB-NFIB fusion oncogene in adenoid cystic carcinomas of the breast and head and neck using whole-genome sequencing, demonstrating that the t(6;9)(q22–23;p23–24) translocation fusing MYB exon 14 to the last coding exon(s) of NFIB drives ACC pathogenesis in the majority of cases. Garden et al. (1995) examined the influence of surgical margin status and perineural invasion in ACC of the head and neck treated with surgery and radiation, establishing that positive margins and nerve invasion adversely affected locoregional control and arguing for high-dose adjuvant radiotherapy — fundamental data underpinning current combined-modality management. Balamucki, Mendenhall et al. (2012) reported on 120 patients with ACC treated from 1966–2008, confirming that surgery with adjuvant RT offered the best locoregional control, elective nodal irradiation was appropriate in lymphatic-rich sites, and that some unresectable ACC patients could be cured with RT alone. Moskaluk (2013) provided a comprehensive review of the clinical and molecular features of ACC, synthesising the molecular landscape including MYB-NFIB (present in ~85% of ACC by FISH), NOTCH1 mutations (predominantly in solid variant), CDKN2A loss, and emerging therapeutic targets.

## Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma and Perineural Invasion

### Epidemiology and Incidence

- ACC constitutes approximately **10–15% of all salivary gland malignancies** (second or third most common malignant salivary tumour depending on the series)
- **Most common malignant tumour of minor salivary glands** (particularly palate, base of tongue, sinonasal tract) — responsible for ~30% of minor salivary gland malignancies
- Most common malignant tumour of the **submandibular gland** (40% of malignant submandibular tumours) and **sublingual gland**
- In the parotid, it ranks behind MEC; 15–20% of malignant parotid tumours
- **Age:** Peak incidence 4th–6th decade; no strong sex predilection (slight female predominance)
- **Sites (in order):** Parotid > submandibular > minor salivary (palate most common minor site) > sublingual

---

### Pathology

#### Gross Appearance
- Firm, unencapsulated or poorly encapsulated grey-white tumour
- Infiltrative borders; often larger than clinical appearance suggests (iceberg phenomenon)
- Cut surface: homogeneous white-grey; may show small cystic spaces (cribriform pattern)

#### Histological Patterns (Perzin/Szanto Grading)

| Pattern | Description | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Cribriform** (Grade 1) | Characteristic "Swiss cheese" or "cylinder" pattern; nests of cells with round pseudocystic spaces containing mucoid matrix | Best — 15-year DFS ~35% |
| **Tubular** (Grade 1) | Dual-layered tubular structures (inner ductal, outer myoepithelial cells) | Good (similar to cribriform) |
| **Solid** (Grade 3) | Sheets of basaloid cells with minimal luminal differentiation; >30% solid component in high-grade | Worst — 15-year DFS <10%; higher rate of early distant metastasis |

> **The Perzin grading rule:** High-grade ACC = solid pattern comprising >30% of the tumour. Any solid component >30% upgrades the tumour to Grade III regardless of cribriform/tubular areas.

#### Cytological Features
- **Ductal cells:** small, darkly staining, forming inner layer of tubules or lining of pseudocysts
- **Myoepithelial cells:** larger, pale-staining, angular; form outer layer; positive for SMA, calponin, S100, p63, CK5/6
- Pseudocysts: contain **basement membrane material** (laminin, collagen IV) — NOT true secretion
- Mitoses: generally sparse; necrosis uncommon except in solid variant

#### Immunohistochemistry
- **CK7+, EMA+** (ductal cells)
- **SMA+, S100+, p63+** (myoepithelial cells)
- **c-Kit (CD117)+** in >90% of ACC — important diagnostic marker; also mechanistically linked to perineural invasion
- **Ki67:** low in cribriform; higher in solid

---

### Molecular Pathology

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **MYB-NFIB fusion** | t(6;9)(q22–23;p23–24); present in ~50–85% of ACC by FISH; activates MYB transcription factor target genes (cell cycle, apoptosis) |
| **MYB amplification** | Present in some MYB-NFIB–negative cases; alternative mechanism of MYB overactivation |
| **MYBL1-NFIB** | Rarer alternative fusion involving MYBL1 (MYB family) |
| **NOTCH1 mutations** | Primarily in solid variant; gain-of-function; associated with high grade and worse outcome; targetable with gamma-secretase inhibitors |
| **CDKN2A/CDKN2B loss** | Seen in high-grade transformation |
| **FGFR, EGFR** | Overexpressed but rarely amplified; limited targeted therapy responses |
| **c-Kit overexpression** | Very high (>90%) but KIT mutations are rare; imatinib disappointing in clinical trials |

---

### Clinical Behaviour

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Growth pattern** | Characteristically slow but relentless; may be present for years before diagnosis |
| **Pain** | Common early symptom (perineural invasion); facial pain, toothache, ear pain, or paresthesia |
| **Facial nerve** | Palsy in parotid ACC if facial nerve involved |
| **Late recurrence** | Hallmark of ACC — local and distant recurrences >10 years after primary treatment are well documented; follow-up for life mandatory |
| **Distant metastasis** | Lung (most common) — "cannonball" metastases; bone (second); liver; brain (late) |
| **Nodal metastasis** | Relatively uncommon — 10–15% incidence; lymph node involvement historically underestimated |
| **Skip lesions** | Microscopically tumour-free segments along a nerve between points of perineural involvement — explains inadequate margins at frozen section despite apparent clearance |
| **Prognosis** | 5-year survival 60–80%; 10-year survival 40%; 15-year survival <30%; solid pattern 5-year survival ~50% |

### Perineural Invasion in Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma

Perineural invasion (PNI) is the **defining biological behaviour** of ACC. It is present in **50–90% of ACC cases**, giving ACC the **highest rate of clinically significant PNI of any head and neck tumour**.

#### Definition and Distinction
- **Perineural invasion (PNI):** tumour cells within any layer of the nerve sheath (epineurium, perineurium, endoneurium) or in the perineural space
- **Perineural spread (PNS):** macroscopic involvement of a named nerve with spread along its course — detectable clinically or radiologically
- These are distinct: PNI is a histological finding; PNS is a clinical/radiological phenomenon (though they are causally related)

#### Incidence and Significance in ACC

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Histological PNI | 50–90% of ACC cases |
| Named nerve PNS | ~30–40% (clinically or radiologically detectable) |
| Comparison with other tumours | SCC: 5–15% PNI; MEC: 10–20% PNI; ACC: 50–90% PNI — highest of all |
| Impact on prognosis | PNI doubles the risk of local recurrence; PNS to skull base is associated with dismal locoregional control |

#### Molecular Mechanisms of Perineural Invasion in ACC

ACC cells demonstrate an unusual molecular affinity for neural tissue, mediated by multiple mechanisms:

1. **Neural Cell Adhesion Molecule (NCAM/NCAM1) overexpression:**
   - ACC cells express high levels of N-CAM (CD56), which mediates homophilic binding to the perineural space
   - NCAM promotes invasion through basement membrane components of the nerve sheath

2. **c-Kit (CD117) — Stem Cell Factor (SCF) pathway:**
   - c-Kit is expressed in >90% of ACC cells; SCF is expressed in Schwann cells and endoneurial cells
   - This axis acts as a chemoattractant: ACC cells migrate towards SCF-secreting neural tissue
   - c-Kit inhibition (imatinib) did not improve outcomes in clinical trials, suggesting the signalling pathway is more complex than receptor tyrosine kinase activation alone

3. **Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) / TrkB:**
   - ACC cells express TrkB; BDNF is secreted by neural tissues; this paracrine loop promotes neural tropism

4. **Glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) / RET:**
   - GDNF–RET signalling promotes migration along nerves in ACC

5. **MMP (matrix metalloproteinase) upregulation:**
   - MMP-2 and MMP-9 facilitate basement membrane degradation around nerve sheaths, enabling ACC cell invasion into the perineural space

6. **Myoepithelial cell phenotype:**
   - The myoepithelial component of ACC shares surface molecules (SMA, calponin) with Schwann cells, potentially enabling intercellular adhesion

#### Named Nerve Involvement by Site

| Primary Site | Named Nerves at Risk |
|---|---|
| **Parotid ACC** | Facial nerve (CNVII) — main trunk, pes anserinus branches |
| **Submandibular ACC** | Lingual nerve (CN Vc via submandibular ganglion), inferior alveolar nerve, hypoglossal nerve (CNXII) |
| **Minor salivary (palate)** | Greater palatine nerve, lesser palatine nerve; tracking to pterygopalatine fossa (PPF) → CN V2 (infraorbital nerve → foramen rotundum → Meckel's cave) |
| **Sinonasal ACC** | Infraorbital nerve (CN V2), ethmoid nerves, olfactory |
| **Lacrimal gland ACC** | Lacrimal nerve (CN V1), Zygomaticotemporal nerve |
| **Sublingual ACC** | Lingual nerve, submandibular ganglion |

#### Patterns of Perineural Spread

| Pattern | Description |
|---|---|
| **Antegrade** | Spread distally (away from brain) — towards periphery |
| **Retrograde** | Spread proximally (towards skull base and intracranial) — the more dangerous pattern |
| **Skip lesions** | Isolated foci of tumour on a nerve separated by grossly normal-appearing nerve — explains why frozen section margins may be falsely negative |
| **Centripetal spread** | Spread through nerve fascicles towards the CNS |

> **Clinical consequence of skip lesions:** A frozen section margin reported as negative from the cut end of the facial nerve does NOT exclude residual tumour microscopically within the specimen or along the intratemporal nerve segment. Paraffin-section permanent margins on serial cross-sections are required.

#### Clinical Features of Perineural Invasion

| Symptom | Nerve Involved |
|---|---|
| **Facial pain, numbness** (V distribution) | CN V branches |
| **Facial muscle weakness or palsy** | CN VII |
| **Toothache, jaw numbness** | Inferior alveolar nerve |
| **Trismus** | Masticator space involvement, pterygoid nerve |
| **Tongue numbness, weakness** | Lingual nerve, CNXII |
| **Diplopia** | CN III, IV, VI (intracavernous spread) |
| **Proptosis** | Orbital apex involvement |
| **Horner's syndrome** | Pericarotid sympathetic plexus |

#### Imaging of Perineural Involvement

**MRI with gadolinium is the investigation of choice:**

| MRI Feature | Significance |
|---|---|
| **Nerve enlargement** | Compared to contralateral side; first sign |
| **Perineural enhancement** | T1 fat-sat post-Gd; thickening + enhancement of named nerve |
| **Foraminal enlargement** | Widening of foramen ovale, rotundum, stylomastoid foramen; best seen on CT |
| **Fat obliteration in pterygopalatine fossa (PPF)** | T1 fat signal lost; replaced by soft tissue; indicates V2/PPF involvement |
| **Cavernous sinus extension** | T1 enhancement, filling of cavernous sinus; indicates intracranial spread |
| **Perineural tumour in Meckel's cave** | Extension to trigeminal ganglion |
| **Enhancement of skull base foramina** | Foramen rotundum, ovale, spinosum |

> **Key teaching point:** Absence of MRI evidence of PNS does not exclude histological PNI. MRI sensitivity for microscopic perineural invasion is poor. MRI is used to detect *macroscopic* perineural spread.

### Management of Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma

#### Surgical Principles

1. **Wide radical excision with generous margins** is the goal — this is ACC's primary treatment
2. Resection should track along named nerves proximally to achieve clear margins
3. **En bloc resection of at-risk nerves** even if clinically functional (see below)
4. Skull base access procedures (transmandibular, infratemporal fossa approaches) for parapharyngeal and skull base extension

#### Facial Nerve Management

| Scenario | Decision |
|---|---|
| **Preoperative facial nerve palsy** | Nerve invaded/non-functional: mandatory sacrifice |
| **Functional nerve, radiological PNS** | Sacrifice to obtain negative margin; plan immediate reinnervation |
| **Functional nerve, no MRI evidence of PNS, histological PNI** | Sacrifice debated; current evidence favours sacrifice if the nerve passes through or adjacent to the primary; permanent sections should be reviewed |
| **Frozen section margins on nerve stump** | Proceed to skull base if positive; note the skip lesion problem — negative frozen section does not guarantee negative paraffin margins |

#### Neck Dissection

- **Elective neck dissection is controversial in ACC** due to the historically low rate of nodal metastasis (10–15%)
- **Indications for elective ND in ACC:**
  - High-grade (solid) ACC
  - T3–T4 tumours
  - Submandibular primary (higher nodal risk)
  - Sinonasal ACC (retropharyngeal and Level II involvement)
- **Therapeutic ND** for cN+ disease (selective or comprehensive based on extent)
- Most series from Shah JP support **elective ND for high-grade ACC and T3–T4 primaries**

#### Adjuvant Radiotherapy

ACC is considered **relatively radioresistant to conventional photon RT** compared to SCC — this is a relative, not absolute resistance; high-dose RT reduces local recurrence significantly.

| RT Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Dose** | ≥66 Gy to primary tumour bed and tracked nerve course |
| **Perineural nerve track irradiation** | Essential: include proximal nerve pathway to skull base foramen (e.g., foramen ovale for V3, internal auditory canal for VII) within target volume |
| **Elective nodal RT** | Ipsilateral nodal levels II–IV; level Ib if submandibular primary |
| **Technique** | IMRT preferred; proton therapy emerging for parotid/skull base ACC |

**Neutron beam RT:**
- Fast neutron therapy has historically demonstrated improved locoregional control for ACC over photon RT in retrospective series
- Biological basis: neutrons are high-LET radiation; effective against hypoxic, slowly proliferating tumours (ACC cell kinetics)
- Limited availability worldwide; not considered standard of care due to logistical constraints and toxicity profile
- Garden et al. (IJROBP 1995) and subsequent Seattle data established neutron superiority in locoregional control

**Proton therapy:**
- Emerging evidence; dosimetric advantage for skull base ACC; reduces exit dose to critical structures (brainstem, optic nerves, cochlea)
- Increasingly used in dedicated proton centres

#### Surveillance and Follow-Up

| Time Point | Investigation |
|---|---|
| 3-monthly for 2 years | Clinical examination, endoscopy |
| 6-monthly years 2–5 | Clinical + MRI head/neck |
| Annual lifelong | Clinical + CT chest (pulmonary metastasis surveillance) |
| As indicated | PET-CT for suspected distant metastasis |

> **Annual CT chest is mandatory for life** in ACC patients — pulmonary metastases are the most common distant failure, often appearing 7–15 years after primary treatment. Early pulmonary metastasectomy may be beneficial in selected patients.

#### Systemic Therapy for Recurrent/Metastatic ACC

| Agent | Class | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Lenvatinib | VEGFR + multi-kinase inhibitor | ORR ~12–16%; disease stabilisation; approved in some guidelines |
| Axitinib | VEGFR1/2/3 inhibitor | Phase II; modest activity |
| Regorafenib | Multi-kinase inhibitor | Phase II; disease stabilisation |
| Imatinib | c-Kit/PDGFR inhibitor | Clinical trials disappointing; no significant benefit |
| Nivolumab/pembrolizumab | PD-1 inhibitor | Very limited activity; ACC is an "immune cold" tumour |
| Brentuximab vedotin | Anti-CD30 ADC | Not applicable |
| Entrectinib/larotrectinib | TRK inhibitors | Relevant if ETV6-NTRK3 fusion present (secretory carcinoma misclassified as ACC) |
| Olaparib | PARP inhibitor | Investigation for BRCA-mutant ACC |

> **Key clinical point:** There is no established systemic therapy with meaningful response rates for metastatic ACC. Patients with slowly progressing asymptomatic pulmonary metastases may be observed (watch-and-wait) rather than treated immediately. Enrolment in clinical trials is encouraged.

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Adenoid cystic carcinoma is the most common malignant tumour of minor salivary glands and submandibular gland, characterised by cribriform, tubular, and solid histological patterns — the solid pattern (>30%) predicts poor prognosis. The hallmark biological behaviour is perineural invasion, occurring in 50–90% of cases, mediated by NCAM, c-Kit/SCF, and BDNF/TrkB pathways that create neural tropism unique among salivary malignancies. Skip lesions along nerves explain why frozen section margins may be falsely negative. Management requires wide surgical excision tracking named nerves, facial nerve sacrifice when involved, and high-dose adjuvant radiotherapy (≥66 Gy) extending along perineural pathways to the skull base foramen. Lifelong annual CT chest surveillance is mandatory given the characteristic late pulmonary metastases, which may appear more than a decade after apparently curative surgery.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. ACC has the highest rate of perineural invasion of any head and neck tumour (50–90%); skip lesions make histological margins unreliable, especially on frozen section.
> 2. The MYB-NFIB fusion (detected by FISH) is present in ~85% of ACC and is the molecular hallmark; NOTCH1 mutations characterise the aggressive solid variant.
> 3. c-Kit (CD117) is positive in >90% of ACC — a diagnostically useful immunomarker — but imatinib therapy has been ineffective in clinical trials.
> 4. Fat obliteration of the pterygopalatine fossa on T1 MRI is the key imaging sign of perineural spread along V2 from a palate or maxillary primary.
> 5. Annual CT chest surveillance is mandatory for life — pulmonary metastases are the most common distant failure, occurring up to 15 years after primary treatment.
> 6. Solid pattern ACC (>30% solid component) behaves aggressively: earlier distant metastasis, worse 5-year survival (<50%), and higher rate of NOTCH1 mutations.
> 7. A functioning facial nerve in ACC should still be sacrificed if MRI or intraoperative assessment demonstrates perineural spread; immediate reinnervation (masseteric nerve or hypoglossal transfer) should be planned at the time of resection.
> 8. Neutron beam RT is biologically superior for ACC (high-LET, targets slowly proliferating hypoxic cells) but its limited worldwide availability prevents it from being standard of care.
> 9. Elective neck dissection is debated in ACC due to low nodal metastasis rates (10–15%); it should be performed for high-grade (solid) tumours, T3–T4 disease, and submandibular primaries.
> 10. Late recurrence is the natural history of ACC — the patient who is disease-free at 5 years is NOT cured; recurrences beyond 10 years are well documented, mandating lifelong follow-up.
