> House and Brackmann (1985) published the universally adopted six-grade facial nerve grading system in Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery, providing a reproducible clinical scale from Grade I (normal) to Grade VI (no movement) that remains the standard for reporting facial nerve outcomes in parotid surgery and in all clinical trials of facial reanimation techniques. Klebuc (2011) reported outcomes for the masseter-to-facial nerve transfer in a series of facial paralysis patients, demonstrating reliable reinnervation by 4–6 months, recovery to House-Brackmann III in the majority, and spontaneous smiling in 40–60% — establishing this as the fastest and most reliable single-stage dynamic reanimation procedure for complete paralysis. Terzis and Konofaos (2008) published a comparative study of single-stage versus two-stage cross-face nerve grafting combined with free gracilis muscle transplantation for reanimation of the paralysed face, defining the two-stage Terzis technique that prioritises smile symmetry and spontaneity through the slower cross-facial pathway. Manktelow, Tomat, and Zuker (2006) reported smile reconstruction with free gracilis muscle innervated by the motor nerve to masseter in adults, demonstrating effective smile excursion with cerebral adaptation and confirming the masseteric nerve–innervated gracilis as the definitive single-stage option for complete long-term paralysis.

## Facial Nerve Reanimation after Parotidectomy

### House-Brackmann Grading System (1985)

The House-Brackmann (HB) scale is the universally accepted clinical grading system for facial nerve function:

| Grade | Description | Function | Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | Normal | 100% | Symmetric in all areas; normal function |
| **II** | Mild dysfunction | ~75% | Slight weakness on close inspection; complete eye closure with minimal effort; slight asymmetry of smile; no synkinesis |
| **III** | Moderate dysfunction | 50% | Obvious but not disfiguring difference; complete eye closure with effort; asymmetric smile with maximum effort; noticeable synkinesis |
| **IV** | Moderately severe | 25% | Obvious weakness and disfiguring asymmetry; incomplete eye closure; asymmetric smile with effort; severe synkinesis |
| **V** | Severe dysfunction | ~10% | Barely perceptible motion; incomplete eye closure; slight movement of corner of mouth; no synkinesis |
| **VI** | Total paralysis | 0% | No movement; flaccid paralysis |

> **HB VI = Complete facial paralysis** = the scenario following facial nerve sacrifice in parotid cancer surgery.

**FNGS 2.0 (Facial Nerve Grading System 2.0):** Updated system (Ross et al., 2013) assessing individual facial regions (brow, eye, nasolabial, oral) separately on 1–6 scale; more sensitive for partial deficits and synkinesis assessment but less widely used in clinical reporting than HB.

### Anatomy of the Facial Nerve Relevant to Parotidectomy

| Segment | Landmarks | Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Mastoid segment** | Vertical in mastoid, exits stylomastoid foramen | Entry point for nerve identification |
| **Main trunk** | 1–1.5 cm deep to tragal pointer (pointer of Conley); 1 cm inferior and anterior to mastoid tip | Primary identification landmark |
| **Pes anserinus** | Division into upper (temporofacial) and lower (cervicofacial) trunks | Determines extent of parotidectomy |
| **Peripheral branches** | Temporal, zygomatic, buccal, marginal mandibular, cervical | At risk during deep lobe or T4 resection |

**Nerve identification landmarks:**
1. Tragal pointer (tip of cartilaginous tragus) — main trunk lies 1 cm medial and inferior
2. Posterior belly of digastric — main trunk lies superior to its anterior edge at its attachment
3. Tympanomastoid suture — nerve exits 6–8 mm deep to its inferior end

### Sunderland Classification of Facial Nerve Injury

| Grade | Equivalent | Pathology | Recovery |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I (Neuropraxia)** | Sunderland I | Focal demyelination; axons intact; no Wallerian degeneration | Complete; days to weeks (median 6–8 weeks) |
| **II (Axonotmesis)** | Sunderland II | Axonal disruption; endoneurial tubes intact | Complete; slower; ~1 mm/day proximal to distal (months); Wallerian degeneration occurs |
| **III** | Sunderland III | Axonal + endoneurial tube disruption; perineurium intact | Incomplete; variable; aberrant regeneration and synkinesis |
| **IV** | Sunderland IV | Only epineurium intact | Poor spontaneous recovery; requires surgical repair |
| **V (Neurotmesis)** | Sunderland V | Complete nerve section | No recovery without surgical repair |

**In parotid cancer surgery:**
- **Neuropraxia:** thermal injury from bipolar/monopolar, traction injury, prolonged retraction — most common cause of temporary post-parotidectomy paresis
- **Axonotmesis:** more severe stretch or crush injury; recovery possible but slower
- **Neurotmesis:** deliberately sectioned nerve (radical parotidectomy) or severed accidentally

### Post-Parotidectomy Facial Nerve Paresis — Assessment and Timeline

#### Intraoperative Assessment
- **Nerve integrity monitor (NIM):** Electromyographic monitoring; loss of signal during dissection indicates nerve compromise; baseline and response thresholds compared
- Stimulation of main trunk at 0.5–1 mA at end of case: confirms integrity; absence of facial twitch → suspect neurotmesis/significant axonotmesis
- If nerve appears intact but stretched: **observe**; most will recover (neuropraxia/axonotmesis)

#### Post-Operative Recovery
- **Neuropraxia:** Recovery expected 6–12 weeks; complete recovery in >90%
- **Axonotmesis (Grade II–III):** Recovery 3–6 months; reinnervation at ~1 mm/day from site of injury; synkinesis common with Grade III
- **Critical decision point: 18 months**
  - By 18 months, motor end-plate reinnervation capacity diminishes significantly
  - Fibrosis of denervated mimetic muscle occurs; viable motor end plates cannot be reliably reinnervated beyond 24 months
  - Dynamic reanimation techniques requiring motor end-plate function must be initiated before this window closes

---

### Reanimation Strategy: Overview

The approach depends on:
1. **Cause of paralysis:** Nerve intact and recovering (conservative ± physiotherapy) vs. transected (immediate repair or reconstruction needed)
2. **Timing:** Acute (<1 week), early (1–18 months), or late (>18 months)
3. **Completeness:** Partial vs. complete (HB VI)
4. **Age and comorbidity of patient**
5. **Status of donor nerves and muscles**
6. **Priority:** Eye protection is the immediate first priority; smile is the definitive functional goal

---

### Eye Protection: Priority Management (Lagophthalmos)

**Lagophthalmos** (inability to close the eye) is the most urgent complication of facial nerve palsy — corneal exposure leads to keratopathy, ulceration, and permanent vision loss.

#### Immediate Measures
- Lubricating eye drops (artificial tears) hourly while awake
- Lubricating gel at night
- Moisture chamber glasses or protective eye tape at night
- Ophthalmology referral immediately if corneal exposure signs

#### Surgical Management of Lagophthalmos

| Procedure | Mechanism | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **Gold weight implantation (upper lid)** | Gravity-assisted lid closure; 1.2–1.6 g gold weight implanted in upper lid tarsus | Paralytic lagophthalmos; the gold standard static procedure for the upper lid |
| **Platinum chain implant** | Similar principle; more MRI-compatible; thinner profile | Alternative to gold weight |
| **Spring implant (palpebral spring)** | Mechanical spring attached to upper lid provides active closure force independent of gravity | Better in upright posture; technically more complex |
| **Lateral tarsorrhaphy** | Suturing lateral canthi together to reduce palpebral aperture | Temporary or permanent; simple; reduces exposure keratopathy |
| **Lower lid medial/lateral tightening** | Lateral tarsal strip or medial canthoplasty | Paralytic ectropion; prevents lower lid sag |

### Immediate Repair at Time of Nerve Resection

**When the facial nerve is deliberately resected (radical parotidectomy for cancer):**
- Immediate repair at the time of primary surgery is preferred over delayed repair
- Immediate repair preserves the distal facial nerve pathway and motor end plates

#### Epineural Repair (Primary Neurorrhaphy)
- **Gold standard when a tension-free coaptation is achievable**
- Required: clean-cut nerve ends, no tension, adequate length
- Technique: alignment of epineural fascicles; 8-0 or 9-0 nylon sutures; 2–4 sutures sufficient for main trunk
- **Results:** HB III recovery expected in ~60–70% of cases with immediate primary repair

#### Nerve Graft Interposition (when primary repair creates tension or gap >1 cm)
- Harvest from same field or secondary site
- **Greater auricular nerve (GAN):** in the operative field; sacrificed as part of superficial neck dissection; provides 5–7 cm of graft; 3–4 fascicles for main trunk calibre matching
- **Sural nerve:** calf; 30–40 cm available; larger calibre; requires second incision; used for long gaps, multiple branch reconstruction, or when GAN is unavailable
- **Medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve:** forearm; intermediate length option
- Technique: cable graft; multiple fascicular repair; epineural anastomosis; fibrin glue may supplement sutures

> **Key principle:** The more proximal the repair, the better the outcome. Main trunk repair gives better recovery than branch-level repair. Time from denervation to reinnervation is the critical variable.

### Dynamic Reanimation: By Timing

#### Early Phase (<18 months from denervation): Motor End Plates Viable

##### 1. Hypoglossal-Facial (XII-VII) Anastomosis

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Technique | Complete or partial XII-VII coaptation at skull base; XII mobilised distally; VII anastomosed at stylomastoid foramen level |
| Result | Reinnervation 6–12 months; reliable; tone + involuntary movement with tongue movement |
| Disadvantage | Hemitongue atrophy (~50% with complete sacrifice); articulation difficulties; mass movement (XII movement triggers facial movement) |
| Modified technique | Partial XII-VII (split XII) to reduce tongue morbidity; "jumping" XII-VII with nerve graft |

##### 2. Masseteric Nerve (V3 Motor Branch) to Facial Nerve Transfer

**This is currently the most popular nerve transfer for complete facial paralysis** in the early phase.

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Donor nerve | Motor branch to masseter from CN V3 (mandibular nerve) — branch enters masseter deep surface ~3 cm anterior to tragus at the level of the zygomatic arch |
| Technique | Masseteric branch identified in the parotid bed after parotidectomy; transposed to anastomose with the main facial nerve trunk or pes anserinus branches |
| Advantages | Short coaptation distance → fast reinnervation (4–6 months); single-stage; minimal donor morbidity (bilateral innervation of masseter); no tongue involvement |
| Disadvantages | Jaw-winking initially (smile triggered by clenching); cerebral adaptation to spontaneous smile takes 6–12 months; ~40–60% achieve spontaneous smiling with cortical plasticity |
| Results | HB III in majority at 12 months; spontaneous smile 40–60%; faster recovery than XII-VII |

> **Key teaching:** Masseteric nerve transfer is the preferred single-stage nerve transfer for immediate or early facial nerve reconstruction. The patient is trained to "clench" to produce smile initially; spontaneous cortical plasticity develops over time.

##### 3. Cross-Face Nerve Graft (CFNG) — Two-Stage

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Sural nerve graft tunnelled subcutaneously across upper lip (deep to skin) from intact contralateral CNVII buccal branch to the paralysed side; "babysitter" procedure |
| **Stage 2 (9–12 months later)** | Free gracilis muscle transferred; neurovascular anastomosis; muscle end-plate connected to CFNG terminus |
| Advantage | Spontaneous, emotionally-driven smile — most natural outcome |
| Disadvantage | Two stages; slow (12–18 months total recovery); donor sural nerve; requires intact contralateral facial nerve |

---

#### Late Phase (>18 months from denervation): Motor End Plates Fibrosed

When motor end plates are fibrosed (>18–24 months), reinnervation of the native mimetic muscles cannot restore function. **Free functional muscle transfer** is required.

##### Free Gracilis Muscle Transfer

The **gracilis muscle** is the gold standard free muscle for facial reanimation:
- Long, flat, parallel-fibred muscle; ideal for facial mimicry
- Neurovascular pedicle: obturator nerve (motor) + medial circumflex femoral vessels (or adductor longus perforators)
- Harvested as partial (anterior slip) or total based on width required
- Vectors: commissure to zygomatic arch (smile vector), nasolabial fold creation

| Innervation Source | Technique | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Masseteric nerve (single-stage)** | Masseteric nerve coaptated to obturator nerve of gracilis at time of transfer; fastest reinnervation (4–6 months); jaw-winking initially | Preferred for late paralysis without contralateral CFNG in situ; reliable, fast, single-stage |
| **CFNG (two-stage Terzis technique)** | Stage 1: CFNG planted; Stage 2 (9–12 months): gracilis transferred with obturator nerve anastomosed to CFNG terminus | Spontaneous, emotionally driven smile; best aesthetic result; longest timeline |
| **Dual innervation** | Obturator nerve anastomosed to both CFNG AND masseteric nerve (single-stage) | Combines spontaneity of CFNG with speed/reliability of masseter; emerging technique |

**Free gracilis technical steps:**
1. Harvest: medial thigh; anterior gracilis slip ~6 cm wide × 15 cm long; obturator nerve secured; pedicle ligated distally
2. Facial preparation: tunnel created from commissure to zygomatic arch
3. Muscle inset: commissure end sutured to modiolus/orbicularis; proximal end fixed to zygomatic arch periosteum
4. Neural coaptation: obturator nerve end-to-end with masseteric nerve or CFNG terminus
5. Vascular anastomosis: facial artery and vein (or temporal vessels)

### Static Reanimation Procedures

Static procedures do not restore movement but improve resting symmetry and reduce complications:

| Procedure | Region | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Gold weight / platinum chain** | Upper lid | Lagophthalmos correction; corneal protection |
| **Lateral tarsal strip** | Lower lid | Ectropion correction; lower lid support |
| **Fascia lata sling** | Mid-face and oral commissure | Supports drooping commissure and nasolabial fold |
| **Tensor fascia lata (TFL) sling to temporalis** | Brow, commissure | Static elevation; reduces gravitational ptosis |
| **Temporalis muscle transposition** | Mid-face | Dynamic (if nerve intact) or static; uses muscle coronally transposed to commissure |
| **Direct brow lift** | Brow | Forehead/brow ptosis from frontalis paralysis |
| **Botulinum toxin (contralateral side)** | Contralateral normal face | Symmetry improvement; reduces overaction of normal side |

### House-Brackmann VI Post Parotid Cancer: Clinical Protocol

When a patient presents with **HB VI palsy following parotidectomy for parotid cancer**, the following structured approach applies:

#### Step 1: Immediate Priorities (Day 1 post-op)
1. **Eye protection protocol initiated immediately:**
   - Artificial tear drops Q1H
   - Lubricating gel (lacrilube) at night
   - Protective moisture chamber glasses
   - Ophthalmology referral within 48 hours
2. **Assess nerve status:** Was nerve deliberately sacrificed (neurotmesis) or intact?
3. **Review operative note:** Nerve repair performed at time of surgery? Type of repair? Graft used?

#### Step 2: Short-term (1–6 weeks)
- **If nerve repaired at surgery:** Await recovery; Tinel's sign progression; NCS/EMG at 3 months
- **If nerve not repaired (injury discovered post-operatively):** Urgent surgical exploration if neuropraxia/axonotmesis excluded by EMG (absent fibrillation and no EMG activity at 3 weeks = neurotmesis → surgical repair)
- Gold weight implantation for upper lid (can be done under local anaesthesia early)
- Lower lid medial/lateral canthoplasty if ectropion developing
- Fascia lata sling to support commissure

#### Step 3: Definitive Dynamic Reanimation (6–18 months post-op)
- **Primary nerve repair at time of surgery:** Await recovery to 18 months; most will achieve HB III
- **Masseteric nerve transfer (if nerve sacrificed and no repair done):** Perform early (<18 months); fastest route to reinnervation
- **Free gracilis with masseteric innervation:** For patients presenting late (>18 months); most patients post-cancer parotidectomy who did not have immediate nerve repair
- If adjuvant radiotherapy is planned or ongoing: delay definitive free flap reanimation until 6 months post-RT (wound bed preparation, oedema resolution)

#### Step 4: Adjuvant RT Considerations
- Radiotherapy to the parotid bed will be included in most cases of radical parotidectomy for high-grade cancer
- RT does not prevent nerve grafting or masseteric nerve transfer if performed before RT or ≥6 months after RT
- Irradiated tissue has reduced healing and angiogenic capacity: free flap anastomosis is still feasible but complication rates higher; experienced microsurgical team essential

#### Step 5: Physiotherapy and Rehabilitation
- **Mime therapy:** Neuromuscular retraining for synkinesis and movement control post-reinnervation
- **Botulinum toxin:** Contralateral face to improve symmetry; synkinesis management ipsilaterally
- Psychological support: facial paralysis has significant impact on quality of life, social interaction, and depression

### Summary Table: Reanimation Techniques by Timing

| Phase | Nerve Intact? | Options | Preferred |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Immediate** (<1 week, at surgery) | No (sacrificed) | Primary neurorrhaphy; nerve graft (GAN, sural) | Primary repair if gap <1 cm; GAN graft for larger gap |
| **Early** (<18 months) | Yes (recovering) | Physiotherapy, observe; masseteric transfer if no recovery by 6 months | Observe → masseteric if no EMG recovery |
| **Early** (<18 months) | No | Masseteric nerve transfer; XII-VII transfer; CFNG (plant stage 1) | Masseteric nerve transfer |
| **Late** (>18 months) | Irrelevant (end-plates fibrosed) | Free gracilis (masseteric-innervated) single-stage; free gracilis (CFNG two-stage) | Free gracilis + masseteric nerve (fastest); CFNG two-stage (most natural smile) |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Facial nerve reanimation after parotidectomy spans a spectrum from immediate primary neurorrhaphy at the time of cancer resection to late free gracilis muscle transfer for long-standing paralysis. The House-Brackmann scale (I–VI) is the standard reporting system; HB VI represents complete paralysis requiring active reconstruction. Eye protection is the immediate priority — gold weight implantation prevents the devastating complication of corneal exposure keratopathy. The masseteric nerve (V3 motor branch) transfer is now the preferred single-stage early dynamic reanimation, offering the fastest reinnervation (4–6 months) with minimal donor morbidity. Beyond 18 months, when motor end plates are fibrosed, free gracilis muscle transfer innervated by the masseteric nerve (single-stage) or cross-face nerve graft (two-stage, Terzis technique) is the definitive procedure — the latter producing the most emotionally spontaneous smile at the cost of a longer rehabilitation timeline.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The Sunderland classification is more detailed than Seddon (neuropraxia/axonotmesis/neurotmesis): Sunderland III and IV have intact epineurium but damaged endoneurium/perineurium respectively — both require surgical repair for meaningful recovery.
> 2. The 18-month window is critical: reinnervation of denervated mimetic muscle must begin within 18–24 months before motor end-plate fibrosis makes recovery impossible.
> 3. Gold weight implantation in the upper lid is a day-case procedure under local anaesthesia and should be performed within weeks of HB VI palsy to prevent corneal keratopathy.
> 4. The masseteric nerve enters the masseter approximately 3 cm anterior to the tragus at the level of the zygomatic arch — it is in the operative field of parotidectomy and accessible for same-day transfer.
> 5. Cross-face nerve grafting (CFNG) two-stage Terzis technique produces the most emotionally spontaneous smile because it uses axons from the normal contralateral facial nerve — the only technique maintaining cortical-facial nerve axis.
> 6. HB Grade III is the realistic best-case outcome following nerve repair or transfer — complete return to Grade I is rarely achievable after neurotmesis.
> 7. Botulinum toxin injections to the contralateral normal face are a simple office procedure that improve symmetry while awaiting reinnervation and can be maintained long-term.
> 8. Adjuvant radiotherapy after parotid cancer should not prevent facial reanimation — surgery is performed before RT, or delayed 6 months after RT completion; the microsurgical team must account for reduced healing in irradiated beds.
> 9. Free gracilis harvest leaves minimal donor morbidity: adductor longus (synergist) compensates well; thigh scar is the main cosmetic issue.
> 10. Temporalis muscle transposition (Gilles procedure) is an older technique that provides static and limited dynamic reanimation using the temporal branch of CN V via the temporalis tendon — still used in centres without microsurgical capability or in elderly patients unfit for free flap surgery.
