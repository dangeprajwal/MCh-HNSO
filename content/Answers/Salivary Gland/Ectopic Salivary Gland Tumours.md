> Stafne (1942) first described the static bone cavity of the mandible in a series of 35 cases from the Mayo Clinic, reporting a characteristic radiographic lucency at the angle of the mandible below the inferior alveolar nerve canal, caused by ectopic salivary gland tissue from the submandibular gland herniating through a lingual cortical defect. This eponymous lesion — the Stafne bone cyst — requires no treatment beyond radiographic confirmation, as it is a benign incidental finding. Perzin and LiVolsi (1982) published the largest reported series of ectopic salivary gland neoplasms at unusual sites in *Cancer*, documenting 19 cases of salivary tumours in sites including the cervical lymph nodes, neck, thyroid, and parapharyngeal space, demonstrating that the histological spectrum mirrors that of normal salivary glands and that management principles apply equally. Batsakis and Regezi (1977) provided the foundational classification and biological characterisation of accessory salivary tissue, emphasising that accessory parotid tissue — located along Stensen's duct in 21% of individuals — has proportionally higher malignancy rates than the main parotid gland due to its different tissue composition.

## Definition and Classification

**Ectopic salivary gland tissue** is salivary gland parenchyma found at sites other than the three major salivary glands (parotid, submandibular, sublingual) or the normal distribution of minor salivary glands (oral cavity, oropharynx, larynx, trachea).

### Classification of Ectopic Salivary Tissue

| Category | Site | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Accessory parotid** | Along Stensen's duct, cheek | 20-21% of individuals; distinct from main gland |
| **Intraparotid lymph node inclusions** | Within parotid parenchyma | Basis of Warthin tumour |
| **Cervical ectopic salivary** | Neck (levels I-III) | Branchial cleft remnants |
| **Stafne bone cavity** | Lingual mandible near angle | Submandibular gland herniation |
| **Intraosseous** | Mandible (within bone) | Rare, usually pleomorphic adenoma |
| **Lymph node salivary inclusions** | Levels I-III | Nélaton gland; metastatic carcinoma can arise here |
| **Middle ear / mastoid** | Temporal bone | Associated with congenital aural malformation |
| **Thyroid gland** | Within thyroid parenchyma | Very rare; usually adenoma or ACC |
| **Pituitary** | Sella turcica | Extremely rare; causes Rathke-like appearances |
| **Thorax** | Mediastinum | Exceedingly rare |

## Accessory Parotid Gland

### Anatomy and Prevalence

- Present in approximately **20–21%** of individuals
- Located along **Stensen's duct**, between the parotid and the masseter muscle
- Separate from the main parotid gland, connected only by Stensen's duct
- Drains into Stensen's duct via one or more small accessory ducts
- No direct anatomical association with the facial nerve at this level (nerve branches are superficial to the masseter at this point)

### Tumours of Accessory Parotid

- Spectrum of tumours is similar to the main parotid gland
- **Proportionally higher malignancy rate** (45–55% malignant) compared to main parotid (~25% malignant)
  - Likely reflects different tissue composition (less lymphoid stroma, fewer Warthin tumours)
  - Mucoepidermoid carcinoma is the most common malignancy in accessory parotid
- **Clinical presentation**: Cheek mass anterior to the main parotid, separate from parotid proper; may be pulsatile if vascular
- **Imaging**: CT/MRI to confirm relationship to Stensen's duct and facial nerve branches; FNAC for diagnosis
- **Surgical approach**: Local excision following Stensen's duct, ensuring facial nerve branch identification; formal parotidectomy not required but facial nerve monitoring is essential

## Stafne Bone Cavity (Static Bone Cyst)

### Pathology

A **pseudocyst** — not a true cyst — caused by lingual cortical depression occupied by normal **submandibular gland parenchyma** herniated through a thin lingual cortical dehiscence. The defect is developmental.

### Radiographic Features (Pathognomonic)

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| **Location** | Posterior mandible, angle region |
| **Position** | **Below the inferior alveolar nerve canal** (critical — distinguishes from dentigerous cyst above the canal) |
| **Side** | Usually unilateral (bilateral in rare cases) |
| **Appearance on OPG** | Well-defined, corticated (sclerotic rim), unilocular radiolucency |
| **CT** | Lingual cortical dehiscence; soft tissue mass extending from submandibular gland |
| **MRI** | T1/T2 signal characteristics of salivary gland tissue confirm diagnosis |

### Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|
| **Stafne bone cavity** | Below IAC, posterior mandible, corticated |
| **Dentigerous cyst** | Around crown of unerupted tooth, above IAC |
| **Ameloblastoma** | Multilocular or "soap bubble" |
| **Keratocystic odontogenic tumour** | Scalloping, anterior to angle, internal ridges |
| **Central haemangioma** | Flow voids on MRI |

### Management

- **Observation** — the standard if imaging is pathognomonic
- No treatment required: benign, does not change, not pre-malignant
- MRI with gadolinium confirms salivary tissue (removes diagnostic doubt)
- Biopsy only if atypical features suggest an alternative diagnosis
- **Malignant transformation has NOT been reported** in Stafne defects

## Cervical Ectopic Salivary Gland Tissue

### Origin

Failure of complete migration of salivary anlage during embryogenesis, or persistence of branchial pouch/cleft salivary rests. The **first branchial cleft** is the principal source of ectopic parotid tissue in the neck; second branchial pouch remnants can carry minor salivary tissue.

### Clinical Presentation

- Presents as a **painless neck mass**, typically levels I–III, in a patient without evident major salivary gland pathology
- May undergo cystic degeneration (mimicking branchial cleft cyst)
- Tumours arising in ectopic cervical salivary tissue mimic lymph node metastasis or branchial cleft cyst carcinoma

### Workup

1. **MRI/CT**: Characterise the lesion and exclude a primary salivary gland tumour
2. **FNAC**: Salivary cytology confirms origin; cannot distinguish primary ectopic from metastatic
3. **PET-CT**: Evaluate for hidden primary tumour (parotid, minor gland)
4. **Panendoscopy**: Exclude occult mucosal primary

### Management

- Excision is required in all cases (exclude malignancy, treat definitive)
- Principles identical to parotidectomy if facial nerve not adjacent
- If malignancy confirmed: treat as per site-specific salivary gland cancer protocol (surgery + adjuvant)

## Salivary Gland Inclusions in Cervical Lymph Nodes (Nélaton Gland)

- Normal microscopic salivary gland tissue can be found **within lymph nodes** at levels I–III
- Termed **Nélaton's gland** (after Charles Nélaton, French surgeon)
- Embryological origin: salivary anlage entrapped within developing lymph nodes
- Clinical significance:
  1. **Basis for Warthin tumour**: Warthin tumour (cystadenolymphoma) arises from salivary inclusions within parotid lymph nodes; bilateral in 10%, multifocal in 20%
  2. **Metastatic mimicry**: Carcinoma arising in salivary inclusions within a cervical lymph node can mimic squamous cell carcinoma metastasis from unknown primary — requires careful IHC (salivary markers: CK7+, DOG1+, S100+) and imaging to locate the primary

## Intraosseous Salivary Gland Tumours

- Extremely rare; most common: **pleomorphic adenoma within the mandible**
- Origin: entrapped salivary gland rests during mandibular development
- May also arise in maxilla (harder palate)
- Radiograph: expansile radiolucency with cortical thinning
- Management: excision with bony margins; marginal mandibulectomy for benign; segmental for malignant

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Ectopic salivary gland tumours arise from salivary tissue displaced from its normal location due to embryological anomalies. The most clinically important forms are: accessory parotid tissue (20% prevalence, higher malignancy rate than main gland), Stafne bone cavity (benign, observation only, below the inferior alveolar nerve canal on OPG), and cervical ectopic salivary tissue (requires excision and full workup to exclude metastatic salivary carcinoma). Nélaton's gland (salivary inclusions in cervical nodes) underlies Warthin tumour development and can give rise to primary carcinoma mimicking unknown primary. Management principles for ectopic salivary tumours mirror those for in-situ salivary gland neoplasms.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Accessory parotid** is present in 20% of individuals — a cheek mass separate from the parotid proper along Stensen's duct line should trigger this diagnosis; FNAC + excision following the duct
> 2. **Accessory parotid has 45–55% malignancy rate** — higher than main parotid (~25%) due to absence of lymphoid stroma and Warthin-type tumours
> 3. **Stafne bone cavity** is identified by its position **below the inferior alveolar nerve canal** on OPG — this single feature distinguishes it from all other mandibular radiolucencies, which are above the canal
> 4. **Stafne defect requires no treatment** — MRI confirms salivary tissue content; observation is sufficient; malignant transformation has never been reported
> 5. **Nélaton's gland** (salivary inclusions in cervical lymph nodes) is the embryological basis of Warthin tumour — Warthin is bilateral in 10%, multifocal in 20%, and tobacco-associated
> 6. **Carcinoma in Nélaton's gland** can mimic CUP (cancer of unknown primary) — IHC with DOG1, CK7, and salivary-specific markers helps distinguish primary nodal salivary carcinoma from SCC metastasis
> 7. **Ectopic salivary neck mass workup**: MRI + FNAC + PET-CT to exclude parotid/submandibular primary before diagnosing as primary ectopic
> 8. **Middle ear salivary choristoma**: congenital salivary tissue in the middle ear presents with conductive hearing loss; resection requires meticulous facial nerve identification as the nerve may course through the tissue
> 9. **Intraosseous PA of mandible**: expansile mandibular radiolucency with cortical thinning in a young adult — add PA to the differential alongside ameloblastoma and OKC
> 10. **All ectopic salivary malignancies** are treated by the same principles as their major gland counterparts: wide excision with negative margins, selective neck dissection for high-grade histology, adjuvant CRT per indications
