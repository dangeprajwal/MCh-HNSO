## Minor Salivary Gland Tumours

### Epidemiology and General Principles

Minor salivary glands are distributed throughout the oral cavity, oropharynx, nasopharynx, larynx, trachea, and sinonasal tract — numbering approximately 600–1000 glands. They are concentrated on the hard and soft palate, lips, buccal mucosa, floor of mouth, and tongue.

**Key epidemiological differences from major salivary glands:**

| Feature | Minor Salivary Glands | Parotid |
|---|---|---|
| Proportion of all salivary neoplasms | 15–20% | 70–80% |
| Malignant : Benign ratio | ~60–70% malignant | ~80% benign |
| Most common benign tumour | Pleomorphic adenoma | Pleomorphic adenoma |
| Most common malignant tumour | ACC > PAC > MEC | MEC |
| Sublingual gland analogy | Minor glands share high malignancy risk | — |

> [!warning] Critical Principle: Unlike the parotid (80% benign), minor salivary gland tumours are malignant in the majority (60–70%). Any firm, painless submucosal mass on the palate should be considered malignant until proven otherwise.

### Classification of Minor Salivary Gland Tumours

#### Benign Tumours

| Tumour | Frequency | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Pleomorphic adenoma (PA)** | Most common benign (~70%) | Palate most common site; rubbery, submucous; no ulceration; excise with 1–2 mm cuff of normal tissue |
| Canalicular adenoma | Palate, upper lip | Elderly; double-strand cord pattern; benign; simple excision |
| Basal cell adenoma | Rare | Well-encapsulated; benign |
| Myoepithelioma | Rare | May recur if margins close |

#### Malignant Tumours

| Tumour | Frequency (minor) | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC)** | Most common malignant (~30–35%) | Slow growth; perineural invasion; late distant mets; high local recurrence |
| **Polymorphous adenocarcinoma (PAC)** | ~20–25% | Almost exclusively minor glands; palate; indolent; PRKD1 mutations |
| **Mucoepidermoid carcinoma (MEC)** | ~15–20% | Variable grade; CRTC1/3-MAML2 fusion in low-grade |
| Adenocarcinoma NOS | ~10% | Diagnosis of exclusion; variable behaviour |
| Carcinoma ex PA | ~5% | Arising from longstanding PA |
| Polymorphous high-grade (cribriform variant) | Rare | PRKD2/3 mutation; more aggressive; node mets |
| Secretory carcinoma | Rare | ETV6-NTRK3 fusion; targetable with larotrectinib |
| Acinic cell carcinoma | Rare in minor glands | Mainly parotid; minor gland cases behave similarly |

### Polymorphous Adenocarcinoma (PAC) — Formerly PLGA

PAC is unique to minor salivary glands and is the second most common malignancy at these sites.

**Pathological features:**
- Uniform bland cells with vesicular nuclei — cytological monomorphism but architectural polymorphism
- Multiple growth patterns in a single tumour: tubular, ductal, cribriform, papillary, solid, single-file (Indian file)
- Perineural invasion: common but does NOT confer the aggressive behaviour seen in ACC
- Lacks true capsule but is well-circumscribed

**Molecular features:**
- PRKD1 E542K hotspot mutation (~70–80% of classical PAC) — characteristic and diagnostic
- PRKD2 and PRKD3 mutations in high-grade cribriform variant
- No MYB-NFIB (distinguishes from ACC); no CRTC-MAML2 (distinguishes from MEC)

**WHO 2022 reclassification (from PLGA to PAC):**
The 2017 WHO 4th edition and 2022 5th edition both use "Polymorphous Adenocarcinoma" to encompass:
1. Classical PAC (formerly PLGA) — indolent, low rate of nodal metastasis (~10%)
2. **Cribriform adenocarcinoma (high-grade PAC)** — PRKD2/3 mutations; higher rate of nodal metastasis (~20–30%); more aggressive; requires adjuvant RT + elective neck treatment

**Management of PAC:**
- Wide local excision with clear margins (1 cm or bone involvement if applicable)
- N0 neck: observation for classical PAC (low nodal risk); elective neck dissection for cribriform/high-grade variant
- Adjuvant RT: for positive margins, high-grade cribriform subtype, or T3–T4 disease
- Prognosis: 10-year OS >90% for classical PAC; recurrence is local; late recurrences at 10–20 years are reported

### Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma of Minor Salivary Glands

ACC is the **most common malignancy of minor salivary glands overall**, and behaves identically to ACC in major glands with some anatomical site-specific considerations:

**Palate ACC — special considerations:**
- Hard palate ACC: involving palatine bone → infrastructure maxillectomy minimum
- Nerve tracking pathway: ACC of palate spreads along:
  - Greater and lesser palatine nerves → pterygopalatine fossa → V2 (maxillary nerve) → foramen rotundum → cavernous sinus
  - Nasopalatine nerve → incisive foramen
- Imaging: MRI with gadolinium essential to delineate perineural spread to skull base
- RT CTV: must include pterygopalatine fossa and cavernous sinus in cases with proximal PNS
- Dose: 66 Gy or higher; proton/neutron therapy preferred where available for skull-base disease

### Palatal Minor Salivary Gland Tumour Management

#### Benign Palatal Mass (Pleomorphic Adenoma)

The hard palate has no submucosa — PA here is immediately subperiosteal:
- Surgical approach: resect mucosa + periosteum + superficial cortical bone if fixation present
- Do NOT shell out: incomplete excision → seeding → multiple recurrences
- Reconstruct with: palatal obturator, split-thickness skin graft, or allow secondary intention healing for small defects

#### Malignant Palatal Tumour

| T-stage | Surgical Extent | Reconstruction |
|---|---|---|
| T1–T2, bone not involved | Mucosal/submucosal excision ± periosteum | Obturator or local flap |
| T1–T2, superficial bone erosion | Infrastructure maxillectomy (preserves orbital floor) | Obturator prosthesis |
| T3, involving maxillary sinus | Total maxillectomy | Free flap (radial forearm, anterolateral thigh) |
| T4a, orbital floor involved | Total maxillectomy ± orbital exenteration | Microvascular free flap |

Adjuvant RT 60–66 Gy indicated for all malignant palatal tumours except T1 low-grade MEC with clear margins.

### Lip Salivary Gland Lesions

| Lesion | Type | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Mucocele** (mucous retention cyst) | Benign — most common lip lesion | Excision with minor gland; marsupialisation for large; laser vaporisation |
| Pleomorphic adenoma | Benign — most common solid benign | Local excision with cuff |
| Mucoepidermoid carcinoma | Malignant — most common malignant | Wide local excision ± neck |
| Adenoid cystic carcinoma | Malignant | Wide excision + adjuvant RT |
| Canalicular adenoma | Benign | Simple excision |

**Note:** Lower lip salivary tumours are far more common than upper lip malignancies. Mucoceles are almost exclusively lower lip (minor duct trauma → mucus extravasation).

### Surgical Principles for Minor Salivary Gland Malignancies

1. **Wide local excision** with minimum 1 cm margin in soft tissue; periosteum and underlying bone if affixed
2. **Frozen section margins** intraoperatively for assessment — particularly important for ACC (extensive PNI may give impression of clear gross margins)
3. **Neck dissection:**
   - Elective (supraomohyoid or functional) for N0 high-grade histology — ACC, high-grade MEC, adenocarcinoma NOS (occult node rate 15–25%)
   - Classical PAC: neck observation for N0; selective dissection for cribriform/high-grade variant
4. **Adjuvant RT:** as per major gland principles — T3–T4, high-grade, PNI, LVI, ENE, positive margins
5. **ACC specifically:** adjuvant RT always; track nerve to skull base

### Differential Diagnosis of Palatal Submucosal Mass

| Diagnosis | Features |
|---|---|
| Minor salivary gland tumour | Most common firm palatal mass; no inflammation |
| Torus palatinus | Midline bony prominence; symmetric; no treatment needed |
| Periapical abscess / odontogenic cyst | Fluctuant; associated with tooth disease |
| Lymphoma | Soft; diffuse swelling; nodes elsewhere |
| Kaposi sarcoma | Vascular, HIV-associated |
| Metastasis | Rare; breast/lung primary |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Minor salivary gland tumours are malignant in 60–70% of cases — the inverse of parotid gland behaviour — and the most common malignancies are ACC, polymorphous adenocarcinoma, and MEC. The palate (40–50%) is the dominant site, and any firm painless palatal submucosal mass must be treated as potentially malignant. Polymorphous adenocarcinoma (formerly PLGA) is now renamed in WHO classification to reflect the recognition of the high-grade cribriform variant with PRKD2/3 mutations and higher nodal metastasis rates; classical PAC with PRKD1 mutation has an excellent prognosis. Palate ACC requires tracking of the palatine nerves through the pterygopalatine fossa to skull base — both surgically and radiotherapeutically.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Minor salivary gland tumours: 60–70% malignant — the opposite of parotid (80% benign). Any firm palatal submucosal mass is malignant until proven otherwise.
> 2. Palate is the most common site (40–50%), followed by upper lip (20%) and buccal mucosa (10%).
> 3. ACC is the most common malignancy of minor salivary glands overall; PAC is the second most common.
> 4. PLGA is now PAC (WHO 2022) — the rename reflects the high-grade cribriform variant which has different biology (PRKD2/3 mutations, higher node rate) vs. classical PAC (PRKD1 E542K, indolent).
> 5. PAC cytological monomorphism + architectural polymorphism + Indian file pattern = hallmark histology; perineural invasion present but indolent.
> 6. ACC of palate tracks along palatine nerves → pterygopalatine fossa → V2 → skull base; MRI with gadolinium mandatory.
> 7. Hard palate PA must NOT be shelled out — excise with overlying mucosa and periosteum ± cortical bone; seeding causes multiple recurrences.
> 8. Palatal malignancy minimum surgery is infrastructure maxillectomy; total maxillectomy for sinus involvement.
> 9. Mucocele = mucus extravasation cyst, almost always lower lip, not a true salivary gland neoplasm — excise with associated minor gland to prevent recurrence.
> 10. c-Kit expression >80% in ACC — but imatinib failed because no activating mutations; ETV6-NTRK3 in secretory carcinoma is fully actionable with larotrectinib.
