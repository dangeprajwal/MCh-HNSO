## Anatomy of the Lip

### Surface Landmarks
The **upper lip** is defined by the following subunits:
- **Philtrum**: midline vertical groove between two philtral columns; contains orbicularis decussation
- **Cupid's bow**: double-curved free margin of upper lip at vermilion–cutaneous junction
- **White roll** (vermilion border): pale ridge at junction of lip skin and vermilion; forms the visual frame of the lip; critical landmark to restore
- **Vermilion**: red mucosal portion; wet-dry junction marks transition to labial mucosa
- **Commissure**: angle of mouth where upper and lower lips meet; modiolus is the fibromuscular anchor point

The **lower lip** spans from commissure to commissure and is the more common site of squamous cell carcinoma (SCC); contains a single aesthetic subunit.

### Muscle
The **orbicularis oris** is the key muscle: a sphincter without bony attachments. It encircles the oral aperture in two semicircles. Restoring sphincter continuity is the fundamental goal of lip reconstruction. Motor supply is from the buccal and marginal mandibular branches of CN VII.

### Blood Supply
- **Superior labial artery** (branch of facial artery): runs within the orbicularis 2–3 mm behind the vermilion border; supplies upper lip and forms the anastomotic labial arcade
- **Inferior labial artery** (branch of facial artery): same depth within lower lip orbicularis
- The labial arcade is the basis for all cross-lip (Abbe/Estlander) flaps

### Sensory Innervation
- Upper lip: **infraorbital nerve** (CN V2)
- Lower lip and chin: **mental nerve** (CN V3)

## Principles of Lip Reconstruction

1. **Restore sphincter function**: orbicularis continuity for oral competence (drooling prevention)
2. **Reconstruct aesthetic subunits**: vermilion, white roll, Cupid's bow must be precisely aligned
3. **Maintain oral opening**: avoid microstomia, which impairs oral hygiene, speech, and dental care
4. **Match tissue character**: full-thickness lip requires muscle + mucosa + skin; colour and texture match best with cross-lip tissue
5. **Achieve a sensate lip** where possible: improves function and patient quality of life
6. **Respect aesthetic unit boundaries**: incisions placed along subunit junctions (philtral columns, commissures, melolabial folds) minimise visible scarring

## Lower Lip Reconstruction

Lower lip SCC accounts for the majority of lip cancers (90–95%); reconstruction algorithm is based on defect size.

### <1/3 Defect: Primary Closure
- Full-thickness wedge resection with primary two-layer closure
- Key: close orbicularis as a separate layer; precisely align vermilion border at white roll
- Limit: >1/3 creates microstomia if closed directly

### 1/3–2/3 Defect (Central): Karapandzic Flap
The **Karapandzic flap** (1974) is the workhorse for central lower lip defects of 1/3–2/3 size.

**Design**: bilateral circumoral advancement-rotation flaps following the nasolabial folds (bilateral).

**Technique**:
1. Incision follows nasolabial fold lines bilaterally from commissure superiorly then inferiorly to mentolabial sulcus
2. Skin incised; orbicularis carefully dissected preserving the **labial neurovascular bundles** (facial artery branches and labial branches of CN VII entering orbicularis from lateral aspect)
3. Flaps rotated medially to close the central defect
4. Orbicularis reapproximated in the midline

**Advantages**:
- Preserves motor nerve supply to orbicularis → **functional sphincter** maintained
- Preserves sensory nerves → sensate reconstruction
- Single-stage procedure
- Uses like tissue (lip tissue itself)

**Disadvantage**: **microstomia** is inevitable to some degree (reduced oral aperture); commissuroplasty may be needed later.

**Best indication**: central lower lip defects 1/3–2/3, particularly when functional sphincter preservation is paramount.

### 1/3–2/3 Defect (Lateral): Abbe-Estlander Flap
- **Abbe flap**: cross-lip transfer; full-thickness flap from upper lip to lower lip, based on superior labial artery; used for **central** defects; two-stage (pedicle divided at 3 weeks)
- **Estlander flap**: single-stage, full-thickness flap from upper lip for **lateral** lower lip defect involving the commissure; rotates through commissure; reconstructs commissure but creates rounded commissure (blunting); no second stage needed

### >2/3 to Near-Total Defect
- **Gillies fan flap**: bilateral circumoral advancement with fan-shaped rotation; similar principle to Karapandzic but with more extensive tissue release; more microstomia
- **Bernard-Burow procedure**: bilateral cheek advancement flaps with Burow triangle excisions in the nasolabial region; creates new commissures; no real orbicularis reconstruction; used when sensate sphincter function is not achievable

### Total Lower Lip Reconstruction
- **Radial forearm free flap (RFFF)** with static sling (fascia lata or palmaris longus for lip competence)
- **Sensate RFFF**: medial cutaneous nerve of forearm to mental nerve coaptation
- **Gracilis free muscle transfer**: provides dynamic muscle for sphincter function (rare)
- Palmaris longus tendon: interdigitated across defect as static suspension sling; prevents ptosis/drooling

## Upper Lip Reconstruction

Upper lip reconstruction is more complex due to philtrum and Cupid's bow aesthetics.

### <1/3 Defect
- Primary closure; preserve philtral column alignment

### 1/3–1/2 Defect
- **Abbe flap** from lower lip: cross-lip, full-thickness flap based on inferior labial artery; brings skin + muscle + mucosa; matches texture and colour; two-stage (pedicle divided at 3 weeks); reconstructed philtral unit with muscle for dynamic function

### Large Upper Lip Defects (>1/2)
- **Webster cheek advancement**: perialar crescentic excision allows cheek advancement; bilateral for midline defects; no true orbicularis
- **Bilateral Abbe flaps**: two cross-lip flaps for large central defects; technically demanding

## Abbe Flap — Detailed Technique

> Workhorse cross-lip flap for upper lip reconstruction.

**Design** (for upper lip defect):
1. Flap designed on **lower lip** based on **inferior labial artery** (pedicle at medial 1/3–lateral 2/3 junction of lip to avoid commissure tethering)
2. Flap width = ½ the defect width (accounts for bilateral advancement)
3. Full-thickness flap: skin + orbicularis + mucosa
4. Pedicle width ≥5 mm to protect labial artery

**First stage**: rotate flap 180° into upper lip defect; suture in layers (mucosa, muscle, skin); lip is temporarily joined, oral intake via syringe or lateral mouth

**Second stage (3 weeks)**: divide pedicle; inset pedicle remnant back into lower lip; refine donor

**Key principles**:
- Precise **vermilion-to-vermilion** matching
- Orbicularis suture restores sphincter
- White roll alignment critical

---

## Karapandzic Flap — Detailed Technique

1. Mark nasolabial lines bilaterally from commissure; extend inferiorly along mentolabial fold
2. Incise skin and subcutaneous tissue only (preserve orbicularis initially)
3. Under magnification, identify and preserve **labial arteries** (just deep to skin), **branches of CN VII** (buccal and marginal mandibular approaching laterally)
4. Incise orbicularis **between** the preserved nerve-vessel bundles — i.e., the neurovascular pedicles remain intact within the advancing flap
5. Advance flaps medially; close orbicularis centrally; close mucosa and skin

The preservation of the neurovascular hilum entering the orbicularis from the lateral side distinguishes Karapandzic from simple advancement and is the reason for its superior functional outcome.

## Complications of Lip Reconstruction

| Complication | Flap | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Microstomia | Karapandzic, Gillies fan | Commissuroplasty; scar release |
| Oral incompetence/drooling | Total lip; Bernard-Burow | Static sling; exercises |
| Pedicle necrosis | Abbe/Estlander | Immediate re-exploration |
| Notching at white roll | All closures | Precise alignment; Z-plasty |
| Blunted commissure | Estlander | Commissure revision |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Lip reconstruction requires restoration of three functional elements — orbicularis sphincter, vermilion continuity, and oral aperture — with flap selection guided by defect size. The Karapandzic flap is the gold standard for central lower lip defects of 1/3–2/3, uniquely preserving the labial neurovascular supply to achieve a functional sphincter at the cost of some microstomia. For cross-lip defects, the Abbe flap (two-stage) for upper lip and Estlander (single-stage, commissure-involving) for lower lip provide matched full-thickness tissue.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The white roll must be aligned to within 0.5 mm — even slight misalignment is visible at conversational distance; mark with methylene blue before incision.
> 2. Karapandzic uniquely preserves motor nerves to orbicularis by dissecting between nerve bundles entering laterally — this is the single feature distinguishing it from simpler advancement flaps.
> 3. In Abbe flap design, the pedicle must include the full labial artery — confirm with Doppler; pedicle width ≥5 mm minimum.
> 4. The Estlander flap is single-stage because it rotates through the commissure (which itself acts as the pedicle pivot); the commissure becomes rounded and usually requires later revision.
> 5. Total lower lip loss after oncological resection is best reconstructed with sensate RFFF + static fascia lata sling; gracilis is reserved for cases requiring dynamic sphincter function.
> 6. Microstomia after Karapandzic is proportional to the extent of bilateral advancement; mouth-opening exercises and splints begun early (within 2 weeks) can reduce contracture.
> 7. Bernard-Burow procedure does not reconstruct orbicularis and therefore gives static oral competence only — it is not ideal for patients requiring dynamic sphincter function.
> 8. In planning cross-lip (Abbe) flaps, the pedicle width equals ½ the defect width, and the flap is placed lateral to the labial artery midpoint to preserve vascularity of the donor lip halves.
