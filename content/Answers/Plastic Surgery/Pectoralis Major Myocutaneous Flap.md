## Pectoralis Major Myocutaneous Flap

### Anatomy

The PMMC flap is a **Type V (Mathes-Nahai) myocutaneous flap** with a dominant pedicle and secondary segmental supply.

#### Dominant Pedicle
- **Thoracoacromial artery (TAA)**, pectoral branch — arises from axillary artery, descends between pectoral minor and major
- Enters the deep surface of pectoralis major at the medial clavicular region
- Venous drainage: thoracoacromial vein → cephalic vein → axillary vein

#### Secondary Supply
- **Lateral thoracic artery** — medial border of pectoralis minor; supplies lateral portion of muscle
- **Internal mammary artery (IMA) perforators** (2nd–4th intercostal spaces) — supply medial skin paddle; basis for deltopectoral flap

#### Muscle Origin and Insertion
- **Clavicular head:** Medial half of clavicle
- **Sternocostal head:** Sternum and costal cartilages of ribs 2–6 (larger head)
- **Insertion:** Lateral lip of bicipital groove of humerus
- **Motor nerve:** Medial and lateral pectoral nerves (from medial and lateral cords of brachial plexus)

### Mathes-Nahai Classification

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| V | Dominant + secondary segmental | Pectoralis major, Latissimus dorsi |

The dominant thoracoacromial pedicle allows reliable flap elevation even when secondary perforators are divided.

### Flap Design

- **Skin paddle:** Over lower pectoralis major (reliable musculocutaneous perforators from TAA pectoral branch traverse the muscle to overlie skin)
- **Skin paddle dimensions:** Up to **12 × 8 cm** reliably; extended paddles risk distal necrosis
- **Skin paddle position:** Oriented vertically or obliquely over lower sternum/anterior chest wall
- **Pedicle length:** 6–8 cm after tunnelling under clavicle to recipient site
- **Reach:** Oral cavity, oropharynx, hypopharynx, neck; skull base with difficulty

### Surgical Technique

1. **Patient position:** Supine, arm abducted 90° on arm board; shoulder ipsilateral side slightly elevated
2. **Surface markings:** Mark clavicle, coracoid process, deltopectoral groove; outline skin paddle over lower pectoralis
3. **Incision:** Elevate skin paddle from inferior; incise around paddle including pectoralis fascia
4. **Muscle dissection:** Split pectoralis muscle between clavicular (superior) and sternocostal (inferior) heads at the deltopectoral groove; elevate sternocostal head with skin paddle
5. **Pedicle identification:** Identify thoracoacromial artery on deep surface of clavicular head — do not divide
6. **Tunnelling:** Create subcutaneous tunnel under clavicle to neck; tunnel must be generous (≥3 fingerbreadths) to avoid pedicle compression
7. **Flap delivery:** Pass flap through tunnel; avoid acute angulation at clavicle
8. **Inset:** Secure muscle to neck wound edges; suture skin paddle to mucosal/skin defect
9. **Donor site:** Primary closure in most cases; large paddles may require drain

### Use in Pharyngeal Reconstruction

- PMMC provides **partial pharyngeal wall coverage** as a myocutaneous patch flap
- **Not suitable for circumferential pharyngeal reconstruction** — tubed PMMC has very high fistula/stricture rates
- Best indication: anterior or lateral pharyngeal wall defect after partial pharyngectomy
- Myocutaneous flap: skin paddle faces the lumen; muscle faces externally
- Combined with RFFF or jejunal free flap for circumferential defects

### Pedicle Lengthening Techniques

When additional pedicle length is required (e.g., for skull base reach):
1. Divide the clavicular head of pectoralis major at its sternal origin — releases 2–3 cm
2. Divide pectoralis minor tendon from coracoid — releases pedicle from compression
3. Partial resection of medial clavicle — gains 2–4 cm pedicle length (use sparingly)
4. Deepening subcutaneous tunnel to supra-clavicular level

### Advantages

- **Reliable blood supply** — Type V pattern, dominant pedicle from TAA
- **Large bulk** — excellent dead space obliteration in neck after radical surgery
- **Tolerates irradiation well** — suitable for post-radiation salvage when free flap not feasible
- **Carotid coverage** — most important indication; protects carotid in vessel-depleted neck
- Avoids microsurgery — relevant when patient physiology precludes prolonged anaesthesia
- Familiar anatomy, rapid harvest
- Can be elevated simultaneously with neck dissection

### Disadvantages

| Disadvantage | Clinical Impact |
|-------------|----------------|
| Excessive bulk | Poor functional result for tongue/FOM reconstruction |
| Trapdoor deformity | Bunching under clavicle after tunnelling — aesthetic and functional problem |
| Limited reach | Cannot reliably reach skull base without pedicle lengthening |
| Functional shoulder morbidity | Resection of sternocostal head causes aesthetic contour loss; rarely functional deficit |
| Hair-bearing skin | Intraoral use requires epilation |
| Poor tongue mobility | Bulky muscle limits tongue movement |
| Chest scar | Visible in women; nipple distortion possible |

### Indications

| Indication | Priority |
|------------|----------|
| Carotid artery coverage after radical neck dissection | Primary |
| Pharyngocutaneous fistula repair | Primary — most common indication |
| Coverage after failed free flap | Salvage |
| Partial pharyngeal reconstruction | Secondary |
| Vessel-depleted neck (post-radiation salvage) | Primary |
| Large neck soft tissue defects | Primary |
| Dead space obliteration after skull base surgery | Secondary |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The pectoralis major myocutaneous flap, described by Ariyan in 1979, is a Mathes-Nahai Type V myocutaneous flap based on the pectoral branch of the thoracoacromial artery, tunnelled under the clavicle to reach the head and neck. It remains the most important pedicled regional flap in head and neck surgery for carotid coverage, pharyngocutaneous fistula repair, and salvage reconstruction in post-radiation or vessel-depleted fields. Its main limitation is excessive bulk, making it unsuitable for tongue and floor-of-mouth reconstruction where pliable thin tissue is required for functional recovery.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Ariyan (1979) landmark paper — PMMC was the first flap to reliably bring large volumes of tissue to the head and neck without microsurgery
> 2. Pedicle is the **pectoral branch of thoracoacromial artery** — not the lateral thoracic artery, which is a secondary supply
> 3. Mathes-Nahai Type V: dominant pedicle (TAA) + secondary segmental (IMA perforators) — can sacrifice secondary supply safely
> 4. The deltopectoral flap and PMMC are both from the chest but have different pedicles: deltopectoral = IMA perforators 2–4; PMMC = TAA pectoral branch
> 5. Tunnelling must be at least 3 fingerbreadths wide under the clavicle — pedicle compression is the commonest cause of partial flap failure
> 6. PMMC is the **best option for carotid coverage** — its bulk and reliability in irradiated tissue is unmatched by fasciocutaneous flaps
> 7. Circumferential pharyngeal reconstruction with PMMC tube is associated with high fistula rates — prefer jejunal free flap or RFFF/ALT tubed
> 8. Trapdoor deformity at the clavicular tunnel is a recognised complication — can be corrected by dividing the clavicular skin bridge
> 9. The skin paddle must overlie the pectoralis muscle (not below the inframammary fold) — below-fold paddle skin has unreliable perforators
> 10. Shoulder function is generally preserved as the clavicular head remains intact and is not included in the standard PMMC harvest
