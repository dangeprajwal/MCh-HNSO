## Limberg Rhomboid Flap

### Definitions and Terminology

- **Rhomboid flap** = general term for transposition flaps based on a rhomboid (parallelogram) defect
- **Limberg flap** = specific rhomboid transposition flap with strict geometric construction (angles of 60° and 120°)
- **Dufourmentel flap** = modification allowing smaller angle rhomboids and greater design flexibility
- **Webster modification** = back-cut at terminal limb of Limberg flap (30° back-cut at point F)

### Geometry of the Limberg Flap

A rhomboid (parallelogram) has:
- **Two pairs of equal sides**
- **Two acute angles of 60°** and **two obtuse angles of 120°**
- **Two diagonals** — a long diagonal (connects 120° angles) and a short diagonal (connects 60° angles)

#### Construction Steps

1. **Convert the defect to a rhomboid shape** (ABCD): sides equal; angles 60° and 120°; mark corners A, B, C, D going clockwise
2. **Identify the short diagonal:** In rhomboid ABCD, the short diagonal connects the two 60° angle corners (e.g., B and D)
3. **Extend the short diagonal:** From the 60° angle corner at D, extend the short diagonal outward by one side-length to reach point E (DE = one side of rhomboid)
4. **Draw the flap limb:** From E, draw EF parallel to one side of the rhomboid (e.g., CD) and of equal length
5. **The flap is triangle EFD** — this is the Limberg flap
6. **Transposition:** The flap rotates along the axis FE, with F as the mobile end; the flap is transposed into the defect

#### The Four Possible Limberg Flaps

For every rhomboid defect there are **four possible Limberg flap configurations** (one from each of the four 60°-angle sides). Choose the configuration that:
- Orients the closure tension perpendicular to RSTLs (relaxed skin tension lines)
- Has the most available lax donor tissue
- Produces the least visible secondary scar

### Webster Modification (1978)

- A **30° back-cut** at point F allows greater angular transposition of the flap
- Reduces the **standing cone (dog-ear deformity)** at F
- Allows use in areas with moderate donor skin laxity
- The back-cut should not extend more than 30% of flap limb length (risk of distal tip necrosis)

### Dufourmentel Modification

- Designed for rhomboid defects with **angles other than 60°/120°**
- The flap axis bisects the angle between the extended short diagonal and the adjacent side
- Provides more flexibility in orientation — can be adapted to nearly any defect shape
- Less geometrically prescriptive than classic Limberg

### Comparison of Rhomboid Flap Variants

| Feature | Classic Limberg | Webster Modified | Dufourmentel |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Defect angles | 60°/120° exactly | 60°/120° | Any angle |
| Back-cut | None | 30° at F | Variable |
| Dog-ear | Moderate | Reduced | Variable |
| Design flexibility | Limited | Moderate | High |
| Tension at closure | Moderate | Reduced | Variable |

### Blood Supply

- **Random-pattern** fasciocutaneous flap — no defined axial vessel
- Reliability based on: (a) short flap dimensions (L:W ratio respected), (b) thick subcutaneous base, (c) retained subdermal plexus
- Well-vascularised due to short limb lengths and broad base
- Excellent reliability in facial skin (rich subdermal plexus)

### Advantages

| Advantage | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Predictable geometry | Mathematical design minimises errors |
| Distributes tension | Closure tension spread across two vectors rather than one |
| Minimal distortion | Avoids pin-cushioning when correctly designed |
| Inconspicuous scar | Limb scars can be oriented along RSTLs |
| Four design options | Flexibility to choose optimal orientation per patient |
| Minimal undermining needed | Compared to rotation or advancement flaps |

### Disadvantages

| Disadvantage | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Standing cone deformity | At pivot point — requires back-cut or z-plasty |
| Requires donor tissue laxity | Cannot be used in areas with limited adjacent skin |
| Geometric planning errors | Common in inexperienced hands — leads to poor tension distribution |
| Small defects only | Suitable for defects up to 3–4 cm; larger defects need regional flaps |
| Not suitable if donor scarred | Post-radiation skin or previously operated areas |

### Indications in Head and Neck Surgery

| Location | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Scalp (posterior/vertex) | Lax scalp allows primary donor closure |
| Cheek | Medium-sized cutaneous defects |
| Posterior neck | Useful after excision of melanoma, SCC |
| Preauricular region | Excellent laxity for Limberg design |
| Nasal dorsum | Small defects; limited by nasal skin laxity |
| Chin/mental region | Post-excision reconstruction |
| Temporal region | After excision of skin malignancy |

### Design Pearls

1. **Mark all four options** on skin before committing — compare tension vectors and scar orientation for each
2. **Choose orientation** placing maximum tension perpendicular to RSTLs (cosmetically optimal) and scar lines along RSTLs
3. **Back-cut (Webster)** if tension at point F appears excessive during trial approximation
4. **Undermine adjacent skin** generously at same subcutaneous plane as flap — redistributes closure tension
5. **Side equality is critical** — all limbs of the rhomboid and flap must be equal length; verify with ruler
6. Do not design flap too close to free margins (eyelid, alar rim, lip) — distortion from closure tension

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The Limberg rhomboid flap is a random-pattern transposition flap designed as a geometric rhomboid with 60° and 120° angles, where a triangular flap is elevated adjacent to the short diagonal and transposed 60° into the defect. Four flap configurations are possible per defect; the optimal choice aligns closure tension with maximal skin laxity and scars along relaxed skin tension lines. Webster's modification adds a 30° back-cut at the terminal point to reduce dog-ear deformity. In head and neck surgery it is most useful for small to medium cutaneous defects of the scalp, cheek, posterior neck, and preauricular region where adjacent skin laxity is present.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The classic Limberg flap requires **exactly 60°/120°** rhomboid angles — if the defect does not conform, use the Dufourmentel modification
> 2. All sides of the rhomboid and flap limbs must be **equal length** — verify with a ruler before incision
> 3. Four possible Limberg configurations exist for each defect — always plan all four and choose the best
> 4. Orient the final closure scar **along RSTLs** and place the maximum tension vector perpendicular to free margins
> 5. Webster back-cut (30° at F) reduces the standing cone — add it when donor skin laxity is moderate
> 6. Dog-ear at pivot point is common — plan correction at the time of design with back-cut, not as an afterthought
> 7. The flap is random-pattern — keep L:W ≤ 3:1; in head and neck (richly vascularised) slightly greater ratios are tolerated
> 8. Generous subcutaneous undermining of the adjacent defect margins is essential to reduce closure tension
> 9. Limberg flap is not for defects > 4 cm — beyond this size, tension is excessive and regional flaps should be considered
> 10. In post-irradiated skin, random-pattern flaps are unreliable — consider local axial-pattern flap or free flap for irradiated fields
