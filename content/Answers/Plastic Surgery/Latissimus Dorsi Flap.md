## Latissimus Dorsi Flap

### Anatomy

The latissimus dorsi (LD) is the **largest muscle in the body** and one of the most versatile donor muscles in reconstructive surgery.

#### Pedicle
- **Dominant pedicle:** Thoracodorsal artery (TDA) — branch of subscapular artery from axillary artery
- **Pedicle length:** 8–12 cm from axilla to muscle entry (makes it ideal as a **free flap** with a long pedicle)
- **Venous drainage:** Thoracodorsal vein → subscapular vein → axillary vein
- **Secondary supply (segmental):** Posterior intercostal arteries (segmental perforators from ribs 9–12 and lumbar arteries) — allows **reverse-flow latissimus** in rare cases
- **Angular artery:** Branch of thoracodorsal artery; supplies serratus anterior and the tip of the scapula — allows composite flap harvest with rib/serratus

#### Muscle
- **Origin:** Spinous processes T7–L5, posterior iliac crest, lower 3–4 ribs, inferior angle of scapula
- **Insertion:** Bicipital groove (intertubercular sulcus) of humerus
- **Motor nerve:** Thoracodorsal nerve (C6, C7, C8 — posterior cord of brachial plexus)
- **Function:** Shoulder extension, adduction, internal rotation

#### Mathes-Nahai Classification
- **Type V:** Dominant pedicle (thoracodorsal artery) + secondary segmental (posterior intercostal arteries)
- Can be elevated on dominant pedicle alone; secondary segmental arteries may be divided without compromise

### Flap Design Variants

| Variant | Composition | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Muscle only | LD muscle without skin | Bulk flap for dead space obliteration |
| Myocutaneous | LD + skin paddle (direct perforators) | Standard design; paddle up to 25 × 15 cm |
| Extended myocutaneous | LD + serratus anterior ± rib | Composite flap for complex defects |
| Free flap | LD ± skin, microsurgical anastomosis | Commonest use in H&N |
| Pedicled (to H&N) | Arc through axilla, tunnel under skin bridge | Limited by pedicle length for most H&N sites |
| Perforator flap (TDAP) | Thoracodorsal artery perforator flap | Skin only; muscle spared |

### Head and Neck Applications

#### Free LD Flap (Primary H&N Use)
The LD is primarily used as a **free flap** in head and neck surgery due to the limitations of pedicle reach.

| Defect | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| Tongue (hemiglossectomy/total glossectomy) | Large myocutaneous flap for total tongue; less favoured than ALT due to positioning difficulty |
| Scalp reconstruction (large defects > 100 cm²) | Muscle ± STSG; free anastomosis to superficial temporal or occipital vessels |
| Large cheek/parotid defects | After radical parotidectomy + skin excision |
| Pharyngeal reconstruction | As free flap when pedicled flaps unavailable |
| Skull base dead space obliteration | Free muscle flap to obliterate dural defects |

#### Pedicled LD Flap in H&N
- The arc of rotation of a pedicled LD reaches only the **posterior neck, mastoid region, and occiput**
- Route: axilla → subcutaneous tunnel over shoulder → posterior neck
- **Indications (pedicled):** Posterior neck defects after radical excision, posterior scalp coverage, post-auricular/mastoid region defects

### Advantages

- **Largest flap available** — largest surface area of any single donor site
- **Long reliable pedicle** (8–12 cm) — excellent for microsurgical anastomosis
- **Versatile:** Can include serratus anterior, rib, skin, or muscle alone
- **Donor site preserved function** in most patients — shoulder function largely maintained if thoracodorsal nerve not divided
- Sacrifice of nerve (denervation) allows muscle atrophy over time — reduces bulk in oral cavity reconstructions

### Disadvantages

| Disadvantage | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Position change | Requires lateral decubitus — adds operative time |
| Posterior donor scar | Visible scar on back; cosmetically acceptable |
| Seroma | Seroma rate 20–40% at donor site — drain required for 2 weeks |
| Wing deformity | Axillary fullness from pedicle |
| Limited pedicle for H&N pedicled use | Typically insufficient length to reach oral cavity anteriorly |
| Learning curve | Lateral decubitus setup and thoracodorsal anatomy |

### Donor Site Management

- Primary closure is usually achievable for paddles ≤10 cm wide
- Drains retained for minimum 2 weeks to prevent seroma
- Wide skin paddles (>12 cm) may require STSG to donor site
- Shoulder physiotherapy commences at 2 weeks post-operatively

### Functional Considerations

- The dominant upper extremity LD should be **preserved when possible** (athletes, manual workers, bilateral arm amputees)
- Thoracodorsal nerve division leads to progressive muscle atrophy — useful when reducing bulk in oral cavity over time
- Serratus anterior should be preserved (long thoracic nerve) to avoid winging of scapula

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The latissimus dorsi flap is a Mathes-Nahai Type V myocutaneous flap based on the thoracodorsal artery (branch of subscapular artery), with a long pedicle of 8–12 cm making it primarily useful as a free flap in head and neck surgery for large tongue, scalp, and cheek defects. As a pedicled flap its reach is limited to the posterior neck and mastoid region. Its principal limitations in H&N are the requirement for lateral decubitus positioning, high seroma rate, and posterior donor scar; its key advantage is the large available tissue volume with a long reliable pedicle for microsurgical reconstruction.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Thoracodorsal artery branches from the subscapular artery from the axillary artery — know this three-vessel chain for exams
> 2. Mathes-Nahai Type V = dominant pedicle (TDA) + secondary segmental (intercostal) — same as pectoralis major
> 3. Free LD is the preferred option for large scalp defects > 100 cm² — anastomose to superficial temporal or occipital vessels
> 4. Seroma is the most common donor site complication (20–40%) — keep drains for 2 weeks minimum
> 5. Lateral decubitus positioning adds ~30 minutes to operative time — factor into total anaesthesia time
> 6. Denervating the flap (dividing thoracodorsal nerve) allows progressive muscle atrophy — reduces bulk in tongue reconstructions over months
> 7. The angular artery (branch of TDA) supplies serratus anterior and rib — composite LD + rib or LD + serratus is possible
> 8. Pedicled LD only reaches the posterior neck/mastoid — not suitable for oral cavity/oropharynx without microsurgery
> 9. Preserve the dominant upper extremity LD in manual workers and athletes
> 10. The thoracodorsal artery perforator (TDAP) flap spares the muscle — use when muscle sacrifice is undesirable
