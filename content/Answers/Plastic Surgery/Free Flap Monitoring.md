## Free Flap Monitoring

### Critical Monitoring Period

The first **72 hours** are the highest-risk period for free flap failure:
- **90% of vascular compromises** occur within the first 24–48 hours
- Early detection (< 4 hours of compromise) is associated with 75% salvage rate
- Delays > 12 hours from compromise to re-exploration result in near-total flap loss

### Pathophysiology of Vascular Compromise

#### Arterial Compromise
- **Cause:** Anastomotic thrombosis, vasospasm, intimal flap, pedicle kinking, haematoma compression
- **Mechanism:** No inflow → ischaemia → tissue death
- **Clinical signs:** Flap pale, cool, mottled; **absent** capillary refill; **no** bleeding on pinprick; collapsed surface turgor
- **Doppler signal:** Absent or weak

#### Venous Compromise
- **Cause:** Venous thrombosis, compression by haematoma, pedicle twisting, anastomotic technical error
- **Mechanism:** Outflow obstruction → venous hypertension → arterial inflow cessation → ischaemia
- **Clinical signs:** Flap congested, **blue-purple**, oedematous; **brisk** capillary refill; **dark (venous)** bleeding on pinprick; tense turgor
- **Frequency:** Venous compromise is MORE common than arterial (approximately 60% of failures)
- **Doppler signal:** May initially have arterial signal with absent venous signal

### Clinical Monitoring (Gold Standard for Accessible Flaps)

#### Visual Assessment
| Sign | Arterial Failure | Venous Congestion | Normal |
|------|-----------------|------------------|--------|
| Colour | Pale/white | Blue-purple | Pink |
| Temperature | Cold | Warm initially → cold | Warm |
| Turgor | Soft/collapsed | Tense/oedematous | Normal |
| Capillary refill | Absent or slow (>3s) | Brisk (<1s) | 1–2 seconds |
| Pinprick bleed | Absent or very pale | Dark, rapid, venous | Bright red, moderate |

#### Monitoring Frequency Protocol
| Time Period | Frequency |
|------------|-----------|
| 0–24 hours | Every 15 minutes |
| 24–48 hours | Every 30 minutes |
| 48–72 hours | Hourly |
| Day 3–5 | 2–4 hourly |
| Day 5–7 | Nurse-initiated if concerns |

### Monitoring Devices

#### 1. Implantable Doppler Probe (Cook-Swartz Probe)
- **Most widely used for buried flaps** (e.g., total glossectomy, pharyngeal reconstruction)
- Probe is placed around the **venous anastomosis** at time of surgery
- Provides a continuous audible Doppler signal of venous flow
- Probe exits through skin as a 20-gauge wire; removed at bedside after **72 hours**
- **Sensitivity:** >97% for venous compromise detection; very sensitive
- **Limitation:** Only monitors at probe site; probe may dislodge; interpretation requires training
- **Advantage:** Continuous monitoring of buried flaps otherwise inaccessible to visual assessment

#### 2. Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (NIRS/StO₂ Monitoring)
- Measures regional oxygen saturation (rSO₂ or StO₂) of tissue non-invasively
- Sensor placed on flap surface; measures haemoglobin saturation (oxy:deoxy ratio)
- **Thresholds for alarm:**
  - Absolute: StO₂ < 30%
  - Relative: > 20% drop from baseline
- **Advantages:** Continuous, non-invasive, real-time; works for buried flaps with surface patch
- **Limitations:** Sensitive to ambient light, probe movement, and skin pigmentation
- Useful as **adjunct** to clinical monitoring

#### 3. Laser Doppler Flowmetry
- Measures red blood cell velocity in subdermal capillary plexus
- **Sensitive** — detects early ischaemia before clinical signs
- **Limitation:** High motion artifact; cannot distinguish arterial from venous; relative values only
- Primarily a research and adjunctive tool

#### 4. Tissue pH Monitoring
- Ischaemia → anaerobic metabolism → tissue acidosis
- Lactate probe or pH electrode placed in flap tissue
- Tissue pH < 7.0 or rising lactate = alarm threshold
- **Advantage:** Very early marker of ischaemia (precedes clinical signs by 30–60 minutes)
- **Limitation:** Invasive; calibration drift; not widely available clinically

#### 5. Microdialysis
- Samples interstitial fluid lactate/glucose ratio from flap tissue
- Rising lactate:glucose ratio indicates ischaemia
- **Primarily a research tool** — not standard clinical practice
- Can detect metabolic disturbance 30–60 minutes before clinical failure

#### Comparison of Monitoring Methods

| Method | Type | Buried Flap | Early Detection | Cost | Clinical Use |
|--------|------|------------|----------------|------|-------------|
| Clinical assessment | Visual/tactile | No | Good | Nil | Universal |
| Implantable Doppler | Venous flow | Yes | Excellent | Moderate | Widely used |
| NIRS/StO₂ | Oxygen saturation | Partial | Good | High | Increasing |
| Laser Doppler | Flow velocity | No | Very good | High | Research/adjunct |
| Tissue pH/lactate | Metabolic | Yes | Excellent | High | Research |
| Microdialysis | Metabolic | Yes | Excellent | Very high | Research |

### Re-exploration Decision

#### Indications for Immediate Re-exploration
- Any clinical sign of vascular compromise (colour change, capillary refill change, temperature drop)
- Loss of implantable Doppler signal
- NIRS drop > 20% from baseline
- Any deteriorating trend on monitoring

#### Timing and Salvage Rates
| Time from Compromise to Re-exploration | Salvage Rate |
|----------------------------------------|-------------|
| < 4 hours | ~75% |
| 4–8 hours | ~50% |
| 8–12 hours | ~25% |
| > 12 hours | <10% |

#### Re-exploration Protocol
1. Return to theatre urgently — do not delay
2. Release all compressive dressings and sutures
3. Identify and release pedicle (haematoma, kinking)
4. Assess anastomosis under microscopy
5. Thrombectomy if arterial: Fogarty catheter technique; heparin flush
6. Venous: Fogarty + streptokinase local infusion; redo anastomosis with vein graft if needed
7. Coupler device (venous): divide and redo with new coupler

### Post-operative Medical Management to Prevent Failure

| Measure | Rationale |
|---------|-----------|
| Anticoagulation (heparin/aspirin) | Reduce thrombosis risk at anastomosis; protocol varies by unit |
| Normothermia | Vasospasm occurs with hypothermia (<36°C) |
| Normovolaemia | Hypovolaemia → vasoconstriction → pedicle vasospasm |
| Head elevation at 30° | Reduces venous hypertension in flap |
| Avoid neck compression (dressings) | Pedicle compression risk |
| Dextran (low molecular weight) | Rheological agent; reduces red cell aggregation; reduces thrombosis risk |
| Normoglycaemia | Hyperglycaemia impairs wound healing |
| Avoid vasopressors | Vasoconstrictors reduce flap perfusion |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Free flap monitoring is essential in the first 72 hours when 90% of vascular complications occur; venous compromise is more common than arterial and presents with flap congestion, dark bleeding, and brisk capillary refill. Clinical monitoring (colour, capillary refill, pinprick) remains the gold standard for accessible flaps, while the implantable Doppler probe (Cook-Swartz) is the preferred method for buried flaps, detecting venous compromise with >97% sensitivity. Re-exploration within 4 hours of detected compromise gives a 75% salvage rate, making rapid recognition and timely return to theatre the most critical factors in free flap salvage.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Venous compromise is more common than arterial (60% vs 40%) — dark, rapid bleeding on pinprick is the cardinal sign
> 2. The implantable Doppler probe is placed around the VENOUS anastomosis — this is where compromise is most likely
> 3. Re-exploration threshold should be LOW — "when in doubt, explore" is the guiding principle
> 4. Salvage rate is time-dependent: 75% within 4 hours, declining sharply thereafter
> 5. NIRS threshold for alarm: absolute StO₂ < 30% OR relative drop > 20% from baseline
> 6. Avoid hypotension, hypothermia, and vasopressors — all cause pedicle vasospasm and increase failure risk
> 7. The implantable Doppler wire exits through skin and is removed at **72 hours** at the bedside after the highest-risk period
> 8. Quarter-hourly observations in the first 24 hours — requires dedicated nursing on a specialised ward
> 9. Haematoma is the most preventable cause of pedicle compression — meticulous haemostasis and drain placement are essential
> 10. When exploring a compromised flap, always check the **pedicle first** (kinking/haematoma) before revising the anastomosis
