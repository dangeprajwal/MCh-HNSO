## Maxillary Defect Classification

### Aramany Classification (1978) — Prosthodontic Focus
Designed for restorable prosthetic planning based on remaining dental arches:
- **Class I**: resection on one side of midline; posterior teeth intact on opposite side
- **Class II**: unilateral posterior resection; anterior teeth and contralateral arch intact
- **Class III**: central palatal resection; bilateral anterior teeth intact
- **Class IV**: bilateral posterior resection; no anterior teeth
- **Class V**: posterior bilateral with anterior involvement
- **Class VI**: anterior only

Aramany classification guides obturator design and retention planning but does not address vertical extent.

### Brown/Shaw Classification (2010) — Current Standard

The Brown/Shaw classification uses two axes — **vertical** (Roman numerals I–VI) and **horizontal** (lowercase a–c) — and is the most widely used contemporary classification for surgical planning.

#### Vertical Component
| Class | Description | Orbital Involvement |
|---|---|---|
| I | Maxillectomy not causing oronasal fistula (palate intact) | No |
| II | Low maxillectomy (below orbital floor) | No |
| III | Subtotal maxillectomy (involves orbital floor; orbit preserved) | Floor involved |
| IV | Total maxillectomy with orbital exenteration | Yes — exenteration |
| V | Orbitomaxillary defect (orbital contents preserved) | Orbit preserved with soft tissue defect |
| VI | Nasomaxillary defect | Nasal bones/septum |

#### Horizontal Component
| Class | Description |
|---|---|
| a | ½ or less of hard palate (unilateral) |
| b | Greater than half of hard palate |
| c | Complete bilateral hard palate \| total palate loss |

**Clinical application**: Brown Class IIa = low maxillectomy, half palate → obturator option; Brown IIb or III and above → free flap generally required.

## Prosthetic Obturator

### Types of Obturators
1. **Immediate surgical obturator**: placed intraoperatively at time of resection from pre-operative dental impression; allows oral intake and speech immediately; acts as wound dressing; replaced at 5–7 days
2. **Transitional obturator** (4–8 weeks): replaces immediate obturator as wound matures; used during healing; adjusted as tissue volume changes
3. **Definitive obturator** (6–12 months post-surgery, after RT completion): custom-fabricated acrylic/vinyl prosthesis; optimally retentive; requires involvement of maxillofacial prosthodontist

### Retention Mechanisms
- **Tooth-retained**: clasps on remaining natural teeth — best retention; requires minimum 2–3 teeth in opposing arch
- **Implant-retained**: osseointegrated implants in remaining maxillary bone or zygomatic implants; for edentulous patients
- **Anatomical retention**: undercuts in nasal floor, cheek skin flap remnants; limited

### Advantages of Obturator
- **Oncological surveillance**: cavity directly visible at each obturator removal; recurrence can be detected early
- **No additional surgery**: avoids free flap morbidity
- **Reversible**: if recurrence requires further resection, no flap tissue lost
- **Speech and swallowing**: immediate rehabilitation from day 1 (surgical obturator)
- **Shorter operating time and hospital stay**
- **Lower immediate risk** in medically frail patients

### Limitations / Disadvantages of Obturator
- **Retention problems**: displacement during eating/speaking; particularly with bilateral defects (no contralateral teeth)
- **Hypernasality**: residual VPI (velopharyngeal insufficiency) if obturator incompletely seals posterior defect
- **Patient burden**: must be removed and cleaned 2–3× daily; psychologically distressing
- **Regular refabrication**: tissue changes (post-RT atrophy, natural aging) require repeated modifications or new prostheses
- **Large defects (>half palate, IIb)**: poor retention; speech rarely normalises
- **Edentulous patients**: no tooth retention; implants needed or poor outcomes
- **Orbital exenteration**: cannot volumetrically replace orbit with prosthesis alone

## Surgical Reconstruction with Free Flaps

### Indications for Surgical (Free Flap) Reconstruction
- **Brown Class IIb and above** (bilateral or >half palate)
- **Edentulous patients** (no dental retention for obturator)
- **Orbital exenteration** (Class IV/V): volumetric orbital reconstruction mandatory
- Patients **unable to maintain** obturator hygiene (cognitive/physical limitations)
- Patients who **refuse prosthesis** (psychological or cultural)
- Failed obturator with persistent fistula or inadequate speech despite prosthesis
- Need for rigid orbital floor support (Class III) — soft tissue + titanium mesh
- Young patients with long life expectancy (better long-term outcome with permanent reconstruction)

### Free Flap Options for Maxillary Reconstruction

#### Anterolateral Thigh Flap (ALT) — Most Commonly Used for Class II–III
- Thin or thick depending on perforator dissection
- Can be folded: one surface for palatal lining, one for external cutaneous coverage
- Long pedicle (LCFA descending branch, 8–12 cm)
- Large available skin territory
- Versatile — multiple skin paddles, can include fascia, vastus lateralis muscle
- **Best for**: Class IIa/b soft tissue only; Class III with titanium mesh orbital floor reconstruction

#### Free Fibula — Class IIa/III with Bone Requirement
- Provides rigid bony framework for dental implants
- Configured to reconstruct alveolus ± orbital floor with multiple osteotomies
- Skin paddle for palatal lining
- **Best for**: Brown Class IIa + bone needed, edentulous; Class III orbital floor support

#### Radial Forearm Free Flap (RFFF) — Thin Palatal Lining
- Thin, pliable; excellent for palatal lining reconstruction
- Limited bulk — not useful for orbital exenteration or large volumetric defects
- Long pedicle; reliable perforators
- **Best for**: Class I or small IIa with thin lining need

#### DCIA (Deep Circumflex Iliac Artery) — Bone Height Match
- Iliac crest provides greater vertical bone height (25–30 mm) than fibula
- Preferred for **short posterior alveolar defects** where dental implants are planned and vertical height is critical
- Internal oblique muscle can be harvested for soft tissue lining (oromandibular combined defects)
- Higher donor morbidity (iliac crest: hernia risk, gait disturbance)

### Combined Reconstruction
- **ALT + titanium mesh**: ALT provides soft tissue; titanium mesh provides orbital floor support (Class III)
- **Fibula + ALT**: fibula for bone/alveolus; ALT for additional soft tissue/orbital exenteration fill

## Decision Algorithm: Obturator vs Free Flap

```
Maxillectomy defect
│
├── Brown Class I (palate intact / alveolus only)
│   └── Obturator (if teeth present) or RFFF
│
├── Brown Class IIa (low, <½ palate)
│   ├── Teeth present → Obturator
│   └── Edentulous / large alveolar need → ALT or fibula
│
├── Brown Class IIb (low, >½ palate)
│   └── Free flap strongly preferred (ALT)
│
├── Brown Class III (orbital floor, orbit preserved)
│   └── Free flap + titanium mesh (ALT or fibula)
│
└── Brown Class IV (orbital exenteration)
    └── Free flap mandatory (ALT for volume; fibula if bone needed)
```

## Speech and Swallowing Outcomes

| Reconstruction | Nasality | Swallowing | Dental Rehabilitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Obturator (good fit) | Near-normal | Good | Via prosthesis teeth |
| ALT free flap | Variable VPI | Good | Implants if bone present |
| Fibula free flap | Good with palatal design | Good | Implants in fibula bone |
| No reconstruction | Severe hypernasal, aspiration | Poor | Not possible |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Maxillary reconstruction decisions hinge on the Brown/Shaw classification: defects of Class IIa with intact dentition are well-served by prosthetic obturators offering oncological surveillance and immediate rehabilitation, while Class IIb and above generally require free flap reconstruction (ALT for soft tissue, fibula for bony alveolar support) due to inadequate retention and the failure of prostheses to volumetrically replace larger defects. The obturator remains valuable as an immediate surgical device in all cases to maintain palatal seal intraoperatively and in the early post-operative period.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The Brown/Shaw classification (2010) is the current standard and must be quoted in exams; Aramany (1978) is prosthodontic and guides obturator design, not surgical planning.
> 2. The surgical obturator is placed in every patient at the time of surgery regardless of definitive reconstruction plan — it maintains speech and swallowing from day 1 and acts as a wound dressing.
> 3. Orbital exenteration (Brown Class IV) mandates free flap reconstruction; no obturator can replace orbital volume and provide corneal protection.
> 4. For Brown Class IIb and above, obturator retention is mechanically inadequate because bilateral support teeth are absent — free flap is nearly always preferred.
> 5. The key advantage of the obturator over free flap is direct visual surveillance of the resection cavity — recurrence is detectable at each prosthesis removal.
> 6. ALT flap is the most versatile option for soft-tissue maxillary reconstruction: a single long pedicle, large skin territory, and ability to be folded for palatal/cutaneous bilaminar closure.
> 7. When planning implant rehabilitation after maxillary free flap, fibula provides superior cortical bone stock compared to ALT (no bone); fibula with implants gives the best long-term dental prosthetic outcomes.
> 8. Zygomatic implants (placed in zygoma) are an alternative retention solution for obturators in patients without adequate residual alveolar bone, avoiding free flap surgery.
