## Anatomy of the Floor of Mouth

The **floor of mouth (FOM)** is the crescentic space between the tongue and the mandibular arch, containing:

### Muscles
- **Mylohyoid**: primary FOM muscle; forms a muscular diaphragm spanning from the mylohyoid line of the mandible to the hyoid body and contralateral mylohyoid (raphe); innervated by the nerve to mylohyoid (CN V3)
- **Geniohyoid**: paired muscles from genial tubercle to hyoid body; lie superior to mylohyoid; elevate hyoid during swallowing
- **Anterior belly of digastric**: contributes to FOM depression and mandibular stabilisation

### Neurovascular Structures
- **Lingual nerve** (CN V3): runs in the lateral FOM along the medial surface of the mandible, dipping below the submandibular duct before ascending to the tongue
- **Lingual artery**: branch of external carotid; runs in the lateral FOM beneath hyoglossus; major blood supply to tongue
- **Submandibular (Wharton's) duct**: exits submandibular gland, runs anteriorly through the lateral FOM to open at the **sublingual papilla** (caruncle) adjacent to the lingual frenulum

### Functional Significance
- Enables tongue mobility (bolus formation, oral propulsion, speech articulation)
- Maintains mandibular arch integrity
- Provides conduit for salivary flow (Wharton's duct)
- Tethering of tongue to FOM mucosa (via reconstruction scarring) severely impairs speech and swallowing

## Defect Classification and Reconstruction Options

### Small Defects (<2 cm): Primary Closure or Secondary Intention
- Mucosal only: heal by secondary intention (well-vascularised area; 4–6 weeks)
- Superficial muscle involvement with adequate margins: primary closure
- Preserve tongue mobility by avoiding direct suture to tongue undersurface

### Moderate Defects (2–5 cm, No Mandible Involved): RFFF or Submental Flap
- **Radial forearm free flap (RFFF)**: gold standard (**see below for detail**)
- **Submental island flap**: submental artery (branch of facial artery); thin skin; limited by oncological concerns (submental lymph nodes in flap territory; oncologically questionable for N+ disease)
- **Nasolabial flap** (superiorly-based): for anterior FOM; limited arc of rotation; two stages if used as interpolation flap

### Large Defects (>5 cm ± Mandible): RFFF + Fibula or ALT
- **RFFF alone**: for large mucosal-only or soft tissue FOM defects
- **Fibula osteocutaneous flap**: if mandible simultaneously resected (composite defect); skin paddle from fibula lines FOM
- **ALT flap**: very large defects requiring more bulk (extended FOM to tongue base); thinned ALT can provide thin pliable lining similar to RFFF

## Radial Forearm Free Flap — Gold Standard for FOM

### Why RFFF Is Preferred for FOM Reconstruction
1. **Thin and pliable**: minimal bulk allows residual tongue mobility to be preserved; excessive bulk is the enemy of speech and swallowing function
2. **Long pedicle** (radial artery up to 20 cm; cephalic vein): easy reach to neck recipient vessels
3. **Reliable vascularity**: consistent anatomy; low flap failure rate (<5%)
4. **Dual-layer capability**: can be folded on itself for double-layer closure (FOM floor + ventral tongue resurfacing)
5. **Sensate option**: medial cutaneous nerve of forearm can be coapted to lingual nerve for sensory restoration

### Donor Site Assessment
- **Allen's test mandatory** pre-operatively: confirms adequate ulnar artery collateral flow to the hand; test positive = ulnar artery adequate = safe to harvest
- **Modified Allen's test**: compress both radial and ulnar arteries; release ulnar only; hand flushes pink within 5 seconds = safe

### Pedicle
- **Radial artery**: 2.0–3.5 mm diameter; anastomosed to facial artery or superior thyroid artery (end-to-end) or external carotid (end-to-side)
- **Cephalic vein** (superficial) or **venae comitantes** (deep): venous drainage; cephalic provides larger-calibre vein and often preferred

### Harvest Technique
1. Allen's test confirmed
2. Arm exsanguinated with Esmarch bandage; tourniquet applied
3. Flap designed on volar forearm; palmaris longus tendon identified (do not harvest — preserves hand tendon mechanics unless tendon graft needed)
4. Lateral incision first: identify cephalic vein, raise flap laterally
5. Radial artery dissected from proximal to distal, ligating fasciocutaneous perforators
6. Medial incision; tourniquet deflated; assess flap perfusion
7. Flap pedicle ligated distally and divided; flap transferred
8. **Donor site**: STSG from thigh (primary closure only if width <3 cm); volar splint 5–7 days to prevent flexion contracture; forearm scar typically acceptable

### Inset Technique for FOM
1. Flap thinned of excess fat if needed (preserve dermal blood supply)
2. Mucosal-to-flap suturing: interrupted vicryl; attach flap margins to FOM mucosal edges and ventral tongue margin
3. **Critical**: suture flap to ventral tongue in a way that preserves tongue excursion — avoid creating a broad scar bridge between tongue body and mandible
4. Two-layer (folded) inset: for through-and-through FOM + ventral tongue resection
5. Pedicle brought through tunnel under mandible to reach neck vessels

## Management of Wharton's Duct

- **Preserve if possible**: identify duct during FOM dissection; cannulate with lacrimal probe; preserve anatomy
- **If duct sacrificed**: marsupialise proximal duct stump (open new ductal orifice into FOM reconstruction); prevents ranula formation
- **Ligation** (if marsupialisation impractical): causes submandibular salivary suppression; gland may atrophy; transient xerostomia on ipsilateral side; acceptable if contralateral duct and parotid intact

## Functional Outcomes After FOM Reconstruction

### Speech
- Depends primarily on **tongue mobility**: if >50% mobile tongue retained, speech intelligibility near-normal
- Tongue tethering to reconstruction = decreased articulation (especially lingual consonants: /l/, /n/, /d/, /t/)
- Sensate RFFF improves articulation over time (sensory feedback loop)

### Swallowing
- **Oral phase**: affected most — bolus formation and propulsion impaired if tongue tethered
- **Pharyngeal phase**: less affected (FOM reconstruction does not directly impair pharyngeal musculature)
- Aspiration risk related to extent of tongue base resection, not FOM alone
- Post-RT dysphagia (fibrosis of FOM + tongue base) is major long-term morbidity

### Post-radiation Changes
- RT to FOM causes mucosal fibrosis, reduced tongue mobility, xerostomia
- Thin pliable RFFF less affected by radiation fibrosis than thicker flaps
- Swallowing rehabilitation (SLP) essential post-RT

## Composite FOM + Mandible Reconstruction

When FOM carcinoma invades mandible (requiring segmental mandibulectomy), the **free fibula osteocutaneous flap** reconstructs both simultaneously:
- Fibula segment for mandible
- Skin paddle for FOM lining (orientated superiorly for intraoral coverage)
- If additional bulk needed: second flap or chimeric design

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Floor of mouth reconstruction demands thin, pliable tissue that restores mucosal lining without tethering the tongue — the radial forearm free flap is the gold standard because it uniquely combines thinness, a long reliable pedicle, dual-layer folding capacity, and sensate potential. When mandibular resection is concurrent, the fibula osteocutaneous flap reconstructs bone and provides an integrated skin paddle for FOM lining, eliminating the need for a second flap in most cases.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Allen's test must be performed bilaterally before radial forearm flap harvest; a positive test (ulnar artery adequate) on the non-dominant arm is preferred donor.
> 2. The most functionally devastating complication of FOM reconstruction is tongue tethering — always suture the flap's superior edge to mobile tongue mucosa with interrupted absorbable sutures that allow 1–2 cm of tongue excursion.
> 3. Wharton's duct should be marsupialised (not simply ligated) when sacrificed to prevent symptomatic ranula formation in the early post-operative period.
> 4. For through-and-through defects (FOM + ventral tongue), the RFFF can be folded at its midpoint — one surface reconstructs FOM floor, the other ventral tongue surface; both supplied by the single radial artery pedicle.
> 5. Palmaris longus tendon is preserved during RFFF harvest (it lies medial to radial artery) — preserving it maintains hand mechanics and avoids donor morbidity.
> 6. In composite FOM + mandibular defects, a single fibula osteocutaneous flap should be the first choice: one anastomosis, one donor site, simultaneous bone and soft-tissue reconstruction.
> 7. Post-radiation FOM reconstruction carries higher risk of wound dehiscence and fistula; RFFF handles radiation better than bulky muscle flaps due to its independent perforator-based vascularity.
> 8. Sensory reinnervation of RFFF (medial cutaneous nerve of forearm to lingual nerve) is achievable and improves oral function over 12–18 months; this should be performed routinely when nerve ends are available.
