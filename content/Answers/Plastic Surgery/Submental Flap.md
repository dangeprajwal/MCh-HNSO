## Submental Island Flap

### Anatomy

The submental flap is a fasciocutaneous (± myocutaneous) flap based on the **submental artery**, a branch of the **facial artery**.

- **Pedicle:** Facial artery → submental artery (arises from FA at the lower border of mandible, courses medially in the submental triangle)
- **Course:** Submental artery runs along the inferior border of the mylohyoid, crosses the anterior belly of digastric, and divides into cutaneous perforators to the chin/submental skin
- **Venous drainage:** Submental vein → facial vein → internal jugular vein
- **Skin territory:** Submental triangle — skin from chin to hyoid, width 7–16 cm
- **Flap composition:** Skin + platysma + anterior belly of digastric (included for pedicle protection)
- **Pivot point:** Point where facial artery crosses lower border of mandible

### Technique (Martin 1993)

1. **Patient position:** Supine, neck extended; head ring support
2. **Design:** Mark elliptical skin paddle in the submental crease (horizontal ellipse), dimensions up to **16 × 7 cm**
3. **Incision:** Incise along inferior margin first; elevate deep to platysma from inferior direction
4. **Muscle inclusion:** Include anterior belly of digastric and mylohyoid fascia with flap to protect submental artery perforators
5. **Pedicle identification:** Identify submental artery at its origin from facial artery at mandibular border; preserve 2–3 cm of facial artery above origin for added pedicle length
6. **Division of superior attachment:** Divide platysma and cervical skin superior to paddle; island flap on pedicle
7. **Arc of rotation:** Rotate 180° on facial artery pivot; arc reaches oral cavity, floor of mouth, tongue, cheek, lower lip, chin
8. **Tunnelling:** Create subcutaneous tunnel or external skin bridge division (two-stage) to access oral cavity
9. **Inset:** Suture skin paddle to mucosal defect with absorbable sutures

### Dimensions

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Skin paddle length | Up to 16 cm |
| Skin paddle width | Up to 7 cm |
| Pedicle length | 5–7 cm (facial artery to FA origin) |
| Arc of rotation | 180° |
| Reach | Oral cavity, tongue, lower lip, cheek |

### Advantages

- **Excellent colour and texture match** for lower face and oral cavity (same subunit)
- Thin and pliable — good functional restoration for tongue and floor of mouth
- Single-stage procedure
- No microsurgery required
- **Inconspicuous donor scar** — lies in submental crease
- Hair-bearing skin in men may be advantageous for beard zone lower lip defects
- Simultaneous harvest with neck dissection (same field)

### Disadvantages

- Limited bulk — inadequate for large volumetric defects
- **Short pedicle** — limited arc of rotation; cannot reach posterior oral cavity reliably
- **Oncological concern** (see below) — submental lymph nodes in flap territory
- Not suitable after ipsilateral level I neck dissection (disrupts submental artery)
- Not suitable in N+ oral cavity cancer with bilateral level I dissection
- Hair-bearing skin is a problem for women and for posterior oral cavity coverage

### Oncological Safety — Critical Concern

This is a frequently examined topic in MCh HNSO.

The submental flap harvests tissue from the **submental triangle**, which is a sub-region of **Level Ia** in cervical nodal staging. Submental lymph nodes are primary drainage nodes for:
- Anterior floor of mouth
- Tip of tongue
- Lower lip
- Chin

**Risk:** If submental lymph nodes are not adequately cleared or if an occult node is inadvertently included in the flap, this constitutes:
1. Leaving behind nodal tissue in the field
2. Transposing potentially tumour-bearing nodes into the oral cavity reconstruction

**Published oncological concerns (Patel 2007 and subsequent series):**
- Submental lymph nodes may be harvested within the flap in 12–30% of cases
- Oncological compromise is a theoretical risk in oral cavity cancer (N0 or N+)
- Some authors mandate frozen section of flap-contained nodal tissue

**Safe use conditions:**
- Oral cavity cancer with **contralateral** submental flap (avoids ipsilateral nodal territory)
- T1 tumours with histologically confirmed N0 neck after selective dissection
- Submental nodes cleared from flap base before transposition
- Non-oncological indications (trauma, benign disease)

**Contraindications from oncological standpoint:**
- T3/T4 oral cavity cancer with ipsilateral neck dissection
- N+ disease at Level I on ipsilateral side
- Recurrent oral cancer

### Indications

| Defect | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| Floor of mouth (anterior) | Primary indication — thin pliable skin |
| Tongue tip and ventral tongue | Single-stage, excellent match |
| Lower lip reconstruction | Best colour match |
| Chin and mental region | Aesthetic unit match |
| Cheek (medial) | After tumour resection or trauma |
| After marginal mandibulectomy | Thin flap fits mucoperiosteal defects well |

### Modifications

- **Reverse submental flap:** Based on retrograde flow via submental vein; extends reach to upper face/nose (unreliable, rarely used)
- **Extended submental flap:** Include more lateral skin (lateral submental, submandibular skin) on extended pedicle
- **Contralateral submental flap:** Used when ipsilateral level I is dissected; pivot from contralateral facial artery

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The submental island flap, described by Martin in 1993, is based on the submental artery (branch of facial artery) and provides thin, pliable skin with excellent colour match for oral cavity and lower facial reconstruction without requiring microsurgery. Its principal limitation in oral cancer surgery is the oncological concern that Level Ia submental lymph nodes are included within the flap territory, potentially compromising the oncological adequacy of neck dissection; the flap is therefore safest when used contralaterally or in strictly N0 disease after confirmed Level Ia clearance. It cannot access the posterior oral cavity due to pedicle length constraints.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Submental artery is a branch of facial artery, not the lingual artery — exam favourite distinction
> 2. The anterior belly of digastric is included in the flap to protect the neurovascular pedicle running along its deep surface
> 3. Oncological contraindication: do NOT use ipsilateral submental flap in N+ oral cancer or where Level I has been dissected
> 4. Contralateral submental flap is oncologically safer when ipsilateral neck has been dissected
> 5. Donor scar lies in the submental crease and is cosmetically acceptable — this is an advantage over pectoralis major flap
> 6. Pivot point is the facial artery crossing the mandible — rotation is around this point, not the submental origin
> 7. The flap can be harvested simultaneously with neck dissection in the same operative field, saving time
> 8. Hair-bearing skin in men (submental beard) requires epilation if used for intraoral mucosal lining
> 9. Frozen section of harvested nodal tissue within flap base should be considered in oral cancer cases
> 10. Pedicle length is 5–7 cm — inadequate for defects beyond anterior oral cavity; free flap preferred for posterior defects
