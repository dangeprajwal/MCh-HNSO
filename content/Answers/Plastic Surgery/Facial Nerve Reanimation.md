## Indications for Facial Nerve Reanimation

Facial nerve (CN VII) sacrifice or palsy in H&N surgery occurs in:
- **Total parotidectomy** for malignancy (parotid SCC, high-grade carcinoma, nerve-invasive disease)
- **Temporal bone resection** (squamous cell carcinoma of external auditory canal)
- **Skull base surgery** (acoustic neuroma — vestibular schwannoma; surgical sacrifice or post-radiation palsy)
- **Radical neck dissection** (marginal mandibular branch sacrifice for level I clearance)
- **Post-radiation facial palsy** (delayed — radiation-induced nerve damage)
- **Congenital** or idiopathic (Bell's palsy sequelae — discussed in Salivary/Ear sections)

---

## Timing Classification and Decision Framework

Timing of reanimation fundamentally determines which techniques are available:

### Immediate (at time of nerve sacrifice)
- Nerve ends identifiable in the operative field
- **Primary neurorrhaphy** or **nerve grafting** possible
- **Best outcomes** — nerve reconstruction before Wallerian degeneration begins
- Every effort should be made to identify and mark nerve ends at the time of parotidectomy/resection for immediate or early reconstruction

### Early (0–12 months after palsy)
- Distal nerve ends still identifiable by electrical stimulation
- Motor end plates (neuromuscular junctions) still viable — nerve reinnervation effective
- **Nerve grafting** + **cross-facial nerve graft (CFNG)** feasible
- Motor end plate viability: begins to degenerate 12–18 months after denervation without reinnervation

### Late (>12–18 months after palsy)
- Motor end plates degenerated and replaced by fibrous tissue → denervated muscle fibrosis
- Nerve grafting to native facial musculature futile (no viable end-plates to accept reinnervation)
- Only options: **static procedures** or **dynamic muscle transfer** (new muscle with its own motor end plates)

---

## Static Procedures (No Movement — Tone and Symmetry Restoration)

Static procedures restore facial symmetry at rest but do not restore movement. Used when:
- Late presentation (>18 months)
- Elderly frail patients not fit for prolonged microsurgery
- Supplementary to other dynamic procedures

### Gold Weight Implantation (Upper Eyelid)
- **Indication**: lagophthalmos / exposure keratopathy from paralytic ectropion
- Gravity-assisted passive eye closure
- Implant: 0.8–1.6 g titanium or gold weight; placed in pretarsal plane via upper lid crease incision
- Size selection: trial weights pre-operatively; complete closure without ptosis
- **Most important static procedure** in facial palsy management

### Fascia Lata Sling (Static Facial Suspension)
- Fascia lata strips from lateral thigh (15–20 cm)
- Tunnelled from zygomatic arch to modiolus (corner of mouth) and nasolabial fold
- Suspends ptotic cheek and drooping commissure
- Restores facial symmetry at rest; no movement
- Long-lasting; some fascial elongation over years

### Additional Static Procedures
- **Alar base repositioning**: for nasal alar collapse
- **Brow lift**: brow ptosis (frontalis paralysis); direct brow lift or endoscopic
- **Lower lip wedge resection**: for lip asymmetry (marginal mandibular branch loss)
- **Lateral tarsorrhaphy**: eye closure augmentation alongside gold weight

## Dynamic Reanimation — Nerve-Based Procedures

These restore movement (voluntary or spontaneous) by reinnervating existing or transplanted musculature.

### Primary Neurorrhaphy (Direct Repair)
- **Indication**: immediate reconstruction when nerve divided with clean ends; gap <5 mm
- **Technique**: epineural suture with 8-0 or 9-0 nylon; tension-free coaptation essential
- **Results**: 70–80% of pre-injury function; begins recovering at 3–4 months; full by 12–18 months
- Tension is the enemy of neurorrhaphy; even small tension dramatically reduces outcomes

### Nerve Grafting (Cable Graft)
- **Indication**: gap >5 mm that cannot be bridged without tension; most cases of intraoperative nerve sacrifice
- **Donor nerves**:
  - **Sural nerve** (most common): lateral foot/ankle; sensory only; 35–40 cm harvestable; multiple fascicles; low donor morbidity (small area of dorsal foot numbness)
  - **Greater auricular nerve**: same operative field (neck); 6–8 cm; useful for short gaps; sensory
  - **Medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve (MABN)**: from arm; alternative
- **Technique**: cable grafts interposed between proximal and distal CN VII stumps; epineural repair at each end; multiple fascicles matched (diameter matching)
- **Results**: similar to primary repair for short gaps; functional recovery begins 6–12 months; 12–18 months for full recovery

### Hypoglossal-Facial Nerve Anastomosis (XII-VII)
- **Indication**: proximal CN VII unavailable (temporal bone, skull base resection); no proximal stump; late reconstruction with viable distal musculature (<18 months from onset)
- **Principle**: CN XII (hypoglossal — tongue motor) redirected as motor source to CN VII distal stump; reinnervates native facial musculature
- **Full XII-VII anastomosis**: proximal CN XII divided and anastomosed to CN VII stump; ipsilateral tongue atrophy and fasciculation inevitable (hemiglossal atrophy)
- **Partial (split) XII-VII**: 30–50% of CN XII fascicles redirected to CN VII (nerve split longitudinally); preserves ipsilateral tongue function; less muscle bulk for reinnervation
- **Interpositional sural nerve jump graft** (Terzis modification): sural graft from CN XII branch to CN VII; preserves CN XII continuity; reduced hypoglossal morbidity
- **Outcomes**: facial tone restored; voluntary movement (tongue-driven smile — bite-activated); **synkinesis** common (mass movement, not isolated expressions); recovery 6–12 months
- Functional recovery grade: House-Brackmann III–IV

### Cross-Facial Nerve Graft (CFNG)
- **Indication**: distal CN VII stump present; donor ipsilateral CN VII unavailable; used as first stage before gracilis free muscle transfer
- **Principle**: sural nerve graft tunnelled from **contralateral functioning CN VII** (buccal branch) to **ipsilateral paralysed distal CN VII stump** or to donor nerve of transferred muscle
- **Mechanism**: spontaneous/emotional smile preserved from contralateral hemisphere; axons grow across sural bridge (12–18 months for axon traverse)
- **Axon regrowth**: confirmed by Tinel's sign at nerve end; Martini test (gentle percussion on emerging end); neurophysiology
- **Used with gracilis** (see below): CFNG performed 12 months before gracilis to provide donor axons; gracilis coapted to CFNG end at second stage

## Dynamic Reanimation — Muscle Transfer

Required when native facial muscles are not viable (>18 months denervation) or when large muscle mass needed.

### Gracilis Free Muscle Transfer — Gold Standard for Total/Long-Standing Palsy

**Indication**: irreversible facial palsy >12–18 months; total facial palsy after total parotidectomy + RT; best outcomes for spontaneous emotional smile.

#### Anatomy
- **Muscle**: thin, long, medial thigh muscle (adductor compartment)
- **Pedicle**: medial circumflex femoral artery (gracilis perforator) or gracilis branch from profunda femoris; 6–8 cm pedicle length
- **Motor nerve**: anterior branch of obturator nerve; 10–12 cm nerve length harvestable

#### Two-Stage Protocol (with CFNG — Best for Spontaneous Smile)
1. **Stage 1** (CFNG placement): sural nerve graft from contralateral buccal branch of CN VII; tunnelled subcutaneously across upper lip to ipsilateral cheek; buried; 12–18 months for axon traverse
2. **Stage 2** (gracilis transfer): gracilis harvested with obturator nerve; inseted from zygomatic arch to commissure and upper lip; motor nerve coapted to **CFNG end**; microvascular anastomosis to facial artery/vein
3. **Reinnervation**: 6–12 months for gracilis muscle reinnervation; commissure excursion typically 1–2 cm
4. **Result**: spontaneous, emotionally-driven smile (cortical pathway from contralateral hemisphere); best cosmetic outcome

#### Single-Stage Protocol (Masseteric Nerve — Masseter-Driven Smile)
1. Gracilis harvested and inseted as above
2. Motor nerve coapted to **masseter nerve** (motor branch of CN V3 entering deep surface of masseter at sigmoid notch)
3. Smile is bite/jaw-clench driven (masseteric contraction → gracilis contraction → smile)
4. Single-stage: no waiting for CFNG axon traverse; faster recovery (commissure motion at 6 months)
5. With training, patients can learn to dissociate bite from smile (cortical adaptation)
6. Less natural spontaneous smile compared to CFNG protocol; better for older patients or those who cannot wait 2–3 years for two-stage

#### Summary: CFNG vs Masseteric Nerve
| Feature | CFNG + Gracilis | Masseteric Nerve + Gracilis |
|---|---|---|
| Stages | Two (18 months apart) | Single |
| Smile type | Spontaneous/emotional | Bite-driven (voluntary) |
| Total time to motion | 3–4 years | 6–12 months |
| Cosmetic quality | Best | Good |
| Best for | Young patients, bilateral palsy | Older patients, single stage preferred |

### Temporalis Muscle Transfer (Rubin Modification)
- **Principle**: temporalis (CN V3 motor — deep temporal nerve) transposed to modiolus
- **Traditional**: temporalis turned over zygomatic arch; bulk unsatisfactory; arch deformity
- **Rubin (1997)**: elongated temporalis tendon (via fascia lata graft extension) inset into modiolus and commissure; does not require arch transposition
- **McLaughlin modification**: direct tendon pull from coronoid process to commissure
- Provides single vector of pull (smile); no microsurgery needed
- Outcomes: less excursion and dynamic quality than gracilis; reserved for patients not suitable for microsurgery

## Outcomes Summary

| Procedure | Timing | Recovery | Spontaneous Smile |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary neurorrhaphy | Immediate | 70–80% function; 12–18 months | Yes |
| Nerve graft | Immediate–early | 60–70% function; 12–18 months | Yes |
| XII-VII anastomosis | Early (<18 months) | HB grade III–IV; 6–12 months | No (tongue-driven) |
| CFNG + Gracilis | Late | HB grade III; 3–4 years total | Yes (emotional) |
| Masseteric + Gracilis | Late | HB grade III; 12 months | No (voluntary bite) |
| Temporalis transfer | Late | Moderate; 6–12 months | No |
| Static sling | Any | Immediate symmetry | No |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Facial nerve reanimation after CN VII sacrifice is stratified by timing: immediate/early cases (<18 months) permit nerve-based reconstruction (primary repair, cable graft, or XII-VII transfer) that reinnervates native facial musculature, while late cases require dynamic muscle transfer with the gracilis free muscle flap (gold standard), driven by either the masseteric nerve (single-stage, bite-driven smile) or cross-facial nerve graft (two-stage, spontaneous emotional smile). Static procedures — particularly gold weight implantation for lagophthalmos and fascia lata sling for commissure suspension — address functional and cosmetic deficits immediately and complement dynamic reconstruction.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The motor end plate survives 12–18 months of denervation: nerve grafting after this window is futile for native facial musculature; this is the hard cutoff for nerve-based vs muscle transfer reconstruction.
> 2. At the time of parotidectomy, if CN VII is sacrificed, immediately mark/tag the proximal and distal nerve stumps with a non-absorbable suture — this reduces the difficulty of reconstruction enormously, whether performed at the same or a later operation.
> 3. CFNG + gracilis produces the most natural smile (spontaneous, emotionally driven, separate from jaw motion) but requires 3–4 years total — patient selection and counselling are critical.
> 4. Masseteric nerve is the preferred single-stage motor source: it is consistently located at the sigmoid notch, easily accessible in the same operative field, provides high axon count, and gives reliable gracilis motion within 6 months.
> 5. XII-VII anastomosis (partial split preferred): preserves 60–70% of CN XII continuity; reduces hemiglossal atrophy; still provides adequate motor drive to CN VII for tone and voluntary (tongue-associated) facial movement.
> 6. Gold weight (0.8–1.6 g) for lagophthalmos must be sized individually with trial weights pre-operatively; undersizing causes persistent exposure keratopathy; oversizing causes cosmetically unacceptable ptosis.
> 7. Synkinesis (mass movement, e.g., eye closure when smiling) is an expected and unavoidable consequence of nerve graft reinnervation; botulinum toxin to hyperactive muscles can effectively manage synkinesis post-recovery.
> 8. For bilateral facial palsy (e.g., after bilateral acoustic neuroma in NF2), gracilis free muscle transfer is performed bilaterally using both masseteric nerves as motor sources; spontaneous smile is not achievable bilaterally (no intact contralateral CN VII for CFNG).
