## Classification of Flaps in Head and Neck Reconstruction

### I. Mathes-Nahai Muscle Blood Supply Classification (1981)

This is the most commonly examined classification in reconstructive surgery.

| Type | Pattern | Key Feature | Examples |
|------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **I** | Single dominant pedicle | One artery supplies entire muscle | Tensor fascia lata, Gastrocnemius, Abductor hallucis |
| **II** | Dominant + minor pedicle(s) | One dominant + one or more minor vessels | Gracilis, Sternocleidomastoid, Platysma, Soleus |
| **III** | Dual dominant pedicles | Two dominant pedicles (either can perfuse entire muscle) | Gluteus maximus, Rectus abdominis (superior + inferior epigastric), Serratus anterior |
| **IV** | Segmental supply | Multiple equal-sized segmental arteries; no single dominant | Sartorius, Tibialis anterior, External oblique |
| **V** | Dominant + secondary segmental | One dominant pedicle + secondary segmental vessels | Latissimus dorsi, Pectoralis major, Internal oblique |

#### Clinical Significance
- **Type I:** Most reliable — divide all secondary supply safely; entire muscle perfused by single artery
- **Type II:** Can use dominant pedicle alone; must not divide dominant vessel; minor pedicle division safe
- **Type III:** Can base on either dominant pedicle; allows split-muscle flaps
- **Type IV:** Each segment depends on its own vessel — unreliable for transposition flaps; poor for myocutaneous design
- **Type V:** Dominant pedicle perfuses majority; secondary segmental vessels may be divided safely

#### Head and Neck Relevant Muscles

| Muscle | Type | Dominant Pedicle |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| Pectoralis major | V | Thoracoacromial artery (pectoral branch) |
| Latissimus dorsi | V | Thoracodorsal artery |
| Sternocleidomastoid | II | Occipital artery (dominant), superior thyroid artery, thyrocervical trunk |
| Platysma | II | Submental artery (dominant), facial artery perforators |
| Trapezius | II | Transverse cervical artery (dominant) |
| Temporalis | III | Deep temporal arteries (anterior + posterior from maxillary artery) |

### II. Classification by Tissue Composition

| Flap Type | Components | Examples in H&N |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Cutaneous | Skin only | Local skin flaps (rotation, advancement) |
| Fasciocutaneous | Skin + deep fascia | RFFF, SCF, nasolabial, deltopectoral |
| Myocutaneous | Muscle + skin (± fat) | PMMC, LD myocutaneous |
| Muscle only | Muscle alone | LD muscle flap for dead space |
| Osseous | Bone only | Fibula (cortical), iliac crest |
| Osteocutaneous | Bone + skin | Free fibula, DCIA/DIEP |
| Osteomyocutaneous | Bone + muscle + skin | LD + rib composite |
| Sensate | Nerve included for reinnervation | RFFF with lateral antebrachial cutaneous nerve |

### III. Classification by Vascularity

| Pattern | Description | Examples |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Random-pattern | No defined axial vessel; depends on subdermal plexus; L:W ≤ 3:1 | Local rotation flaps, V-Y advancement |
| Axial-pattern | Named artery within flap axis; allows longer dimensions | RFFF, supraclavicular flap, deltopectoral flap |
| Perforator flap | Skin supplied by perforators from deep source vessel; source vessel preserved | ALT perforator, DIEP, TDAP |

### IV. Regional and Free Flaps in Head and Neck Surgery

#### Pedicled Regional Flaps (Exam Essentials)

| Flap | Pedicle | Reach | Best Indication |
|------|---------|-------|----------------|
| Pectoralis major myocutaneous | TAA pectoral branch | Oral cavity, pharynx, neck | Carotid coverage, fistula repair, salvage |
| Supraclavicular island flap | Transverse cervical artery | Oral cavity, oropharynx, skull base | Single-stage thin coverage |
| Submental island flap | Facial artery → submental artery | Oral cavity, lower face | FOM, tongue, lower lip |
| Deltopectoral flap | IMA perforators 2–4 | Upper neck, lower face | Historical; partial pharynx |
| Temporalis muscle flap | Deep temporal artery | Orbit, palate, skull base | Orbital reconstruction, palatal fistula |
| Infrahyoid myocutaneous flap | Superior thyroid artery | Anterior oral cavity, FOM | Alternative to RFFF in thin patients |
| Nasolabial flap | Angular/facial artery | Nasal ala, anterior intraoral | Nasal reconstruction, FOM |
| Trapezius flap | Transverse cervical artery | Posterior neck, skull base | Posterior scalp/neck coverage |

#### Free Flaps in Head and Neck (Most Used)

| Flap | Pedicle | Dimensions | Best H&N Application |
|------|---------|------------|---------------------|
| Radial forearm free flap (RFFF) | Radial artery + cephalic/venae comitantes | 15 × 8 cm skin; pedicle 20 cm | Tongue, FOM, pharynx — thin pliable skin |
| Anterolateral thigh (ALT) | Descending branch lateral circumflex femoral artery | 25 × 15 cm; pedicle 8 cm | Large tongue, pharynx, large cheek |
| Free fibula osteocutaneous | Peroneal artery | Up to 25 cm bone; skin paddle | Mandibular reconstruction |
| Deep circumflex iliac artery (DCIA) | Deep circumflex iliac artery | 9 cm bone; large skin paddle | Maxillary/mandibular reconstruction |
| Latissimus dorsi free flap | Thoracodorsal artery | Up to 25 × 15 cm; pedicle 10 cm | Scalp, large tongue, total glossectomy |
| Jejunal free flap | Superior mesenteric arcade | Variable length; tubular | Circumferential pharyngo-oesophageal reconstruction |
| Rectus abdominis free flap | Deep inferior epigastric artery | Large flap; long pedicle | Large scalp/skull base defects |

### V. Fasciocutaneous vs Myocutaneous Flaps

| Feature | Fasciocutaneous | Myocutaneous |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Bulk | Thin | Bulky |
| Sensory potential | Better (cutaneous nerves preserved) | Reduced |
| Dead space obliteration | Poor | Excellent |
| Functional restoration (tongue) | Superior (pliable) | Inferior (stiff) |
| Post-radiation use | Less reliable | More reliable (muscle tolerates radiation) |
| Donor morbidity | Lower | Higher (muscle loss) |
| Examples | RFFF, ALT, SCF, nasolabial | PMMC, LD myocutaneous |

### VI. Taylor-Palmer Angiosome Concept

- An **angiosome** is a three-dimensional block of tissue supplied by a single source artery
- Defined by Taylor and Palmer (1987) from cadaveric injection studies
- Foundation of perforator flap design — identify source vessel, elevate tissue connected via perforators without sacrificing source artery
- **Choke vessels:** Reduced-calibre anastomotic vessels between adjacent angiosomes; opening of choke vessels allows expanded territory in "delay" phenomenon

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The Mathes-Nahai classification divides muscles into five types based on their dominant and secondary blood supply patterns, with Type V (dominant + secondary segmental) including the two most important H&N flaps — pectoralis major and latissimus dorsi. In contemporary H&N reconstruction, free fasciocutaneous flaps (RFFF, ALT) are preferred for tongue and pharyngeal reconstruction due to thinness and pliability, while pedicled PMMC remains essential for carotid coverage and fistula repair. Perforator flaps preserve the source vessel and reduce donor morbidity, representing the current evolution of reconstructive technique.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Type V Mathes-Nahai includes both pectoralis major and latissimus dorsi — the two most important H&N pedicled flaps
> 2. Type IV (sartorius) is unreliable for transposition — segmental supply means any elevation divides essential vessels
> 3. RFFF is the workhorse free flap for tongue/FOM: radial artery pedicle, thin pliable skin, long pedicle (20 cm), sensory reinnervation possible
> 4. ALT is preferred over RFFF for large defects (> 6 cm) — more tissue available and no forearm functional loss
> 5. Jejunal free flap is the only reliable option for circumferential pharyngo-oesophageal reconstruction; provides mucosal lining
> 6. Free fibula osteocutaneous flap is the gold standard for mandibular reconstruction — up to 25 cm bone, multiple osteotomies, dental implant compatible
> 7. Perforator flaps (DIEP, ALT perforator, TDAP) allow source vessel preservation — reduces donor morbidity significantly
> 8. The angiosome concept explains why distal portions of random-pattern flaps undergo necrosis — choke vessels limit perfusion beyond one angiosome
> 9. Deltopectoral flap (IMA perforators) was replaced by PMMC for most H&N uses — it remains useful for lower face/upper neck when PMMC has been used previously
> 10. In vessel-depleted neck (post-radiation + prior neck dissection), internal jugular vein may be absent — plan alternative venous recipient (external jugular, cephalic transposition)
