## Defect Classification and Reconstruction Strategy

Tongue reconstruction planning is guided by the **extent of resection** as a proportion of total tongue volume (oral tongue + tongue base):

| Defect | Extent | Goal | Preferred Flap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Partial (hemiglossectomy) | <50% | Restore mobility of residual tongue | RFFF (thin, pliable) |
| Subtotal | 50–75% | Bulk + mobility | ALT (thinned) or RFFF |
| Near-total | 75–90% | Bulk to fill dead space + laryngeal suspension | ALT (bulky) |
| Total glossectomy | 100% | Volume fill, airway, aspiration prevention | ALT or rectus abdominis; tracheostomy; +/- laryngeal suspension or laryngectomy |

The principle is:
- **Partial defects**: prioritise **mobility** — thin flap allows residual tongue to function; avoid excessive bulk
- **Large/total defects**: prioritise **bulk** — fills dead space, reduces aspiration, maintains upper aerodigestive tract configuration; also facilitates laryngeal elevation by palate contact

## Radial Forearm Free Flap (RFFF) — Gold Standard for Partial Glossectomy

### Indications
- Hemiglossectomy (partial glossectomy, <50%)
- Subtotal glossectomy when bulk not the primary need
- Floor of mouth + tongue combined defects (folded flap)

### Properties
- **Thin and pliable**: hallmark advantage; allows residual tongue tip and body to move freely against palate and teeth
- **Sensate potential**: medial cutaneous nerve of forearm → lingual nerve coaptation; restores discriminative sensation; improves bolus management and speech articulation
- **Long pedicle**: radial artery (2.0–3.5 mm); easy neck anastomosis
- **Reliable**: <5% flap failure in experienced hands

### Donor Site
- Pre-operative Allen's test (ulnar artery competence)
- STSG to volar forearm (primary closure only if <3 cm width)
- Palmaris longus tendon preserved

### Functional Advantage
In partial glossectomy, the pliable RFFF does not impede residual tongue movement. Patients with RFFF for hemiglossectomy achieve near-normal speech intelligibility and oral swallowing when residual tongue volume is >50%.

## Anterolateral Thigh (ALT) Flap — Preferred for Subtotal and Total Glossectomy

### Indications
- Subtotal glossectomy (50–75%)
- Near-total and total glossectomy (>75%)
- When large volume is needed to fill FOM dead space

### Anatomy
- **Pedicle**: descending branch of lateral circumflex femoral artery (LCFA); pedicle length 8–12 cm; diameter 2.0–3.0 mm
- **Perforators**: musculocutaneous perforators through vastus lateralis, or septocutaneous perforators through intermuscular septum; map with Doppler 10 cm above and 3 cm lateral to midpoint of thigh
- **Sensory nerve**: lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (LFCN) of thigh — can be harvested and coapted to lingual nerve for sensate reconstruction
- **Chimeric design**: skin + vastus lateralis muscle can be harvested on separate perforators from LCFA for additional bulk

### Thinning the ALT
For subtotal defects where bulk would impair residual function:
- **Primary thinning**: excision of excess subcutaneous fat at harvest; preserve dermal plexus
- **Secondary thinning**: 3–6 months later; surgical debulking
- Allows ALT to approach RFFF in pliability for moderate defects

### Why ALT for Total Glossectomy
- **Volume**: fills 80–100 mL dead space preventing pharyngeal collapse
- **Palate contact**: bulky flap can reach soft palate, maintaining partial velopharyngeal contact even without functional tongue movement
- **Aspiration prevention**: fills the oral cavity and FOM; reduces laryngeal penetration by providing a physical barrier during swallowing
- **Tracheostomy management**: near-total and total glossectomy patients universally require temporary (often permanent) tracheostomy; laryngeal suspension (suturing laryngeal remnant to mandible) helps maintain airway elevation during swallowing

## Functional Outcomes After Tongue Reconstruction

### Speech Outcomes (Q31 Specific)
- **Normal intelligibility**: achievable if >50% native tongue preserved (partial glossectomy + RFFF)
- **Moderate impairment**: subtotal defects (50–75%); intelligibility 60–80% with rehabilitation
- **Severe impairment**: near-total/total; speech largely unintelligible; augmentative communication devices often needed
- **Critical factor**: mobile tongue tip contact with alveolar ridge and palate for consonant production (/l/, /t/, /d/, /n/) — even 2–3 cm of mobile native tongue tip improves intelligibility substantially
- **Sensate reconstruction**: improves articulatory feedback and speech rehabilitation speed

### Swallowing Outcomes
- **Oral phase**: bolus formation impaired proportional to resection; residue in FOM common
- **Propulsion**: tongue base drives bolus into pharynx; loss of tongue base (base of tongue = posterior tongue) severely impairs pharyngeal swallow initiation
- **Aspiration risk**: increases markedly with near-total/total resection; silent aspiration frequent; modified barium swallow essential
- **PEG dependency**: ~20–30% of total glossectomy patients long-term PEG-dependent
- **Laryngeal suspension**: suturing residual hyoid/larynx to mandible or hyoid suspension; improves laryngeal elevation; reduces aspiration in total glossectomy

### Post-radiation Functional Decline
- Late radiation effects (12–24 months post-RT): FOM/tongue fibrosis, reduced compliance; flap tissue less affected than native mucosa but fibrosis in surrounding tissue reduces overall function
- Late dysphagia (LRD): radiation-induced pharyngeal dysphagia worsens over years; 25–40% of HNC survivors develop severe dysphagia 5 years post-RT
- **Aspiration pneumonia**: leading cause of late non-cancer death in HNC survivors; surveillance and SLP essential

### Quality of Life
- RFFF for hemiglossectomy: best QoL outcomes; near-normal diet, intelligible speech
- ALT for subtotal/total: significant QoL impairment; diet restricted; communication impaired; tracheostomy affects QoL
- Multimodal rehabilitation (SLP + dietitian + tracheostomy care) essential from post-op day 1

## Laryngeal Suspension in Total Glossectomy

When total glossectomy is performed and larynx preserved:
- Residual tongue muscle stumps sutured to mandible (mandibular suspension)
- Hyoid suspended to mandible with permanent suture or wire
- Elevation of larynx during swallowing facilitated mechanically
- Reduces aspiration without laryngectomy
- Total laryngectomy sometimes preferred for total glossectomy in tumours with extensive tongue base involvement to eliminate aspiration risk definitively

## Rectus Abdominis Free Flap — Large Volume Alternative

- **TRAM / MS-TRAM / DIEP**: large volume; used when ALT perforators absent or inadequate
- Provides skin + muscle bulk
- Longer operation; higher donor morbidity (abdominal wall weakness)
- Less commonly used than ALT in current practice; ALT has largely replaced

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Tongue reconstruction flap selection is governed by defect extent: the radial forearm free flap is the gold standard for partial glossectomy (<50%) because its thinness and pliability preserve residual tongue mobility for speech and swallowing, while the anterolateral thigh flap is preferred for subtotal and total defects where bulk is needed to fill dead space, maintain palate contact, and reduce aspiration. Functional outcomes deteriorate progressively with increasing resection extent and are further compromised by post-operative radiotherapy.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The single most important factor for speech outcome after partial glossectomy is preserving a mobile tongue tip: even 2–3 cm of native mobile tip dramatically improves intelligibility by enabling alveolar ridge contact.
> 2. For total glossectomy, ALT bulk improves swallowing by providing a physical mass that contacts the soft palate and partially restores the bolus-driving mechanism; do not thin excessively.
> 3. Sensate RFFF (medial cutaneous nerve of forearm to lingual nerve) improves both swallowing safety and speech articulation by restoring sensory feedback — perform routinely when technical conditions allow.
> 4. Near-total and total glossectomy patients have ~20–30% long-term PEG dependency; counsel patients pre-operatively and ensure SLP involvement from the start.
> 5. Laryngeal suspension (mandibular or hyoid suspension) should be performed at the time of total glossectomy when larynx is preserved, to mechanically augment swallowing laryngeal elevation.
> 6. Late radiation dysphagia (LRD) worsens progressively beyond the first year; all patients receiving post-op RT need structured swallowing rehabilitation and aspiration surveillance even if initial swallowing is adequate.
> 7. ALT thinning (primary, at harvest) brings it close to RFFF pliability for subtotal defects where both volume and mobility are needed; secondary thinning at 3–6 months is an option if initial bulk is excessive.
> 8. The LFCN (lateral femoral cutaneous nerve) from the ALT can be coapted to the lingual nerve for sensate large-volume reconstruction — while outcomes are less predictable than RFFF sensory reconstruction, it is worthwhile for near-total defects.
