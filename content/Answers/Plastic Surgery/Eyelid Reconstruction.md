## Anatomy of the Eyelid

### Upper Eyelid Anatomy
- **Tarsal plate** (superior tarsus): 10 mm vertical height; 29 mm width; dense fibrous plate providing structural support; contains Meibomian glands (modified sebaceous)
- **Levator palpebrae superioris (LPS)**: striated muscle (CN III); primary upper lid elevator; aponeurosis inserts into upper tarsus; responsible for upper lid crease
- **Müller's muscle** (superior tarsal muscle): smooth muscle; sympathetically innervated (CN — sympathetic chain); secondary 2 mm lid elevation; Horner's syndrome impairs this
- **Orbital septum**: fibrous layer fusing levator aponeurosis; fat compartments deep to septum
- **Orbicularis oculi** (preseptal and pretarsal portions): CN VII innervation; closes eyelid; responsible for blink reflex and tear pump
- **Upper lid crease**: skin fold 8–10 mm above lid margin (marks LPS aponeurosis insertion)

### Lower Eyelid Anatomy
- **Tarsal plate** (inferior tarsus): 5 mm vertical height; 28 mm width
- **Capsulopalpebral fascia** (lower lid retractor): equivalent of LPS in lower lid; extends from inferior rectus and inferior oblique; maintains inferior fornix depth
- **Orbicularis oculi**: same as upper lid
- **Conjunctiva**: posterior mucosal surface; lines tarsus (tarsal conjunctiva) and fornix (fornical conjunctiva)

### Two-Lamella Concept
Eyelid reconstruction always considers two lamellae:
- **Posterior lamella**: tarsus + conjunctiva (internal; contacts globe)
- **Anterior lamella**: skin + orbicularis (external)

Reconstructing both lamellae is mandatory for full-thickness eyelid defects. Each lamella must have its own blood supply; placing two grafts (no blood supply) against each other is a technique failure.

## Defect Classification and Reconstruction Algorithm

### Lower Eyelid Defects

| Defect Size | Technique | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| <25% | Primary closure | Lateral canthotomy for additional laxity |
| 25–50% | Lateral canthotomy + cantholysis (inferior limb) | Releases lateral tarsus; tension-free closure |
| 50–75% | **Tenzel semicircular flap** | Lateral rotation-advancement; single stage |
| >75%, total | **Hughes tarsoconjunctival flap** + FTSG | Gold standard; two-stage |
| Large with medial extension | **Mustardé rotation flap** | Cheek rotation for anterior lamella |

### Upper Eyelid Defects

| Defect Size | Technique | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| <25% | Primary closure ± canthotomy | |
| 25–50% | Primary closure + lateral cantholysis | |
| >50% | **Cutler-Beard flap** (lower lid bridge flap) | Two-stage; full-thickness lower lid donor |
| Large/complex | Free flap or forehead flap | Rarely needed; for orbital exenteration coverage |

## Tenzel Semicircular Flap

**Indication**: Lower eyelid defects 50–75%.

**Design**:
- Semi-circular incision from lateral canthus, curving superiorly into upper eyelid (towards brow)
- Incision in skin and orbicularis; canthotomy + cantholysis of inferior crus releases lateral tarsus
- Flap advanced medially to fill defect

**Advantages**: single-stage; fast; uses similar skin tissue; good cosmesis.

**Disadvantages**: limited advancement (<50% defect if wide); lateral canthal blunting; lower lid laxity over time.

---

## Hughes Tarsoconjunctival Flap — Gold Standard for Lower Lid

**Indication**: Lower eyelid defects >75%; most important technique to know.

### Anatomy of the Flap
- Donor: **upper eyelid** (posterior lamella — tarsus + conjunctiva)
- Upper lid tarsal plate provides structural support (posterior lamella replacement)
- Conjunctiva provides the mucosal lining in contact with cornea

### Technique (Two-Stage Procedure)

**Stage 1**:
1. Assess lower lid defect (full-thickness, >75%)
2. Evert upper eyelid over Desmarres retractor
3. Incise through conjunctiva and tarsus 4 mm from upper lid margin (preserve 4 mm margin to prevent upper lid notching and trichiasis)
4. Dissect tarsoconjunctival flap from LPS aponeurosis superiorly; develop pedicle on superior tarsal artery and Müller's muscle (conjunctival pedicle)
5. Advance flap inferiorly to fill lower lid posterior lamella defect
6. Suture to lower lid conjunctival edges (mucosal-to-mucosal)
7. Anterior lamella (skin + orbicularis): reconstruct with **FTSG** from upper eyelid (contralateral), retroauricular skin, or local cheek skin flap
8. Eye closed during pedicle phase (eye cannot open as upper and lower lids are joined)

**Stage 2** (6–8 weeks later):
1. Divide pedicle at 6–8 weeks (tarsoconjunctival bridge)
2. Upper portion: return to upper lid; close defect
3. Lower portion: contoured and inset to form new lower lid margin
4. Skin refinements as needed

### Advantages
- Provides true tarsal plate for structural support + conjunctiva for corneal protection
- Best posterior lamella reconstruction available
- Highly reliable vascular supply

### Disadvantages
- **Two-stage** (eye closed between stages); not suitable for single-eyed patients or patients who cannot tolerate monocular vision for 6–8 weeks
- Requires contralateral or ipsilateral donor eyelid

---

## Mustardé Rotation Flap

**Indication**: Large lower lid defects (>75%) + adjacent cheek involvement; medial canthal + lower lid.

**Design**: Large cheek rotation flap based on subcutaneous vessels from cheek; chevron incision from lateral canthus towards the preauricular area.

**Features**: provides anterior lamella (skin + subcutaneous tissue); posterior lamella must be separately reconstructed (tarsoconjunctival flap or chondromucosal graft).

**Key** for total lower lid loss with cheek skin involvement.

---

## Cutler-Beard Flap (Upper Lid Reconstruction)

**Indication**: Upper eyelid defects >50%.

**Technique** (Two-stage):
1. Full-thickness lower lid flap harvested below lower lid margin (5 mm below lid margin — "bridge" preserved)
2. Flap advanced under the preserved lower lid bridge and up into upper lid defect
3. Posterior lamella (conjunctiva/cartilage graft) inserted separately
4. Division at 6–8 weeks; lower lid margin restored

**Risk**: lower lid margin ischaemia and notching from the "bridge" compression.

---

## Other Techniques for Specific Scenarios

### Anterior Lamella Reconstruction Options
- **FTSG** (from upper eyelid, retroauricular, supraclavicular, or contralateral lower eyelid): thin, non-hair-bearing; best skin match
- **Fricke flap**: forehead transposition/interpolation; used for anterior lamella; hair-bearing if taken from lateral forehead → depilate first
- **Cheek advancement**: for inferolateral defects (Mustardé-type advancement)

### Posterior Lamella Reconstruction Options (when tarsus + conjunctiva not available)
- **Nasal septal mucoperiosteum**: cartilage (structural support equivalent to tarsus) + mucosa (conjunctival equivalent); harvested from nasal septum
- **Hard palate mucoperiosteum**: alternative posterior lamella substitute
- **Tarsoconjunctival graft from contralateral upper eyelid**: best substitute when same-eye tarsal plate not available

### Gold Weight Implant (For Lagophthalmos)
- After facial nerve palsy or CN VII sacrifice → incomplete eye closure → **exposure keratopathy**
- Gold weight (0.8–1.6 g) implanted in preseptal plane of upper eyelid
- Gravity assists eye closure (passive); does not restore blink reflex
- Adjustable by exchanging for heavier/lighter weight
- Titanium weights also available

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Eyelid reconstruction requires simultaneous reconstruction of both the posterior lamella (tarsus + conjunctiva, for corneal protection) and anterior lamella (skin + orbicularis, for coverage); placing two avascular elements together violates the core principle. The Hughes tarsoconjunctival flap from the ipsilateral upper eyelid is the gold standard for lower lid defects exceeding 75%, providing true tarsal structure and mucosal lining in a two-stage procedure, while Tenzel's semicircular flap handles 50–75% defects in a single stage.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The two-lamella principle is inviolable: always reconstruct posterior and anterior lamellae separately; each must have independent blood supply; never appose two avascular elements (graft against graft).
> 2. The Hughes tarsoconjunctival flap preserves at least 4 mm of upper lid tarsus at the lid margin — below this, upper lid trichiasis and notching result; this 4 mm margin is the key technical safety parameter.
> 3. After Hughes flap, the patient's eye is closed for 6–8 weeks; this is unacceptable in single-eyed patients (other eye blind) — use alternative posterior lamella (nasal septal mucoperichondrium or hard palate) to allow single-stage reconstruction.
> 4. Nasal septal mucoperiosteum or hard palate mucoperiosteum is the best substitute for tarsal plate + conjunctiva when ipsilateral upper lid tarsus is unavailable; both provide structural cartilage + mucosal lining.
> 5. FTSG for anterior lamella should come from upper eyelid (ideal match), contralateral lower eyelid, or retroauricular skin — these are the most colour-matched, thin, non-hair-bearing donor sites.
> 6. Gold weight implantation (0.8–1.6 g) for lagophthalmos after CN VII sacrifice: size determined by placing trial weights and assessing closure; titanium weights preferred in irradiated patients.
> 7. Tenzel's semicircular flap (50–75% defects) requires lateral canthotomy + inferior cantholysis to release the lateral canthal tendon — without cantholysis the advancement is insufficient.
> 8. For total lower lid reconstruction combining posterior lamella (Hughes tarsoconjunctival) and anterior lamella (Mustardé cheek rotation), plan the skin advancement vector before harvesting the tarsoconjunctival flap to avoid geometric conflict.
