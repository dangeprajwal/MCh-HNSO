## Nasolabial Flap

### Overview

The nasolabial flap is a **local fasciocutaneous flap** harvested from the nasolabial fold and adjacent cheek. It is one of the most versatile local flaps in facial and intraoral reconstruction, with two principal designs based on pivot point:

1. **Superiorly based** — pivot near alar base; based on angular/facial artery perforators
2. **Inferiorly based** — pivot near oral commissure level; random-pattern supply

### Anatomy

#### Blood Supply
- **Superiorly based flap:** Angular artery (terminal branch of facial artery) and infraorbital artery perforators from the medial cheek subdermal plexus — reliable axial territory
- **Inferiorly based flap:** Relies on subdermal plexus in random-pattern fashion; less reliable than superiorly based design; supplied by facial artery perforators from the lower face
- **Venous drainage:** Facial vein tributaries

#### Surface Anatomy
- The nasolabial fold represents a strong skin crease between the cheek and upper lip
- Skin in this region is thick, sebaceous, and well-vascularised
- The fold overlies the levator labii superioris and zygomaticus minor muscles
- Donor scar is placed within or along the nasolabial fold — concealed in the natural crease

### Types of Nasolabial Flap

#### 1. Superiorly Based Nasolabial Flap
- **Pivot point:** Near alar base (medial cheek, adjacent to nasal ala)
- **Blood supply:** Angular artery + infraorbital artery perforators (axial-pattern superiorly)
- **Applications:**
  - Nasal alar reconstruction (primary indication)
  - Nasal vestibule and columella
  - Medial cheek defects
  - Upper lip vermilion/skin defects
- **Design:** Skin paddle 3–5 cm × 1.5–3 cm along nasolabial fold; pivot at alar base; rotates superiorly/medially
- **Single-stage** if rotated onto facial surface; **two-stage** if tunnelled into nasal vestibule (divide pedicle at 3 weeks)

#### 2. Inferiorly Based Nasolabial Flap
- **Pivot point:** At or below oral commissure level
- **Blood supply:** Random-pattern from facial artery perforators — length-to-width ratio must be respected (≤3:1)
- **Applications:**
  - Anterior floor of mouth
  - Ventral tongue tip
  - Lower lip defects
  - Retromolar trigone (anterior)
- **Design:** Skin paddle along nasolabial fold, pivot inferiorly; tunnelled subcutaneously under cheek skin to reach intraoral defect; **two-stage** (divide at 3 weeks) or **one-stage** (divide subcutaneous tissue bridge)
- Less reliable than superiorly based design due to random-pattern distal supply

#### 3. Bipedicle Nasolabial Flap
- Both ends based — used for upper lip reconstruction
- Limited use; mostly historical

### Surgical Technique — Inferiorly Based (Intraoral Application)

1. **Patient position:** Supine, head neutral or slightly extended
2. **Design:** Mark nasolabial fold; design skin paddle (3–4 cm wide, 6–8 cm long) with pivot point at commissure level
3. **Incision:** Incise lateral margin first; elevate flap in subcutaneous plane deep to SMAS/superficial musculoaponeurotic system; avoid damaging the cheek muscles
4. **Tunnelling:** Create subcutaneous tunnel through cheek from pivot point to intraoral defect; width ≥ flap width to prevent compression
5. **Passage:** Pass flap through tunnel into oral cavity
6. **Inset (Stage 1):** Suture skin paddle to oral mucosal defect; skin surface faces the oral cavity (becomes lining)
7. **Pedicle division (Stage 2, ~3 weeks):** Divide pedicle at oral commissure; trim excess flap at commissure and in nasolabial fold; close skin incision

### Advantages

| Advantage | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Excellent colour/texture match | Facial skin for facial reconstruction; thin cheek skin for intraoral |
| Inconspicuous donor scar | Lies within natural nasolabial fold |
| Minimal functional morbidity | No muscle sacrifice; no functional deficit |
| Single-surgeon procedure | No complex planning |
| Can be combined bilaterally | Two nasolabial flaps for wider defects |
| Easily repeatable | Second nasolabial flap possible if first fails or too small |

### Disadvantages

| Disadvantage | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Limited reach | Cannot access posterior oral cavity, oropharynx |
| Limited area per flap | Narrow paddle — inadequate for large defects |
| Two-stage for intraoral use | Second operation required for pedicle division |
| Visible contour change | Nasolabial fold deepening or dog-ear at pivot |
| Random-pattern (inferiorly based) | Less reliable; tip necrosis possible |
| Not suitable for large defects | Need regional or free flap for defects > 6 cm |

### Indications in Head and Neck Surgery

| Defect | Flap Design |
|--------|-------------|
| Nasal alar reconstruction | Superiorly based |
| Nasal vestibule/columella | Superiorly based, two-stage |
| Anterior floor of mouth | Inferiorly based, two-stage tunnelled |
| Ventral tongue tip | Inferiorly based |
| Retromolar trigone (anterior) | Inferiorly based |
| Medial cheek defects | Superiorly based rotation |
| Lower lip small defects (<1/3 width) | Inferiorly based |
| Upper lip defects | Superiorly based or bilateral |

### Comparison with Buccinator Myomucosal Flap

| Feature | Nasolabial Flap | Buccinator Myomucosal Flap |
|---------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Tissue type | External skin | Intraoral mucosa + buccinator muscle |
| Lining quality | Good (external skin) | Excellent (mucosal — same as oral cavity) |
| Donor site | Nasolabial fold (external) | Buccal mucosa (intraoral) |
| Sensation | Some (random sensory) | Better sensory preservation |
| Best indication | Nasal/facial \| anterior intraoral | Posterior intraoral, soft palate, tongue |
| Limitation | External donor visible | Limited width; pedicle from facial artery |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The nasolabial flap is a versatile local fasciocutaneous flap available in superiorly based (angular artery, reliable) and inferiorly based (random-pattern) designs. The superiorly based design is ideal for nasal alar and medial cheek reconstruction as a single-stage procedure, while the inferiorly based tunnelled design provides thin skin coverage for anterior intraoral defects in a two-stage procedure. Its principal advantages are inconspicuous donor scar within the nasolabial fold, excellent colour match for facial reconstruction, and minimal donor morbidity; its main limitation is restricted reach that precludes use for posterior oral cavity or oropharyngeal defects.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Superiorly based nasolabial flap = angular artery supply (axial-pattern) — reliable; inferiorly based = random-pattern — less reliable
> 2. For intraoral reconstruction, the skin surface becomes the lining — epithelialisation from skin makes it suitable for oral cavity lining
> 3. Two-stage technique for tunnelled design: Stage 1 = inset; Stage 2 = pedicle division at ~3 weeks after neovascularisation
> 4. The nasolabial donor scar is cosmetically excellent — the fold deepens with aging and the scar blends naturally
> 5. Length-to-width ratio for random-pattern inferiorly based flap should not exceed 3:1 — exceeding this risks tip necrosis
> 6. Can raise **bilateral nasolabial flaps** simultaneously for larger anterior oral cavity defects
> 7. Baker's *Local Flaps in Facial Reconstruction* is the definitive reference — know the superiorly vs inferiorly based distinction
> 8. For nasal alar reconstruction, design the flap in the same aesthetic subunit; folding the tip of the flap creates alar rim support
> 9. The nasolabial flap cannot access defects beyond the first molar/retromolar — use regional or free flap for posterior defects
> 10. Dog-ear at pivot point is common — plan z-plasty or back-cut correction at Stage 2
