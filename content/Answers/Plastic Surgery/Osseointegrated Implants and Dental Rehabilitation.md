## Definition and Biology of Osseointegration

**Osseointegration** (Brånemark, 1952–1965): direct structural and functional connection between living bone and the surface of a load-bearing titanium implant, without intervening fibrous connective tissue.

### Biological Sequence
1. **Titanium surface** exposed to blood at time of placement
2. **Protein adsorption**: plasma proteins coat TiO₂ oxide layer within seconds
3. **Platelet activation**: inflammatory cascade; growth factor release (BMP-2, TGF-β, PDGF)
4. **Osteoblast recruitment**: migration and attachment to titanium surface (2–4 weeks)
5. **Woven bone deposition**: periimplant new bone formation
6. **Lamellar bone maturation**: remodelling to dense cortical bone contact (3–6 months)
7. **Result**: direct bone-to-implant contact (BIC ratio >70% at osseointegration)

### Implant Surface Science
- **Machined (smooth) surface**: early Brånemark implants; 3–6 months osseointegration required
- **Rough surface** (sandblasted, acid-etched — SLA; or anodised — TiUnite): accelerated osseointegration; loading possible at 6–8 weeks in non-irradiated bone
- **Titanium alloy (Ti-6Al-4V)**: superior mechanical strength; used for wide-diameter implants

## Timing of Implant Placement in H&N Reconstruction

### Primary (Immediate) Implant Placement
- **Timing**: at time of free flap harvest and reconstruction (same operation)
- **Advantages**: single anaesthetic episode; implants placed into native bone quality tissue; VSP-guided placement for optimal positioning; avoids second surgery
- **Disadvantages**: implants cannot be loaded for 3–6 months post-placement; if adjuvant RT required, further delay needed (RT affects osseointegration — loading deferred until 12 months post-RT)
- **Best for**: patients not requiring adjuvant RT; immediate implant planning with VSP

### Secondary (Delayed) Implant Placement
- **Timing**: 3–6 months after reconstruction (non-irradiated); 12 months after RT completion (irradiated)
- **Advantages**: bone stock can be assessed; vascularity of reconstruction stable; adjuvant treatment completed
- **Disadvantages**: additional surgery; second anaesthetic
- **Recommended** when: adjuvant RT planned; uncertainty about bone stock; immediate implant not technically feasible

## Dental Implants in Free Fibula Mandibular Reconstruction

The combination of **free fibula + osseointegrated implants** is the functional benchmark for mandibular rehabilitation:

### Why Fibula Is Ideal for Implants
- **Dual cortical bone**: fibula has two cortices with dense cancellous bone → excellent primary implant stability
- **Adequate width**: ~15 mm diameter fibula diameter; accommodates standard 3.75–5 mm implants
- **Vertical height**: ~14–16 mm fibula height (lower than native mandible 30 mm); manageable with extended abutments or double-barrel technique
- **Reliable vascularity**: periosteal blood supply maintained after osteotomies → good bone healing and implant osseointegration
- **Length**: enough bone for full arch reconstruction (5–6 implant positions)

### Vertical Height Discrepancy
Fibula height (14–16 mm) < Native mandibular height (30–35 mm) creates an **alveolar step** which can impair implant-to-prosthesis ratios.
Solutions:
1. **Extended abutments**: longer abutment transects the height discrepancy (prosthetic solution)
2. **Double-barrel fibula**: fibula folded on itself, stacking two segments to approximate native height (surgical solution; requires additional osteotomy and microsurgical planning)
3. **Distraction osteogenesis**: gradual bone transport increases fibula vertical height after reconstruction (delayed; 2+ operations)
4. **Implant-retained prosthesis with cantilever**: prosthetic design compensates for height difference

### Implant Protocol in Free Fibula
1. **Planning**: VSP with CT-merged model of fibula + mandible; implant positions, diameters, and angulations planned; surgical drill guides manufactured
2. **Placement**: at time of free fibula harvest (primary), or as delayed procedure
3. **Osseointegration**: 3–6 months (non-irradiated fibula) or 12 months (post-RT)
4. **Abutment connection**: after confirmed osseointegration on periapical/panoramic X-ray (bone contact visible)
5. **Prosthesis fabrication**: maxillofacial prosthodontist; removable or fixed prosthesis based on implant positions
6. **Loading**: progressive loading (initial soft diet, advancing to normal)

## Post-Radiation Considerations for Dental Implants

### Radiation-Induced Bone Changes
- Radiation causes **hypoxic-hypocellular-hypovascular (3H)** tissue (Marx's triad) in bone
- Osteoblast function impaired → poor new bone formation around implants
- Reduced marrow vascularity → increased infection risk → **osteoradionecrosis (ORN) risk** with implant placement in irradiated bone
- Effect is dose-dependent: >50 Gy significantly impairs osseointegration

### Success Rates
| Condition | Implant Success Rate |
|---|---|
| Non-irradiated fibula | 95–98% |
| Irradiated fibula (>50 Gy) | 75–90% |
| Irradiated native mandible | 60–80% |
| Irradiated maxilla | 50–70% |

### Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBO) Therapy
- **Mechanism**: high-pressure oxygen breathing (100% O₂ at 2.4 ATA); increases tissue pO₂; stimulates angiogenesis and osteoblast proliferation
- **Protocol**: 20 sessions pre-operatively + 10 sessions post-operatively (Marx protocol)
- **Evidence**: Marx RB (1983, 1985) established HBO for ORN and implants in irradiated bone
- **Current status**: evidence for implant success improvement is moderate; used selectively; not universally mandated

### Post-Radiation Waiting Period
- Minimum **12 months** after RT completion before implant placement in irradiated bone
- During this period, vascularity partially recovers; osteoblast function improves
- Panoramic + CBCT to assess bone density before implant planning

## Implants for Facial Prostheses (Craniofacial Implants)

Osseointegrated implants are used for extraoral prostheses in H&N oncology:

| Prosthesis | Implant Site | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Auricular prosthesis | Mastoid bone | Total auriculectomy; microtia |
| Nasal prosthesis | Nasal bones / frontal bone | Total rhinectomy |
| Orbital prosthesis | Orbital rim / zygomatic arch | Orbital exenteration |
| Palatal/obturator | Residual maxillary bone / zygoma | Maxillectomy, edentulous |

**BAHA (bone-anchored hearing aid)**: implant in mastoid; for conductive hearing loss; used after temporal bone resection.

## Virtual Surgical Planning (VSP) for Implant Reconstruction

VSP is now standard of care for complex mandibular reconstruction with dental implant planning:

### Workflow
1. **Pre-operative CT**: mandible (tumour + planned resection) + fibula (bilateral)
2. **3D segmentation**: tumour margins, mandible, fibula
3. **Virtual surgery**: resect tumour virtually; plan fibula osteotomies to fit mandible contour; plan implant positions
4. **Manufacture**: fibula cutting guides, mandibular resection guides, pre-bent reconstruction plate, implant drill guide
5. **Intraoperative**: guides ensure accuracy; plate fits precisely; implants in planned positions

### Benefits
- Reduced operative time (flap ischaemia time)
- Improved condylar positioning
- Optimised implant position for prosthetic load distribution
- Reproducible and precise outcomes; surgeon-independent variability reduced

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Osseointegration — the direct bone-implant contact described by Brånemark (1983) — underpins dental rehabilitation after mandibular and maxillary reconstruction; the free fibula provides dual-cortical bone with sufficient length and quality for a full-arch implant-supported prosthesis, achieving 95–98% success in non-irradiated bone. Post-radiation implants require a minimum 12-month waiting period and achieve 75–90% success in fibula; HBO therapy and virtual surgical planning both optimise outcomes in this challenging setting.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Brånemark's key insight (1965) was that titanium — unlike other metals — achieves direct bone contact without fibrous encapsulation; this is osseointegration and is unique to titanium's TiO₂ oxide surface.
> 2. Primary implant placement at time of free fibula mandibular reconstruction is preferred when adjuvant RT is NOT planned; VSP allows perfect pre-planning and a single operative episode.
> 3. When adjuvant RT is planned, delay implant placement to 12 months post-RT; irradiated bone placed with immediate implants has higher failure and ORN risk during the RT healing phase.
> 4. The vertical height discrepancy (fibula ~15 mm vs mandible ~32 mm) is the most commonly asked practical limitation of fibula implant reconstruction; double-barrel fibula is the surgical solution, extended abutments the prosthetic solution.
> 5. HBO therapy (Marx protocol: 20 pre-op + 10 post-op dives) is recommended for implant placement in previously irradiated mandible; evidence is moderate but the biological rationale (3H bone reversal) is sound.
> 6. CBCT (cone-beam CT) provides the best assessment of implant osseointegration and peri-implant bone levels; annual CBCT recommended for post-RT implants to detect early ORN.
> 7. Craniofacial implants (mastoid for auricular prosthesis, orbital rim for orbital prosthesis) follow the same osseointegration principles; they are placed in non-irradiated bone where possible and are critical for quality of life after radical H&N resection.
> 8. VSP reduces free fibula ischaemia time by 30–60 minutes in complex reconstructions — a clinically significant benefit that directly reduces flap failure risk.
