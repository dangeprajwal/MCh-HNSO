## Part A: Parathyroid Glands — Anatomy Relevant to Surgery

### Normal Anatomy

- **Number:** Typically 4 glands (superior and inferior pair); 5% have supernumerary glands
- **Location:**
  - **Superior parathyroids** (P4): Derived from 4th pharyngeal pouch; more consistent in position — posterior to RLN at the level of the cricothyroid joint, near the posterior surface of the upper thyroid lobe
  - **Inferior parathyroids** (P3): Derived from 3rd pharyngeal pouch (with thymus); more variable — usually below the inferior thyroid artery, in the thyrothymic ligament, or within the thymic tissue
- **Size:** ~5 × 3 × 1 mm; weight ~35–40 mg per gland; yellow-tan in colour (oxyphil/chief cells)
- **Blood supply:** Inferior thyroid artery (both superior and inferior glands in most cases)

### Relations of Parathyroids to RLN and EBSLN

| Structure | Relation to Parathyroid |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **RLN** | The RLN crosses the inferior thyroid artery near the superior parathyroid gland — the "danger triangle." Superior parathyroid lies **posterior** to the RLN; inferior parathyroid lies **anterior** to the RLN at the level of the lower thyroid pole |
| **EBSLN** | Descends to cricothyroid muscle; passes close to the superior pole vessels — the superior parathyroid may be encountered during ligation of the superior thyroid artery if EBSLN identification is not performed |
| **Inferior thyroid artery** | The blood supply for both parathyroids — must be preserved during thyroidectomy; preserved by ligating thyroid branches distally rather than the artery itself |

**Key surgical principle:** Superior parathyroids lie POSTERIOR to the RLN; inferior parathyroids lie ANTERIOR to the RLN. The RLN is the key landmark for parathyroid identification.

### Ectopic Locations (in order of frequency)

1. **Intrathyroidal** — 1–5% of cases
2. **Thyrothymic ligament** — inferior glands descended with thymus
3. **Superior mediastinum / thymus** — fully descended inferior gland (most common cause of "missed" adenoma)
4. **Tracheo-oesophageal groove** — superior gland
5. **Retrooesophageal / retrolaryngeal** — superior gland that migrated posterior
6. **Carotid sheath**
7. **Undescended (superior to thyroid pole)** — superior gland

## Part B: Primary Hyperparathyroidism

### Pathological Causes

| Cause | Frequency | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| **Solitary adenoma** | 85–95% | Single gland; monoclonal expansion; usually lower glands |
| **Multiglandular hyperplasia** | 2–15% | All 4 glands enlarged; 80% sporadic, 20% familial (MEN1, MEN2A, HPT-JT) |
| **Double adenoma** | 2–6% | Two distinct adenomas; more common in older patients |
| **Parathyroid carcinoma** | 0.5–2% | See Part D |

**Success rate of surgery:** 95–98% (Stell & Maran)

### Clinical Features

- **Biochemistry:** ↑ PTH + ↑ serum calcium (concomitant elevation of both = diagnostic)
- **Classic symptomatic triad:** "Bones, Stones, Groans, Moans"
  - **Bones:** Osteitis fibrosa cystica, subperiosteal resorption, pathological fractures, "salt and pepper" skull, Brown tumours
  - **Stones:** Renal calculi (calcium oxalate/phosphate), nephrocalcinosis
  - **Groans:** Nausea, vomiting, pancreatitis, peptic ulcer disease (gastrin ↑)
  - **Moans:** Depression, cognitive impairment, fatigue, proximal muscle weakness
- Many patients now diagnosed incidentally on routine calcium screening (asymptomatic pHPT)

### Indications for Surgery (Asymptomatic pHPT)

- Age <50 years
- Serum calcium >0.25 mmol/L above upper limit of normal (>2.85 mmol/L)
- Creatinine clearance <60 mL/min
- BMD T-score ≤−2.5 at any site
- Renal calculi or calcinosis on imaging
- 24-hour urine calcium excretion >400 mg/day with elevated stone risk

## Part C: Parathyroid Scintigraphy (Sestamibi Scan)

### Principle

**99mTc-sestamibi (MIBI = methoxyisobutylisonitrile):**
- A lipophilic cationic complex taken up by cells with high mitochondrial density
- **Parathyroid adenomas are rich in oxyphil cells** (mitochondria-rich) → preferential and prolonged uptake and retention
- Thyroid tissue also takes up sestamibi but **washes out faster** (shorter retention)
- This differential retention is the basis for both subtraction and dual-phase (washout) techniques

### Techniques

**1. Dual-Phase (Washout) Technique:**
- **Early image** (15–20 minutes): Both thyroid and parathyroid adenoma show uptake
- **Late image** (2–3 hours): Thyroid activity cleared; parathyroid adenoma retains activity → seen as persistent hot spot
- Simple; widely available; preferred technique in most centres

**2. Subtraction Technique (Stell & Maran):**
- 99mTc-sestamibi administered → SPECT image acquired
- 123I given → thyroid-specific image acquired (thyroid only)
- Digital subtraction of thyroid activity from sestamibi image → parathyroid adenoma isolated
- Advantage: Better separation of thyroid from adjacent parathyroid
- Combined with SPECT-CT for 3D anatomical localization

**3. SPECT-CT (Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography):**
- Adds anatomical CT information to functional nuclear medicine image
- Localizes functional abnormality to specific anatomical location (e.g., posterior to upper thyroid pole vs. mediastinum)
- Particularly valuable for ectopic glands (mediastinal, retrooesophageal)

### Sensitivity (Stell & Maran — Systematic Review)

| Pathology | Sensitivity (95% CI) |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Solitary adenoma | **88.44%** (87.48–89.40%) |
| Multigland hyperplasia | **44.46%** (41.13–47.80%) |
| Double adenoma | 29.95% (variable) |
| Parathyroid carcinoma | ~33% |

**Factors reducing sestamibi sensitivity (Stell & Maran):**
- Adenoma volume <1 cm
- Histologically poor in oxyphil cells (chief cell predominant adenoma)
- **Upper position** (superior parathyroid glands) — less likely to be localized

### Complementary Imaging

| Modality | Role |
|----------|------|
| **Ultrasound (USS)** | First-line alongside sestamibi; no radiation; real-time; guided biopsy possible; misses retrosternal/deep ectopic glands |
| **4D-CT** | Parathyroid enhancement pattern on 4-phase CT (pre-contrast, arterial, venous, delayed); excellent for reoperative cases |
| **MRI** | Useful for ectopic glands; T2-hyperintense lesions; no radiation |
| **11C-Methionine PET** | For negative sestamibi + USS with ongoing biochemical pHPT (Stell & Maran) |

## Part D: Surgical Procedure for Solitary Parathyroid Adenoma

### Pre-operative Workup

1. **Confirm biochemical pHPT:** Elevated PTH + elevated adjusted calcium on at least 2 occasions
2. **Localization imaging:** Sestamibi scan + USS neck (concordant findings → MIP feasible)
3. **DXA bone density** (if asymptomatic — indications for surgery)
4. **Renal imaging:** USS kidneys (calculi)
5. **24-hour urine calcium:** Exclude familial hypocalciuric hypercalcaemia (FHH) — low urinary calcium
6. **Laryngoscopy:** Baseline vocal cord assessment before parathyroid/neck surgery

### Surgical Options

**Option 1: Minimally Invasive Parathyroidectomy (MIP) — Focused Approach**
- **Prerequisite:** Concordant sestamibi + USS confirming single adenoma in accessible location; no history/genetics of MEN/familial disease
- **Approach:** Focused cervicotomy (transverse incision 2–3 cm) over localized side
- **Steps:**
  1. Divide platysma; develop subplatysmal flaps
  2. Divide strap muscles on ipsilateral side
  3. Retract thyroid medially
  4. Identify and dissect parathyroid adenoma (yellow-tan, discrete, capsulated mass)
  5. Excise adenoma with intact capsule — avoid capsular rupture
  6. Confirm success with ioPTH monitoring (see below)
- **Advantages:** Day case surgery; less morbidity (avoids bilateral exploration); lower rate of hypocalcaemia; local anaesthesia possible
- **Optional aids:** Gamma probe (radio-guided MIP if 99mTc-sestamibi injected 2 h before surgery)

**Option 2: Bilateral Four-Gland Exploration**
- **Indications:** Failed localization; discordant imaging; clinical suspicion of multigland disease; familial HPT; MEN syndromes; re-operative cases
- **Technique:** Both sides of neck explored; all 4 glands identified; macroscopically enlarged glands removed; normal glands biopsied and marked with liga-clips
- **Enhanced by:** Intraoperative frozen section + ioPTH monitoring

### Intraoperative PTH (IOPTH) Monitoring — The Miami Protocol

**Principle (Stell & Maran):**
> *In 1987, the development of a two-site immunochemiluminescent assay for human PTH paved the way for intraoperative PTH monitoring (ioPTH) as a reliable indicator of success during parathyroidectomy. The half-life of PTH is approximately 3–5 minutes.*

**Procedure:**

| Time Point | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| **T-0 (baseline)** | Blood sample BEFORE any surgical manipulation (manipulation can cause PTH spikes → false elevation of baseline) |
| **T-5** | 5 minutes after excision of adenoma |
| **T-10** | 10 minutes after excision |

**Miami Criterion (Success):**
- PTH falls **≥50% from the highest pre-excision value** (T-0) at either T-5 or T-10
- AND falls into (or close to) the normal range
- Indicates all hyperfunctioning tissue has been removed

**Accuracy:**
- ~95% accuracy in **single gland disease**
- Less reliable in **multigland disease** — false-positive decreases in up to 75% (PTH falls >50% but other glands remain hyperfunctioning)

**Limitations of IOPTH (Stell & Maran):**
1. **Multigland disease** — false positive in up to 75%: removing one enlarged gland drops PTH >50% transiently but other glands remain hyperfunctioning
2. **MEN syndromes / familial HPT** — unreliable; germline mutations → false-positive IOPTH predicting cure
3. **Secondary/tertiary HPT in renal disease** — prolonged PTH half-life → normal assay timing not applicable
4. **Cost** — expensive; not universally available; controversial in many institutions
5. **Ectopic adenoma** — may not be excised even if technically performed
6. **Double adenomas** — PTH may fall adequately after first adenoma is removed, missing second adenoma

**Biochemical Indicators of Success (post-operatively):**
- **Normocalcaemia** within 24–48 hours — most reliable indicator
- **PTH normalization** at 24–48 hours
- **Normocalcaemic hyperparathyronaemia** (elevated PTH with normal calcium post-op) — occurs in ~20% of initially successful operations; resolves with calcium + vitamin D supplementation in most; in ~1% signals early operative failure

## Part E: Parathyroid Carcinoma

### Epidemiology

- **Incidence:** 0.5–2% of all cases of primary HPT; some series report up to 5% (Stell & Maran)
- **Age:** Fifth and sixth decades — approximately a decade younger than adenomas
- **Sex:** No gender predilection (unlike adenoma which has 3:1 female predominance)

### Clinical Features (Stell & Maran)

- **Severely hypercalcaemic** — typically calcium >3.5 mmol/L; often >4.0 mmol/L at presentation
- Active bone disease (pathological fractures, Brown tumours, subperiosteal resorption)
- Active renal disease (nephrolithiasis, nephrocalcinosis, renal impairment)
- **Palpable neck mass** — present in majority at presentation (unlike adenoma)
- Severe symptoms of hypercalcaemia

### Molecular Pathology

- **HRPT2/CDC73 gene mutation** — most important; encodes parafibromin (tumour suppressor)
  - Germline HRPT2 mutations in HPT-JT syndrome (hyperparathyroidism-jaw tumour)
  - Somatic HRPT2 mutations in sporadic carcinoma
- **Cyclin D1 overexpression** (chromosome 11 pericentric inversion involving PTH gene)
- **Loss of Rb gene** (tumour suppressor)
- **Parafibromin IHC loss** — diagnostic marker; absent in carcinoma, retained in adenoma

### Histological Criteria for Parathyroid Carcinoma (Stell & Maran Table 19.10)

**First-order criteria (absolute — either one sufficient for diagnosis):**
1. Invasion into surrounding tissues (thyroid, oesophagus, nerves, connective tissue)
2. Histologically documented locoregional or distant metastasis

**Second-order criteria (facultative — at least 2 required if first-order absent):**
1. Capsular invasion
2. Vascular invasion (pericapsular — virtually diagnostic when present; only 10–15% of cases)
3. Mitoses >5 per 10 hpf
4. **Broad intralesional fibrous septa** with division into nodules (most characteristic feature)
5. Coagulative type necrosis
6. Diffuse growth with elevated nucleus:cytoplasm ratio
7. Diffuse cellular atypia
8. Abundant macronucleoli

**Important note:** Parathyroid carcinoma may be deceptively bland — biological behaviour ultimately defines it.

### Natural History

| Feature | Data (Stell & Maran) |
|---------|---------------------|
| Local recurrence | ~30% (usually within 3 years) |
| Metastasis rate | ~30% |
| Metastasis sites | Lungs (40%), lymph nodes (30%), liver (10%), bone |
| 5-year survival | 60–85% |
| 10-year survival | 40–70% |
| Cause of death | Metabolic complications of hypercalcaemia (not tumour burden) |

### Management

**Surgery — En-bloc Resection at First Operation (most important):**
- En-bloc resection of: **parathyroid tumour + ipsilateral thyroid lobe + isthmus + any adherent structures** (muscles, recurrent nerve if involved)
- Capsular rupture/spillage must be avoided (risk of parathyromatosis)
- **Complete excision at first operation** affords the best chance of cure
- Intraoperative suspicion: troublesome dissection owing to adherence to contiguous structures → alert surgeon to possibility of malignancy

**Neck dissection:** Central (level VI) dissection for clinically positive nodes; elective ND not routinely performed

**Adjuvant radiotherapy:** Limited role; considered for positive margins or high-risk features

**Chemotherapy:** Poor response

**Palliation and medical management:**
- **Repeated surgery** for local recurrence/metastases — palliates hypercalcaemia
- **Cinacalcet** (calcimimetic) — reduces PTH secretion; reduces calcium; standard medical management
- **Bisphosphonates** (zoledronic acid) — reduces bone resorption
- **Denosumab** (anti-RANKL) — for refractory hypercalcaemia
- **Anti-PTH immunotherapy** — specialist use for refractory, severe hypercalcaemia (Stell & Maran)

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Parathyroid anatomy: superior parathyroids (P4, 4th pouch) lie POSTERIOR to the RLN; inferior parathyroids (P3, 3rd pouch with thymus) lie ANTERIOR to the RLN and are more variable in position
> 2. Sestamibi (99mTc-MIBI) sensitivity: solitary adenoma 88.44%; multigland hyperplasia 44.46% (Stell & Maran systematic review); less sensitive for small (<1 cm), oxyphil-poor, and upper-position glands
> 3. Subtraction technique: 99mTc-sestamibi + 123I subtraction; SPECT-CT adds anatomical localization; 11C-methionine PET for negative conventional localization
> 4. Causes of pHPT: solitary adenoma 85–95%; hyperplasia 2–15%; double adenoma 2–6%; carcinoma 0.5–2%
> 5. IOPTH (Miami criterion): PTH falls ≥50% from highest pre-excision level at T-5 or T-10; PTH half-life 3–5 minutes; 95% accurate for single gland; unreliable (false-positive in 75%) for multigland disease
> 6. MIP vs four-gland exploration: MIP when concordant sestamibi + USS; four-gland exploration for failed localization, MEN, or familial disease
> 7. Parathyroid carcinoma: 0.5–2% of pHPT; 5th–6th decade; no gender bias; severe hypercalcaemia + palpable neck mass
> 8. Carcinoma diagnosis: first-order = invasion into surrounding tissue OR documented metastasis; second-order (2+) = broad fibrous septa, capsular/vascular invasion, mitoses >5/10 hpf, necrosis, atypia, macronucleoli
> 9. Treatment of carcinoma: en-bloc resection (ipsilateral thyroid + adherent structures) at FIRST operation; repeated surgery for recurrence; cinacalcet for hypercalcaemia; adjuvant RT has limited role
> 10. HRPT2/CDC73 mutation and loss of parafibromin (IHC) are key molecular markers in parathyroid carcinoma

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Superior parathyroids always lie posterior to the RLN; inferior parathyroids anterior — use RLN as the landmark, not the thyroid vessels
> - Sestamibi "warms up" slowly and "stays warm" — the adenoma persists on late images when thyroid has washed out; this is the dual-phase principle
> - IOPTH Miami criterion: ">50% from highest pre-excision" — the "highest" is key; manipulation spikes must be accounted for by using T-0 before any dissection
> - Troublesome intraoperative dissection with adherence = immediate suspicion of carcinoma → frozen section before completing dissection
> - Broad intralesional fibrous septa dividing the tumour into nodules = the most characteristic histological feature of parathyroid carcinoma (but not sufficient alone)
> - IOPTH is unreliable in multigland disease — false positive in 75%; do NOT stop after removing one gland just because PTH falls >50% in MEN/familial settings
> - "Death from hypercalcaemia, not tumour burden" — this is the unique feature of parathyroid carcinoma prognosis
