## Part A: Classification of Soft Tissue Sarcomas

### WHO Classification (Harrison, Table 29.1)

Soft tissue sarcomas (STS) are classified by histogenesis according to the WHO classification:

| Histologic Type | Cell of Origin | HN Frequency | Key Features |
|-----------------|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Undifferentiated Pleomorphic Sarcoma (UPS)** (formerly MFH) | Fibrohistiocytic | Most common STS in adults | Pleomorphic; previously called "malignant fibrous histiocytoma" — term now obsolete |
| **Rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS)** | Skeletal muscle | Most common STS in children | Embryonal (best prognosis), alveolar, pleomorphic |
| **Synovial Sarcoma** | Primitive mesenchyme | Uncommon | Biphasic (epithelial + spindle) or monophasic; TMJ region |
| **Liposarcoma** | Adipocytic | Uncommon | 4% of all liposarcomas occur in HN |
| **Fibrosarcoma** | Fibroblasts | Rare | Herringbone pattern; congenital-infantile form has better prognosis |
| **Leiomyosarcoma** | Smooth muscle | Rare | Vascular walls; post-RT risk |
| **Angiosarcoma** | Endothelium | HN-specific: scalp + face of elderly | Post-RT; chronic solar exposure; vinyl chloride |
| **Hemangiopericytoma / Solitary Fibrous Tumour** | Pericytes of Zimmermann | HN-specific: sinonasal cavity | Now reclassified as SFT; NAB2-STAT6 fusion |
| **MPNST** | Neural sheath | NF1 associated | 10% lifetime risk in neurofibromatosis type 1 |
| **Dermatofibrosarcoma Protuberans** | Dermal fibroblasts | Low neck, supraclavicular skin | t(17;22); PDGF-B fusion; low-grade; imatinib-sensitive |
| **Alveolar Soft Part Sarcoma** | Uncertain | Orbit, head, neck | t(X;17); indolent local, delayed mets |
| **Epithelioid Sarcoma** | Uncertain | Rare in HN | Paradoxical EMA/cytokeratin positive; nodal spread |
| **Kaposi Sarcoma** | Endothelium | Oral mucosa; AIDS-related | HHV-8; epidemic (AIDS) vs classic; nasal/oral | 

### Prototypical HN Soft Tissue Sarcomas (Harrison)

Harrison identifies unique predispositions in the head and neck:
- **Non-pleomorphic rhabdomyosarcoma** — predominantly alveolar histology in HN
- **Angiosarcoma** — scalp and facial regions of elderly white males
- **Hemangiopericytoma (SFT)** — sinonasal region
- **Dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans** — dermal regions of low neck and supraclavicular region

## Part B: Pathobiology of Soft Tissue Tumours

### Molecular Genetics and Cytogenetic Abnormalities (Harrison Table 29.2)

| Histologic Subtype | Translocation | Genes Involved |
|-------------------|--------------|---------------|
| **Rhabdomyosarcoma (alveolar)** | t(2;13)(q35;q14) or t(1;13)(p36;q14) | **PAX3** (or PAX7) / **FOXO1** (formerly FKHR) |
| **Rhabdomyosarcoma (embryonal)** | Loss of heterozygosity at 11p15 | — |
| **Synovial sarcoma** | t(X;18)(p11.2;q11.2) | **SYT/SSX1** (biphasic) or **SYT/SSX2** (monophasic) |
| **Myxoid liposarcoma** | t(12;16)(q13;p11) | **TLS/CHOP** (also known as FUS/DDIT3) |
| **Dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans** | t(17;22)(q22;q13) | Collagen type 1α1 / **PDGF-B** |
| **Desmoplastic small round-cell** | t(11;22)(p13;q12) | **EWS/WT1** |
| **Clear cell sarcoma** | t(12;22)(q13;q12) | **EWS/ATF1** |
| **Hemangiopericytoma / SFT** | NAB2-STAT6 gene fusion (inversion Chr12) | **NAB2-STAT6** → STAT6 nuclear expression (IHC) |
| **Ewing sarcoma** | t(11;22)(q24;q12) | **EWS/FLI1** (80%); alternate ETS partners |

**Key diagnostic point:** Molecular genetic techniques allow precise subtype diagnosis — especially in poorly differentiated tumours where histogenetic classification is otherwise difficult.

### Grading (French Federation of Cancer Centres — 3-tier system)

The most widely employed grading system evaluates three parameters:
1. **Differentiation score** — how much the tumour resembles normal tissue
2. **Mitotic count** — per 10 high-power fields
3. **Necrosis** — % tumour necrosis

Grades I–III (low, intermediate, high); grade is incorporated into TNM staging as a prognostic factor.

**Limitations:** Some STS are "ungradable": well-differentiated liposarcoma, epithelioid sarcoma, clear cell sarcoma, angiosarcoma; MPNST does not stratify prognostically by grade (Harrison).

### Etiology and Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Associated Sarcoma |
|-------------|------------------|
| **Prior radiotherapy** | Most important in HN; radiation-induced STS (usually in low-dose edge of RT field); latency 10–30 years; may arise from EBRT or brachytherapy |
| **NF1 (neurofibromatosis type 1)** | MPNST — 10% cumulative lifetime risk; NF1 gene (neurofibromin) on chr 17q11.2 |
| **Li-Fraumeni syndrome** | Osteosarcoma + STS; germline TP53 mutations |
| **Retinoblastoma** | Rb gene (chr 13q14) dysfunction — predisposition to OS and STS |
| **Chemical exposure** | Thorotrast (angiosarcoma); vinyl chloride (angiosarcoma); phenoxy acetic acids; chlorophenols; arsenic |
| **Chronic lymphoedema** | Stewart-Treves syndrome (post-mastectomy lymphangiosarcoma) |
| **Genetic: Maffucci syndrome** | Skull base chondrosarcoma |

## Part C: Specific HN Soft Tissue Sarcomas

### Rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS)

- Most common STS in **children** (>30% of HN sarcomas in children); rare in adults
- See dedicated section in [[Answers/Eye and Orbit/Orbital Malignancies and Exenteration|Orbital RMS]] for orbital-specific management
- **Alveolar RMS** (most common HN subtype in adults/adolescents): PAX3/7-FOXO1 — worst prognosis
- **Embryonal RMS** (most common overall): loss of heterozygosity 11p15 — better prognosis
- Treatment: VAC chemotherapy (Vincristine, Actinomycin D, Cyclophosphamide) ± RT; surgery reserved for biopsy and salvage

### Undifferentiated Pleomorphic Sarcoma (UPS / former MFH)

- **Most common STS of middle and late adulthood** (Harrison); no particular predilection for HN
- Old term "MFH" (malignant fibrous histiocytoma) is **obsolete** — now reclassified as:
  - **Undifferentiated high-grade pleomorphic sarcoma** (storiform-pleomorphic variant)
  - **Myxofibrosarcoma** (myxoid variant)
- Treatment: surgery + adjuvant RT; anthracycline-based chemotherapy for metastatic/unresectable

### Synovial Sarcoma

- **t(X;18)** translocation → SYT-SSX fusion — diagnostic (confirmed by molecular testing)
- SSX1: biphasic histology (epithelial + spindle); SSX2: monophasic (spindle only)
- HN location: frequently TMJ region, pharynx
- Treatment: wide excision + adjuvant RT; ifosfamide-based chemotherapy

### Angiosarcoma (HN-specific)

- **Scalp and face of elderly white males** — prototypical HN angiosarcoma
- Risk: prior RT, chronic solar exposure, vinyl chloride, arsenic
- Aggressive behaviour; high propensity for regional and distant metastasis
- CD31, CD34, Factor VIII IHC positive
- Treatment: surgery (difficult margins) + RT; chemotherapy (paclitaxel, anthracycline)

## Part D: Hemangiopericytoma / Sinonasal Solitary Fibrous Tumour

### Historical Background and Reclassification

- **Originally described by Stout and Murray (1942)** as arising from **pericytes of Zimmermann** — contractile cells surrounding capillaries
- Now reclassified under the umbrella of **Solitary Fibrous Tumour (SFT)** due to identification of shared NAB2-STAT6 molecular alteration
- **Thompson et al. (2003, Am J Surg Pathol):** 104-case study of "sinonasal-type hemangiopericytoma" — demonstrated perivascular myoid differentiation; now termed "sinonasal glomangiopericytoma" or "sinonasal SFT"
- **Robinson et al. (2013, Nat Genet):** Identified NAB2-STAT6 gene fusion as defining alteration in all SFT (including hemangiopericytoma)

### Clinical Features (Harrison — Chapter 20)

- **Location:** Primarily sinonasal cavity (nasal fossa, paranasal sinuses); also orbit, parapharyngeal space
- **Presentation:**
  - **Epistaxis** — most common presenting symptom
  - Soft, pale, rubbery lesion on endoscopy
  - Nasal obstruction
  - Rarely: proptosis (orbital involvement)
- **Behaviour:** Low-grade malignancy; metastases rare; propensity for **aggressive local behaviour** and **recurrence after inadequate resection**

### Histopathology and IHC

- **"Staghorn" or "antler-like" vascular pattern** — thin-walled vessels with branching sinusoidal architecture surrounded by spindle cells
- **IHC:** CD34+ (most consistent), CD99, STAT6 nuclear staining (NAB2-STAT6 fusion) — highly specific
- WHO grade 1–3 (based on mitotic count, cellularity, atypia, necrosis)
- Malignant SFT features: mitoses >4/10 hpf, necrosis, p53 overexpression

### Management

| Situation | Treatment |
|-----------|----------|
| **Primary resectable disease** | **Aggressive surgical resection** with clear margins; endoscopic resection for limited sinonasal lesions |
| **Positive/close margins** | Postoperative radiotherapy (60 Gy) |
| **Unresectable disease** | Primary radiotherapy (Harrison) |
| **Recurrence** | Re-excision; RT if not previously irradiated |
| **Metastatic / unresectable SFT** | Antiangiogenic agents (bevacizumab, sunitinib); temozolomide + bevacizumab |

## Part E: Management Principles for Head and Neck STS

### Biopsy Approach (Harrison)

> *"All soft tissue masses deep to the investing fascia should be considered to be sarcoma until proven otherwise."*

- **Core needle biopsy** (preferred): provides consistent tissue; guides molecular testing; less contamination risk than open biopsy
- **FNAB**: useful for recurrence confirmation; controversial as primary diagnostic tool
- **Open biopsy**: rarely indicated; used when needle biopsy fails (~10%)
- **Critical principle:** Image the lesion BEFORE biopsy — to avoid compromising staging and treatment planning
- Needle tract contamination must be planned for (surgeon and radiation oncologist plan biopsy route)

### Staging Workup

1. **MRI** (preferred) — multiplanar; best soft tissue contrast; optimal for local tumour extent
2. **CT** — better for bony detail; useful when calcium/matrix important (chondrosarcoma, synovial sarcoma)
3. **CT chest** — primary distant staging (lung is the predominant site of haematogenous spread in STS)
4. **PET-CT** — useful for high-grade STS and suspected metastases
5. **Lymph node assessment:** Lymph node involvement unusual in most STS — exceptions: RMS, epithelioid sarcoma, alveolar soft part, clear cell, synovial sarcoma

### Surgical Principles

- **Wide excision with clear margins** — the cornerstone of curative treatment
- **Compartmental resection** is the ideal but often not feasible in HN (proximity to critical structures)
- **R0 resection** (negative microscopic margins) — most important prognostic factor for local control
- Reconstruction planned in advance; major free flap reconstruction often required
- **High-volume centres** show better survival outcomes for HN sarcoma (Harrison cites 4,205 cases) — multidisciplinary team essential

### Adjuvant Radiotherapy

- **Indications:** Close or positive margins; high-grade; large tumours (>5 cm); recurrent disease
- **Dose:** 60–66 Gy (EBRT); brachytherapy (LDR/HDR) for selected cases (Harrison: brachytherapy supports function preservation)
- **RT field:** Tumour bed with generous margins; shaped to spare critical HN structures

### Chemotherapy

| Indication | Regimen | Comment |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Pediatric RMS** | VAC (Vincristine + Actinomycin D + Cyclophosphamide) ± RT | Standard; excellent outcomes for embryonal |
| **Adult STS (metastatic/unresectable)** | Doxorubicin ± ifosfamide | First-line; responses ~25–35% |
| **Synovial sarcoma** | Ifosfamide (high-dose) | More sensitive than other STS |
| **DFSP** | **Imatinib** (STI571) | Targeted therapy; PDGF-B fusion; very effective |
| **SFT/HPC (metastatic)** | Bevacizumab + temozolomide | Anti-angiogenic; reasonable disease control |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Prototypical HN STS: UPS (adult), RMS (children); HN-specific: angiosarcoma (scalp, elderly), hemangiopericytoma/SFT (sinonasal), DFSP (low neck skin), non-pleomorphic alveolar RMS
> 2. MFH is now obsolete — reclassified as undifferentiated high-grade pleomorphic sarcoma (storiform variant) or myxofibrosarcoma (myxoid variant)
> 3. Key molecular markers: synovial sarcoma = t(X;18) SYT-SSX; alveolar RMS = t(2;13) PAX3/7-FOXO1; DFSP = t(17;22) PDGF-B; Ewing = t(11;22) EWS-FLI1; SFT/HPC = NAB2-STAT6 fusion (STAT6 nuclear IHC)
> 4. French 3-tier grading system: differentiation + mitoses + necrosis; incorporated in TNM staging
> 5. Radiation-induced sarcoma: latency 10–30 years; arises in low-dose edge of previous RT field; most important aetiological factor in HN
> 6. NF1 → MPNST (10% lifetime risk); Li-Fraumeni (TP53) → osteosarcoma + STS; retinoblastoma (Rb) → OS + STS
> 7. Hemangiopericytoma: pericytes of Zimmermann; sinonasal cavity; epistaxis + pale rubbery mass; staghorn vessels; CD34+, STAT6 IHC+; aggressive local behaviour despite rare metastases; wide resection ± RT
> 8. Core biopsy preferred; image BEFORE biopsy; all deep masses = sarcoma until proven otherwise
> 9. Surgery (R0) is the cornerstone; adjuvant RT for positive/close margins; CT chest mandatory for staging
> 10. Nodal spread unusual in STS — exceptions: RMS, epithelioid, clear cell, alveolar soft part, synovial sarcoma; routine neck dissection not indicated for others

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - "MFH is dead" — the question may still use this old term but examiners expect the answer to state it is now called undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma
> - Hemangiopericytoma is now SFT — cite NAB2-STAT6 and STAT6 IHC; staghorn vascular pattern is the histological hallmark
> - Image FIRST, biopsy SECOND — do NOT biopsy before imaging; this contaminates staging and treatment planning
> - DFSP is sensitive to imatinib (Gleevec) — this is the classic example of targeted therapy in STS, exploiting the PDGF-B fusion
> - Angiosarcoma of the scalp + face = elderly white male; CD31/CD34 IHC; poor prognosis due to multifocal spread
> - RT-induced sarcoma appears at the LOW-DOSE edge of the prior radiation field — often in areas the clinician did not irradiate intentionally
