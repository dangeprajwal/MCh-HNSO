## Introduction

Dysphagia is one of the most significant late toxicities of chemoradiation for oropharyngeal cancer, affecting **40-80% of patients** at some point during or after treatment. Severe dysphagia causes aspiration pneumonia (a leading cause of non-cancer death in HNC survivors), PEG tube dependence, nutritional compromise, and profound quality of life deterioration. The concept of **DARS (Dysphagia/Aspiration-Related Structures)** was introduced by Eisbruch et al. (2004) to define the specific anatomic structures whose radiation-induced damage leads to chronic swallowing dysfunction. By identifying and sparing these structures during IMRT planning, the severity and incidence of post-treatment dysphagia can be significantly reduced without compromising disease control.

## Definition and Anatomy of DARS

### What Are DARS?

DARS (Dysphagia/Aspiration-Related Structures) are the anatomic structures critical for normal swallowing whose radiation-induced damage leads to chronic dysphagia and aspiration. The concept recognises that swallowing is a complex coordinated process involving multiple muscular and neural structures, and that targeted dose reduction to these structures can improve functional outcomes.

### Individual DARS Components

#### 1. Pharyngeal Constrictor Muscles

The pharyngeal constrictors are the primary effectors of the pharyngeal phase of swallowing. They contract sequentially in a superior-to-inferior wave (pharyngeal peristalsis) to propel the bolus from the oropharynx to the oesophageal inlet.

**Superior Pharyngeal Constrictor (SPC):**
- **Origin:** Medial pterygoid plate, pterygomandibular raphe, mandible, tongue
- **Insertion:** Pharyngeal raphe (median fibrous strip posteriorly)
- **Function:** Initiates pharyngeal peristalsis; creates the Passavant ridge for velopharyngeal closure
- **Level on CT:** From skull base to approximately the upper border of C3
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Very high — the most critical DARS structure

**Middle Pharyngeal Constrictor (MPC):**
- **Origin:** Greater horn of hyoid, lesser horn of hyoid, stylohyoid ligament
- **Insertion:** Pharyngeal raphe
- **Function:** Continues pharyngeal peristaltic wave
- **Level on CT:** From hyoid bone to approximately C4

**Inferior Pharyngeal Constrictor (IPC):**
- **Origin:** Thyroid cartilage (oblique line) and cricoid cartilage
- **Insertion:** Pharyngeal raphe
- **Components:** Thyropharyngeus (upper) and cricopharyngeus (lower)
- **Level on CT:** From thyroid cartilage to lower border of cricoid
- **Function:** Final pharyngeal peristaltic contraction; cricopharyngeus acts as the upper oesophageal sphincter

#### 2. Supraglottic Larynx

- Includes the epiglottis, aryepiglottic folds, false vocal cords, and arytenoids
- **Function:** Laryngeal closure during swallowing (airway protection)
- Radiation damage causes impaired laryngeal elevation, reduced epiglottic retroflexion, and aspiration
- **Level on CT:** From tip of epiglottis to just above the true vocal cords

#### 3. Glottic Larynx

- True vocal cords and anterior commissure
- **Function:** Final line of airway protection during swallowing (glottic closure reflex)
- Damage leads to aspiration during and after swallowing
- **Level on CT:** Level of the true vocal cords

#### 4. Base of Tongue (BOT)

- Posterior one-third of the tongue, extending from the circumvallate papillae to the valleculae
- **Function:** Bolus propulsion during the oral-pharyngeal transition; BOT retraction is essential for driving the bolus into the pharynx
- Radiation-induced fibrosis causes reduced BOT retraction and vallecular residue
- **Level on CT:** From circumvallate papillae to the hyoid bone

#### 5. Cricopharyngeal Muscle (Upper Oesophageal Sphincter)

- The lowest fibres of the inferior pharyngeal constrictor
- **Function:** Relaxes during swallowing to allow bolus passage into the oesophagus; maintains tonic contraction at rest to prevent reflux
- Radiation-induced fibrosis causes cricopharyngeal dysfunction, leading to post-swallow aspiration and pharyngeal residue
- **Level on CT:** At the level of the cricoid cartilage

#### 6. Oesophageal Inlet

- The transition from the hypopharynx to the cervical oesophagus
- Immediately below the cricopharyngeus
- Radiation damage causes stricture and obstruction

### Identification of DARS on CT (Christianen Guidelines)

The Christianen et al. (2011) guidelines provide standardised CT-based delineation for radiotherapy planning:

| Structure | Superior Border | Inferior Border | Key Landmarks |
|---|---|---|---|
| Superior constrictor | Caudal tip of pterygoid plates | Lower border of C2 | Posterior pharyngeal wall, pterygomandibular raphe |
| Middle constrictor | Lower border of C2 | Lower border of hyoid | Hyoid bone, posterior pharyngeal wall |
| Inferior constrictor | Lower border of hyoid | Lower border of cricoid | Thyroid and cricoid cartilage |
| Supraglottic larynx | Tip of epiglottis | False vocal cords | Aryepiglottic folds, pre-epiglottic space |
| Glottic larynx | False vocal cords | Lower border of cricoid | True vocal cords |
| Cricopharyngeus | Lower cricoid | 1 cm below cricoid | Posterior to cricoid |
| Base of tongue | Circumvallate papillae | Valleculae/hyoid | Genioglossus, hyoglossus |

## Dose Constraints for Each DARS

### Established Dose Thresholds

Based on the Eisbruch, Feng, and Duprez data:

| Structure | Dose Constraint | Clinical Endpoint |
|---|---|---|
| Superior pharyngeal constrictor | Mean <50 Gy | Reduced pharyngeal dysmotility |
| Middle pharyngeal constrictor | Mean <50 Gy | Preserved peristalsis |
| Inferior pharyngeal constrictor | Mean <50 Gy | Preserved UES function |
| Supraglottic larynx | Mean <50 Gy | Preserved laryngeal closure |
| Glottic larynx | Mean <50 Gy | Preserved glottic closure |
| Cricopharyngeus | Mean <40-50 Gy | Preserved UES relaxation |
| Oesophageal inlet | Mean <45 Gy | Prevent stricture |
| Base of tongue | As low as achievable | Preserved BOT retraction |

### Key Dose-Response Findings

- **Feng 2007:** Mean dose to the pharyngeal constrictors >60 Gy associated with significantly higher rates of aspiration and stricture
- **Eisbruch 2004:** Dose to the superior and middle constrictors was the strongest predictor of late dysphagia
- **Duprez 2013:** Systematic review confirmed that the pharyngeal constrictors are the most consistently identified dose-limiting structures for swallowing outcomes
- **General principle:** Each 10 Gy reduction in mean constrictor dose is associated with a measurable improvement in swallowing function

## IMRT/VMAT Planning Techniques for DARS Sparing

### Dysphagia-Optimised IMRT (Do-IMRT)

The technique involves adding DARS as optimisation constraints in the inverse planning algorithm while maintaining adequate target coverage.

#### Planning Steps

1. **Contouring:**
   - Delineate all DARS structures on the planning CT according to Christianen guidelines
   - Contour the pharyngeal constrictors (SPC, MPC, IPC) as separate structures
   - Contour the supraglottic and glottic larynx
   - Contour the cricopharyngeus and oesophageal inlet

2. **Target Definition:**
   - GTV: Gross tumour volume (primary + nodes)
   - CTV70: High-risk CTV (GTV + margin)
   - CTV60/63: Intermediate-risk CTV
   - CTV54/56: Elective CTV (elective nodal volumes)
   - PTV: CTV + setup margin (typically 3-5 mm)

3. **Optimisation:**
   - Add DARS structures as OARs with dose constraints
   - Prioritise: target coverage > DARS sparing (do not compromise tumour control)
   - The portion of DARS lying **within** the high-dose PTV cannot be spared
   - The portion of DARS lying in the **elective volume or outside any target** is the region where dose reduction is achievable
   - Apply mean dose constraints: SPC <50 Gy, MPC <50 Gy, IPC <50 Gy (for portions outside high-dose PTV)

4. **Beam Arrangement:**
   - VMAT (volumetric modulated arc therapy) offers superior DARS sparing compared to step-and-shoot IMRT
   - 7-9 beam IMRT can also achieve acceptable sparing
   - Avoid opposed lateral beams that deliver high dose across the entire pharyngeal constrictor

5. **Plan Evaluation:**
   - Review DVH for each DARS structure
   - Confirm target coverage (D95 > 95% of prescribed dose)
   - Compare standard IMRT plan vs Do-IMRT plan to quantify dose reduction achieved

### VMAT vs Step-and-Shoot IMRT

| Parameter | VMAT | Step-and-Shoot IMRT |
|---|---|---|
| DARS dose reduction | Superior | Moderate |
| Treatment time | Shorter | Longer |
| Conformality | Higher | Adequate |
| Availability | Widespread | Universal |

## Clinical Evidence for Reduced Dysphagia

### Key Studies

| Study | Design | N | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Eisbruch 2004 | Retrospective | 26 | Identified DARS; SPC and MPC dose strongest predictors of dysphagia |
| Feng 2007 | Prospective phase II | 73 | Do-IMRT feasible; reduced constrictor doses; good swallowing outcomes |
| Duprez 2013 | Systematic review | 7 studies | Confirmed pharyngeal constrictors as key dose-limiting structures |
| Roe 2016 (DARS trial) | Phase III RCT (protocol) | Target 112 | Do-IMRT vs S-IMRT; primary endpoint MDADI score at 12 months |

### DARS Trial (Roe 2016)

- **Design:** Phase III, multicentre, randomised controlled trial
- **Arms:** Do-IMRT (pharyngeal constrictor sparing) vs standard IMRT (S-IMRT)
- **Population:** Pharyngeal cancer (oropharynx and hypopharynx) treated with definitive chemoradiation
- **Primary endpoint:** MD Anderson Dysphagia Inventory (MDADI) composite score at 12 months post-RT
- **Secondary endpoints:** Objective swallowing assessment (videofluoroscopy, FEES), loco-regional control, overall survival, QOL measures
- **Mandatory dose constraint (Do-IMRT arm):** Mean dose to SPC/MPC or IPC (outside high-dose PTV) <50 Gy
- **Significance:** First RCT to provide level 1 evidence for DARS-sparing approaches
- **Results (published 2023 in Lancet Oncology):** Do-IMRT improved patient-reported swallowing function compared with S-IMRT; recommended as new standard of care

## Integration with Disease Control

### The Fundamental Principle

**DARS sparing must never compromise tumour control.** The primary goal of radiation therapy is oncologic cure — functional outcomes are secondary (though critically important).

### Practical Approach

- DARS structures **within the high-dose GTV/CTV** cannot be spared — these are receiving therapeutic dose
- DARS structures in the **elective nodal volume** (CTV54/56) are the primary targets for dose reduction
- DARS structures **outside any target volume** should receive the lowest possible dose (scatter dose only)
- The benefit of DARS sparing is greatest in patients with **lateralised tumours** where only one side of the pharyngeal constrictors is in the high-dose volume

### Disease Control Outcomes

Multiple studies (Feng 2007, DARS trial 2023) have confirmed that:
- Do-IMRT does not increase local or regional recurrence rates
- Loco-regional control is equivalent between Do-IMRT and S-IMRT
- Overall survival is not compromised by DARS-sparing approaches
- No excess of marginal failures at the boundary of spared DARS regions

## Patient Selection

### Ideal Candidates for DARS Sparing

- Lateralised oropharyngeal cancers (tonsil, lateral BOT)
- [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-positive]] tumours with favourable prognosis
- Unilateral nodal disease (N1-N2b)
- Patients receiving definitive chemoradiation (non-surgical management)
- Good baseline swallowing function

### Less Suitable Candidates

- Midline tumours (BOT, soft palate) where bilateral constrictors are in the high-dose volume
- Extensive bilateral nodal disease (N2c, N3)
- Tumours directly involving DARS structures (e.g., posterior pharyngeal wall tumours)
- Patients with pre-existing severe dysphagia from tumour bulk

## Challenges

### 1. Tumour Proximity to DARS

- Oropharyngeal tumours (especially BOT primaries) often directly abut or invade DARS structures
- The superior and middle pharyngeal constrictors form the posterior wall of the oropharynx
- When the tumour involves these structures, therapeutic dose must be delivered — sparing is not possible
- The constrictors contralateral to the tumour and the constrictors below the primary site (MPC, IPC) are the most amenable to sparing

### 2. Delineation Variability

- Inter-observer variability in DARS contouring remains significant
- The Christianen (2011) guidelines have reduced but not eliminated variability
- Contouring workshops and atlas-based auto-segmentation can improve consistency
- Accurate delineation is essential — over-contouring reduces the benefit, under-contouring may miss critical structures

### 3. Organ Motion

- Swallowing motion during treatment causes DARS to shift position
- This is particularly relevant for the pharyngeal constrictors and larynx
- Strategies: image-guided RT (IGRT), adequate PTV margins, adaptive replanning

### 4. Predicting Which Patients Will Benefit Most

- Not all patients develop dysphagia — pre-treatment identification of high-risk patients remains imprecise
- Factors predicting higher dysphagia risk: concurrent chemotherapy, older age, pre-existing dysphagia, larger tumour volume, bilateral nodal disease
- Baseline swallowing assessment ([[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|FEES or videofluoroscopy]]) can identify pre-treatment swallowing deficits

## Swallowing Outcomes

### Assessment Methods

| Method | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| MDADI (MD Anderson Dysphagia Inventory) | Patient-reported swallowing-specific QOL |
| FEES (Fibreoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing) | Direct visualisation of pharyngeal swallowing events |
| Videofluoroscopy (VFSS) | Radiologic assessment of all phases of swallowing |
| PAS (Penetration-Aspiration Scale) | Severity of airway invasion during swallowing |
| CTCAE dysphagia grade | Clinician-reported toxicity grading |
| Diet texture (FOIS scale) | Functional oral intake |

### Expected Improvements with DARS Sparing

- **Feng 2007:** Patients treated with Do-IMRT showed preservation of near-baseline swallowing function
- **DARS trial:** Clinically meaningful improvement in MDADI composite score with Do-IMRT
- Reduced rates of:
  - Aspiration pneumonia
  - PEG tube dependence
  - Pharyngeal stricture requiring dilatation
  - Severe weight loss
- Improved long-term [[Answers/General/Quality of Life in Head and Neck Cancer|quality of life]]

## Key Points for Examination

1. **DARS** = Dysphagia/Aspiration-Related Structures, first defined by Eisbruch (2004)
2. **Key DARS:** pharyngeal constrictors (SPC, MPC, IPC), supraglottic larynx, glottic larynx, BOT, cricopharyngeus, oesophageal inlet
3. **Pharyngeal constrictors** are the most important DARS — dose to the SPC and MPC is the strongest predictor of late dysphagia
4. **Dose constraint:** Mean dose to DARS <50 Gy (for portions outside high-dose PTV)
5. **Do-IMRT** (Dysphagia-Optimised IMRT) achieves dose reduction to DARS without compromising tumour control
6. **DARS trial** (Roe 2016, results 2023): first phase III RCT — Do-IMRT superior to S-IMRT for patient-reported swallowing function
7. The benefit is greatest in **lateralised oropharyngeal cancers** where contralateral constrictors can be spared
8. Christianen (2011) guidelines provide the standard for DARS contouring on CT
