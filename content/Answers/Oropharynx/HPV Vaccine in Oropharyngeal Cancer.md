## Overview

Human papillomavirus (HPV) vaccination represents a paradigm-shifting strategy for the **primary prevention** of HPV-associated oropharyngeal cancer. While HPV vaccines were developed primarily for cervical cancer prevention, the recognition that [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer]] has become the dominant HPV-associated malignancy in many high-income countries has raised the critical question of whether vaccination can prevent this rising epidemic. Current evidence strongly supports that prophylactic HPV vaccines reduce oral HPV infection prevalence, though direct evidence for OPC prevention will require decades of follow-up given the long latency between infection and cancer development.

## Epidemiological Context

### The Oropharyngeal Cancer Epidemic

- HPV-positive OPC incidence has **increased by 225%** in the United States from 1988 to 2004, while HPV-negative HNC has declined
- In the US and UK, HPV-positive OPC has **surpassed cervical cancer** as the most common HPV-associated malignancy
- **HPV-16** is responsible for >90% of HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancers
- Male-to-female ratio of HPV-positive OPC is approximately **4:1**
- Median age at diagnosis: 55-60 years
- Risk factors: Number of lifetime oral sexual partners, younger age at sexual debut, history of genital warts

### Oral HPV Prevalence (Gillison 2012)

The NHANES 2009-2010 survey of 5,579 US adults found:
- Overall oral HPV prevalence: **6.9%**
- High-risk HPV types: **3.7%**
- HPV-16 specifically: **1.0%**
- Prevalence in men: **10.1%** (vs 3.6% in women)
- Peak prevalence: Ages 60-64 years (bimodal distribution with smaller peak at 30-34)
- Estimated 26 million prevalent oral HPV infections in the US

## Available HPV Vaccines

### Vaccine Types

| Vaccine | Manufacturer | HPV Types Covered | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cervarix (bivalent) | GSK | 16, 18 | Available in some countries |
| Gardasil (quadrivalent) | Merck | 6, 11, 16, 18 | Discontinued in US (replaced by Gardasil 9) |
| **Gardasil 9 (nonavalent)** | Merck | 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58 | **Current standard** |

### Mechanism of Action

1. **Virus-like particles (VLPs)**: Recombinant L1 capsid proteins self-assemble into VLPs that mimic the virus structure but contain no viral DNA
2. **Immune response**: VLPs induce strong neutralising antibody responses (10-100x higher than natural infection)
3. **Adjuvant**: Aluminium-based adjuvants (Gardasil) or AS04 (Cervarix, containing aluminium hydroxide + monophosphoryl lipid A)
4. **Protection mechanism**: Neutralising antibodies in mucosal secretions (IgG transudate) prevent initial HPV infection at mucosal surfaces
5. **No therapeutic effect**: Vaccines prevent new infections but do not clear existing HPV infections or treat established lesions

### Gardasil 9 Efficacy (Joura 2015)

The pivotal phase 2b-3 trial enrolled 14,215 women aged 16-26:
- **96.7% efficacy** against high-grade cervical, vulvar, and vaginal disease related to HPV types 31, 33, 45, 52, 58
- **Non-inferior** antibody responses to HPV 6, 11, 16, 18 compared to quadrivalent vaccine
- HPV types in Gardasil 9 account for approximately **90%** of cervical cancers and potentially **>95%** of HPV-associated oropharyngeal cancers

## Evidence for Oral HPV Prevention

### Costa Rica Vaccine Trial (Herrero 2013)

This was the most important study directly assessing HPV vaccine efficacy against oral HPV infection:

- **Design**: Randomised clinical trial; 7,466 women aged 18-25 randomised to bivalent HPV vaccine (Cervarix) or hepatitis A vaccine (control)
- **Assessment**: Oral rinse specimens collected 4 years post-vaccination from 5,840 participants
- **Results**:
  - Oral HPV16/18 prevalence: **1 case in vaccine group vs 15 in control group**
  - Estimated vaccine efficacy against oral HPV16/18: **93.3%** (95% CI 63-100%)
  - Significant cross-protection against other oncogenic HPV types
- **Significance**: First direct evidence that HPV vaccination can prevent oral HPV infection
- **Limitations**: Young women only; cannot directly extrapolate to men or to oropharyngeal cancer prevention

### NHANES Population Data (Chaturvedi 2018)

Real-world evidence from the US population:

- **Design**: Cross-sectional analysis of NHANES 2011-2014 data; 2,627 adults aged 18-33
- **Comparison**: Oral HPV vaccine-type (16/18/6/11) prevalence by vaccination status
- **Results**:
  - Vaccinated individuals: **0.11%** prevalence of oral HPV vaccine types
  - Unvaccinated individuals: **1.61%** prevalence
  - Estimated **88.2% reduction** (95% CI 5.7-98.5%) in prevalence after adjustment
- **Significance**: Real-world population-level evidence supporting vaccine effectiveness against oral HPV infection
- **Limitation**: Cross-sectional design; cannot prove causation

### Summary of Evidence Chain

```
HPV Vaccine → Prevents oral HPV infection (93% efficacy, Herrero 2013)
                                    ↓
        Oral HPV infection → Persistent infection → OPC (latency 10-30 years)
                                    ↓
        Expected reduction in OPC incidence (estimated 2040-2060)
```

The evidence chain is compelling but the final link (reduction in actual OPC incidence) will take decades to demonstrate due to the long latency between HPV infection and cancer development.

### Current Approved Indications (Gardasil 9)

| Indication | Age Group | Sex |
|---|---|---|
| Cervical cancer prevention | 9-45 years | Female |
| Vulvar and vaginal cancer prevention | 9-45 years | Female |
| Anal cancer prevention | 9-45 years | Male and Female |
| Genital warts prevention | 9-45 years | Male and Female |
| Oropharyngeal cancer prevention | **Not specifically approved** | — |

**Note**: In 2020, the FDA updated the Gardasil 9 label to include prevention of oropharyngeal and other head and neck cancers caused by HPV types covered by the vaccine, though this was based on extrapolation rather than direct trial evidence for OPC prevention.

## Vaccination Guidelines and Recommendations

### CDC/ACIP Recommendations (United States)

- **Routine vaccination**: Age 11-12 years (can start at age 9)
- **Catch-up vaccination**: Through age 26 for all individuals not adequately vaccinated
- **Ages 27-45**: Shared clinical decision-making (not routine)
- **Gender-neutral**: Both males and females

### WHO Recommendations

- **Priority target**: Girls aged 9-14 years
- **Extended target**: Males where feasible and affordable
- **Single-dose schedule**: WHO endorsed single-dose HPV vaccination in 2022 based on emerging evidence

### Vaccination Rates

| Population | Vaccination Rate (approximate) |
|---|---|
| US girls (age 13-17, ≥1 dose) | ~65% |
| US boys (age 13-17, ≥1 dose) | ~60% |
| US adults (age 18-26, completed series) | ~40% |
| UK girls (age 12-13) | ~85% |
| UK boys (introduced 2019) | ~70% |
| India | Low (<5% in most states) |
| Australia | ~80% (both sexes) |

## Challenges for OPC Prevention

### Age of Vaccination vs Age of OPC

- HPV vaccination is most effective before sexual debut (age 9-14)
- Median age of OPC diagnosis is 55-60 years
- This creates a **30-50 year lag** between vaccination and expected reduction in OPC incidence
- Population-level impact on OPC will not be evident until approximately **2040-2060**

### Male Vaccination

- OPC has a 4:1 male predominance
- Male vaccination programmes were introduced later than female programmes (US: 2011 for boys, 2006 for girls)
- Catch-up vaccination for adult males is limited by low uptake
- **Herd immunity** from female vaccination provides some indirect protection to males, but is insufficient for OPC prevention given the predominance in men

### Catch-Up Vaccination

- Vaccination is less effective in individuals already exposed to HPV
- Benefit of vaccination after age 26 is reduced but not zero (protection against types not yet encountered)
- Cost-effectiveness of catch-up vaccination decreases with age

### India-Specific Challenges

- **Cost**: HPV vaccines are expensive; Gardasil 9 costs approximately INR 4,000-10,000 per dose
- **Infrastructure**: Cold chain requirements for rural distribution
- **Awareness**: Low public awareness of HPV and OPC among both patients and healthcare providers
- **Cultural barriers**: Vaccination against a sexually transmitted infection faces resistance
- **Indigenous vaccines**: Cervavac (Serum Institute of India) — quadrivalent vaccine approved in 2022, more affordable
- **Government programmes**: Limited state-level HPV vaccination programmes (Punjab, Sikkim)

### Limitations of Current Vaccines for OPC Prevention

1. **No direct trial evidence**: No randomised trial has demonstrated reduction in OPC incidence
2. **HPV type coverage**: While HPV-16 causes >90% of HPV+ OPC, rare non-16 types may not be covered
3. **Therapeutic gap**: Vaccines do not treat existing infections; the current cohort of HPV-infected adults remains at risk
4. **Latency**: Even with universal vaccination today, OPC incidence will continue to rise for decades before declining

## Therapeutic HPV Vaccines

Unlike prophylactic vaccines (which prevent infection), therapeutic vaccines aim to **treat existing HPV infections and HPV-associated cancers** by stimulating cell-mediated immune responses against HPV E6 and E7 oncoproteins.

### Key Therapeutic Vaccine Approaches

| Vaccine | Type | Target | Stage of Development |
|---|---|---|---|
| MEDI0457 (INO-3112) | DNA vaccine + IL-12 | HPV16/18 E6/E7 | Phase I/II |
| ISA101/ISA101b | Synthetic long peptide | HPV16 E6/E7 | Phase II |
| TG4001 (Tipapkinogene sovacivec) | Modified vaccinia Ankara (MVA) | HPV16 E6/E7 | Phase II |
| ADXS11-001 (axalimogene filolisbac) | Live attenuated Listeria vector | HPV E7 | Phase II/III |
| PDS0101 | Lipid nanoparticle + peptide | HPV16 E6/E7 | Phase II |

### ISA101 + Nivolumab

- **Design**: Phase II study of ISA101b (synthetic long peptide HPV16 vaccine) combined with nivolumab in recurrent/metastatic HPV16-positive OPC
- **Rationale**: Therapeutic vaccine primes anti-HPV T cell responses; PD-1 blockade releases immune checkpoint inhibition
- **Results**: Overall response rate of 33% (vs ~15% for nivolumab alone in historical data)
- **Status**: Further investigation ongoing; not yet standard of care

### MEDI0457 + Durvalumab

- DNA vaccine encoding HPV16/18 E6/E7 with IL-12 plasmid
- Combined with anti-PD-L1 antibody durvalumab
- Phase Ib/II in HPV-associated recurrent/metastatic HNC
- Showed enhanced HPV-specific T cell responses

### Challenges for Therapeutic Vaccines

1. **Tumour immune evasion**: HPV-positive cancers develop multiple immune escape mechanisms
2. **Combination needed**: Therapeutic vaccines likely require combination with checkpoint inhibitors
3. **Patient selection**: Best candidates may be those with minimal residual disease or as adjuvant therapy
4. **Regulatory pathway**: Complex endpoints for approval

## Cost-Effectiveness

### Economic Analyses

- HPV vaccination is **highly cost-effective** for cervical cancer prevention (below WHO threshold in most countries)
- For OPC prevention specifically, cost-effectiveness is less well-established due to:
  - Lower incidence than cervical cancer globally
  - Long time horizon to benefit
  - Uncertainty about vaccine efficacy specifically for OPC
- **Male vaccination**: Cost-effectiveness depends on female vaccination coverage; where female coverage is >80%, male vaccination adds incremental benefit primarily for non-cervical HPV cancers (including OPC)
- **Australia model**: With 80%+ vaccination rates in both sexes, projected near-elimination of HPV-associated cancers including OPC by 2060-2080

## Current Guidelines for Head and Neck Surgeons

1. **Advocate for HPV vaccination**: Head and neck surgeons see the consequences of HPV-related OPC and should actively promote vaccination
2. **Counsel patients**: Inform OPC patients about HPV vaccination for their children and unvaccinated family members
3. **Support gender-neutral vaccination**: Emphasise the importance of male vaccination given male predominance of OPC
4. **Do not offer vaccination as OPC treatment**: Prophylactic vaccines have no role in treating established cancer
5. **Monitor therapeutic vaccine trials**: Emerging therapeutic vaccines may become part of future OPC treatment

## Key Points for Exam

1. Three prophylactic HPV vaccines exist: bivalent (Cervarix), quadrivalent (Gardasil), and nonavalent (Gardasil 9); Gardasil 9 is the current standard
2. Vaccines use virus-like particles (VLPs) made of L1 capsid protein; they contain no viral DNA and cannot cause infection
3. Gardasil 9 covers HPV types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58 with **96.7% efficacy** against high-grade disease (Joura 2015)
4. The Costa Rica Vaccine Trial (Herrero 2013) showed **93.3% efficacy** against oral HPV16/18 infection at 4 years
5. Population-level data (Chaturvedi 2018) confirms **88.2% reduction** in oral HPV vaccine-type infections among vaccinated US adults
6. There is currently **no direct evidence** that HPV vaccination prevents oropharyngeal cancer (requires decades of follow-up)
7. Population-level impact on OPC incidence expected around **2040-2060** given 30-50 year latency
8. Male vaccination is crucial for OPC prevention given 4:1 male predominance
9. Therapeutic vaccines (ISA101, MEDI0457) targeting E6/E7 oncoproteins are in clinical development for treatment of established HPV-associated cancers
10. In India, Cervavac (Serum Institute) offers a more affordable vaccine option; national programme expansion is needed
