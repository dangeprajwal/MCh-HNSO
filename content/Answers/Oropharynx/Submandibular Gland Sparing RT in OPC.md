## Introduction

Xerostomia (dry mouth) is one of the most common and debilitating late side effects of radiation therapy for head and neck cancer, profoundly affecting quality of life. The submandibular glands are responsible for **60-65% of unstimulated (resting) saliva production**, which is the dominant saliva type during sleep, eating, and general daily comfort. Despite their smaller size compared to the parotid glands, the submandibular glands produce a mucin-rich, viscous saliva that is crucial for mucosal coating, swallowing lubrication, and oral homeostasis. Strategies to spare the submandibular glands during radiation therapy for oropharyngeal cancer are therefore of paramount importance in reducing the severity of chronic [[Answers/General/Xerostomia Management|xerostomia]].

## Anatomy of the Submandibular Gland

### Gland Anatomy

- **Location:** Occupies the submandibular (digastric) triangle, deep to the body of the mandible
- **Size:** ~10-15 g (approximately half the size of the parotid gland)
- **Type:** Mixed seromucinous gland (predominantly mucous secretion)
- **Capsule:** Enclosed within a split of the investing layer of deep cervical fascia
- **Relations:**
  - Superficial: Platysma, mandible, facial vein, marginal mandibular nerve
  - Deep: Mylohyoid muscle (gland wraps around posterior free border), hyoglossus, lingual nerve, hypoglossal nerve
  - The gland has a superficial (larger) and deep (smaller) lobe connected around the posterior free border of mylohyoid

### Wharton's Duct (Submandibular Duct)

- **Length:** ~5 cm
- **Course:** Emerges from the deep portion of the gland, courses anteriorly between the mylohyoid (laterally) and hyoglossus/genioglossus (medially)
- **Opening:** Sublingual papilla on either side of the lingual frenulum in the floor of the mouth
- **Relations:** The lingual nerve loops around Wharton's duct — passes lateral, inferior, then medial to the duct (twice crosses the duct)
- **Surgical significance:** The duct's close relationship to the lingual nerve makes dissection hazardous during gland transfer procedures

### Relationship to Level Ib

The submandibular gland is the dominant structure within **level Ib** of the neck (submandibular triangle). Level Ib is bounded by:
- **Superior:** Body of the mandible
- **Anterior:** Anterior belly of digastric
- **Posterior:** Posterior belly of digastric (stylohyoid)
- **Inferior (floor):** Mylohyoid muscle

Level Ib dissection in neck dissection inherently removes or devascularises the submandibular gland. In oropharyngeal cancer, the question of whether level Ib must be included in the radiation field or surgical dissection is crucial for submandibular gland preservation.

### Blood Supply and Innervation

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Arterial supply | Facial artery (courses through or deep to the gland) |
| Venous drainage | Facial vein |
| Parasympathetic supply | Superior salivary nucleus via chorda tympani to lingual nerve to submandibular ganglion |
| Sympathetic supply | Superior cervical ganglion via plexus on facial artery |

## Pathophysiology of Radiation-Induced Xerostomia

### Mechanisms of Salivary Gland Damage

Radiation damages salivary glands through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Acute phase (during RT):**
   - Direct damage to serous acinar cells (most radiosensitive)
   - Salivary flow can decrease by 50-60% within the first week of RT
   - Inflammatory changes and interstitial oedema
   - p53-dependent apoptosis of acinar cells

2. **Chronic phase (weeks to months post-RT):**
   - Progressive fibrosis of glandular parenchyma
   - Ductal stenosis and metaplasia
   - Vascular damage and ischaemia
   - Loss of stem/progenitor cell population
   - Replacement of functional tissue by fibrous tissue

3. **Irreversible phase (>12 months):**
   - Complete acinar cell loss
   - Dense fibrosis
   - Permanent loss of function if dose threshold exceeded

### Why Are Salivary Glands So Radiosensitive?

Salivary glands are paradoxically radiosensitive despite being composed of well-differentiated, slowly dividing cells (which would predict radioresistance by the Law of Bergonie and Tribondeau). The proposed mechanisms include:

- **Membrane-mediated apoptosis:** Ceramide-sphingomyelin pathway activation independent of DNA damage
- **High water content:** Serous acinar cells have high water content, generating abundant free radicals
- **Granule-dependent mechanisms:** Zymogen granule disruption causing autolysis

### Relative Contribution of Salivary Glands to Total Saliva

| Gland | Stimulated Saliva | Unstimulated Saliva |
|---|---|---|
| Parotid | 50-60% | 20-25% |
| Submandibular | 30-35% | **60-65%** |
| Sublingual | 2-5% | 7-8% |
| Minor salivary glands | 2-5% | 10-15% |

**The submandibular glands produce the majority of resting saliva.** Unstimulated saliva is the dominant saliva type for most of the 24-hour cycle — it provides the baseline mucosal lubrication essential for comfort, swallowing, speech, and oral health. This explains why submandibular gland sparing can have a dramatic impact on patient-reported xerostomia despite the parotid being a larger gland.

### Composition of Submandibular Saliva

- **Mixed seromucinous:** Rich in mucins (MUC5B, MUC7) that provide the viscoelastic coating of the oral mucosa
- Contains lysozyme, lactoferrin, IgA, and other antimicrobial factors
- Higher viscosity than parotid saliva — better for mucosal protection
- Loss of submandibular saliva leads to dry, unprotected mucosa, dental caries, and dysphagia

## Rationale for Submandibular Gland Sparing

### Clinical Impact of Xerostomia

Chronic xerostomia after radiation therapy causes:

- **Dysphagia:** Inadequate lubrication for bolus formation and swallowing
- **Dental caries:** Rampant radiation caries due to loss of salivary buffering and antimicrobial properties
- **Mucositis:** Prolonged healing time, secondary infections
- **Taste alteration (dysgeusia):** Often persists even when taste buds recover if saliva is absent
- **Speech difficulty:** Dry mucosa impairs articulation
- **Sleep disturbance:** Nocturnal dryness causes frequent awakening
- **Psychosocial impact:** Depression, social withdrawal, reduced quality of life
- **Nutritional compromise:** Difficulty eating leads to weight loss and malnutrition

### Why Submandibular Sparing Adds Value Beyond Parotid Sparing

- Parotid sparing primarily preserves stimulated saliva (important during meals)
- Submandibular sparing preserves **unstimulated saliva** (important for the remaining 22+ hours of the day)
- Combined parotid + submandibular sparing provides the most comprehensive xerostomia reduction
- Even with successful bilateral parotid sparing, patients may still have significant xerostomia if both submandibular glands are ablated
- The mucin-rich nature of submandibular saliva provides qualitatively superior mucosal protection

## Dose Constraints for Submandibular Gland Sparing

### Dose-Response Relationships

The Murdoch-Kinch (2008) study established the critical dose thresholds:

| Metric | Threshold | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Mean dose to SMG | **<39 Gy** | Recovery of function likely |
| Mean dose to SMG | >39 Gy | Permanent loss of function |
| Mean dose to SMG | <26 Gy | Optimal preservation |
| QUANTEC recommendation | Mean <39 Gy | To limit severe xerostomia |

### Comparison of Dose Constraints: Parotid vs Submandibular

| Gland | Dose Constraint | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Parotid (bilateral) | Mean <25 Gy (at least one gland) | QUANTEC |
| Parotid (unilateral) | Mean <20 Gy (contralateral) | QUANTEC |
| Submandibular | Mean <39 Gy | Murdoch-Kinch 2008 |
| Submandibular (optimal) | Mean <26 Gy | Saarilahti 2006 |

## IMRT Technique for Submandibular Gland Sparing

### Patient Selection

Contralateral submandibular gland (cSMG) sparing is appropriate when:

- **No contralateral level Ib nodal disease** on imaging
- **Primary tumour does not cross midline** or extend to the contralateral side
- The contralateral neck is being treated **electively** (not therapeutically)
- No adverse features suggesting bilateral neck involvement
- Most commonly applicable in **lateralised oropharyngeal cancers** (especially tonsil primaries)

### Planning Technique

1. **Contouring:**
   - Contour the contralateral submandibular gland as an organ at risk (OAR) on the planning CT
   - Delineate carefully using axial, coronal, and sagittal views
   - Include the entire gland but not surrounding fat or lymph nodes

2. **Optimisation:**
   - Add cSMG as a dose constraint in the inverse planning algorithm
   - Target mean dose <39 Gy (ideally <26 Gy)
   - Ensure this does not compromise target coverage (PTV) in adjacent regions
   - Use a "split-neck" technique when appropriate — high-dose PTV on the ipsilateral side, elective dose on the contralateral side

3. **Verification:**
   - Review dose-volume histogram (DVH) for the cSMG
   - Confirm adequate target coverage is maintained
   - Check for cold spots in level Ib target coverage

### Feasibility

In lateralised oropharyngeal cancers (particularly T1-T3 tonsil primaries with unilateral N+ disease):
- cSMG sparing is achievable in **70-90%** of patients
- Mean cSMG dose can typically be reduced to <30 Gy with IMRT
- No significant compromise in target coverage
- Ipsilateral submandibular gland sparing is rarely feasible due to proximity to the primary tumour and therapeutic target volumes

## Surgical Transfer Technique

### Concept

The submandibular gland transfer (SGT) technique, pioneered by Jha and Seikaly, involves surgically relocating the submandibular gland to the submental space prior to radiation therapy. The submental space lies outside the typical radiation field, allowing the gland to be shielded during RT.

### Surgical Procedure

1. **Timing:** Performed at the time of primary surgery and neck dissection, **before** radiation therapy
2. **Approach:** Standard submandibular incision
3. **Steps:**
   - Identify and preserve the submandibular gland during level Ib dissection
   - Carefully preserve the facial artery and vein (vascular pedicle)
   - Preserve the Wharton's duct
   - Mobilise the gland on its neurovascular pedicle
   - Transfer the gland to the **submental space** (below the chin, anterior to the anterior belly of digastric)
   - Secure the gland in its new position with sutures
   - Mark the gland's new position with radio-opaque clips for RT planning

4. **During RT planning:**
   - The transferred gland (now in the submental space) is contoured as an OAR
   - The radiation plan is optimised to spare the transferred gland
   - A lead shield or IMRT constraints exclude the gland from the high-dose volume

### RTOG 0244 Results (Jha 2012)

- **Design:** Phase II multicenter study
- **N:** 44 evaluable patients (63.6% oropharynx primary)
- **Technique compliance:** SGT was "per protocol" or within acceptable variation in 77.3%
- **Xerostomia prevention:** 74% of patients prevented from acute radiation-induced xerostomia
- **Conclusion:** SGT is reproducible in a multicenter setting and effective for xerostomia prevention

### Limitations of Surgical Transfer

- Requires concurrent neck dissection (not applicable to non-surgical treatment)
- Additional surgical time (~30-45 minutes)
- Risk of damage to the marginal mandibular nerve or lingual nerve
- The gland must be free of metastatic disease (histological confirmation)
- In the era of IMRT, the added benefit over IMRT-based gland sparing alone is debated
- Not applicable to patients receiving primary chemoradiation without surgery

## Outcomes: Submandibular Gland Sparing on Xerostomia and Quality of Life

### Xerostomia Reduction

| Study | Approach | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Saarilahti 2006 | IMRT cSMG sparing | Higher salivary flow at 1 year when mean SMG dose <26 Gy; no recurrences near spared gland |
| Jha 2012 (RTOG 0244) | Surgical transfer | 74% prevented from acute xerostomia |
| Seikaly 2001 | Surgical transfer | No patients developed xerostomia; transferred gland functional post-RT |
| Murdoch-Kinch 2008 | IMRT dose analysis | Recovery of SMG function over time when mean dose <39 Gy |

### Quality of Life Impact

- Rieger et al. (2012) demonstrated that submandibular gland transfer was superior to oral pilocarpine for:
  - Speech intelligibility
  - Swallowing function
  - Overall quality of life
  - Recommended SGT as the treatment of choice between the two approaches

### Comparison of Approaches: IMRT Sparing vs Surgical Transfer

| Feature | IMRT cSMG Sparing | Surgical Gland Transfer |
|---|---|---|
| Applicable to | All patients (surgical and non-surgical) | Only patients undergoing neck dissection |
| Invasiveness | Non-invasive | Requires additional surgery |
| Efficacy | Moderate to good (dose-dependent) | Excellent (if gland successfully shielded) |
| Bilateral sparing | Rarely feasible (usually only contralateral) | Usually unilateral |
| Current relevance | High (standard IMRT approach) | Decreasing (IMRT achieves acceptable sparing) |
| Limitation | Cannot always achieve dose <39 Gy due to target proximity | Additional surgical time; requires viable pedicle |

## Comparison with Parotid Sparing

### Parotid-Sparing IMRT

- Well-established standard of care since the landmark Eisbruch and PARSPORT studies
- QUANTEC recommends: mean parotid dose <25 Gy (or at least one parotid <20 Gy)
- Primarily preserves stimulated saliva flow
- Parotid sparing alone may not fully prevent patient-reported xerostomia

### Combined Parotid + Submandibular Sparing

- The **optimal strategy** is to spare both parotid glands AND at least one submandibular gland
- Preserves both stimulated (parotid) and unstimulated (submandibular) saliva
- Achievable in lateralised oropharyngeal cancers with IMRT
- Provides the greatest reduction in patient-reported xerostomia
- Should be the goal in every IMRT plan when oncologically safe

## Safety Considerations

### Oncologic Safety of Level Ib Sparing

The safety of submandibular gland sparing depends on the rate of level Ib nodal metastasis in oropharyngeal cancer:

| Primary Site | Level Ib Metastasis Rate |
|---|---|
| Tonsil | 1-5% (contralateral <2%) |
| Base of tongue | 5-10% (contralateral 3-5%) |
| Soft palate | 3-8% |

- **Contralateral level Ib involvement is rare (<2%)** in lateralised tonsil primaries
- This low rate provides the oncologic rationale for contralateral submandibular gland sparing
- Ipsilateral level Ib sparing is not recommended due to higher risk of subclinical disease
- If contralateral level Ib nodes are suspicious on imaging, sparing should not be attempted

### Monitoring

- Regular follow-up imaging to exclude recurrence in the spared region
- No prospective randomised evidence of oncologic compromise with cSMG sparing in selected patients
- Retrospective series consistently show no increased local or regional failure

## Key Points for Examination

1. The submandibular glands contribute **60-65% of unstimulated saliva** — sparing them is at least as important as parotid sparing for reducing patient-reported xerostomia
2. **Mean dose threshold: <39 Gy** (Murdoch-Kinch) for submandibular gland function preservation; <26 Gy is optimal (Saarilahti)
3. **Contralateral SMG sparing** by IMRT is feasible in 70-90% of lateralised oropharyngeal cancers without compromising target coverage
4. **Submandibular gland transfer** to the submental space (Seikaly/Jha technique) is an alternative for surgical patients, validated by RTOG 0244
5. The optimal strategy is **combined parotid + submandibular sparing** for both stimulated and unstimulated saliva preservation
6. Oncologic safety is supported by the low rate (<2%) of contralateral level Ib nodal metastasis in lateralised OPC
