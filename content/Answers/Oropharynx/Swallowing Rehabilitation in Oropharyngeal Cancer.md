(2012) published a landmark case series characterising late dysphagia after radiotherapy-based treatment of head and neck cancer, defining the entity of late radiation-associated dysphagia (RAD) occurring >=5 years post-treatment with progressive fibrosis of pharyngeal muscles and cranial neuropathy. Eisbruch et al. (2004) identified the dysphagia/aspiration-related structures (DARS) -- pharyngeal constrictors and glottic/supraglottic larynx -- and demonstrated that IMRT could potentially spare these structures to reduce post-treatment dysphagia. Feng et al. (2010) published the prospective clinical outcomes of DARS-sparing IMRT with concurrent chemotherapy for oropharyngeal cancer, showing that swallowing function was only slightly worse than pre-treatment levels, representing significant improvement over conventional RT approaches. Logemann et al. (2008) published an analysis of swallowing disorders in the first year after radiation and chemoradiation for head and neck cancer, identifying the key swallowing impairments including reduced tongue base retraction, reduced pharyngeal contraction, and impaired laryngeal elevation. Carnaby-Mann et al. (2012) published the Pharyngocise randomised controlled trial of preventative exercises to maintain muscle structure and swallowing function during head and neck chemoradiotherapy, demonstrating that active swallowing exercises preserved muscle structure and functional swallowing compared with usual care.

## Anatomy of the Oropharyngeal Swallowing Phase

### Normal Oropharyngeal Swallow

The oropharyngeal phase is a rapid, coordinated, semi-voluntary phase lasting approximately 1 second:

1. **Oral propulsive phase** -- tongue elevates sequentially (anterior to posterior) against hard palate, driving bolus posteriorly
2. **Triggering of pharyngeal swallow** -- bolus contacts posterior tongue/faucial pillars; afferent input via CN IX, X triggers medullary swallowing centre
3. **Pharyngeal phase events** (simultaneous, coordinated):
   - Velopharyngeal closure (soft palate elevation -- tensor/levator veli palatini)
   - Tongue base retraction against posterior pharyngeal wall (Passavant ridge)
   - Sequential pharyngeal constriction (superior --> middle --> inferior constrictors)
   - Hyoid and laryngeal elevation and anterior excursion (suprahyoid muscles)
   - Epiglottic inversion (passive, due to hyoid/laryngeal elevation + bolus pressure)
   - True vocal fold closure (airway protection -- CN X)
   - Upper oesophageal sphincter (UES/cricopharyngeus) relaxation

### Key Structures for Oropharyngeal Swallowing

| Structure | Function | Innervation |
|---|---|---|
| Tongue base | Bolus propulsion, base retraction | CN XII (hypoglossal) |
| Soft palate | Velopharyngeal closure | CN V3 (tensor), CN X via pharyngeal plexus (levator) |
| Superior constrictor | Pharyngeal contraction | CN X (pharyngeal plexus) |
| Middle constrictor | Pharyngeal contraction | CN X (pharyngeal plexus) |
| Inferior constrictor | Pharyngeal contraction | CN X (pharyngeal plexus, recurrent laryngeal) |
| Suprahyoid muscles | Hyolaryngeal elevation | CN V3, VII, XII |
| Larynx | Airway protection | CN X (SLN, RLN) |
| Cricopharyngeus | UES opening | CN X (pharyngeal plexus) |

## Impact of OPC Treatment on Swallowing

### Surgery-Related Dysphagia

| Resection | Swallowing Impact |
|---|---|
| Tonsillectomy / lateral oropharyngectomy | Mild -- usually compensated; transient pharyngeal phase deficit |
| BOT resection (<50%) | Moderate -- reduced tongue base retraction, bolus propulsion deficit |
| BOT resection (>50%) | Severe -- major propulsive deficit, aspiration risk, often PEG-dependent |
| Soft palate resection | Velopharyngeal insufficiency -- nasal regurgitation, nasal air escape |
| Posterior pharyngeal wall | Reduced pharyngeal contraction, bolus residue |
| Composite resection + mandibulotomy | Combined oral preparatory + oropharyngeal phase deficits |

**Factors determining surgical dysphagia severity:**
- Volume of tissue resected (most important factor)
- Sensory nerve sacrifice (CN IX, superior laryngeal nerve)
- Reconstruction quality -- see [[Answers/Oropharynx/Pharyngeal Reconstruction|Pharyngeal Reconstruction]]
- Preservation of hyoid bone and suprahyoid muscles

### Radiation / Chemoradiation-Related Dysphagia

#### Acute Phase (During and Immediately After RT, 0--3 months)

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Mucositis | Direct mucosal injury -- pain, odynophagia |
| Oedema | Soft tissue swelling -- pharyngeal narrowing |
| Xerostomia | Salivary gland damage -- dry mucosa, reduced bolus lubrication |
| Taste alteration | Taste bud injury -- decreased appetite, oral intake |
| Thick secretions | Altered salivary composition |

#### Subacute/Chronic Phase (3--24 months)

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Fibrosis | Progressive fibrosis of pharyngeal constrictors, tongue base |
| Reduced tongue base retraction | Fibrosis + denervation of tongue base musculature |
| Reduced pharyngeal contraction | Constrictor muscle fibrosis and atrophy |
| Impaired laryngeal elevation | Fibrosis of suprahyoid/strap muscles, scarring |
| Trismus | Pterygoid and masseter fibrosis -- limits oral opening |
| Pharyngoesophageal stricture | Circumferential fibrosis -- progressive obstruction |
| Lymphoedema | Pharyngeal and laryngeal lymphoedema -- reduced airway, impaired sensation |

#### Late Radiation-Associated Dysphagia (RAD, >5 years)

Hutcheson et al. (2012) characterised this distinct entity:
- **Progressive** dysphagia developing years after apparently stable swallowing
- **Mechanisms:** cranial neuropathy (CN IX, X, XII), progressive fibrosis, microvascular damage
- **Presentation:** new-onset aspiration, pneumonia, weight loss in long-term survivor
- **Prognosis:** often irreversible; may require permanent gastrostomy
- **Management:** supportive; aspiration precautions; swallowing therapy has limited benefit

### Dysphagia/Aspiration-Related Structures (DARS)

Eisbruch et al. (2004) identified the critical anatomical structures whose radiation damage causes dysphagia:

| DARS Structure | Dose Threshold (Mean) | Clinical Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Superior pharyngeal constrictor | >50 Gy | Reduced pharyngeal contraction |
| Middle pharyngeal constrictor | >50 Gy | Reduced pharyngeal contraction |
| Inferior pharyngeal constrictor | >50 Gy | UES dysfunction, aspiration |
| Glottic larynx | >50 Gy | Vocal fold fixation, aspiration |
| Supraglottic larynx | >50 Gy | Impaired laryngeal vestibule closure |
| Cricopharyngeus | >50 Gy | UES dysfunction |

## Assessment Tools

### Instrumental Assessment

#### Modified Barium Swallow (MBS) / Videofluoroscopic Swallowing Study (VFSS)

- **Gold standard** for comprehensive swallowing assessment
- Evaluates oral, pharyngeal, and oesophageal phases in real time
- Identifies: aspiration (before/during/after swallow), residue location, structural abnormalities
- Standardised protocol: Multiple consistencies (thin liquid, nectar, puree, solid)
- **Penetration-Aspiration Scale (PAS):** 8-point scale (1 = normal, 8 = silent aspiration)
- **MBSImP (Modified Barium Swallow Impairment Profile):** Standardised scoring of 17 components

#### Fibreoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing (FEES)

- Flexible nasendoscopy during swallowing with dyed boluses
- Evaluates pharyngeal phase, laryngeal sensation, secretion management
- Advantages: portable, repeatable, no radiation; can assess sensation
- Limitations: cannot visualise during swallow ("white-out" period); cannot assess oral phase
- Useful for serial monitoring during treatment

### Patient-Reported Outcome Measures

| Tool | Description |
|---|---|
| **MDADI** (MD Anderson Dysphagia Inventory) | 20-item validated questionnaire; global, emotional, functional, physical domains |
| **EAT-10** (Eating Assessment Tool) | 10-item screening tool; score >=3 indicates abnormal swallowing |
| **SWAL-QOL** | Swallowing quality of life questionnaire |
| **PSS-HN** (Performance Status Scale for Head and Neck) | Normalcy of diet, eating in public, speech |

### Clinician-Rated Scales

| Tool | Description |
|---|---|
| **CTCAE v5.0 Dysphagia Grading** | Grade 1 (symptomatic, regular diet) to Grade 5 (death) |
| **FOIS** (Functional Oral Intake Scale) | 7-point scale from nothing by mouth to total oral diet |
| **ASHA NOMS** | American Speech-Language Hearing Association National Outcome Measurement System |
| **Functional Communication Measures** | Standardised functional assessment |

### Baseline Assessment Protocol

Every oropharyngeal cancer patient should undergo **pre-treatment swallowing assessment:**
1. Clinical swallowing evaluation by SLP
2. Instrumental assessment (MBS or FEES)
3. Patient-reported outcome measure (MDADI or EAT-10)
4. Nutritional status assessment (weight, BMI, albumin)
5. Dental assessment
6. Trismus measurement (maximum interincisal opening)

## Preventive Swallowing Exercises

### Rationale for Prophylactic Exercise

- **"Use it or lose it" principle** -- disuse atrophy compounds radiation-induced muscle damage
- Patients who exercise during RT maintain better muscle structure (Carnaby-Mann 2012)
- Prophylactic exercise preserves range of motion and prevents fibrosis-related contracture
- Patients who begin exercises before or during treatment have better long-term swallowing outcomes than those who start after treatment

### Key Exercises

#### 1. Shaker Exercise (Head Lift)

- **Target:** Suprahyoid muscles (anterior belly of digastric, mylohyoid, geniohyoid)
- **Technique:** Supine; lift head to see toes without lifting shoulders; hold 1 minute; repeat 3 times; then 30 consecutive head lifts
- **Mechanism:** Strengthens muscles responsible for hyolaryngeal elevation and UES opening
- **Evidence:** Improves UES opening diameter and reduces aspiration

#### 2. Mendelsohn Manoeuvre

- **Target:** Laryngeal elevation, UES opening duration
- **Technique:** During swallow, voluntarily hold larynx in elevated position for 2--3 seconds before releasing
- **Mechanism:** Prolongs hyolaryngeal elevation and UES opening
- **Evidence:** Improves pharyngeal clearance and reduces residue

#### 3. Effortful Swallow

- **Target:** Tongue base retraction, pharyngeal pressure generation
- **Technique:** Swallow with maximum effort, squeezing all swallowing muscles as hard as possible
- **Mechanism:** Increases tongue base to posterior pharyngeal wall contact; improves pharyngeal pressure
- **Evidence:** Increases pharyngeal pressure and bolus clearance

#### 4. Tongue-Strengthening Exercises

- **Target:** Tongue base and oral tongue strength
- **Techniques:**
  - Tongue press against palate (isometric)
  - Tongue hold (Masako manoeuvre) -- swallow with tongue protruding between teeth
  - Resistance exercises with tongue depressor
  - Iowa Oral Performance Instrument (IOPI) for biofeedback
- **Mechanism:** Maintains tongue bulk and propulsive force

#### 5. Supraglottic Swallow

- **Target:** Airway protection
- **Technique:** Take a breath, hold it, swallow while holding breath, cough immediately after
- **Mechanism:** Voluntarily closes vocal folds before and during swallow; cough clears residue

#### 6. Jaw Stretching / Trismus Prevention

- **Target:** Pterygoid, masseter muscles
- **Technique:** TheraBite or stacked tongue depressors; progressive stretching 3x daily
- **Mechanism:** Maintains mandibular range of motion; prevents radiation-induced trismus
- See also [[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|Trismus Prevention]]

### Exercise Protocol During Treatment

| Phase | Exercises | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-treatment (1--2 weeks before) | Full exercise programme + baseline assessment | 3x daily |
| During RT/CRT (weeks 1--7) | Continue all exercises as tolerated | 3x daily (reduce if mucositis severe) |
| Acute recovery (0--3 months post) | Resume full exercises as mucositis resolves | 3x daily |
| Long-term maintenance | Continue indefinitely | 2x daily minimum |

## Prophylactic vs Reactive Rehabilitation

| Approach | Definition | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Prophylactic (proactive)** | Exercises begin before or during treatment | Superior outcomes; better muscle preservation; reduced long-term dysphagia |
| **Reactive** | Exercises begin only after treatment completion or when problems arise | Less effective; rehabilitation of already-damaged structures is more difficult |

**Key evidence supporting prophylactic approach:**
- Carnaby-Mann 2012 (Pharyngocise): Active exercises during CRT maintained muscle structure and swallowing function
- Exercise adherence is the main challenge -- patients are symptomatic and fatigued during treatment
- SLP supervision and motivational support critical for adherence
- Even partial adherence (some exercise vs none) shows benefit

## Role of the Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP)

The SLP is the **central clinician** in swallowing rehabilitation:

1. **Pre-treatment counselling** -- educate patient on expected swallowing changes; set realistic expectations
2. **Baseline assessment** -- instrumental evaluation (MBS/FEES) before treatment
3. **Exercise prescription** -- individualised preventive exercise programme
4. **During-treatment monitoring** -- weekly assessment during RT; adjust exercises for mucositis/pain
5. **Diet modification** -- recommend appropriate food textures and liquid consistencies
6. **Compensatory strategies** -- chin tuck, head rotation, supraglottic swallow
7. **Post-treatment rehabilitation** -- progressive exercise programme; serial instrumental assessments
8. **Long-term surveillance** -- monitor for late RAD; adjust programme accordingly

## DARS-Sparing Radiotherapy

### Concept

DARS-sparing IMRT/VMAT aims to reduce radiation dose to uninvolved swallowing structures while maintaining therapeutic dose to the tumour and at-risk nodal volumes.

### Structures to Spare

| Structure | Dose Constraint (Mean) |
|---|---|
| Superior pharyngeal constrictor (uninvolved) | <50 Gy |
| Middle pharyngeal constrictor (uninvolved) | <50 Gy |
| Inferior pharyngeal constrictor | <50 Gy |
| Glottic larynx | <40 Gy |
| Supraglottic larynx (uninvolved) | <50 Gy |
| Oral cavity (uninvolved) | <40 Gy |
| Parotid glands | <26 Gy mean (at least one) |
| Submandibular glands | <35 Gy |

### Evidence

- **Eisbruch 2004:** Identified DARS and demonstrated feasibility of IMRT sparing
- **Feng 2010:** Prospective study showed DARS-sparing IMRT with chemotherapy achieved excellent tumour control with swallowing function only slightly worse than baseline
- **DARS phase III trial (Nutting, Lancet Oncol 2023):** Confirmed DARS-optimised IMRT reduced patient-reported swallowing difficulties at 12 months compared with standard IMRT

### Limitations

- Cannot spare structures directly involved by tumour
- Trade-off between DARS sparing and tumour coverage
- Bilateral oropharyngeal tumours limit sparing opportunities
- Benefit most pronounced for lateralised tonsillar tumours

## Surgical Rehabilitation

### Pharyngeal Augmentation / Flap Procedures

- **Pharyngeal flap** -- for velopharyngeal insufficiency after soft palate resection
- **Palatal obturator** -- prosthetic closure of soft palate defect
- **Pharyngeal augmentation** -- injection or implant to reduce pharyngeal lumen and improve bolus transit
- See [[Answers/Oropharynx/Pharyngeal Reconstruction|Pharyngeal Reconstruction]] for detailed reconstructive options

### Dilatation for Stricture

- **Endoscopic balloon dilatation** -- first-line for radiation-induced pharyngeal/oesophageal stricture
- Often requires serial dilatations (3--6 sessions)
- Adjunctive intralesional steroid injection (triamcinolone)
- Mitomycin C topical application (antifibrotic)
- Refractory strictures may require surgical revision or stenting

### Cricopharyngeal Myotomy / Botulinum Toxin

- For UES dysfunction (failure of relaxation)
- **Botulinum toxin injection** -- endoscopic injection into cricopharyngeus; temporary effect (3--6 months)
- **Cricopharyngeal myotomy** -- surgical division of cricopharyngeus; for persistent UES dysfunction
- **Dilatation** -- alternative first-line for cricopharyngeal bar

### Laryngeal Suspension / Medialization

- For patients with aspiration due to impaired laryngeal closure
- Vocal fold medialization (injection or thyroplasty) for glottic incompetence
- Laryngeal suspension procedures for impaired hyolaryngeal elevation

## Timing of Rehabilitation

| Phase | Focus | Key Interventions |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-treatment** (1--4 weeks before) | Baseline assessment, education, exercise initiation | MBS/FEES, SLP consult, exercise teaching, nutritional optimization |
| **During treatment** (RT weeks 1--7) | Maintain exercise, manage acute symptoms | Continue exercises, diet modification, PEG placement if needed |
| **Early post-treatment** (0--3 months) | Symptom management, resume exercises | Gradual exercise intensification, serial assessment |
| **Intermediate** (3--12 months) | Active rehabilitation, functional recovery | MBS/FEES reassessment, diet upgrade, stricture management |
| **Long-term** (>12 months) | Maintenance, surveillance for late RAD | Ongoing exercises, annual assessment, manage complications |

## Multidisciplinary Approach

Effective swallowing rehabilitation requires coordination among:

| Team Member | Role |
|---|---|
| Head and neck surgeon | Tumour resection, reconstruction, salvage surgery |
| Radiation oncologist | DARS-sparing RT planning, dose optimization |
| Medical oncologist | Chemotherapy selection, toxicity management |
| Speech-language pathologist | Swallowing assessment, exercise prescription, ongoing rehabilitation |
| Dietitian/nutritionist | Caloric and protein optimization, PEG management, texture modification |
| Dentist/prosthodontist | Dental clearance, fluoride trays, palatal obturator |
| Physiotherapist | Trismus management, cervical spine mobility |
| Psychologist | Adjustment to dysphagia, quality of life, depression screening |
| Palliative care | Symptom management in advanced/refractory cases |

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **DARS = pharyngeal constrictors + glottic/supraglottic larynx** -- Eisbruch 2004; IMRT can spare these to reduce dysphagia
2. **Prophylactic exercises are superior to reactive rehabilitation** -- "use it or lose it"; begin exercises BEFORE treatment starts
3. **Late RAD** (Hutcheson 2012) is a distinct entity: progressive dysphagia >=5 years post-RT due to fibrosis + cranial neuropathy; often irreversible
4. **MBS/VFSS is the gold standard** for comprehensive swallowing assessment; FEES is complementary for pharyngeal phase and sensation
5. **Tongue base retraction is the most critical function** impaired after oropharyngeal cancer treatment -- drives most rehabilitation strategies
6. The **Pharyngocise trial** (Carnaby-Mann 2012) provides level 1 evidence for preventive exercises during CRT
7. **SLP involvement from pre-treatment** is essential -- not just a post-treatment referral
8. **Mean constrictor dose >50 Gy** is the key threshold associated with significant dysphagia -- keep DARS doses below this when possible
9. **EAT-10 >=3** is the screening threshold for dysphagia referral
