Ferris et al. (2022) published the ECOG-ACRIN E3311 phase II randomised trial of transoral surgery followed by risk-stratified adjuvant therapy in p16+ OPC, demonstrating excellent 2-year PFS across all risk groups and validating the surgery-based de-escalation strategy.

## History and Development of TORS

### Evolution of Transoral Surgery

| Era | Approach | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-1990s | Open mandibulotomy / lip-split | Significant morbidity, bone cuts, tracheostomy required |
| 1990s | Transoral laser microsurgery (TLM) | 2D vision, limited instrument manoeuvrability, steep learning curve |
| 2000s | Transoral robotic surgery (TORS) | Overcomes line-of-sight limitations with 3D optics and wristed instruments |

### Key Milestones

- **2005**: Weinstein and O'Malley at University of Pennsylvania begin preclinical TORS development
- **2006**: First published TORS for base of tongue neoplasms (O'Malley et al.)
- **2007**: First TORS radical tonsillectomy series (Weinstein et al.)
- **2009**: FDA grants de novo clearance for TORS in T1-T2 oropharyngeal cancer
- **2013**: TORS adopted into NCCN guidelines as a treatment option for OPC
- **2019**: ORATOR trial provides first randomised comparison of TORS vs RT
- **2022**: E3311 validates surgery-based de-escalation framework

### The da Vinci Surgical System

The da Vinci system (Intuitive Surgical, Sunnyvale, CA) provides:

1. **3D high-definition visualisation**: Binocular endoscope with 10x magnification
2. **Wristed instruments**: 7 degrees of freedom (vs 4 for rigid instruments), mimicking the human wrist
3. **Tremor filtration**: Eliminates physiological hand tremor
4. **Motion scaling**: Surgeon movements can be scaled down for precision
5. **Ergonomic console**: Surgeon operates seated at a console, reducing fatigue

**Components**:
- Surgeon console (remote from patient)
- Patient-side cart with 3-4 robotic arms
- Vision cart with image processing
- EndoWrist instruments (5 mm diameter): spatula cautery, Maryland dissector, needle driver, scissors

## Patient Selection and Contraindications

### Indications for TORS in OPC

| Criterion | Details |
|---|---|
| Tumour stage | T1-T2 (selected T3 in experienced centres) |
| Subsite | Tonsil, base of tongue, soft palate, posterior pharyngeal wall |
| p16/HPV status | Both p16+ and p16- eligible (most commonly p16+) |
| Prior treatment | Primary untreated or selected recurrent disease |
| Nodal disease | N0-N2c (AJCC 7th) / N0-N2 (AJCC 8th p16+) |

### Absolute Contraindications

1. **T4b disease**: Carotid encasement, prevertebral fascia invasion, pterygoid plate involvement
2. **Trismus** (inter-incisal opening <2 cm): Prevents adequate oral access
3. **Mandibular body involvement**
4. **Unresectable neck disease** (N3, fixed nodes, carotid encasement)
5. **Significant comorbidities** precluding general anaesthesia

### Relative Contraindications

1. **T4a disease**: Case-by-case assessment in experienced centres
2. **Deep infiltration of tongue base** (>50% involvement): risk of severe dysphagia
3. **Retromolar trigone extension** requiring mandibulectomy
4. **Prior radiation** to the oropharynx (salvage TORS has higher complication rates)
5. **Retropalatal narrowing** or unfavourable anatomy limiting access
6. **Lingual artery proximity** (MRI assessment recommended)
7. **Obesity / short thick neck** (limited transoral access)

### Pre-operative Assessment

- **Examination under anaesthesia (EUA)**: Essential to assess transoral access before committing to TORS
- **MRI oropharynx**: Evaluate tumour extent, depth, relationship to carotid artery and prevertebral fascia
- **CT neck with contrast**: Nodal staging
- **[[Answers/General/PET-CT in Head and Neck Cancer|PET-CT]]**: Distant staging
- **Dental assessment**: Particularly if adjuvant RT anticipated
- **Swallowing baseline**: FEES or videofluoroscopy for pre-operative functional assessment

## Surgical Technique

### Operating Room Setup

1. **Patient positioning**: Supine, shoulder roll, head extended (Rose position)
2. **Anaesthesia**: Nasal intubation (preferred) or oral RAE tube positioned to one side
3. **Mouth retractor**: Feyh-Kastenbauer (FK) retractor or Crowe-Davis mouth gag
4. **Robot positioning**: da Vinci cart positioned at head of bed, approaching from cephalad
5. **Surgical team**: Surgeon at console, bedside assistant at patient (suction, retraction, clip application)

### Docking and Instrument Placement

- **Central arm**: 0-degree or 30-degree down endoscope
- **Left arm**: 5 mm Maryland dissector (retraction and grasping)
- **Right arm**: 5 mm spatula tip cautery (primary dissection instrument)
- **Third arm** (optional): Additional retraction instrument
- Arms positioned in a triangulated configuration through the oral cavity

### TORS for Tonsillar Carcinoma (Radical Tonsillectomy)

1. **Mucosal incision**: Begin anteriorly along the anterior tonsillar pillar, extending superiorly to soft palate and inferiorly to tongue base
2. **Dissection of tonsillar fossa**: Dissect laterally through the superior constrictor muscle to the parapharyngeal fat
3. **Identification of landmarks**: Parapharyngeal fat, styloglossus muscle, internal carotid artery (lateral boundary)
4. **Superior margin**: Soft palate (partial palatectomy if involved)
5. **Inferior margin**: Base of tongue mucosa/musculature
6. **Posterior margin**: Posterior pharyngeal wall if needed
7. **Haemostasis**: Bipolar cautery, clips for larger vessels, ligature of ascending pharyngeal branches
8. **Specimen orientation and marking**: Critical for margin assessment

### TORS for Base of Tongue (BOT) Carcinoma

1. **Exposure**: Tongue retracted anteriorly using suture or FK retractor
2. **Mucosal incision**: Circumferential around tumour with planned margins (≥1 cm)
3. **Deep dissection**: Through tongue musculature (genioglossus, hyoglossus, styloglossus)
4. **Key landmarks**: Lingual artery (deep and lateral), vallecula, pre-epiglottic space
5. **Margin considerations**: Frozen section analysis of deep margin (critical)
6. **Extent**: May include partial epiglottectomy if tumour extends to vallecula

### TORS for Soft Palate Carcinoma

1. **Incision**: Around tumour with margins
2. **Depth**: Full-thickness resection through palatal musculature
3. **Reconstruction considerations**: Defects >50% of soft palate may cause velopharyngeal insufficiency
4. **Margins**: Frozen section guided

### Margins

- **Target**: ≥1 cm clinical margin (≥5 mm on pathological assessment)
- **Frozen section**: Mandatory for all margins (superficial and deep)
- **Deep margin on BOT**: Most challenging and most important for oncologic control
- **Re-resection**: If positive margins on frozen section, immediate re-excision or conversion to open approach

## Neck Dissection Timing and Approach

### Standard Approach

- **Selective neck dissection** (levels II-IV) ipsilateral, performed at the same operation as TORS or staged (within 2-3 weeks)
- **Bilateral neck dissection** for midline tumours (BOT) or bilateral nodal disease
- **Same-sitting vs staged**: Both acceptable; same-sitting preferred by most centres unless operative time concern

### Considerations

- **N0 neck**: Elective selective neck dissection (levels II-IV) recommended for all but the smallest T1 tumours
- **N+ neck**: Comprehensive modified radical neck dissection (levels I-V) or selective dissection (II-IV) based on extent of disease
- **Sentinel node biopsy**: Investigational in oropharyngeal cancer; not standard of care

## Adjuvant Therapy After TORS

### Risk Stratification (Based on E3311)

| Risk Category | Pathological Features | Adjuvant Therapy |
|---|---|---|
| Low risk | Clear margins, ≤1 mm PNI, no LVI, no ENE, ≤4 nodes | Observation or RT (50 Gy) |
| Intermediate risk | Close margins (1-3 mm), LVI, PNI, ≤4 nodes, no ENE | RT 50-60 Gy |
| High risk | Positive margins, ENE >1 mm, ≥5 positive nodes | RT 60-66 Gy + cisplatin |

### Indications for Adjuvant Radiation

- Positive or close margins
- [[Answers/General/Perineural Invasion|Perineural invasion]]
- Lymphovascular invasion
- Multiple positive nodes (>1)
- pT3-T4 primary
- [[Answers/General/Extranodal Extension|Extranodal extension]]

### Indications for Adjuvant Chemoradiation

- Positive surgical margins (after re-resection attempt)
- Extranodal extension >1 mm
- Multiple high-risk features

## Reconstruction After TORS

### Healing by Secondary Intention

- **Most common approach** for TORS defects
- Suitable for: tonsillectomy defects, small BOT defects (<3 cm), soft palate defects <50%
- Granulation tissue fills the defect over 4-6 weeks
- Advantages: No donor site morbidity, simplified surgery, good functional outcomes
- Disadvantages: Delayed healing, temporary swallowing difficulty, rare pharyngocutaneous fistula

### Local and Regional Flaps

| Flap | Indication | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buccal fat pad | Tonsillar fossa/soft palate | Simple, reliable, same-field | Limited volume |
| Facial artery musculomucosal (FAMM) | Soft palate, tonsil | Good soft tissue bulk, pedicled | Limited arc of rotation |
| Pharyngeal flap | Soft palate | Addresses VPI directly | Limited applications |

### Free Tissue Transfer

For larger defects (>50% BOT, through-and-through, circumferential):

| Flap | Best Indication | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Radial forearm free flap (RFFF) | BOT and soft palate defects | Thin, pliable, good for resurfacing; best for moderate defects |
| Anterolateral thigh (ALT) | Large BOT or composite defects | Greater volume, can be thinned; long pedicle |
| Rectus abdominis | Large-volume defects | Excellent bulk for total glossectomy defects |
| Fibula osteocutaneous | Mandibular involvement | Bony reconstruction if marginal mandibulectomy needed |

### Reconstructive Algorithm After TORS

1. **Small tonsil/BOT defect (<3 cm)**: Secondary intention
2. **Medium tonsil defect (3-5 cm)** without soft palate: Secondary intention or local flap
3. **Soft palate defect >50%**: Local flap (FAMM) or RFFF
4. **Large BOT defect (>50% tongue base)**: RFFF or ALT free flap
5. **Circumferential pharyngeal defect**: ALT or jejunal free flap
6. **Defect involving mandible**: Fibula free flap

### Velopharyngeal Insufficiency (VPI) After Soft Palate Resection

- Risk increases with resection of >50% of soft palate
- **Assessment**: Nasendoscopy, videofluoroscopy
- **Management**: Palatal obturator, pharyngeal flap, sphincter pharyngoplasty
- **Prevention**: Limit soft palate resection when possible; consider primary flap reconstruction

## Management of Recurrent HPV-positive OPC

### After Primary TORS

For recurrence after TORS ± adjuvant therapy:

1. **Local recurrence (small, resectable)**:
   - Re-do TORS if technically feasible and no prior RT
   - Open salvage surgery (mandibulotomy approach) if TORS not feasible
   - Salvage RT/CRT if no prior radiation

2. **Local recurrence after TORS + adjuvant RT**:
   - Salvage surgery (open approach usually required)
   - Re-irradiation with or without chemotherapy (selected cases)
   - Systemic therapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) for unresectable recurrence

3. **Regional recurrence (neck)**:
   - Salvage neck dissection
   - Consider re-irradiation to neck if not previously irradiated

4. **Distant metastatic recurrence**:
   - Systemic therapy: pembrolizumab (first-line for PD-L1 CPS ≥1) or platinum-based chemotherapy
   - Clinical trial enrollment

### Salvage TORS After Primary RT/CRT

- **Increasing role**: Selected patients with persistent/recurrent OPC after definitive RT/CRT
- **Higher complication rates**: 20-30% (vs 5-10% for primary TORS)
- **Key complications**: Pharyngocutaneous fistula (10-15%), haemorrhage (5-10%), flap necrosis
- **Selection**: Small-volume recurrence (≤T2), adequate transoral access, no carotid involvement
- **Outcomes**: 2-year OS 50-74% for salvage TORS (better than historical open salvage)

## Oncologic Outcomes

### Multi-institutional Data (de Almeida 2015)

In 410 patients treated with TORS across 11 institutions:

| Outcome | 2-year Rate |
|---|---|
| Overall survival | 91.0% |
| Disease-specific survival | 94.5% |
| Locoregional control | 91.8% |
| Distant control | 93.8% |

### ORATOR Trial (Nichols 2019)

First randomised comparison of TORS vs primary RT:

| Outcome | TORS + ND | Primary RT |
|---|---|---|
| 1-year MDADI (QoL) | 80.1 | 86.9 (p=0.042) |
| 2-year OS | 92% | 90% |
| 2-year PFS | 85% | 78% |
| Serious adverse events | 11% | 4% |

**Key finding**: Radiotherapy achieved better swallowing-related quality of life at 1 year. However, OS and PFS were comparable. Long-term follow-up showed convergence of QoL outcomes.

### E3311 Trial (Ferris 2022)

| Risk Arm | Adjuvant Therapy | 2-year PFS |
|---|---|---|
| A (low risk) | Observation | 96.9% |
| B (intermediate) | 50 Gy RT | 94.9% |
| C (intermediate) | 60 Gy RT | 96.0% |
| D (high risk) | 66 Gy + cisplatin | 90.7% |

## Functional Outcomes

### Swallowing

- **Short-term** (0-3 months): Significant dysphagia in 30-50% (worse for BOT than tonsil)
- **Long-term** (>12 months): Normal swallowing in 85-90% without adjuvant RT, 70-80% with adjuvant RT
- **Gastrostomy dependence**: <5% at 1 year (vs 15-20% with primary CRT)
- **Key predictor**: Volume of tongue base resected and need for adjuvant therapy

### Speech

- **Articulation**: Generally well preserved after tonsillectomy; BOT resection may cause mild articulatory impairment
- **Resonance**: Velopharyngeal insufficiency risk with soft palate resection >50%
- **Voice**: Preserved (no laryngeal involvement)

### Comparison with Primary RT/CRT

| Functional Outcome | TORS ± adj RT | Primary CRT (70 Gy) |
|---|---|---|
| Swallowing (MDADI) at 1 year | 80-85 | 85-90 |
| Long-term dysphagia (>2 years) | 10-15% | 20-30% |
| Xerostomia | Mild (if adj RT) | Moderate-severe |
| Taste dysfunction | Mild | Moderate-severe |
| Trismus | 5-10% | 10-15% |
| Feeding tube at 1 year | <5% | 10-15% |
| Osteoradionecrosis | Rare | 5-8% |

**Net functional balance**: TORS avoids significant late radiation toxicity (xerostomia, fibrosis, ORN) but has short-term surgical morbidity. With adjuvant RT, the functional advantage of TORS diminishes.

## Complications of TORS

### Intraoperative

- **Haemorrhage**: 2-5% (usually from tonsillar/lingual branches)
- **Incomplete resection**: Conversion to open approach if margins cannot be achieved
- **Dental/mucosal injury**: From retractor placement

### Early Postoperative (0-30 days)

| Complication | Incidence |
|---|---|
| Post-tonsillectomy haemorrhage | 3-8% |
| Dysphagia requiring NG/PEG | 15-30% (temporary) |
| Aspiration pneumonia | 2-5% |
| Transient tongue weakness | 5-10% (BOT) |
| Wound infection | 2-3% |
| Tracheostomy (temporary) | 5-15% |

### Late Complications

| Complication | Incidence |
|---|---|
| Pharyngocutaneous fistula | 1-3% (higher in salvage) |
| Chronic dysphagia | 5-15% |
| Velopharyngeal insufficiency | 5-10% (soft palate resection) |
| Carotid blowout | <1% (higher in salvage/re-irradiation) |
| Trismus | 5-10% |

### Fatal Haemorrhage Risk

- **Carotid blowout**: Rare but devastating complication, especially in salvage settings
- Risk factors: Prior RT, fistula, exposed carotid artery
- Prevention: Carotid coverage with muscle flap if vessel exposed during TORS
- Management: [[Answers/General/Balloon Occlusion Test|Balloon occlusion test]] and embolisation if high-risk; urgent surgical exploration

## Key Points for Exam

1. TORS was developed by Weinstein and O'Malley at University of Pennsylvania, first published in 2006
2. FDA cleared TORS for T1-T2 OPC in 2009
3. da Vinci system provides 3D vision, wristed instruments, tremor filtration, and motion scaling
4. Patient selection requires EUA to confirm transoral access; trismus (<2 cm) is an absolute contraindication
5. E3311 demonstrates the surgery-based de-escalation framework: TORS → pathological risk stratification → risk-adapted adjuvant therapy
6. ORATOR trial showed comparable oncologic outcomes between TORS and RT, but RT had better swallowing QoL at 1 year
7. Most TORS defects heal by secondary intention; free flaps (RFFF, ALT) reserved for large defects
8. Post-tonsillectomy haemorrhage is the most common serious complication (3-8%)
9. Salvage TORS after RT/CRT is feasible but has higher complication rates (20-30%)
10. The main functional advantage of TORS over CRT is avoidance of long-term radiation toxicity (xerostomia, fibrosis, ORN), but this advantage diminishes if adjuvant RT is needed
