## Introduction

18F-fluorodeoxyglucose positron emission tomography-computed tomography (FDG-PET/CT) has become an indispensable imaging modality in the management of oropharyngeal cancer (OPC). It combines the metabolic information from PET (identifying areas of increased glucose metabolism characteristic of malignancy) with the anatomic detail of CT, providing superior diagnostic accuracy compared to either modality alone. In oropharyngeal cancer specifically, PET-CT plays critical roles across the entire patient journey: initial staging, detection of unknown primaries, radiation treatment planning, post-treatment response assessment, and surveillance for recurrence. The role of PET-CT has become even more nuanced with the recognition of [[Answers/General/HPV in Oral and Oropharyngeal Cancer|HPV-positive]] oropharyngeal cancer as a distinct entity with different biological behaviour and imaging characteristics.

## Rationale for PET-CT in OPC

### Why PET-CT Is Particularly Valuable in Oropharyngeal Cancer

1. **High metabolic activity:** OPC (especially SCC) is highly FDG-avid, providing excellent tumour-to-background contrast
2. **Complex anatomy:** The oropharynx has complex anatomy with deep tissue planes — PET adds functional information to anatomic imaging
3. **Nodal disease is common:** 60-80% of OPC patients present with cervical nodal metastases; PET-CT is superior for detecting involved nodes
4. **HPV-positive disease:** The rising incidence of HPV-positive OPC creates unique staging and surveillance challenges where PET-CT excels
5. **Non-surgical management predominates:** Most OPC is treated with chemoradiation, making post-treatment imaging (rather than pathological assessment) the primary method of response evaluation
6. **Distant metastases:** OPC (especially HPV+) can metastasise to unusual sites — whole-body PET-CT detects these

### Principle of FDG-PET

- **18F-FDG** is a glucose analogue taken up by cells via GLUT transporters (GLUT-1 upregulated in cancer)
- Phosphorylated by hexokinase to FDG-6-phosphate but cannot proceed further in glycolysis
- Trapped intracellularly, accumulating in metabolically active cells
- Cancer cells exhibit the **Warburg effect** (increased aerobic glycolysis) — hence avid FDG uptake
- PET detects the 511 keV annihilation photons produced when the positron from 18F encounters an electron
- **SUV (Standardised Uptake Value)** = semi-quantitative measure of FDG uptake, normalised to body weight and injected dose

## Staging Accuracy vs CT/MRI

### Primary Tumour (T Stage)

| Modality | Sensitivity | Specificity | Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| CT alone | 70-80% | 75-85% | Bony invasion, anatomy |
| MRI | 80-90% | 80-90% | Soft tissue detail, perineural spread, BOT extension |
| PET-CT | 85-95% | 80-90% | Metabolic activity, distinguishes tumour from post-treatment changes |

- **MRI remains the gold standard** for T staging in OPC due to superior soft tissue contrast
- PET-CT adds value in distinguishing tumour from surrounding oedema/inflammation
- PET-CT is superior for detecting synchronous primaries and distant metastases

### Nodal Disease (N Stage)

| Modality | Sensitivity | Specificity | Key Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| CT | 65-80% | 70-85% | Size criteria, necrosis |
| MRI | 70-85% | 75-85% | Soft tissue detail |
| PET-CT | **85-95%** | **85-95%** | Detects metabolically active nodes regardless of size |

PET-CT is superior for nodal staging because:
- Detects metastases in **normal-sized lymph nodes** (<1 cm) that appear normal on CT/MRI
- Identifies **contralateral nodal disease** (critical for treatment planning)
- Better at detecting **retropharyngeal** nodal metastases
- Can identify nodes with only partial replacement that retain normal morphology on CT

### Distant Metastases (M Stage)

- PET-CT is the most sensitive modality for detecting distant metastases in HNC
- Sensitivity for distant metastases: **85-100%**
- Particularly important in advanced OPC (N2-N3 disease) where distant failure risk is significant
- Detects metastases in unusual sites (bone, brain, solid organs) that may be missed by conventional imaging
- HPV-positive OPC has a disseminating phenotype with atypical metastatic patterns

### Overall Staging Accuracy

- PET-CT changes the clinical stage in **10-30%** of HNC patients
- **Upstaging** is more common than downstaging
- Changes treatment plan in **15-20%** of cases (e.g., detection of distant metastases precluding curative-intent therapy)

## Detection of Unknown Primary

One of the most valuable applications of PET-CT in head and neck oncology is the workup of **cervical nodal metastasis with an unknown primary** (CUP). The oropharynx (particularly the tonsil and base of tongue) is the most common site of the occult primary in SCC-CUP.

### PET-CT Performance in CUP

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Detection rate of occult primary | 25-55% |
| Sensitivity | 80-90% |
| Specificity | 85-95% |
| False positive rate | 5-15% |

### Clinical Protocol for CUP

1. Clinical examination including pharyngoscopy
2. CT neck with contrast and MRI
3. **PET-CT** if primary not identified
4. Examination under anaesthesia (EUA) with directed biopsies (including bilateral tonsillectomy and BOT biopsies)
5. PET-CT guides directed biopsies at EUA — improving yield

### Advantages of PET-CT in CUP

- Identifies small, submucosal primaries in the tonsil or BOT
- Guides targeted biopsies during EUA, increasing diagnostic yield
- Detects synchronous primaries
- Identifies unsuspected distant metastases
- Can reduce the volume of mucosal irradiation if the primary is localised (avoiding pan-mucosal RT)

## Role in Treatment Planning (GTV Delineation)

### PET-CT for Radiotherapy Planning

Troost et al. (2010) demonstrated the value of PET-CT in radiotherapy planning:

- **GTV delineation:** PET can reduce inter-observer variability in GTV contouring
- PET-defined GTV is often **smaller** than CT-defined GTV (reduces unnecessary irradiation of normal tissue)
- PET can identify tumour extension not visible on CT (e.g., deep base of tongue invasion)
- PET highlights areas of highest metabolic activity within the GTV that may benefit from dose escalation

### Practical Application

1. **PET-CT simulation:** Patient undergoes PET-CT in the treatment position with immobilisation mask
2. **Co-registration:** PET images are fused with the planning CT
3. **GTV contouring:** The radiation oncologist uses both CT and PET to delineate the GTV
4. **SUV thresholds:** Various methods for PET-based GTV definition:
   - Fixed SUV threshold (e.g., SUV >2.5)
   - Percentage of SUVmax (e.g., >40% of SUVmax)
   - Gradient-based methods
   - No single method is universally accepted

### Benefits

- Reduced inter-observer variability in GTV contouring
- Potentially smaller treatment volumes (less toxicity)
- Identification of nodal disease not seen on CT (better target coverage)
- Basis for **dose painting** — delivering higher doses to metabolically active subvolumes

### Limitations in Treatment Planning

- FDG uptake in inflamed tissue (post-biopsy, post-tonsillectomy) causes false positives
- Spatial resolution of PET (~4-5 mm) is inferior to CT/MRI
- PET-defined volumes vary with the threshold used
- Motion artefacts (swallowing, breathing) can blur PET signal
- PET-CT simulation adds time and cost to the planning process

## Post-Treatment Response Assessment

### Optimal Timing: 12 Weeks Post-Treatment

**The critical issue in post-treatment PET-CT is timing.** Scanning too early leads to high false-positive rates due to radiation-induced inflammation.

| Timing | False Positive Rate | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| <8 weeks | Very high (>40%) | Not recommended |
| 8-10 weeks | Moderate (20-30%) | Suboptimal |
| **12 weeks** | **Low (10-15%)** | **Recommended standard** |
| >16 weeks | Low | Acceptable but may delay detection of persistent disease |

### The 12-Week Standard

- **12 weeks (3 months) post-completion of chemoradiation** is the recommended timing
- By 12 weeks, most treatment-related inflammation has resolved
- FDG uptake from residual viable tumour persists while inflammatory uptake subsides
- This timing was validated by the PET-NECK trial (Mehanna 2016)

### Interpretation

| PET-CT Finding | Clinical Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Complete metabolic response (CMR) | No residual FDG uptake at primary or nodal sites | Surveillance |
| Partial metabolic response | Reduced but persistent FDG uptake | Close follow-up; repeat PET at 4-6 weeks; consider biopsy |
| No response / progression | Persistent or increased FDG uptake | Biopsy; consider salvage surgery |
| New lesions | Distant metastases or second primary | Biopsy to confirm; change management |

## PET-NECK Trial (Mehanna 2016)

### Study Design

- **Type:** Multicentre, randomised, non-inferiority trial
- **Population:** Advanced nodal disease (N2/N3) head and neck SCC treated with chemoradiotherapy
- **N:** 564 patients (282 per arm)
- **Arms:**
  - PET-CT-guided surveillance (neck dissection only if incomplete/equivocal response on PET-CT at 12 weeks)
  - Planned neck dissection (all patients undergo ND regardless of response)
- **Primary endpoint:** Overall survival

### Key Results

| Outcome | PET-CT Surveillance | Planned ND | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2-year OS | 84.9% | 81.5% | Non-inferior (p=0.004 for non-inferiority) |
| Neck dissections performed | **19%** | **100%** | 80% reduction in ND |
| Quality of life | Better | Worse | Fewer complications |
| Cost | Lower | Higher | Savings from avoided surgery |

### Clinical Impact

- **PET-CT surveillance is now the standard of care** for post-chemoradiation management of N2/N3 neck disease
- Eliminated the need for planned neck dissection in ~80% of patients
- Neck dissection reserved only for patients with incomplete metabolic response on PET-CT
- Applicable to oropharyngeal cancer (the majority of trial participants had OPC)

### Decision Algorithm (Post-PET-NECK)

```
12 weeks post-CRT
        |
   PET-CT scan
        |
  ______|_______
  |            |
CMR         Incomplete response
  |            |
Surveillance   Neck dissection
  |            (or repeat PET at 4-6 weeks)
Follow-up
```

## Negative Predictive Value

The exceptionally high **negative predictive value (NPV)** of PET-CT is its greatest clinical strength in post-treatment assessment:

| Site | NPV (Gupta 2011 meta-analysis) |
|---|---|
| Primary site | **95.1%** (93.5-96.5%) |
| Neck | **94.5%** (93.1-95.7%) |

### Clinical Implication

- A **negative PET-CT at 12 weeks** provides >94% confidence that there is no residual viable disease
- This high NPV is the foundation for surveillance-based management (avoiding unnecessary neck dissection)
- The positive predictive value is lower (~50-60%) — a positive PET-CT requires further evaluation (biopsy) before intervention

## SUVmax as Prognostic Value

### Pre-Treatment SUVmax

| Parameter | Significance |
|---|---|
| High SUVmax (>10-15) | Associated with more aggressive biology, higher risk of treatment failure |
| Low SUVmax (<5) | May indicate less aggressive tumour or HPV+ biology |
| Nodal SUVmax | Higher nodal SUV associated with worse nodal control |
| Metabolic tumour volume (MTV) | Total volume of metabolically active tumour — emerging prognostic marker |
| Total lesion glycolysis (TLG) | SUV x MTV — integrates metabolic activity and tumour burden |

### Post-Treatment SUV

- **Complete metabolic response (SUVmax <3 or below background):** Excellent prognosis
- **Residual uptake (SUVmax >5):** Associated with persistent disease; warrants further investigation
- **Percentage reduction in SUVmax:** >80% reduction associated with favourable outcome

### Prognostic Value Summary

- Pre-treatment MTV and TLG are **stronger prognostic markers** than SUVmax alone
- High MTV/TLG predict poorer overall survival independent of T and N stage
- These PET-based metabolic parameters may be incorporated into future staging and risk stratification systems

## PET-Adapted Treatment

### Concept

Using PET-CT response during or after treatment to adapt the treatment strategy — an emerging paradigm particularly relevant in HPV-positive OPC where treatment de-intensification is being explored.

### Applications

1. **Mid-treatment PET (adaptive RT):**
   - PET-CT during week 2-3 of chemoradiation
   - Early metabolic response may predict final outcome
   - Could guide adaptive replanning (dose escalation to non-responding regions or de-escalation in responders)
   - Currently investigational

2. **Post-induction chemotherapy PET:**
   - Assessing response after induction chemotherapy to guide subsequent treatment
   - Good metabolic response may support de-intensification
   - Poor response may warrant intensification

3. **De-escalation trials (HPV+ OPC):**
   - Using PET-CT response to select patients for reduced-dose radiation
   - E.g., good PET response after low-dose RT may avoid completion to full dose
   - Multiple ongoing clinical trials (ECOG-ACRIN EA3311, OPTIMA)

## Limitations of PET-CT

### False Positives

| Cause | Mechanism | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Post-radiation inflammation | FDG uptake in inflamed tissue | Wait 12 weeks post-RT |
| Post-biopsy inflammation | Tissue disruption, healing | Delay PET-CT 2-4 weeks after biopsy |
| Infection (abscess, mucositis) | Neutrophil and macrophage FDG uptake | Treat infection first; correlate clinically |
| Physiological uptake | Tonsils, BOT, vocal cords | Know normal variants |
| Brown fat | Cold-induced metabolic activity | Warm patient; pre-medicate with beta-blocker |
| Dental artefact | Metal artefact on CT component | Dental work before PET if possible |

### False Negatives

| Cause | Mechanism | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Small tumours (<8 mm) | Below PET spatial resolution | Combine with MRI/clinical exam |
| Mucinous or cystic tumours | Low metabolic activity | Correlate with CT morphology |
| Post-treatment necrotic nodes | Viable tumour at periphery missed | Biopsy equivocal findings |
| Recent chemotherapy | Suppressed tumour metabolism | Delay PET 2-3 weeks after last chemo cycle |
| Hyperglycaemia | Competitive inhibition of FDG uptake | Check glucose (<200 mg/dL); fast 6 hours |

### General Limitations

- **Cost:** Significantly more expensive than CT or MRI alone
- **Availability:** Not universally available, particularly in resource-limited settings
- **Radiation exposure:** Additional radiation dose from the CT component and the radiotracer
- **Spatial resolution:** ~4-5 mm — inferior to MRI for defining soft tissue borders
- **Operator dependent:** Interpretation variability among readers

## HPV-Positive vs HPV-Negative PET Patterns

### Key Differences (Subramaniam 2014)

| Feature | HPV-Positive OPC | HPV-Negative OPC |
|---|---|---|
| Primary tumour SUVmax | Often lower | Often higher |
| Primary tumour size | Frequently smaller (T1-T2) | Frequently larger (T3-T4) |
| Nodal disease | Large, cystic nodes (may have low FDG uptake due to cystic component) | Solid, necrotic nodes (typically FDG-avid) |
| Nodal SUVmax | Variable (cystic nodes may show peripheral uptake only) | Typically high |
| Distant metastases | Unusual sites (bone, brain, liver — "disseminating phenotype") | Lung predominant |
| Response to treatment | Excellent — high CMR rate | More variable |
| PET-CT timing | May need longer interval (>12 weeks) as cystic nodes resolve slowly | Standard 12-week timing |

### Implications for PET-CT Interpretation in HPV+ OPC

- **Cystic nodes:** HPV-positive OPC commonly metastasises to cervical nodes that become large and cystic. These may show:
  - Low or absent central FDG uptake (cystic centre)
  - Rim enhancement only
  - May be falsely classified as non-avid on PET
  - **Correlate with CT/MRI morphology — do not rely on PET alone for cystic nodes**

- **Post-treatment assessment:**
  - HPV+ patients generally have higher complete response rates
  - But residual cystic nodes may persist for months after CRT even in complete responders
  - A persistent cystic node with no FDG uptake is likely a treated node — not persistent disease
  - These patients benefit from extended follow-up rather than immediate intervention

- **Surveillance:**
  - Late recurrences (>3 years) are more common in HPV+ OPC than HPV-
  - PET-CT surveillance may need to be extended beyond the traditional 2-year window
  - Atypical metastatic sites require whole-body PET coverage

## Cost-Effectiveness

### PET-NECK Trial Health Economic Analysis

- PET-CT-guided surveillance saved approximately GBP 1,492 per patient compared to planned neck dissection
- Savings came from avoided surgical procedures (80% reduction in ND)
- Even accounting for the cost of PET-CT scans, the overall strategy was cost-effective
- **Quality-adjusted life years (QALYs):** PET-CT surveillance was dominant (better outcomes at lower cost)

### General Cost-Effectiveness Considerations

| Scenario | PET-CT Value |
|---|---|
| Initial staging (advanced OPC) | Cost-effective — changes management in 15-20%, avoids futile treatment |
| Unknown primary workup | Cost-effective — reduces number of biopsies, guides directed sampling |
| Post-treatment response (N2/N3) | Cost-effective — avoids 80% of planned neck dissections (PET-NECK) |
| Routine surveillance (all HNC) | Not cost-effective — reserved for clinical suspicion of recurrence |
| Low-stage disease (T1N0) | Not cost-effective — conventional staging sufficient |

## Key Points for Examination

1. **PET-CT at 12 weeks post-CRT** is the standard for response assessment in OPC treated with chemoradiation
2. **PET-NECK trial (Mehanna 2016):** PET-CT surveillance is non-inferior to planned neck dissection for OS in N2/N3 HNC, reducing unnecessary ND by 80%
3. **NPV >94%** — a negative PET-CT at 12 weeks provides very high confidence of no residual disease
4. **PPV is lower (~50-60%)** — a positive PET-CT requires biopsy confirmation before intervention
5. PET-CT changes staging in **10-30%** of HNC patients and treatment plan in **15-20%**
6. **HPV+ OPC** has distinct PET features: cystic nodes (low FDG), smaller primaries, disseminating metastatic pattern
7. **CUP workup:** PET-CT detects occult primary in 25-55% of cases; guides directed biopsies
8. **SUVmax, MTV, and TLG** are emerging prognostic markers independent of TNM stage
9. PET-CT for **GTV delineation** reduces inter-observer variability and may improve target definition for radiotherapy planning
10. **False positives** (inflammation, infection) are the main limitation — mitigated by appropriate timing (12 weeks post-RT)
