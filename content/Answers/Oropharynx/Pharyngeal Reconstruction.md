(2005) published the landmark functional outcomes study comparing jejunal interposition with anterolateral thigh flap for circumferential pharyngoesophageal reconstruction, demonstrating that the ALT flap achieved shorter hospital stays and better speech and swallowing outcomes. Yu et al. (2006) compared the anterolateral thigh free flap with the jejunal free flap for pharyngoesophageal reconstruction, reporting equivalent complication rates but better functional outcomes and lower hospital costs with the ALT flap. Murray et al. (2007) reported functional outcomes and donor-site morbidity of circumferential pharyngoesophageal reconstruction using the anterolateral thigh flap with a salivary bypass tube, showing a 14% stricture rate and no fistulae. (2010) published a comprehensive review of circumferential pharyngeal reconstruction with a critical analysis of techniques and historical evolution, providing evidence-based therapeutic recommendations for defect-based flap selection.

## Goals of Pharyngeal Reconstruction

1. **Swallowing** -- restore a watertight pharyngeal conduit capable of propelling a bolus from oral cavity to oesophagus
2. **Speech** -- maintain intelligible speech; enable tracheoesophageal puncture (TEP) voice if total laryngectomy performed
3. **Airway** -- protect the airway (if larynx preserved); create a functional stoma (if total laryngectomy)
4. **Wound healing** -- provide well-vascularised tissue to irradiated or previously operated fields
5. **Aesthetics** -- acceptable cosmetic outcome and donor-site morbidity
6. **Prevention of complications** -- minimise pharyngocutaneous fistula, stricture, and aspiration

## Classification of Pharyngeal Defects

### By Extent of Resection

| Defect Type | Definition | Percentage of Circumference |
|---|---|---|
| Partial (Type I) | Less than half the circumference | <50% |
| Subtotal (Type II) | More than half but not complete | 50--75% |
| Near-circumferential (Type III) | Only a narrow strip of mucosa remaining | 75--99% |
| Circumferential (Type IV) | Complete removal of pharyngeal tube | 100% |

### By Location

| Location | Resection Type | Common Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Oropharyngeal | Partial/total pharyngectomy | Oropharyngeal SCC |
| Hypopharyngeal | Partial/total pharyngolaryngectomy | Hypopharyngeal/piriform SCC |
| Cervical oesophageal | Pharyngolaryngo-oesophagectomy | Advanced hypopharyngeal/cervical oesophageal SCC |
| Combined | Extended resection | Locally advanced tumours |

### By Functional Deficit

- **Mucosal deficit only** -- pharyngeal lining requires reconstruction
- **Full-thickness deficit** -- mucosa + musculature; requires bulk and lining
- **Composite deficit** -- includes mandible, soft palate, or laryngeal structures
- **Conduit deficit** -- complete interruption of the alimentary conduit requiring neoesophagus

## Reconstructive Ladder for Pharyngeal Defects

### 1. Primary Closure

- **Indication:** Small partial defects (<30% circumference) with adequate residual mucosa
- **Technique:** Tension-free multilayer closure; T-closure or linear closure
- **Advantages:** Simplest option; no donor-site morbidity; rapid recovery
- **Limitations:** Risk of stricture if closed under tension; typically only for small defects
- **Fistula rate:** 5--15% (higher in irradiated fields)

### 2. Local and Regional Flaps

#### Pectoralis Major Myocutaneous Flap (PMMF)

- **Workhorse regional flap** for pharyngeal reconstruction before free flap era
- **Blood supply:** Pectoral branch of thoracoacromial artery
- **Indications:**
  - Partial pharyngeal defects
  - Salvage reconstruction when free flap not feasible
  - Fistula repair in irradiated fields
  - Augmentation/buttressing over primary closure
- **Advantages:** Reliable; robust blood supply; short operative time; no microsurgical expertise needed
- **Limitations:** Bulky; limited arc of rotation for high oropharyngeal defects; poor match for mucosal lining; higher fistula and stricture rates than free flaps; cosmetic donor-site deformity in women
- **Fistula rate:** 10--25%

#### Supraclavicular Artery Island Flap (SCAIF)

- **Blood supply:** Supraclavicular artery (branch of transverse cervical artery)
- **Indications:** Partial pharyngeal defects, oropharyngeal lining, salvage cases
- **Advantages:** Thin, pliable; good colour match; same operative field; no microsurgical expertise
- **Limitations:** Limited arc; size limitation; unreliable in previously irradiated or dissected neck
- **Fistula rate:** 5--15%

#### Other Regional Options

- **Deltopectoral flap** -- historically used; now largely replaced by PMMF and free flaps
- **Submental flap** -- useful for oropharyngeal mucosal defects; oncologically controversial if neck positive
- **Platysma flap** -- limited bulk and unreliable blood supply in irradiated fields

### 3. Free Flaps

#### Radial Forearm Free Flap (RFFF) -- Workhorse for Partial Defects

- **Blood supply:** Radial artery and venae comitantes
- **Skin paddle:** Thin, pliable, hairless forearm skin
- **Indications:**
  - Partial pharyngeal defects (oropharyngeal, hypopharyngeal)
  - **Tubed RFFF** for near-circumferential or circumferential defects
  - Soft palate reconstruction
  - BOT reconstruction
- **Advantages:** Thin, pliable -- ideal for mucosal replacement; long vascular pedicle; reliable anatomy; two-team approach; excellent for partial defects
- **Limitations:** Donor-site morbidity (skin graft, tendon exposure, forearm weakness); limited bulk; requires Allen test; size limited for large circumferential defects
- **Tubed RFFF for circumferential defects:**
  - Skin paddle folded into a tube to create neopharynx
  - Minimum skin paddle width ~8 cm to create adequate lumen
  - Higher stricture rate than jejunal flap (15--30%)
  - Better speech outcomes than jejunal flap (allows TEP voice)
- **Fistula rate:** 5--15%

#### Anterolateral Thigh Flap (ALT)

- **Blood supply:** Descending branch of lateral circumflex femoral artery; musculocutaneous or septocutaneous perforators through vastus lateralis
- **Indications:**
  - Partial or circumferential pharyngeal defects
  - Large-volume defects requiring bulk
  - Composite oropharyngeal defects
- **Advantages:** Large skin paddle available; can be thinned; long pedicle; minimal donor-site morbidity (primary closure possible); can harvest with vastus lateralis for bulk; versatile
- **Tubed ALT for circumferential defects:**
  - Excellent option for neopharynx creation
  - Lower stricture rate than tubed RFFF
  - Better speech outcomes than jejunal flap (Lewin 2005)
  - Shorter hospital stays than jejunal (Yu 2006)
- **Limitations:** Variable perforator anatomy; may be too bulky in obese patients; learning curve
- **Fistula rate:** 5--12%

#### Jejunal Free Flap

- **Blood supply:** Jejunal mesenteric arcade (branches of superior mesenteric artery)
- **Indications:**
  - **Circumferential pharyngeal defects** -- traditional gold standard
  - Pharyngolaryngo-oesophagectomy defects
- **Advantages:** Pre-formed tube -- no need for flap tubing; secretory mucosa aids bolus transit; good for circumferential defects; low stricture rate (5--15%)
- **Limitations:**
  - Requires laparotomy (donor-site morbidity, ileus, adhesions)
  - Wet, secretory mucosa -- excessive secretions
  - Short vascular pedicle
  - Limited tolerance for ischaemia (harvested segment must be placed quickly)
  - Poor speech outcomes with TEP -- peristalsis interferes with voice prosthesis
  - Cannot monitor flap externally
  - Mortality risk from laparotomy in frail patients
- **Fistula rate:** 10--20%
- **Stricture rate:** 5--15%

#### Other Free Flap Options

- **Latissimus dorsi flap** -- large surface area; useful for massive defects but very bulky
- **Lateral arm flap** -- thin alternative to RFFF but shorter pedicle
- **Fibula flap with skin paddle** -- when composite reconstruction with mandible needed
- **Scapular/parascapular flap** -- versatile but requires lateral decubitus positioning

### 4. Gastric Pull-Up (Gastric Transposition)

- **Indication:** Extensive pharyngolaryngo-oesophagectomy defects extending to or below thoracic inlet
- **Technique:** Mobilise stomach; divide left gastric and short gastric vessels; pull up through posterior mediastinum to pharyngeal remnant
- **Advantages:** Single anastomosis; pre-formed conduit; reliable blood supply; rapid restoration of alimentary continuity
- **Limitations:** Major operation; high morbidity/mortality (5--10%); mediastinitis risk; reflux; dumping syndrome; nutritional consequences
- **Fistula rate:** 10--25%

## Algorithm by Defect Type

### Partial Defect (<50% Circumference)

```
Small defect (<30%) --> Primary closure +/- PMMF buttress
                        |
                        v
Medium defect (30-50%) --> Free flap preferred:
                           1st choice: RFFF (thin, pliable)
                           2nd choice: ALT (if bulk needed)
                           3rd choice: PMMF (if free flap not available)
```

### Subtotal/Near-Circumferential Defect (50-99%)

```
Oropharyngeal/Hypopharyngeal --> RFFF patch or ALT
                                 |
                                 v
                          If residual strip too narrow
                          for primary + patch --> Treat as circumferential
```

### Circumferential Defect (100%)

```
Short segment (<6 cm) --> 1st: Tubed ALT
                          2nd: Tubed RFFF
                          3rd: Jejunal free flap
                          |
Long segment (>=6 cm) --> 1st: Jejunal free flap
or extends below         2nd: Tubed ALT
thoracic inlet           3rd: Gastric pull-up
```

### Decision Factors for Circumferential Reconstruction

| Factor | ALT / RFFF (Tubed) | Jejunal | Gastric Pull-Up |
|---|---|---|---|
| Defect length | Short-medium | Any | Long / intrathoracic |
| TEP voice required | Yes (preferred) | Poor voice quality | Not applicable |
| Prior abdominal surgery | Advantage (no laparotomy) | Relative contraindication | Relative contraindication |
| Patient fitness | Good | Requires abdominal tolerance | High fitness required |
| Stricture rate | 15--30% (RFFF) / 10--20% (ALT) | 5--15% | 10--20% |
| Fistula rate | 5--15% | 10--20% | 10--25% |

## Pharyngocutaneous Fistula Prevention

Pharyngocutaneous fistula (PCF) is the most common major complication after pharyngeal reconstruction, with rates of 10--25% overall.

### Risk Factors

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| Prior radiation/CRT | Greatest risk factor (2--3x increased) |
| Hypothyroidism | Impaired wound healing |
| Malnutrition (albumin <3.0) | Poor tissue healing |
| Diabetes mellitus | Microvascular compromise |
| Positive surgical margins | Higher fistula rates |
| Circumferential defect | Greater than partial |
| Salvage surgery | Combined effect of prior treatment |

### Prevention Strategies

1. **Well-vascularised tissue interposition** -- free flap or pedicled flap buttress over suture lines
2. **Tension-free closure** -- fundamental surgical principle
3. **Salivary bypass tube** -- silicone stent placed at time of reconstruction to divert saliva (Murray 2007)
4. **Nutritional optimisation** -- preoperative feeding, albumin >3.0 g/dL
5. **Prophylactic flap augmentation** -- vascularised tissue overlay in salvage settings
6. **Meticulous surgical technique** -- watertight closure, avoidance of dead space
7. **Drain placement** -- closed suction drains to prevent seroma/haematoma
8. **Early nutritional support** -- nasogastric or gastrostomy feeding; delay oral intake 7--14 days

### Fistula Management

- **Small, low-output fistula** --> conservative management: NPO, wound care, antibiotics, nutritional support
- **Large or persistent fistula** --> surgical repair with vascularised tissue (PMMF, SCAIF, free flap)
- **Carotid exposure** --> URGENT flap coverage to prevent carotid blowout

## Functional Outcomes

### Swallowing

| Reconstruction | Oral Diet Achieved | Gastrostomy Removal Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Primary closure | 85--95% | 80--90% |
| RFFF (partial) | 80--90% | 75--85% |
| ALT (partial) | 80--90% | 75--85% |
| Tubed ALT (circumferential) | 60--75% | 55--70% |
| Jejunal (circumferential) | 55--70% | 50--65% |
| Gastric pull-up | 50--65% | 45--60% |

### Voice / Speech

| Reconstruction | TEP Voice Success | Speech Intelligibility |
|---|---|---|
| Partial pharyngectomy (larynx preserved) | N/A | Near-normal |
| Tubed RFFF/ALT | 60--80% | Good |
| Jejunal | 20--40% | Poor (peristalsis) |
| Gastric pull-up | 30--50% | Fair |

### Stricture Management

- **Incidence:** 10--30% depending on reconstruction type
- **Presentation:** Progressive dysphagia weeks to months post-reconstruction
- **Diagnosis:** Barium swallow, flexible endoscopy
- **Treatment:**
  - Endoscopic balloon dilatation (first-line; often repeated)
  - Rigid oesophagoscopy with bougie dilatation
  - Steroid injection (intralesional triamcinolone at stricture site)
  - Mitomycin C application (topical antifibrotic)
  - Surgical revision (for refractory strictures)

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **RFFF is the workhorse** for partial pharyngeal defects -- thin, pliable, reliable
2. **ALT is increasingly preferred** for both partial and circumferential defects -- versatile, minimal donor-site morbidity
3. For **circumferential defects**, tubed ALT or jejunal flap are the main options; choice depends on TEP voice needs and defect length
4. **Jejunal flap produces poor TEP voice** due to peristalsis -- avoid if tracheoesophageal voice is desired
5. **Salvage surgery in irradiated fields** demands well-vascularised free flap tissue -- never rely on primary closure alone
6. **Salivary bypass tube** (Murray 2007) reduces fistula rates in circumferential reconstruction
7. **Gastric pull-up** is reserved for defects extending below the thoracic inlet or when free flap options are exhausted
