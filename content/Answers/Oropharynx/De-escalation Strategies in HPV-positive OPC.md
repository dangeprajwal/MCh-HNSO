Chen et al. (2017) published a single-arm phase 2 study of reduced-dose radiotherapy (54-60 Gy) with concurrent chemotherapy for HPV-associated OPC, reporting excellent 2-year locoregional control and reduced toxicity compared to standard 70 Gy regimens.

## Rationale for De-escalation in HPV-positive OPC

### Why De-escalate?

HPV-positive oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma (OPSCC) represents a biologically distinct entity with dramatically superior outcomes compared to HPV-negative disease. The rationale for de-escalation includes:

1. **Excellent prognosis**: 3-year OS exceeding 80-90% with standard chemoradiation, compared to 50-60% for HPV-negative OPSCC
2. **Younger patient population**: Median age 55-60 years (vs 65+ for HPV-negative), meaning patients live longer with treatment toxicity
3. **Significant long-term morbidity**: Standard cisplatin-based CRT at 70 Gy causes dysphagia (50-60%), xerostomia (>80%), osteoradionecrosis (5-8%), feeding tube dependence (15-20%), hearing loss, and nephrotoxicity
4. **Different biology**: HPV-positive tumours retain wild-type [[Answers/General/Tumour Suppressor Genes|p53]], making them more radiosensitive and chemosensitive

### Patient Selection for De-escalation

Current risk stratification for de-escalation:

| Factor | Favourable (Low-risk) | Unfavourable (High-risk) |
|---|---|---|
| HPV/p16 status | Positive | Negative |
| Smoking history | <10 pack-years | ≥10 pack-years |
| T stage | T1-T3 | T4 |
| N stage | N0-N2c (AJCC 7th) | N3 |
| Performance status | ECOG 0-1 | ECOG ≥2 |

**Key principle**: De-escalation should only be pursued within clinical trials or established protocols. The NCCN and ESMO guidelines do not currently recommend de-escalation outside of trials.

## Strategies for De-escalation

### Strategy 1: Substitution of Cisplatin with Cetuximab

The hypothesis was that replacing cisplatin with [[Answers/General/Monoclonal Antibodies in Head and Neck Cancer|cetuximab]] would maintain efficacy while reducing toxicity.

#### De-ESCALaTE HPV Trial (Mehanna 2019)

- **Design**: Open-label, randomised phase 3 trial (UK, Netherlands, Ireland)
- **Population**: 334 patients with low-risk HPV-positive OPC (T3 N0-N2c or T4 N0-N2c non-smokers)
- **Arms**: RT (70 Gy) + cisplatin 100 mg/m² (3 cycles) vs RT (70 Gy) + cetuximab weekly
- **Primary endpoint**: Severe toxicity events at 24 months
- **Results**:
  - 2-year OS: **97.5% cisplatin vs 89.4% cetuximab** (HR 5.0; 95% CI 1.7-14.7; p=0.001)
  - 2-year recurrence: **6.0% cisplatin vs 16.1% cetuximab** (HR 3.4; p=0.004)
  - Severe toxicity: **No significant difference** between arms
- **Conclusion**: Cetuximab was inferior for both oncologic outcomes and failed to reduce toxicity

#### RTOG 1016 (Gillison 2019)

- **Design**: Randomised, multicentre, [[Answers/General/Non-Inferiority Clinical Trials|non-inferiority trial]] (USA)
- **Population**: 849 patients with HPV-positive OPC (p16+ by IHC)
- **Arms**: RT (70 Gy) + cisplatin 100 mg/m² vs RT (70 Gy) + cetuximab weekly
- **Primary endpoint**: Overall survival (non-inferiority margin HR 1.45)
- **Results**:
  - 5-year OS: **84.6% cisplatin vs 77.9% cetuximab** (HR 1.45; 95% CI 1.03-2.05; p=0.0163)
  - 5-year PFS: **78.4% cisplatin vs 67.3% cetuximab** (HR 1.72; p=0.0002)
  - Cetuximab **failed non-inferiority** for OS
  - Acute toxicity: cetuximab had fewer grade 3-4 events but more locoregional failures
- **Conclusion**: Cisplatin-based CRT is the standard of care; cetuximab substitution is not recommended

**Impact of these two trials**: Cetuximab-based de-escalation is definitively abandoned. Any future substitution strategy must demonstrate non-inferiority in a properly powered trial.

### Strategy 2: Reduced-Dose Radiation Therapy

#### E1308 (Marur 2017)

- **Design**: Phase II trial (ECOG-ACRIN)
- **Population**: 90 patients with HPV16+ or p16+ resectable stage III-IV OPSCC
- **Strategy**: Induction chemotherapy (cisplatin + paclitaxel + cetuximab x 3 cycles) → assessment → complete responders receive **54 Gy IMRT + cetuximab** (vs 69.3 Gy for incomplete responders)
- **Results**:
  - Primary site complete response rate to induction: 70%
  - In 54 Gy arm: 2-year PFS 80%, 2-year OS 94%
  - Swallowing function preserved in majority (MDADI scores significantly better than historical controls at standard dose)
- **Significance**: Proof of concept that dose reduction to 54 Gy is feasible in selected responders to induction chemotherapy

#### Chen 2017 (Lancet Oncol)

- **Design**: Single-arm phase 2 study
- **Population**: 44 patients with HPV-associated OPSCC (T1-T3, N0-N2c, <10 pack-years)
- **Strategy**: Reduced-dose IMRT (54 Gy to low-risk volumes, 60 Gy to high-risk) with concurrent weekly cisplatin or carboplatin
- **Results**:
  - 2-year locoregional control: 100%
  - 2-year PFS: 92%
  - Significantly reduced rates of dysphagia and xerostomia compared to historical 70 Gy cohorts
- **Significance**: Demonstrated that 15-20% radiation dose reduction maintains tumour control while improving functional outcomes

#### NRG HN002 (Yom 2021)

- **Design**: Phase II randomised trial
- **Population**: 308 patients with p16+ OPC (T1-2 N1-2b or T3 N0-2b)
- **Arms**: 60 Gy CRT with cisplatin vs 60 Gy RT alone
- **Results**:
  - 60 Gy + cisplatin: 2-year PFS 90.5% (met primary endpoint)
  - 60 Gy RT alone: 2-year PFS 87.6% (also met primary endpoint)
- **Significance**: Supports 60 Gy as a feasible reduced dose; awaits phase III confirmation

### Strategy 3: Transoral Surgery-Based De-escalation

#### ECOG-ACRIN E3311 (Ferris 2022)

- **Design**: Phase II randomised trial
- **Population**: 359 patients with p16+ stage III-IVa OPC amenable to transoral resection
- **Strategy**: Transoral surgery (TOS) + selective neck dissection → pathological risk stratification → risk-adapted adjuvant therapy:
  - **Arm A (low risk)**: Negative margins, ≤1 mm perineural invasion, no lymphovascular invasion, <1 mm extranodal extension (ENE), ≤4 positive nodes → **observation only**
  - **Arm B (intermediate risk, randomised)**: Close margins, ≤4 nodes, no ENE → **50 Gy RT**
  - **Arm C (intermediate risk, randomised)**: Same criteria → **60 Gy RT**
  - **Arm D (high risk)**: Positive margins, >1 mm ENE, ≥5 nodes → **66 Gy RT + cisplatin**
- **Results**:
  - 2-year PFS: Arm A 96.9%, Arm B 94.9%, **Arm C 96.0%**, Arm D 90.7%
  - De-escalated 50 Gy arm performed comparably to 60 Gy
  - Functional outcomes (swallowing, speech) significantly better with de-escalated therapy
- **Significance**: Validates surgery-first approach with risk-stratified de-escalation. Phase III (EA3311) is ongoing.

#### PATHOS Trial

- **Design**: Phase III multicentre trial (UK)
- **Population**: p16+ OPC patients undergoing transoral surgery
- **Strategy**: Post-operative risk stratification:
  - Intermediate risk: Randomised to RT (60 Gy) vs reduced RT (50 Gy)
  - High risk: Randomised to CRT (60 Gy + cisplatin) vs RT alone (60 Gy)
- **Primary endpoint**: Swallowing function (MDADI) with non-inferiority for oncologic outcomes
- **Status**: Recruitment completed; results awaited

### Strategy 4: Immunotherapy-Based De-intensification

#### DART-HPV (NCT02908477)

- **Design**: Phase III randomised trial
- **Population**: HPV-positive OPC
- **Strategy**: Nivolumab + low-dose RT (30-36 Gy in 3 fractions, SBRT approach) vs standard CRT
- **Rationale**: PD-1 blockade may enhance radiation-induced immune responses, allowing substantial dose reduction
- **Status**: Ongoing

#### Keynote-412 and Other Checkpoint Inhibitor Trials

- Pembrolizumab added to standard CRT investigated in locoregionally advanced HNSCC
- JAVELIN Head and Neck 100 (avelumab + CRT): did not meet primary endpoint
- Current consensus: immunotherapy-based de-escalation remains investigational

### Strategy 5: Induction Chemotherapy for Selection

- **Concept**: Use response to induction chemotherapy as a biomarker to select patients for reduced-intensity definitive therapy
- **E1308 model**: Complete responders receive reduced-dose RT
- **Optima trial**: Induction chemotherapy → response assessment → dose-adapted RT (50 Gy for complete response vs 75 Gy for partial response)
- **Limitation**: Adds toxicity of induction chemotherapy; no randomised evidence yet supporting this approach over standard CRT

## Current Guidelines and Recommendations

### NCCN Guidelines

- **Standard of care**: Cisplatin-based CRT (70 Gy) or primary surgery + risk-adapted adjuvant therapy
- De-escalation **not recommended** outside clinical trials
- Clinical trial enrollment strongly encouraged for HPV-positive patients

### ESMO Guidelines

- No de-escalation outside trials
- HPV-positive status should not alter standard treatment recommendations
- Risk stratification (HPV, smoking, stage) should guide trial enrollment

### Practical Recommendations

1. **Outside trials**: Treat HPV-positive OPC with standard cisplatin-CRT (70 Gy) or surgery + adjuvant therapy per pathological risk
2. **Counsel patients** about ongoing trials; consider referral to trial centres
3. **Do not substitute** cetuximab for cisplatin based on HPV status alone
4. **Surgical de-escalation** (TORS-based) is the most promising current strategy, with E3311 data supporting risk-stratified adjuvant reduction
5. **Monitor** for mature phase III data from PATHOS, DART-HPV, and NRG trials

## Key Points for Exam

1. HPV-positive OPC has 80-90% 5-year OS with standard CRT, creating the rationale for de-escalation
2. **De-ESCALaTE and RTOG 1016** definitively showed cetuximab cannot replace cisplatin
3. **E3311** is the strongest evidence for surgery-based de-escalation with risk-adapted adjuvant RT
4. Reduced-dose RT (54-60 Gy) shows promise in phase II but awaits phase III validation
5. De-escalation must only be pursued in clinical trials; premature de-escalation risks inferior outcomes
6. Smoking status is a critical modifier: heavy smokers with HPV-positive disease have worse outcomes and should not be de-escalated
7. The three pillars of de-escalation strategies are: (a) agent substitution (failed), (b) dose reduction, (c) surgery-based risk stratification
