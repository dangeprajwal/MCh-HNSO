> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Amit et al. published the largest single-institution series on induction chemotherapy for sinonasal undifferentiated carcinoma (SNUC) in the Journal of Clinical Oncology in 2019, analysing 95 previously untreated patients treated at MD Anderson Cancer Center between 2001 and 2018. Patients received platinum-based doublet induction chemotherapy (cisplatin 60–80 mg/m² day 1 with etoposide 100–120 mg/m² days 1–3, or docetaxel 75 mg/m² in 21 patients). The overall response rate to induction chemotherapy was 67.4%. In responders who underwent definitive concurrent chemoradiotherapy, the 5-year disease-specific survival was 81%, compared to 54% for responders who proceeded to surgery and adjuvant radiotherapy. This study established induction chemotherapy as the preferred first step in SNUC management, using response as a bioselection tool: complete and partial responders proceed to definitive chemoradiotherapy (avoiding extensive surgery and its morbidity), while non-responders undergo surgical resection.
>
> London et al. published institutional experience from Ohio State University and Johns Hopkins (2020) in Head & Neck, reporting a contemporary series of 21 SNUC patients managed with an institutional protocol shift toward TPF induction chemotherapy followed by concurrent chemoradiotherapy since 2016, demonstrating that this sequence was achievable and associated with no evidence of disease at a mean follow-up of 16.8 months.

## Role of Induction Chemotherapy in Paranasal Sinus Tumours

### Rationale for Induction Chemotherapy in PNS Cancer

Paranasal sinus and sinonasal tumours present late because of the silent expansion of the nasal cavity and sinuses. The majority are T4 at diagnosis, with skull base involvement, orbital invasion, or pterygoid infiltration. Induction (neoadjuvant) chemotherapy (ICT) serves several critical purposes in this anatomical context:

1. **Assessment of chemosensitivity**: Tumour response to ICT is the strongest indicator of biological behaviour and predicts long-term outcome. Non-responders are biologically chemoresistant and should not be subjected to prolonged cycles; early identification allows timely shift to surgical or radiotherapy strategies.

2. **Downstaging to resectability**: T4b tumours with skull base erosion or cavernous sinus involvement may achieve partial response sufficient to allow R0 resection that was not technically feasible at initial presentation. In SNUC, approximately 50% of initially unresectable cases become operable after ICT.

3. **Organ preservation — orbit**: The most clinically important application. If MRI after ICT demonstrates intact orbital periosteum (periorbitum) without fat invasion, the orbit can be preserved during total maxillectomy. ICT converts orbital T4b (fat invasion, periorbitum breached) to T4a (contained by periorbitum) in selected cases.

4. **Early treatment of micrometastatic disease**: Sinonasal tumours with a propensity for haematogenous spread (SNUC, SNEC, small cell NEC) benefit from early systemic therapy before commitment to locoregional treatment. The risk of distant failure is high (25–40%) in these histologies without upfront systemic control.

5. **Bioselection for definitive treatment modality**: Analogous to the larynx preservation paradigm, ICT response guides the choice between definitive chemoradiotherapy (responders) and surgical resection (non-responders or partial responders). This avoids extensive craniofacial resection in patients destined to respond to radiation.

### Tumour-Specific Indications and Evidence

#### 1. Sinonasal Undifferentiated Carcinoma (SNUC)
**ICT is the current standard of care for all T3-T4 SNUC.**

- **Regimen**: Cisplatin 60–80 mg/m² day 1 + etoposide 100–120 mg/m² days 1–3, q21 days × 3 cycles (PE regimen)
- Alternative: Carboplatin AUC 5–6 + etoposide (cisplatin-ineligible patients)
- **Response rate**: 60–70% (CR + PR combined)
- **Treatment algorithm after ICT**:
  - Complete or partial response → definitive concurrent chemoradiotherapy (cisplatin 100 mg/m² q3 weeks concurrent with 66–70 Gy); 5-year DSS 81%
  - Less-than-partial response / progression → surgical resection (CFR or eCFR) + adjuvant RT ± chemotherapy; 5-year DSS 54%
- **Key trial**: Amit et al. 2019, JCO (DOI: 10.1200/JCO.18.00353) — n=95, MD Anderson

---

#### 2. Olfactory Neuroblastoma / Esthesioneuroblastoma (ENB) — Kadish C and D
- **Regimens**: Cisplatin + etoposide (PE); or VAC (vincristine 1.5 mg/m², doxorubicin 60 mg/m², cyclophosphamide 1000 mg/m² q3 weeks — historically used in paediatric and high-grade cases)
- **Rationale**: Kadish C/D tumours often involve the dura, cavernous sinus, or orbit; ICT is used to reduce bulk, assess biological aggressiveness, and convert to resectable before craniofacial resection + adjuvant RT
- ICT is not universally mandated in Kadish C but is standard practice at most high-volume centres for T4b or N+ disease
- **Benefit**: Patients achieving CR with ICT + RT may avoid surgical morbidity entirely; however, long-term data favour multimodality treatment including surgery in Kadish C

---

#### 3. Sinonasal Neuroendocrine Carcinoma (SNEC) — Small Cell NEC
- **Regimen**: Cisplatin 80 mg/m² + etoposide 100 mg/m² q3 weeks × 4–6 cycles (same as small cell lung cancer paradigm)
- Small cell NEC of the sinonasal region follows the biological behaviour of pulmonary SCLC with high risk of systemic dissemination
- ICT followed by concurrent chemoradiation is the preferred approach; surgery reserved for residual locoregional disease or isolated recurrence
- Prophylactic cranial irradiation (PCI) should be discussed (data extrapolated from pulmonary SCLC)

---

#### 4. Sinonasal Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)
- ICT is **not standard** outside of clinical trials for sinonasal SCC
- Available evidence (mainly retrospective series) suggests ICT (TPF or PF) can downstage T4 sinonasal SCC in approximately 30–40% of cases
- TPF regimen: docetaxel 75 mg/m² day 1, cisplatin 75 mg/m² day 1, 5-FU 750 mg/m²/day days 1–5, q3 weeks — 3 cycles
- Primarily used in T4b unresectable cases to achieve resectability; not a routine sequence
- The oral cavity IORCA trial data and head and neck SCC literature (TAX 323/TAX 324) provide the framework but direct sinonasal SCC trial data are lacking

---

#### 5. Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma (ACC) of PNS
- **ICT is generally NOT used** in ACC
- ACC grows slowly and is poorly responsive to conventional cytotoxic chemotherapy
- Cisplatin-based regimens yield response rates of only 10–20% in advanced ACC
- Preferred strategy: surgery + adjuvant RT (including perineural routes); for unresectable/metastatic ACC, targeted therapy (axitinib, lenvatinib — VEGFR; regorafenib) or immunotherapy in selected cases

---

#### 6. Intestinal-Type Adenocarcinoma (ITAC)
- Limited prospective data
- ICT with platinum-based regimens considered for unresectable T4b ITAC
- Response rates modest; role largely as a bridge to surgery or RT

### Response Assessment After Induction Chemotherapy

- **Timing**: CT neck and paranasal sinuses + MRI skull base / orbit after 2–3 cycles; do not wait until completion of all cycles before assessment
- **Criteria**: RECIST v1.1
  - Complete response (CR): no measurable residual disease
  - Partial response (PR): ≥30% reduction in sum of longest diameters
  - Stable disease (SD): neither PR nor PD
  - Progressive disease (PD): ≥20% increase or new lesion
- **MRI orbit after ICT**: critical for orbit preservation decision — intact periorbitum (bright linear signal on T2 fat-suppressed MRI) = orbit can be spared; loss of periorbitum signal with fat infiltration = exenteration required
- **PET-CT**: SUV response is supplementary; useful for detecting occult nodal or distant response that CT/MRI may underestimate
- **MDT decision after response assessment**: Oncology, surgery (HN + neurosurgery), radiation oncology — consensus on next treatment step

Approximately 30–40% of initially orbit-threatened cases can have orbital contents preserved with ICT-based downstaging.

### Risks and Limitations of ICT

| Issue | Detail |
|---|---|
| Delay to definitive treatment | 9–12 weeks added before surgery or RT; accepted if response achieved |
| Toxicity | Neutropenia, nephrotoxicity, neuropathy (cisplatin); myelosuppression (etoposide); mucositis (5-FU) |
| Non-responders | Exposed to chemotherapy toxicity with no benefit; progressive disease during ICT portends poor prognosis |
| Anaemia and nutritional compromise | Worsened by ICT; PEG feeding often required before ICT begins |
| Requires MDT | Response assessment and treatment decision must be made at experienced multidisciplinary team — not appropriate for low-volume centres |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Induction chemotherapy in paranasal sinus tumours is principally established as standard of care for SNUC (cisplatin + etoposide × 3 cycles), where it serves as a bioselection tool: complete and partial responders proceed to definitive concurrent chemoradiotherapy with 5-year disease-specific survival of approximately 80%, while non-responders undergo surgical resection. In olfactory neuroblastoma (Kadish C/D) and sinonasal small cell NEC, induction chemotherapy similarly precedes definitive locoregional treatment, using the PE regimen or VAC respectively. The most clinically important oncological application is orbital preservation — MRI assessment of the periorbitum after ICT determines whether orbital contents can be spared at the time of maxillectomy, converting otherwise exenterative cases to orbit-preserving resections in approximately 30–40% of initially threatened cases. Adenoid cystic carcinoma is the notable exception where conventional ICT is not used given poor responsiveness, and sinonasal SCC lacks prospective trial data to support routine ICT outside T4b unresectable presentations.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. ICT in SNUC is the most robustly evidence-based application: cisplatin + etoposide × 3 cycles, response-stratified treatment — responders to definitive CRT, non-responders to surgery (Amit et al. 2019, JCO).
> 2. The "bioselection" concept means ICT is both therapeutic and diagnostic: it simultaneously treats the tumour and identifies patients who will benefit from non-surgical definitive treatment.
> 3. Orbit preservation depends critically on post-ICT MRI: intact periorbitum on T2 fat-suppressed MRI = orbit preserved; breached periorbitum = orbital exenteration.
> 4. Small cell SNEC is treated like pulmonary SCLC — cisplatin + etoposide, definitive CRT, consider prophylactic cranial irradiation.
> 5. TPF (docetaxel, cisplatin, 5-FU) is used for unresectable sinonasal SCC as a bridge to resectability; it is not standard in resectable cases.
> 6. Adenoid cystic carcinoma does NOT benefit from conventional cytotoxic ICT — response rates are 10–20%; targeted therapy (axitinib, lenvatinib) is preferred for recurrent/metastatic disease.
> 7. Response assessment by CT/MRI is performed after cycles 2–3, not at completion of all cycles — early identification of non-responders avoids unnecessary toxicity.
> 8. PEG feeding should be established before or during ICT in patients with swallowing compromise, as ICT worsens nutritional status.
> 9. VAC chemotherapy (vincristine, doxorubicin, cyclophosphamide) is used for ENB in the paediatric population and for high-grade (Hyams III/IV) adult ENB.
> 10. ICT adds 9–12 weeks to total treatment time; this delay is acceptable only when meaningful response is achieved — progressive disease during ICT is an absolute indication for immediate surgical review.
