> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> The modern total maxillectomy procedure was codified in the mid-twentieth century, building on the work of Ohngren (1933) and subsequently refined by multiple groups. Roux (1827) performed the first partial maxillary resection; Fergusson (1845) and Weber popularised the facial incision that bears their name. The Weber–Ferguson incision remains the standard for total maxillectomy and extended procedures, providing access to the entire maxilla, orbit, infratemporal fossa, and nasopharynx through a single external approach. The technique of midfacial degloving, described by Casson et al. (1974), offered an alternative for bilateral sinonasal access without an external facial scar.
>
> Complications of maxillectomy have been systematically studied in retrospective series. Ganly et al. (2005) in the International Collaborative Study of craniofacial resection reported on 334 patients and found an overall complication rate of 33%, with CNS complications (meningitis, CSF leak, brain abscess) in 8% and wound complications in 15%; perioperative mortality was 4.7%. Peri-orbital complications — epiphora, enophthalmos, diplopia — are the most common late functional sequelae of total maxillectomy with orbital floor removal, and their prevention through nasolacrimal management and titanium mesh reconstruction is a core aspect of the modern procedure.

## Maxillectomy: Procedure and Complications

### Pre-operative Assessment

Thorough pre-operative preparation is essential given the significant functional, cosmetic, and oncological implications of maxillectomy.

| Assessment | Key Points |
|---|---|
| Imaging review | CT (bone erosion, extent) + MRI (orbital floor, dural, PPF) — reviewed by surgeon and neuroradiologist in MDT |
| Dental assessment | OPG, dental panoramic view; teeth in the line of osteotomy identified; extraction of compromised teeth pre-operatively reduces infection risk |
| Ophthalmology review | Formal visual acuity, fields, and ocular motility before any procedure involving the orbit |
| Anaesthetic assessment | Extensive blood loss anticipated; cross-match 4–6 units; cell salvage; invasive arterial monitoring; nasal intubation vs. tracheostomy (if laryngoscopy compromised by trismus) |
| Prosthetic team consultation | Surgical obturator fabricated pre-operatively from dental impressions; placed at the end of surgery |
| Speech and language therapy | Pre-operative baseline swallowing and voice assessment |
| Nutritional assessment | Nasogastric or PEG tube if prolonged swallowing difficulty anticipated |

### Surgical Incisions

#### Weber–Ferguson Incision (Standard for Total Maxillectomy)

The Weber–Ferguson incision provides wide access to the anterior maxilla, orbit, and infratemporal fossa through a single external approach:

**Components of the incision:**
1. **Lip-splitting component**: begins at the midline of the upper lip philtrum, divides the lip in the midline, carries around the base of the columella and along the nasolabial fold
2. **Lateral nasal component**: curves around the alar base, up the nasolabial groove to the level of the alar-facial junction
3. **Subciliary extension** (for orbital access, total maxillectomy): extends from the medial canthus in the subciliary crease to the lateral canthus, or along the infraorbital rim
4. **Lynch extension** (for orbital/frontal access in extended resections): extends superiorly from the medial canthus toward the medial brow

The incision is marked pre-operatively with gentian violet. It is infiltrated with 1:100,000 adrenaline in 1% lignocaine. All cuts are made full thickness (skin → muscle → mucosa at the lip).

#### Alternative Incisions

| Incision | Access | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral rhinotomy (without lip split) | Medial maxilla, nasal cavity, ethmoids | T2–T3 medial maxillary tumours; inverted papilloma; avoids lip deformity |
| Midfacial degloving | Bilateral maxilla, nasal cavity, bilateral ethmoids | Bilateral tumours; avoids all external scar; via sublabial and transfixion incisions |
| Endoscopic approaches | See Endoscopic Surgery file | Selected medial tumours; medial maxillectomy; eCFR |

### Steps of Total Maxillectomy

The following describes a standard total maxillectomy (Brown Class IV) for a T3–T4a maxillary sinus malignancy.

**Step 1 — Positioning and exposure**
- Patient supine, head ring, 15° reverse Trendelenburg
- Corneal shields placed (scleral lenses or temporary tarsorrhaphy)
- Throat pack placed; anaesthetic tube secured at contralateral commissure

**Step 2 — Weber–Ferguson incision and cheek flap elevation**
- Incision as described above
- The cheek flap is elevated in a subperiosteal plane over the anterior maxillary surface, from the infraorbital rim superiorly to the canine fossa/buccal sulcus inferiorly
- The infraorbital nerve (V2) is identified at the infraorbital foramen and divided (or preserved if outside resection margin)
- Flap is reflected laterally to expose the full anterior maxillary face, malar eminence, and medial orbit

**Step 3 — Periorbital elevation**
- For total maxillectomy: the periorbita (periosteum of the orbital floor and medial wall) is elevated in a subperiosteal plane
- Identify and divide the nasolacrimal duct where it enters the nasolacrimal canal at the inferior aspect of the lacrimal fossa
- The orbital contents are retracted superiorly with a malleable retractor
- Anterior and posterior ethmoidal arteries are cauterised/ligated as encountered at the medial orbital wall

**Step 4 — Palatal osteotomy**
- Incise the palatal mucosa with diathermy along the midline or paramedian to the ipsilateral maxillary alveolus
- Periosteum is elevated
- Oscillating saw: palatal osteotomy along the midline (or paramedian if crossing the midline is required), from the canine teeth posteriorly to the junction of hard and soft palate

**Step 5 — Anterior and lateral maxillary wall osteotomies**
- Oscillating saw: osteotomy through the piriform aperture (medial osteotomy), connecting the palatal cut to the inferior orbital rim
- Frontal process of maxilla is divided just below the medial canthal tendon
- Lateral osteotomy: through the zygomaticomaxillary junction, connecting the inferior orbital rim cut to the zygomatic arch
- For extended maxillectomy: divide the zygomatic arch at its root; allow access to the infratemporal fossa

**Step 6 — Posterior maxillary wall and pterygoid plate separation**
- This is the most critical and potentially haemorrhagic step
- A curved osteotome is placed behind the tuberosity of the maxilla, directed posteriorly and superiorly
- The osteotome separates the maxilla from the pterygoid plates (pterygomaxillary separation)
- **Internal maxillary artery and its branches** are at risk here — haemostasis requires packing, ties, or bipolar cautery
- Alternatively, a reciprocating saw under direct vision with retraction of the pterygopalatine fossa contents

**Step 7 — Specimen removal**
- All osteotomies completed; the specimen is delivered by grasping the palate and rotating the maxilla inferiorly and laterally ("down-fracture")
- Soft tissue attachments at the posterior choana and nasopharynx are divided sharply
- The specimen is handed off for fresh-section and orientation marking

**Step 8 — Haemostasis and margin assessment**
- Pack the maxillectomy defect with adrenaline-soaked swabs
- Frozen section of all margins: posterior antral wall, orbital floor, palatal, nasal, and any suspicious areas
- Bipolar diathermy of major bleeders; haemostatic agents (Surgicel, oxidised cellulose) at pterygoid fossae

**Step 9 — Reconstruction and closure**
- Surgical obturator placed into the defect (pre-fabricated by prosthodontist) to separate oral and nasal cavities
- If free flap reconstruction: microsurgical anastomosis performed to facial or superficial temporal vessels
- Weber–Ferguson incision closed in layers: lip orbicularis mucle with 3-0 Vicryl; subcutaneous 4-0 Vicryl; skin with 5-0 nylon or subcuticular
- Nasolabial fold and alar base aligned with reference marks made pre-incision

**Step 10 — Post-operative care**
- Nasal airway monitoring; nurse head-up 30–45°
- Nasogastric tube feeding until oral intake re-established with obturator
- Nasal saline irrigation from day 2 to clear crust and debris
- Obturator inspection at day 5–7; wound review at 7–10 days

### Surgical Landmarks

| Structure | Location | Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Infraorbital nerve (V2) | Exits infraorbital foramen 5–7 mm below infraorbital rim in midpupillary line | Divides during cheek flap elevation; sensory loss to cheek, upper lip, upper gingiva post-operatively |
| Anterior ethmoidal artery | Crosses medial orbit to enter the roof of the ethmoid complex (10–14 mm posterior to anterior lacrimal crest) | Identified and controlled during periorbital elevation; controls epistaxis from anterior ethmoid territory |
| Posterior ethmoidal artery | 12–17 mm posterior to anterior ethmoidal artery at orbital wall | Cauterise or ligate at orbital wall; closer to optic canal (6 mm anterior) |
| Internal maxillary artery | Runs through pterygomaxillary fossa; major branches: descending palatine, infraorbital, posterior superior alveolar | At risk during pterygoid plate separation — most common source of major haemorrhage |
| Descending palatine artery | Exits greater palatine foramen at posterior hard palate | Divide during palatal osteotomy; suture-ligate or diathermy |
| Nasolacrimal duct | Descends within nasolacrimal canal (medial maxilla/lacrimal bone) to inferior meatus | Must be managed (DCR or marsupialisation) to prevent epiphora |

### Complications

#### Intraoperative Complications

| Complication | Cause | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Haemorrhage (major) | Internal maxillary artery or its branches during pterygoid separation | Pack firmly; bipolar; direct ligation; interventional radiology if uncontrolled (embolisation) |
| Orbital penetration | Entry into orbital fat or extraocular muscles during periorbital elevation | Assess integrity; if planned exenteration — proceed; if inadvertent — achieve haemostasis, cover with vascularised tissue |
| Dural tear / CSF leak | Inadvertent cribriform plate or skull base entry during extended resection | Direct repair; pericranial patch; immediate neurosurgical assistance if brain exposed |
| Optic nerve injury | Posterior dissection near orbital apex | Check visual acuity intraoperatively (in awake patient if local anaesthesia adjunct); immediate neurosurgical or ophthalmological review |
| Lacrimal duct injury | During periorbital elevation or anterior maxillary wall osteotomy | DCR at time of surgery or planned in post-operative period |

#### Early Post-operative Complications (within 30 days)

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Wound dehiscence | 5–10% | Debridement, secondary closure; hyperbaric O2 if irradiated field |
| Oro-antral/nasal fistula | Variable | Obturator adjustment; local flap closure; free flap if large |
| Haemorrhage (reactionary) | 2–5% | Return to theatre; selective embolisation |
| Infection/abscess | 5–15% | IV antibiotics; drainage |
| Trismus (early) | From pterygoid fibrosis/oedema | Physiotherapy; Therabite jaw-opening device |
| CSF leak | If dura entered | Lumbar drain; head elevation; return to theatre if persistent |
| Meningitis | If dural breach and contamination | IV antibiotics; neurosurgical review |
| Epiphora (acute) | Nasolacrimal duct disruption | DCR if not already performed |

#### Late Complications

| Complication | Cause | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Epiphora (persistent) | Nasolacrimal duct obstruction/transection | DCR (dacryocystorhinostomy); endoscopic or external |
| Enophthalmos | Orbital floor removal without adequate reconstruction; fat atrophy | Secondary titanium mesh orbital floor reconstruction; fat graft |
| Diplopia | Inferior rectus adhesions; orbital volume change; inferior oblique injury | Prism correction; surgical orbital wall reconstruction; strabismus surgery |
| Trismus (late) | Pterygoid muscle fibrosis; scar contracture | Physiotherapy; Therabite; coronoidectomy (if coronoid involved) |
| Velopharyngeal incompetence | Loss of palatal integrity | Obturator; speech therapy; soft palate prosthetic extension |
| Oro-antral/nasal fistula (late) | Wound breakdown or tumour recurrence | Ensure no recurrence; soft tissue flap closure if RT not in field; obturator modification |
| Obturator failure/instability | Loss of alveolar support; RT changes in bone | Zygomatic implants; osseointegrated implants in fibula (if free flap); adhesive retention |
| Dental problems | Loss of dentition in resection field; RT osteoradionecrosis | Pre-radiotherapy dental optimisation; fluoride trays; osseointegrated implants post-RT after 1 year |
| Nasal regurgitation | Palatal seal failure | Obturator adjustment; velopharyngeal port extension |
| Radiation osteoradionecrosis | RT to bone; ischaemia | See dedicated ORN file; hyperbaric oxygen; debridement; sequestrectomy |

### Nasolacrimal Duct Management

The nasolacrimal duct is at risk in any maxillectomy involving the medial maxillary wall or lacrimal bone. Options:

1. **Preservation** (if tumour does not involve the medial wall and lacrimal system): dissect the duct free within the nasolacrimal canal; preserve distal end at inferior meatus
2. **DCR at the time of surgery** (preferred when duct must be divided): marsupialise the lacrimal sac into the nasal mucosa; place a silicone stent (Crawford or monocanalicular); remove at 6 weeks
3. **Delayed DCR** (if division was unintentional and DCR not performed): symptoms develop within 2–4 weeks (epiphora); endoscopic DCR performed at 8–12 weeks post-healing

### Orbital Complications: Management in Detail

**Enophthalmos** (sunken eye due to orbital volume increase or fat atrophy):
- Prevention: titanium mesh reconstruction of orbital floor at time of surgery
- Treatment: secondary orbital floor reconstruction with titanium mesh or porous polyethylene (Medpor); autologous fat graft as supplement

**Diplopia** (double vision due to tethering or palsy of extraocular muscles):
- Most commonly inferior rectus adhesion to orbital floor mesh or scar
- Management: prismatic correction (temporary); surgical release of inferior rectus adhesion with orbital exploration; strabismus surgery

**Epiphora** (watering eye):
- Prevention: DCR at time of surgery
- Treatment: endoscopic DCR (preferred, scar-free) or external DCR if endoscopic access limited; silicone stent for 3–6 months

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Total maxillectomy is performed via the Weber–Ferguson incision, which provides access to the full maxilla, orbit, and infratemporal fossa through a lip-splitting incision with a subciliary extension. The ten key steps are: incision and cheek flap elevation, periorbital dissection, palatal osteotomy, anterior and lateral wall osteotomies, pterygoid plate separation, specimen delivery, haemostasis, margin assessment, obturator placement, and closure. The most hazardous intraoperative step is pterygoid plate separation due to proximity of the internal maxillary artery. Early complications include haemorrhage and CSF leak; late complications include epiphora (nasolacrimal duct injury — managed with DCR), enophthalmos (orbital floor loss — managed with titanium mesh), diplopia (inferior rectus), and velopharyngeal incompetence (palatal loss — managed with obturator). Pre-operative DCR or planned DCR at the time of surgery prevents the most common late functional morbidity (epiphora) after total maxillectomy.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **The Weber–Ferguson incision has three components**: lip split, lateral nasal, and subciliary extension — the subciliary component is added for orbital floor access in total maxillectomy but omitted in infrastructure resections.
> 2. **Pterygoid plate separation is the most haemorrhagic step** — the internal maxillary artery lies in the pterygomaxillary fossa immediately posterior; having bipolar, packs, and ties ready before this step is essential.
> 3. **The infraorbital nerve (V2) is divided in total maxillectomy** — patients must be counselled about permanent anaesthesia of the cheek, upper lip, and upper gingiva; inadvertent division without counselling is a medico-legal issue.
> 4. **Perform DCR at the time of maxillectomy** whenever the nasolacrimal duct must be divided — delayed DCR is technically more difficult in a scarred/irradiated field and epiphora significantly impairs quality of life.
> 5. **Titanium mesh orbital floor reconstruction** at time of surgery prevents enophthalmos and diplopia; delay in reconstruction is a common cause of these avoidable late morbidities.
> 6. **The surgical obturator must be fabricated pre-operatively** from dental impressions — an obturator placed immediately at surgery reduces aspiration, supports the cheek flap, enables early speech rehabilitation, and protects the wound.
> 7. **Midfacial degloving** (sublabial + transfixion incision) avoids all external scarring and provides bilateral sinonasal access — preferable for bilateral or midline tumours where aesthetic outcome is a priority.
> 8. **Trismus** after total maxillectomy results from pterygoid muscle fibrosis and scar contracture — active physiotherapy with a Therabite device from 4 weeks post-operatively reduces the risk of permanent restriction.
> 9. **CSF leak requires early recognition and management** — intraoperative: direct dural repair; post-operative: lumbar drain and head-up nursing; persistent leak (> 5 days): return to theatre.
> 10. **Pre-radiotherapy dental clearance** of non-restorable teeth in the radiation field reduces the risk of post-radiotherapy ORN — a joint maxillofacial/dental assessment at MDT is mandatory before adjuvant RT is planned.
