> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Andrews, Fisch, Valavanis, Aeppli, and Makek published the landmark surgical series of extensive nasopharyngeal angiofibromas managed through the infratemporal fossa approach in 1989 in the Laryngoscope, analysing 15 patients with class III and IV disease. This series established the infratemporal fossa approach as the gold standard for advanced-stage JNA and introduced the most widely used staging classification (Andrews–Fisch staging), which stratified tumours by extent of intracranial and infratemporal fossa involvement. The study demonstrated complete cure in 14 of 15 patients with one recurrence, establishing that technically demanding surgery could achieve excellent results even in advanced disease.
>
> Roger et al. published the first large series establishing exclusively endoscopic resection as a viable first-choice option for small to moderate JNA in 2002, in Archives of Otolaryngology-Head & Neck Surgery, with a retrospective study of 20 patients across six French centres. Mean follow-up was 22 months. The study concluded that endoscopic management was effective, associated with minimal bleeding, shorter operative duration, and acceptable recurrence rates, and should be considered the first-choice approach for Stage I and II tumours.
>
> Boghani et al. published a systematic review and meta-analysis in the Laryngoscope (2013) aggregating data from 1,047 cases across 28 studies, comparing outcomes after exclusively endoscopic, endoscopic-assisted, and open resection. Endoscopic approaches were associated with significantly lower blood loss and shorter hospital stay; recurrence rates were comparable between endoscopic (6.3%) and open approaches (10.6%), establishing that endoscopic surgery was non-inferior for appropriately staged tumours.

## Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma (JNA)

### Definition and General Features

Juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma (JNA) is a **benign but locally aggressive fibrovascular tumour** arising from the **superior margin of the sphenopalatine foramen** (at the junction of the pterygoid process of the sphenoid and the horizontal plate of the palatine bone). Despite histological benignity, it exhibits aggressive local infiltration of surrounding structures and has a high propensity for recurrence after incomplete resection.

Key defining features:
- Occurs almost exclusively in **adolescent males** (peak 14–25 years)
- No malignant transformation documented
- Highly vascular — profuse epistaxis is the hallmark presenting symptom
- Indian subcontinent has a higher incidence than Western populations

---

### Aetiopathogenesis

#### Anatomical Origin
The tumour arises at the **posterolateral nasal wall at the sphenopalatine foramen**, precisely at the junction of the pterygoid process base, horizontal plate of the palatine bone, and the body of the sphenoid. This is the anatomical origin from which all growth patterns can be predicted.

#### Hormonal Dependency (Androgen-Driven Growth)
- Tumour cells express **androgen receptors (AR)** — immunostaining AR-positive is a characteristic diagnostic marker
- Testosterone stimulates tumour proliferation; tumours regress or stabilise after age 25 in some patients (corresponding to declining androgen stimulation)
- Historically this led to use of anti-androgen therapy (flutamide, oestrogen); current evidence does not support routine hormonal therapy due to limited efficacy

#### Molecular Pathogenesis
- **WNT/β-catenin pathway activation**: mutations in **CTNNB1** (encoding β-catenin) are found in the majority of JNA specimens; β-catenin nuclear accumulation is a consistent finding on IHC
- **PDGFR-β (platelet-derived growth factor receptor β)** overexpression drives angiogenesis and stromal proliferation; this is the basis for imatinib (PDGFR-β inhibitor) trials in recurrent/unresectable cases
- **VEGF overexpression**: promotes the characteristic high-density vascular network; target for bevacizumab in recurrent disease

#### Vascular Architecture
- Thin-walled vascular channels **lacking smooth muscle** in their walls — cannot undergo vasoconstriction, explaining the catastrophic haemorrhage characteristic of JNA
- Blood supply: predominantly from branches of the **internal maxillary artery** (IMAX) — specifically the **sphenopalatine artery** and accessory branches; advanced tumours acquire supply from the ascending pharyngeal artery (branch of external carotid) and occasionally from the internal carotid artery (ICA) branches when there is intracranial extension
- **Holman-Miller sign** on imaging: anterior bowing of the posterior antral wall (anterolateral wall of the maxilla) — pathognomonic for JNA on CT

#### Gross Pathology
- Firm, rubbery, unencapsulated
- Tan-red to purple surface with irregular lobulations
- Sessile attachment at sphenopalatine foramen
- No plane of dissection from the skull base at the point of origin

#### Histopathology
- Fibrous stroma of varying cellularity (stellate and spindle-shaped fibroblasts)
- Thin-walled vascular channels of irregular shapes (staghorn, stellate)
- Mast cells present in stroma
- IHC: AR+, CD34+ (endothelium), SMA+ (stromal cells), beta-catenin nuclear+
- No mitoses, no atypia — confirms benign nature

### Staging Systems

#### Andrews–Fisch Staging (Most Widely Used)

| Stage | Extent |
|---|---|
| Stage I | Limited to nasopharynx and nasal cavity; no bone destruction |
| Stage II | Extension to pterygopalatine fossa, maxillary sinus, ethmoid sinus, or sphenoid sinus with or without bone destruction |
| Stage IIIa | Extension to infratemporal fossa or orbit without intracranial involvement |
| Stage IIIb | Extension to infratemporal fossa or orbit **with** intracranial extradural (parasellar) involvement |
| Stage IVa | Intracranial intradural involvement without cavernous sinus, pituitary, or optic chiasm involvement |
| Stage IVb | Intracranial intradural involvement with cavernous sinus, pituitary fossa, or optic chiasm involvement |

Other commonly referenced staging systems:
- **Fisch classification** (1983): four stages based on origin and spread to pterygopalatine fossa, orbit, and intracranial
- **Radkowski classification** (1996): three stages (IA, IB, IIA, IIB, IIC, IIIA, IIIB) — widely used in endoscopic surgery literature
- **Sessions classification** (1981): three stages

### Clinical Presentation

| Symptom/Sign | Frequency | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Unilateral nasal obstruction | ~100% | Nasopharyngeal mass |
| Epistaxis (recurrent, profuse) | ~90% | Thin-walled vessels without smooth muscle |
| Cheek / facial swelling | ~30% | PTF/ITF extension |
| Proptosis | ~15% | Orbital extension |
| Facial pain / neuralgia | ~10% | V2/V3 compression |
| Cranial nerve palsy | Rare | Intracranial extension |
| Conductive hearing loss | Occasional | Eustachian tube obstruction |

**Warning**: Biopsy should **not** be performed in suspected JNA without pre-operative DSA and embolisation — risk of catastrophic haemorrhage.

---

### Diagnostic Imaging

#### CT (with contrast)
- Bone algorithm: erosion of pterygoid plates, pterygomaxillary fissure widening, posterior antral wall bowing (**Holman-Miller sign** — pathognomonic)
- Mass in nasopharynx/nasal cavity extending through sphenopalatine foramen
- Intense homogeneous enhancement on contrast CT
- Identifies extent of bone erosion and sinus involvement

#### MRI (with gadolinium)
- **T1**: intermediate signal mass with multiple signal voids (flow voids from high-velocity vessels)
- **T2**: heterogeneous — high signal in tumour matrix + low signal voids
- **T1 + gadolinium**: intense enhancement
- **"Salt and pepper" appearance on T1** (classic MRI description): alternating areas of haemorrhagic/tumour signal (pepper) and flow voids (salt) — most prominent in larger tumours
- MRI is superior to CT for defining orbital, cavernous sinus, and intracranial extension
- MRI of the orbit: assess periorbitum for fat invasion

#### Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA)
- Gold standard for defining vascular supply before embolisation
- Identifies feeding vessels: IMAX, ascending pharyngeal, accessory feeders
- ICA contribution identified in Stage IIIb/IV — important for embolisation safety
- DSA is performed immediately before surgery (same session as embolisation) or 24–48 h prior

---

### Management

#### Pre-operative Embolisation (Mandatory in All Surgically Planned Cases)

- **Timing**: 24–48 hours before surgery (ideally same or next day); beyond 72 hours, recanalisation of embolised vessels significantly reduces haemostatic effect
- **Agents**: Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) particles (150–500 μm) or gelatin microspheres; do **not** use coils as primary embolisation agent (recanalisation)
- **Vessels targeted**: IMAX branches (sphenopalatine artery), ascending pharyngeal artery, and accessory branches from ECA; ICA branches are NOT embolised (risk of stroke/blindness)
- **Benefit**: Reduces intraoperative blood loss by 50–80%; reduces operative time; improves visualisation
- **Complication of embolisation**: facial nerve palsy, visual loss, stroke (from inadvertent ICA embolisation) — rare with experienced interventional radiology

#### Surgical Resection (Primary Treatment)

##### Endoscopic Approach (Stage I and II)
- Standard of care at most high-volume rhinology/skull base centres for Stage I and II
- Transnasal endoscopic technique:
  1. Wide nasal access with middle turbinate medialization or partial resection
  2. Sphenopalatine artery identified and ligated at the sphenopalatine foramen (prevents re-supply)
  3. Tumour dissected from attachment at the foramen; mucosal flap elevated from the nasopharynx
  4. Angled (45°, 70°) scopes used to visualise pterygopalatine fossa and posterior nasopharynx
  5. Tumour removed piecemeal after vascular control
- Blood loss with embolisation: 200–500 mL
- Recurrence rate (endoscopic, Stage I–II): 6–10% (Boghani 2013)

##### Open Approaches (Stage IIIa, IIIb, IVa)
- **Midfacial degloving / sublabial approach**: excellent visualisation of nasopharynx and PPF without external scar; access to ITF limited
- **Infratemporal fossa approach (Andrews-Fisch)**: standard for Stage IIIa/IIIb with ITF involvement; preauricular incision, zygomatic arch osteotomy, mandibular condyle resection, access to the entire ITF and parasellar region
- **Combined transcranial-extracranial approach**: for Stage IVa — neurosurgical craniotomy for intradural component combined with open extracranial approach for sinonasal/ITF component
- For Stage IVb (cavernous sinus, optic chiasm): surgery is typically limited; radiosurgery or stereotactic RT is preferred to avoid morbidity of ICA injury

##### Endoscopic-Assisted (Hybrid) Approach
- Endoscope visualises the nasal and nasopharyngeal component while surgeon uses open corridor (sublabial or infratemporal) for the lateral and intracranial component
- Increasingly used for Stage IIIa to reduce open dissection while maintaining safe margins

### Management of Recurrent / Residual Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma

#### Definitions
- **Residual tumour**: tumour remaining at the end of the index procedure (incomplete resection); detected on early post-operative MRI (3–6 weeks)
- **Recurrent tumour**: tumour that was completely resected (clean margins) but has re-grown at a subsequent follow-up imaging

#### Recurrence Risk Factors
- Stage IIIb or IV at initial presentation
- Intracranial extension (extradural or intradural)
- Incomplete surgical margins
- Residual at pterygoid base or sphenopalatine foramen (hardest area to clear)
- Failure of embolisation (inadequate devascularisation)

**Recurrence rates**: Endoscopic resection 6–10%; Open resection 10–25%; advanced stage (III–IV) 25–30%

#### Post-operative Surveillance
- **MRI with gadolinium** at 3 months, 6 months, 12 months, then annually × 5 years
- Small residual tumour (<25% of original volume) at 3 months may **spontaneously regress** (particularly in patients aged 22–25, as androgen drive declines) — observation with repeat MRI at 6 months is appropriate
- Larger residual or growing residual at 6 months → active management

#### Management Options for Recurrent/Residual JNA

| Situation | Preferred Management |
|---|---|
| Small residual (Stage I–II), stable/regressing | Observation with serial MRI every 6 months |
| Small residual (Stage I–II), growing | Repeat endoscopic resection ± repeat embolisation |
| Stage IIIa recurrence | Repeat surgery (open ± endoscopic) after repeat DSA + embolisation |
| Stage IIIb recurrence (extradural intracranial) | Combined intracranial-extracranial re-resection; consider stereotactic RT if surgery high-risk |
| Stage IVa/IVb (cavernous sinus, ICA encasement) | Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife) — cavernous sinus residual not amenable to safe surgical resection |
| Anti-angiogenic / targeted (all stages) | Bevacizumab (anti-VEGF), imatinib (PDGFR-β inhibitor) — used for recurrent/unresectable; limited prospective evidence |

#### Radiotherapy for JNA
- **Indications**: intracranial Stage IV (unresectable), recurrent disease not amenable to re-operation, cavernous sinus involvement
- **Dose**: 30–45 Gy in conventional fractionation; stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife, CyberKnife) for cavernous sinus residual/recurrence
- **Concerns**: radiation-induced malignancy (sarcoma, carcinoma) — risk approximately 1.7% at 10–20 years; a significant concern in young patients (age 15–25 at treatment)
- Growth plate damage and endocrine dysfunction: relevant in adolescents
- RT is therefore a **treatment of last resort** in young patients; reserved for unresectable intracranial disease

#### Anti-Angiogenic / Targeted Agents
- **Bevacizumab** (anti-VEGF monoclonal antibody): inhibits angiogenesis in VEGF-driven JNA; used in recurrent/unresectable cases; case series show partial response and disease stabilisation; not standard first-line
- **Imatinib** (PDGFR-β inhibitor): targets PDGFR-β overexpression in JNA stroma; used compassionately in unresectable/multiply recurrent cases
- **Hormonal therapy** (flutamide, oestrogen): historical; limited efficacy; not recommended

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma is a benign but locally aggressive fibrovascular tumour arising at the sphenopalatine foramen in adolescent males, driven by androgen receptor signalling and aberrant WNT/β-catenin and PDGFR-β pathways; the vascular channels lack smooth muscle and cannot vasconstrict, causing profuse haemorrhage on biopsy or manipulation. The Andrews–Fisch staging system (Stages I–IV) guides the surgical approach: Stage I–II are managed by endoscopic resection with pre-operative embolisation, while Stage IIIa–b requires open approaches including the infratemporal fossa approach, and Stage IV (intracranial) may require combined intracranial-extracranial resection or stereotactic radiosurgery. For recurrent disease, small residuals detected at 3 months may be observed as they sometimes spontaneously regress; growing or large recurrences require re-staging with MRI and DSA followed by repeat surgical resection or, for unresectable intracranial recurrence, stereotactic radiosurgery. Radiation therapy carries a 1.7% lifetime risk of radiation-induced sarcoma in the young patient population and is therefore reserved for unresectable intracranial disease only.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. JNA arises at the sphenopalatine foramen at the junction of the pterygoid process of sphenoid and horizontal plate of the palatine bone — this precise origin predicts all patterns of extension.
> 2. The "salt and pepper" sign on T1 MRI — alternating haemorrhagic tumour signal and flow voids — is the classical MRI appearance of JNA.
> 3. The Holman-Miller sign (anterior bowing of the posterior antral wall on CT) is pathognomonic for JNA; it results from tumour expanding through the pterygopalatine fossa and pushing the posterior maxillary wall forward.
> 4. Biopsy without prior embolisation and surgical preparation is contraindicated — thin-walled vessels without smooth muscle cannot vasoconstrict and will produce catastrophic haemorrhage.
> 5. Pre-operative embolisation must be performed 24–48 hours before surgery; beyond 72 hours, recanalisation significantly reduces its haemostatic benefit.
> 6. Androgen receptor positivity (AR+) and nuclear β-catenin on IHC are consistent findings; AR positivity explains the exclusive occurrence in adolescent males and hormonal regression after age 25.
> 7. Small residual tumour in a patient aged 22–25 may spontaneously regress as androgen levels decline — observation is appropriate for small, stable residuals before committing to re-operation.
> 8. Recurrence is most strongly associated with Stage IIIb/IV disease, intracranial extension, and pterygoid base involvement — the hardest area to achieve clear margins.
> 9. Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife) is the treatment of choice for cavernous sinus residual/recurrence — surgical access to the cavernous sinus risks ICA injury and cranial nerve damage.
> 10. Radiation therapy should be a last resort in JNA given the 1.7% lifetime risk of radiation-induced malignancy and growth effects in this young male population.
