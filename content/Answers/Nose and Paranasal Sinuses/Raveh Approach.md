>
> Raveh and colleagues at the University of Bern first reported the subcranial approach in 1993, describing surgical outcomes in 78 patients with fronto-orbital and anteroposterior skull base tumours. The technique was developed originally for skull base trauma and craniofacial anomalies, and was subsequently adapted for oncological resection. The defining principle — a bifrontal bone flap incorporating the orbital bar and nasal skeleton to expose the anterior cranial fossa floor from below — fundamentally altered the requirement for brain retraction in anterior skull base surgery.
>
> Raveh et al. subsequently published long-term results (1995) of the extended anterior subcranial approach in 77 patients across a range of histologies, demonstrating that broad inferior access to the anterior skull base could be achieved while avoiding extensive frontal lobe manipulation and external facial incisions, with consequent reductions in intensive care unit stay and complications compared to traditional combined transcranial-transfacial craniofacial resection.
>
> Moore, Ross, and Marentette (1999) published a comparative analysis of the subcranial approach against traditional transcranial techniques, defining the surgical corridors achievable with each technique and establishing formal criteria for patient selection. Their work consolidated the approach as a mainstream option at high-volume skull base centres.

## Raveh's Approach (Subcranial / Transbasal Approach)

### Background and Eponym

The **subcranial approach** — also called **Raveh's approach** or the **transbasal approach** — was described by Professor Tibor Raveh and colleagues at the University of Bern, Switzerland, in the late 1970s (initially for trauma) and formally reported for tumour surgery in 1993. It is an alternative to the classic combined transcranial-transfacial craniofacial resection (CFR) for lesions of the anterior skull base.

The key innovation is a **bifrontal bone flap** that incorporates the orbital bar and naso-orbital complex. This creates direct inferior access to the anterior cranial fossa floor, olfactory groove, cribriform plate, and planum sphenoidale **without entering the cranial cavity through a separate formal craniotomy** — and importantly, without the need for extensive frontal lobe retraction.

---

### Anatomical Access Provided

The subcranial bone flap, when removed, exposes:

| Structure | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|
| Cribriform plate | Olfactory neuroblastoma, meningioma origin |
| Olfactory groove | Bilateral olfactory tract involvement |
| Planum sphenoidale | Anterior extension of sphenoid lesions |
| Posterior table of frontal sinus | Anterior limit of resection |
| Anterior fossa dura | Dural involvement, repair site |
| Orbital roofs (bilateral) | Orbital invasion assessment |
| Nasal skeleton (nasion to nasal tip) | Nasal reconstruction planning |

---

### Operative Technique

**Step 1: Bicoronal incision and subperiosteal dissection**
- Standard bicoronal (coronal) scalp incision from ear to ear, 3–4 cm posterior to the hairline
- Galeal flap elevated anteriorly in the subperiosteal plane
- Pericranial flap preserved as a vascularised pedicle for subsequent dural reconstruction
- Supraorbital neurovascular bundles protected (may require osteotomy of supraorbital notch if enclosed in a canal)

**Step 2: Bifrontal craniotomy with orbital bar osteotomy — the "subcranial" bone unit**
- Burr holes placed bilaterally at the temporal lines
- Bifrontal craniotomy cut extends across both frontal bones, just above the orbit
- The **orbital bar** (supraorbital rim with roof of orbit) is included in the same osteotomy by extending the cut **through the roof of the orbit bilaterally** (horizontal osteotomy at the orbital roof)
- A transnasal osteotomy at the nasion and a second cut at the base of the nasal bones complete the block
- The entire unit — frontal bone + bilateral orbital roofs + nasal bones — is removed **en bloc** as the "subcranial flap"

**Step 3: Anterior skull base exposure from below**
- With the bone flap removed, the anterior cranial fossa floor (cribriform plate, planum sphenoidale, olfactory groove) is directly visualised from a low, **infra-cerebral** trajectory
- Frontal lobes are gently elevated by gravity with minimal or no retractor use (the approach generates natural pneumatic decompression)
- No brain-splitting incision required; the intradural dissection is confined to the floor

**Step 4: Tumour resection**
- The crista galli is removed to allow contiguous resection
- Olfactory bulbs and tracts are divided as required (anosmia is invariable and patients are counselled pre-operatively)
- En bloc or piecemeal resection of tumour with dural cuff
- Intraoperative frozen section of bone and dural margins

**Step 5: Dural repair**
- Primary water-tight dural closure where possible
- Pericranial flap (harvested at Step 1) rotated inferiorly to reconstruct the anterior cranial fossa floor — the most reliable vascularised barrier
- Fascia lata graft as adjunct if pericranial insufficient
- Fibrin glue sealant; lumbar drain placed if high-flow CSF leak risk

**Step 6: Bone flap replacement and rigid fixation**
- The subcranial bone flap is replaced and secured with **titanium miniplates and screws** at multiple osteotomy sites
- Rigid fixation restores orbital roof continuity, prevents enophthalmos, and provides excellent cosmesis
- Absorbable plates used in children (growth considerations)

### Modifications of the Subcranial Approach

#### Extended Subcranial Approach
Adds a **Le Fort III-type midfacial osteotomy** to the standard subcranial flap. The midface (including the zygoma, orbital floors, and palate) is mobilised and swung inferiorly or anteriorly (facial translocation), providing access to the nasopharynx, clivus, and infratemporal fossa in addition to the anterior skull base. Indicated for tumours with extensive inferior extension.

#### Subcranial–Endoscopic Hybrid
The subcranial approach provides intracranial access and allows dural work under direct vision, while a simultaneous endoscopic approach addresses the sinonasal component (ethmoids, sphenoid, maxillary sinus) through the nares. This hybrid reduces the need for facial osteotomies and is the current approach at several high-volume centres for esthesioneuroblastoma and sinonasal carcinoma with cribriform involvement.

---

### Indications

| Tumour / Condition | Comment |
|---|---|
| Esthesioneuroblastoma (Kadish B/C) | Classic indication; combined olfactory groove and sinonasal resection |
| Sinonasal undifferentiated carcinoma (SNUC) | With cribriform or anterior fossa involvement |
| Sinonasal squamous cell carcinoma (T4b, skull base) | Selected resectable cases |
| Anterior skull base meningioma | Hyperostotic or recurrent |
| Fibrous dysplasia (skull base) | Decompressive craniectomy + orbital decompression |
| Anterior encephalocele / CSF fistula repair | Complex fistulas not amenable to endoscopic repair |
| SNEC / NUT carcinoma | If anterior skull base involved and resectable |

### Advantages Over Traditional Open CFR

| Feature | Subcranial Approach | Traditional CFR (Transcranial + Transfacial) |
|---|---|---|
| Brain retraction | Minimal — gravity-dependent | Substantial frontal lobe retraction |
| Frontal lobe oedema | Reduced | Higher risk |
| Facial incisions | None required (in standard form) | Weber-Ferguson or mid-facial degloving required |
| Orbital roof | Preserved — replated | Orbital exenteration or osteotomy separate |
| Operative team | Single-team procedure possible | Traditionally two-team (neurosurgery + HN surgery) |
| Cosmesis | Excellent — coronal scar concealed | Facial scar visible |
| Exposure of cribriform plate | Superior inferior access | Equivalent from above |
| Blood loss | Moderate–significant | Significant |

---

### Comparison: Subcranial vs. Open CFR vs. Endoscopic CFR (eCFR)

| Parameter | Subcranial (Raveh) | Open CFR | Endoscopic CFR (eCFR) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Access | Bifrontal bone flap, no facial incision | Coronal + Weber-Ferguson | Endonasal, no incision |
| Brain retraction | Minimal | Moderate–significant | None |
| Dural repair | Direct under vision | Direct | Endoscopic multilayer |
| Orbital involvement | Can address directly | Can address directly | Limited to medial orbit |
| Intracranial extension | Well suited | Well suited | Limited (brain not accessible) |
| Reconstruction | Pericranial flap standard | Pericranial flap standard | Endoscopic flaps (nasoseptal, pericranial via periscope) |
| Hospital stay | 7–14 days | 10–21 days | 3–7 days |
| Complication rate | Intermediate | Highest | Lowest (in selected cases) |
| Best indications | T4a anterior skull base; moderate intracranial extension | Extensive T4b; orbital exenteration required | T4a without significant intracranial component; Kadish A/B |

---

### Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| CSF leak / fistula | 5–15% | Revision repair, pericranial patch, lumbar drain |
| Meningitis | 2–5% | Broad-spectrum antibiotics; CSF culture |
| Hardware infection / plate extrusion | 1–3% | Plate removal, re-fixation |
| Bifrontal syndrome (frontal lobe oedema) | Rare | Steroids, ICP monitoring |
| Anosmia | 100% (inevitable) | Pre-operative counselling |
| Enophthalmos | Rare with rigid plate fixation | Orbital floor reconstruction |
| Wound infection / dehiscence | 3–5% | Antibiotics, local wound care |
| Pneumocephalus | Occasional | Positioning, lumbar drain |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The subcranial (Raveh's) approach achieves wide anterior skull base exposure through a bifrontal bone flap incorporating the orbital bar and nasal skeleton, removing the en-bloc "subcranial unit" to expose the cribriform plate and planum sphenoidale from below without requiring a separate craniotomy that enters the brain. Its defining advantage over traditional craniofacial resection is minimal frontal lobe retraction, reduced cerebral oedema, and avoidance of external facial incisions, with excellent cosmetic results through coronal incision alone. The procedure invariably causes anosmia and carries risks of CSF fistula and hardware complications, but its operative mortality and major morbidity are lower than classical CFR at experienced centres. Indications include esthesioneuroblastoma, SNUC, and anterior skull base meningiomas with cribriform or planum involvement; it is complemented by a hybrid endoscopic component for sinonasal tumour clearance. Rigid titanium plate fixation of the replated bone flap prevents enophthalmos and restores facial contour.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The subcranial approach removes a composite bone flap (frontal bone + orbital roofs + nasal bones) as a single "subcranial unit" — this is its defining characteristic, distinguishing it from a standard bifrontal craniotomy.
> 2. Anosmia is universal and irreversible after the subcranial approach; pre-operative counselling and olfactory testing are mandatory.
> 3. The pericranial flap must be harvested at the start (Step 1) before the coronal incision traverses it — this is the vascularised workhorse for anterior skull base dural repair.
> 4. The key oncological advantage is superior exposure of the cribriform plate and olfactory groove from an inferior trajectory, with no requirement for significant frontal lobe retraction.
> 5. Rigid titanium fixation of the bone flap restores intact orbital roof continuity, preventing the enophthalmos that can complicate older techniques requiring separate orbital roof management.
> 6. In the extended subcranial approach, a Le Fort III-type osteotomy is added to the standard flap, providing access to the nasopharynx and clivus for tumours with inferior extension.
> 7. The subcranial-endoscopic hybrid is increasingly preferred: the subcranial component manages intracranial/dural disease under direct vision while simultaneous endoscopy clears the sinonasal component without facial incisions.
> 8. Meningitis and CSF fistula are the most important post-operative complications; lumbar drainage is used prophylactically in high-risk cases.
> 9. Compared to eCFR: subcranial approach is preferred when there is significant intracranial extension, dural involvement, or when the surgical corridor to the cribriform cannot be adequately addressed endoscopically.
> 10. The approach was originally developed for craniofacial trauma before being adapted for oncological resection — an important historical detail for oral examinations.
