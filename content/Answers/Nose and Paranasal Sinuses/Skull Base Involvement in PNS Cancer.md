> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Howard, Lund, and Wei (2006) reported their 25-year experience of craniofacial resection at the Royal National Throat, Nose and Ear Hospital in London — one of the world's largest single-centre series. Of 187 patients with malignant tumours, the 5-year actuarial survival was 59%, falling to 40% at 10 years. The series demonstrated that survival was significantly influenced by histology (adenocarcinoma best, SNUC worst) and margin status. Dural invasion was manageable surgically with an adequate dural margin, but brain parenchymal invasion conferred near-zero 5-year survival.
>
> Ganly et al. (2005), reporting the International Collaborative Study of 334 patients from 17 international centres, confirmed that craniofacial resection for anterior skull base malignancies achieves an overall 5-year disease-specific survival of 54.2%. Key adverse prognostic factors were: positive surgical margins (OR 2.96), intracranial extension (OR 2.11), and orbital involvement (OR 1.77). Perioperative mortality was 4.7%; major complications 36.3%. This series established CFR as a standard procedure with acceptable morbidity in experienced multidisciplinary centres. Batra et al. (2010) subsequently demonstrated that endoscopic resection of selected anterior skull base neoplasms achieved comparable outcomes with lower morbidity (no craniotomy complications, shorter hospitalisation), further validating the shift toward endoscopic approaches where dural invasion is limited.

## Skull Base Involvement in PNS Cancer

### Anterior Skull Base Anatomy

The anterior skull base forms the roof of the nasal cavity, ethmoid sinuses, and orbits, and the floor of the anterior cranial fossa. Key anatomical structures:

**Bony components:**
- **Cribriform plate** (lamina cribrosa): a perforated horizontal plate within the ethmoid bone; transmits approximately 20 olfactory nerve fibre bundles (fila olfactoria) on each side; the thinnest and most vulnerable part of the anterior skull base
- **Crista galli**: midline anterior bony projection; origin of the falx cerebri
- **Fovea ethmoidalis**: the roof of the ethmoid labyrinth (lateral to cribriform plate); part of the orbital plate of the frontal bone; slightly higher than cribriform plate — creates a "step" that is anatomically vulnerable during surgery
- **Planum sphenoidale**: the flat anterior surface of the sphenoid body; posterior to the cribriform plate; roof of the posterior ethmoid cells
- **Orbital roof**: formed by orbital plate of frontal bone; roof of orbit and floor of frontal lobe

**Foramina and their contents:**
| Foramen | Contents | Tumour Spread Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Olfactory foramina (cribriform plate) | Fila olfactoria (CN I fibres), anterior ethmoidal artery, dural sleeve | Primary route for ENB/ITAC to reach intracranial space |
| Foramen rotundum (sphenoid) | Maxillary nerve (V2) | ACC perineural spread from maxilla to middle cranial fossa |
| Pterygoid (Vidian) canal | Vidian nerve (greater superficial + deep petrosal nerves) | Pathway from nasopharynx to foramen lacerum |
| Anterior ethmoidal foramen | Anterior ethmoidal artery and nerve | Orbital roof; artery crosses skull base medially |
| Posterior ethmoidal foramen | Posterior ethmoidal artery | 6 mm anterior to optic canal — landmark for optic nerve protection |
| Optic canal | Optic nerve (CN II), ophthalmic artery | T4b disease; exenteration does not improve prognosis at optic apex |

**Neural relationships:**
- **V2 (maxillary nerve)** exits the middle cranial fossa via foramen rotundum into the pterygopalatine fossa, then divides into infraorbital and palatine branches; the primary perineural route for ACC spread from maxillary sinus to skull base
- **Olfactory bulbs and tracts**: lie on the superior surface of the cribriform plate within the anterior cranial fossa; must be resected en bloc with the cribriform plate in any craniofacial resection for ENB or ITAC involving the cribriform plate

### Significance of Skull Base Involvement: AJCC Staging

| Level of Involvement | AJCC T Stage | Resectability |
|---|---|---|
| Cribriform plate, anterior skull base floor | T4a | Potentially resectable with CFR or eCFR |
| Sphenoid sinus (posterior ethmoid skull base) | T4a | May be resectable; technically demanding |
| Dura of anterior cranial fossa | T4a (if limited) | Resectable with dural margin + reconstruction |
| Brain parenchyma invasion | T4b | Relative contraindication to curative surgery |
| Orbital apex | T4b | Unresectable in most series |
| Middle cranial fossa | T4b | Unresectable |
| Nasopharynx / clivus | T4b | Unresectable |

Dural invasion per se is not a contraindication to craniofacial resection if a margin of dura can be achieved; the dural defect is reconstructed with pericranium, fascia lata, or the nasoseptal flap. Brain parenchymal invasion, however, is generally associated with near-zero long-term survival and is considered a contraindication to curative surgical intent in most centres.

### Orbital Resection: Principles and Decision-Making (T3 Disease)

The decision on orbital management is the most critical oncological and functional judgment in T3–T4 PNS cancer. The **orbital periosteum (periorbita)** is the key anatomical oncological barrier.

#### Anatomical Levels of Orbital Invasion

| Level | Description | Decision |
|---|---|---|
| Level 0 | Orbital floor or medial wall bone erosion; periorbita intact | Resect involved bone; preserve periorbita; preserve orbital contents |
| Level 1 | Periorbita involved but not breached (tumour abuts periosteum) | Resect periorbita with the specimen; close orbital floor with titanium mesh; preserve orbital fat and contents |
| Level 2 | Periorbita breached; tumour in orbital fat, but extraocular muscles and optic nerve spared | **Controversial**: exenteration in most centres for SCC; periorbital stripping with conservation in selected cases (adenocarcinoma, ENB) with frozen section control |
| Level 3 | Extraocular muscle invasion, optic nerve invasion, or orbital apex involvement | Orbital exenteration required; T4b if orbital apex |

**The periorbita as oncological barrier** (Medina series; Ganly series):
- When periorbita is intact or involved but not breached, orbital preservation achieves equivalent locoregional control to exenteration in most histologies
- When orbital fat is invaded (periorbita breached), rates of locoregional control are significantly reduced with orbital preservation for SCC; exenteration improves local control but overall survival benefit is modest due to systemic failure
- For ACC and ENB, selected periorbita-breached but fat-spared cases may be managed with periorbital stripping, intensive adjuvant RT, and close surveillance

**Practical decision at surgery:**
1. MRI pre-operatively identifies periosteal breach (low T1 signal replacing normal orbital fat, or mass entering periorbital fat)
2. Intraoperatively: periorbita is exposed; if intact, it is preserved and orbital floor is reconstructed with titanium mesh
3. If periorbita appears involved: resect periorbita with frozen section of the periosteal margin
4. If fat exposed: frozen section of the fat margin; if clear = conservative eye-preserving resection is possible; if tumour at fat margin = exenteration

#### Indications for Orbital Exenteration

| Absolute | Relative |
|---|---|
| Extraocular muscle invasion | Orbital fat involvement by SCC (centre-dependent) |
| Optic nerve involvement | Bilateral orbital involvement |
| Complete eyelid skin invasion | Poor visual function pre-operatively (< 6/60) |
| Orbital apex involvement (T4b) | Patient preference after full counselling |

**Eye-sparing orbital surgery** with adjuvant RT can achieve local control in selected cases — the eye must have useful vision pre-operatively (≥ 6/60) for preservation to be functionally worthwhile.

### Craniofacial Resection (CFR): Open Approach

#### Concept

CFR, introduced by Ketcham (1963), combines a **transfacial component** (lateral rhinotomy or Weber–Ferguson, for sinonasal tumour) with a **transcranial component** (bifrontal craniotomy, for anterior cranial fossa floor and olfactory bulb resection). The specimen — sinonasal tumour + cribriform plate + dural cuff + olfactory bulbs — is removed as a single en bloc piece.

#### Indications for Open CFR

- T4a tumours with cribriform plate or anterior skull base floor involvement
- Broad or extensive dural invasion (> 2 cm) not amenable to endoscopic margin
- Frontal sinus invasion requiring frontal bone osteotomy
- Intracranial extension with orbital roof involvement
- Prior endoscopic skull base surgery failure
- Tumours requiring simultaneous open maxillectomy (Weber–Ferguson) for lateral extent

#### Surgical Steps (Open CFR)

**Neurosurgical component (performed first or simultaneously in two-team approach):**
1. Bifrontal craniotomy: bilateral skin incision (coronal incision or bicoronal, behind the hairline); bur holes; craniotome; frontal bone flap elevated
2. Brain retraction: bilateral frontal lobes retracted posteriorly with malleable retractors or fixed retractor (minimised to reduce contusion)
3. Identification of cribriform plate from above: dura opened bilaterally parasagittally; olfactory bulbs identified; dural circumferential incision around the planned cribriform resection margin
4. Osteotomies from above: oscillating saw or osteotome divides the cribriform plate, fovea ethmoidalis, and skull base from the cranial side — meeting the transfacial cuts from below

**Transfacial component (simultaneous or sequential):**
1. Lateral rhinotomy or Weber–Ferguson incision
2. Ethmoidectomy and clearance of sinonasal tumour from below
3. Meeting the cranial osteotomies at the skull base
4. Specimen delivered in a single continuous bloc through the transfacial window

**Dural closure:**
- If dura invaded: resect involved dura with margin; replace with pericranial flap, fascia lata graft, or synthetic dural substitute
- Watertight dural closure is essential to prevent CSF rhinorrhoea; fibrin sealant applied
- Lumbar drain placed for 3–5 days post-operatively

**Skull base reconstruction:**
- Pericranial flap (from posterior scalp, rotated forward on pericranium overlying frontal bone): covers the skull base defect from above; provides a vascularised layer to prevent meningitis and CSF leak
- Below: nasoseptal flap (if endoscopic component) or local mucosa from nasal cavity floor
- Absorbable packing; no nasal dressings that would create suction on the skull base closure

### Endoscopic Craniofacial Resection (eCFR)

See full technique in the Endoscopic Surgery for PNS Malignancies file. Key points in the context of skull base involvement:

- **Indication**: T4a with cribriform/dural involvement but no broad dural invasion and no brain parenchymal infiltration; ENB Kadish B–C without wide intracranial extension; ITAC with cribriform involvement
- **Oncological equivalence to open CFR**: established in meta-analyses (Devaiah & Andreoli 2009; Batra et al. 2010) for selected cases; lower morbidity (no craniotomy, no brain retraction, no external scar)
- **Limitation**: requires 4-hand bimanual technique with simultaneous neurosurgical access for emergencies; image-guided navigation mandatory; the dural defect must be reconstructable with the nasoseptal flap

**Combined cranioendoscopic approach:**
When intracranial extension exceeds what is safely managed endoscopically (frontal sinus invasion, superior sagittal sinus proximity, extensive bilateral dural invasion), a combined approach is used:
- Neurosurgical craniotomy for intracranial resection
- Simultaneous endoscopic resection of the sinonasal portion
- Two-team two-corridor approach; synchronised communication between surgeons essential

### Dural Management

| Dural Status | Intraoperative Decision | Reconstruction |
|---|---|---|
| Dura intact (tumour abuts but does not breach) | Resect with en bloc specimen leaving dura intact; frozen section of dural surface | Skull base coverage with pericranial flap or nasoseptal flap |
| Dura breached by tumour | Resect dural margin en bloc; achieve clear dural margin on frozen section | Primary repair if small; pericranial/fascia lata patch if larger; nasoseptal flap to line skull base |
| Extensive dural involvement | Requires larger dural resection; must protect olfactory tracts, optic chiasm, anterior cerebral vessels | Fascia lata free graft + pericranium; waterproof sealant; lumbar drain |
| Brain parenchyma infiltration | Relative contraindication to curative surgery | If proceeding for palliation: neurosurgical debulking followed by RT |

**CSF leak prevention and management:**
- Primary prevention: watertight dural closure (primary sutures + fibrin glue); vascularised soft tissue coverage (pericranial or nasoseptal flap); lumbar drain for 3–5 days
- Early post-operative CSF rhinorrhoea: conservative (head elevation, lumbar drain, avoid nose blowing); failure → return to theatre for secondary repair
- Late CSF rhinorrhoea: rare; endoscopic exploration and repair; MRI to exclude recurrence-related fistula

### MDT Approach to Anterior Skull Base Sinonasal Malignancy

| Team Member | Specific Role |
|---|---|
| Head and neck surgeon | Primary resection; sinonasal portion; maxillectomy if needed; neck dissection; flap planning |
| Neurosurgeon | Craniotomy (if open CFR); brain retraction; dural resection and repair; emergency intracranial haemostasis |
| Neuroradiologist | MRI interpretation; cribriform plate, dural, orbital, and PPF involvement; treatment planning |
| Radiation oncologist | IMRT or proton beam; target volume delineation (include perineural routes: V2, olfactory tracts); adjuvant or definitive CRT |
| Medical oncologist | Induction or concurrent chemotherapy for SNUC, high-grade SCC, ENB |
| Neuro-ophthalmologist | Orbital involvement assessment; visual function testing; exenteration recommendation |
| Reconstructive surgeon | Nasoseptal flap; free tissue transfer (pericranial, ALT, rectus) if needed for skull base and midfacial defects |
| Neuropsychology | Pre- and post-operative cognitive assessment for cases with frontal lobe retraction |
| Speech pathology | Pre- and post-operative voice, speech, swallowing |

### Adjuvant Treatment After CFR

- Standard: **adjuvant IMRT 60–66 Gy** to primary site and skull base; include bilateral elective neck for N0 if high-grade or skull base involvement
- **Proton beam therapy**: strongly preferred for skull base tumours (anterior cranial fossa, orbital apex proximity) to minimise late radiation injury to optic nerves, chiasm, pituitary, and brain; reduces neurocognitive late effects in younger patients
- **Concurrent chemotherapy**: for positive/close margins, N+ disease, high-grade histology (SCC, SNUC); cisplatin-based
- Timing: adjuvant RT begins 4–6 weeks post-CFR once wound healed; do not delay beyond 8 weeks

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Anterior skull base involvement by sinonasal malignancy is classified as T4a (cribriform, dura with margin achievable — resectable) or T4b (orbital apex, brain, middle cranial fossa — unresectable). The orbital periosteum is the key oncological barrier: intact or stripped periosteum allows orbital content preservation with titanium mesh floor reconstruction; invasion of orbital fat mandates exenteration in most histologies. Craniofacial resection (open CFR or endoscopic eCFR) is performed by a neurosurgical and head-and-neck combined team, achieving 5-year disease-specific survival of ~54% (International Collaborative Study). Dural involvement is manageable with margined dural resection and pericranial/nasoseptal flap reconstruction; brain parenchymal invasion is a relative contraindication to curative surgery. Adjuvant IMRT or proton beam radiotherapy (60–66 Gy) is standard post-CFR; proton beam therapy is preferred when orbital and optic structures must be spared.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **T4a vs T4b is the key staging distinction at the skull base** — T4a (anterior orbital contents, skin, pterygoid, cribriform, sphenoid/frontal) is resectable with curative intent; T4b (orbital apex, dura + brain, middle cranial fossa, CN beyond V2, nasopharynx) is generally unresectable.
> 2. **The orbital periosteum is the oncological barrier for orbital preservation** — periosteum intact = preserve the eye; periosteum breached with fat invasion = exenteration required (particularly for SCC); periosteum breached without fat invasion = case-by-case with frozen section.
> 3. **Dural involvement is not a contraindication to curative CFR** — the dura can be resected with a margin and reconstructed with pericranium, fascia lata, or nasoseptal flap; it is brain parenchymal invasion that is the true limit of resectability.
> 4. **The nasoseptal flap must be elevated at the start of the operation** before the nasal septum is disrupted — it is the primary reconstruction for skull base dural defects in eCFR and must be protected throughout resection.
> 5. **Lumbar drain for 3–5 days post-CFR** is standard after any dural resection or reconstruction; it reduces the CSF pressure on the repair and prevents early CSF rhinorrhoea.
> 6. **Proton beam therapy is the preferred modality at the skull base** — the Bragg peak allows high dose to the tumour bed at cribriform/anterior skull base while sparing the optic chiasm, hypothalamus, pituitary, and brainstem from exit dose.
> 7. **eCFR is oncologically equivalent to open CFR** for selected cases without brain invasion or broad dural involvement — the key advantages are no craniotomy, no brain retraction, no bifrontal scar, and shorter hospitalisation.
> 8. **Perineural spread along V2** in adenoid cystic carcinoma is not an indication for exenteration but does require RT target volume extension to the Gasserian ganglion via foramen rotundum.
> 9. **Olfactory neuroblastoma (ENB)** has a unique propensity for intracranial extension through the olfactory foramina — cribriform plate resection en bloc with olfactory bulbs is the oncological principle of CFR for ENB regardless of preoperative imaging.
> 10. **The bifrontal craniotomy approach** requires the frontal brain to be retracted (a source of post-operative cognitive decline) — endoscopic approaches avoiding craniotomy specifically reduce this risk, which is of particular importance in young patients and those with professional cognitive demands.
