> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Sinonasal malignancies are rare and heterogeneous; no randomised controlled trials guide staging or treatment. The foundational staging framework is the AJCC 8th edition (2017), which separately stages maxillary sinus and ethmoid sinus tumours and removes the "nasal cavity" as a distinct site (absorbed into ethmoid staging). The T4 distinction — T4a (moderately advanced, resectable) versus T4b (very advanced, unresectable) — guides the critical surgical decision of resectability. A critical distinction in the AJCC system is that orbital apex, dura, brain, middle cranial fossa, and nasopharyngeal invasion define T4b (considered unresectable), while involvement of the anterior orbital contents, skin, pterygoid plates, and cribriform plate defines T4a (potentially resectable with multimodality therapy). This division is based on surgical series demonstrating that T4b involvement confers near-zero long-term surgical cure.
>
> The epidemiological relationship between wood dust exposure (hardwoods — oak, beech, mahogany) and sinonasal adenocarcinoma was established by Acheson et al. (1968) based on Oxfordshire furniture workers, and was confirmed in IARC Group 1 (definite human carcinogen) reclassification. The attributable risk is strongest for intestinal-type adenocarcinoma (ITAC), where the cumulative lifetime risk in heavily exposed furniture workers approaches 1000-fold baseline. Occupational history is therefore essential in the diagnostic evaluation of all patients with sinonasal tumours.

## PNS Cancer: Staging, Evaluation, and Management

### Epidemiology and Aetiopathogenesis

Sinonasal malignancies are uncommon, comprising < 1% of all cancers and approximately 3% of head and neck malignancies. The maxillary sinus is the most frequently involved site (~60%), followed by the nasal cavity (~25%) and ethmoid sinuses (~15%). Frontal and sphenoid sinus primaries are rare (< 1%).

#### Established Aetiopathological Associations

| Carcinogen | Tumour Type | IARC Category | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wood dust (hardwoods: oak, beech, mahogany) | Intestinal-type adenocarcinoma (ITAC) | Group 1 | Ethmoid sinus predominant; furniture/cabinet workers, shoe making |
| Nickel refining | Squamous cell carcinoma, adenocarcinoma | Group 1 | Nasal cavity and maxillary sinus |
| Chromium (hexavalent) | Squamous cell carcinoma | Group 1 | Industrial exposure |
| Formaldehyde | Nasopharyngeal carcinoma, sinonasal SCC | Group 1 | Embalmers, histology workers |
| Thorotrast (thorium dioxide, contrast agent 1930s–50s) | Angiosarcoma, SCC | Historical | No longer in use |
| Isopropyl oil (strong acid process) | SCC | Group 1 | Chemical manufacturing |
| Tobacco | SCC (weak association) | Group 1 | Less strongly implicated than in oral cavity/larynx |
| Leather dust | Adenocarcinoma | Group 1 | Shoe industry |

Note: Human papillomavirus (HPV) is not definitively established as a causative factor in sinonasal SCC (unlike oropharyngeal SCC). Inverted papilloma has a known association with HPV types 6, 11 and transformation to SCC.

### Histological Classification (WHO 2022)

| Category | Examples | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Squamous cell carcinoma | Conventional SCC, keratinising, non-keratinising | Most common overall (~50%) |
| Adenocarcinoma | ITAC (intestinal-type), non-intestinal type | Second most common; ethmoid sinus |
| Undifferentiated | Sinonasal undifferentiated carcinoma (SNUC) | Aggressive; rapid growth |
| Neuroectodermal | Olfactory neuroblastoma (ENB), sinonasal NEC | See dedicated ENB file |
| Salivary gland type | Adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC), mucoepidermoid carcinoma | Minor salivary gland origin; palate, nasal floor |
| Mesenchymal | Sarcoma (rhabdomyosarcoma in children, chondrosarcoma) | |
| Lymphoid | NK/T-cell lymphoma (midline destructive), diffuse large B-cell | |
| Melanocytic | Sinonasal mucosal melanoma | |
| Miscellaneous | Inverted papilloma, NUT carcinoma, SMARCB1-deficient carcinoma | |

### Ohngren's Line: Historical Prognostic Division

Ohngren (1933) described an imaginary plane drawn from the **medial canthus of the eye** to the **angle of the mandible**:
- **Superstructure** (above Ohngren's line): tumours in the upper compartment, close to the orbit, skull base, and pterygopalatine fossa. Access is difficult, surgical margins are harder to achieve — **worse prognosis**.
- **Infrastructure** (below Ohngren's line): tumours involving the hard palate, alveolar ridge, and lower sinus. Better surgical access, wider soft tissue margins achievable — **better prognosis**.

Ohngren's line is a conceptual teaching aid, not a formal staging component. Modern AJCC staging based on CT and MRI has largely superseded it, but it remains an important examination concept and an aid to preoperative prognostication in infrastructure versus superstructure tumours.

### AJCC 8th Edition Staging (2017)

#### Maxillary Sinus Staging

| T Stage | Definition |
|---|---|
| T1 | Tumour limited to maxillary sinus mucosa, no bone erosion or destruction |
| T2 | Tumour causing bone erosion or destruction, including extension into the hard palate and/or middle nasal meatus, except extension to posterior wall of maxillary sinus and pterygoid plates |
| T3 | Tumour invades any of: bone of posterior wall of maxillary sinus, subcutaneous tissues, floor or medial wall of orbit, pterygoid fossa, ethmoid sinuses |
| T4a | Tumour invades anterior orbital contents, skin of cheek, pterygoid plates, infratemporal fossa, cribriform plate, sphenoid or frontal sinuses |
| T4b | Tumour invades any of: orbital apex, dura, brain, middle cranial fossa, cranial nerves other than maxillary division of trigeminal nerve (V2), nasopharynx, or clivus |

T4b is generally considered unresectable (palliative intent or definitive CRT); T4a is considered potentially resectable with curative intent using multimodality therapy.

#### Ethmoid Sinus Staging

| T Stage | Definition |
|---|---|
| T1 | Tumour confined to the ethmoid sinuses, with or without bone erosion |
| T2 | Tumour extends into the nasal cavity |
| T3 | Tumour extends to the anterior orbit and/or maxillary sinus |
| T4a | Tumour invades any of: anterior orbital contents, skin of nose or cheek, minimal extension to anterior cranial fossa, pterygoid plates, sphenoid or frontal sinuses |
| T4b | As maxillary sinus (orbital apex, dura, brain, middle cranial fossa, CN beyond V2) |

#### Nodal Staging (N) — Sinonasal Malignancies

Uses standard AJCC head and neck nodal staging (N0–N3b). Regional nodal spread is relatively infrequent at presentation (~10–15% for maxillary SCC); however, the risk increases with advanced T stage and high-grade histology. Adenoid cystic carcinoma has a low nodal rate but high perineural and distant spread.

#### Metastasis (M)
- M0: no distant metastasis
- M1: distant metastasis present (lung most common; bone, liver for ACC and ENB)

### Pathways of Spread from the Maxillary Sinus

Understanding the anatomical pathways of spread is essential for staging, surgical planning, and target volume delineation in radiotherapy.

| Direction | Pathway | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Superior | Orbital floor → periorbita → orbital fat → orbital apex | T3 (floor) → T4a (anterior orbit) → T4b (apex); determines orbital preservation vs. exenteration |
| Posterior | Posterior maxillary wall → pterygopalatine fossa (PPF) → foramen rotundum → middle cranial fossa (V2) | T3 (posterior wall) → T4a (pterygoid plates, PTF) → T4b (intracranial via V2); perineural spread in ACC |
| Medial | Medial wall → nasal cavity → septum → contralateral sinus | T2 extension; nasal cavity involvement |
| Inferior | Orbital floor, alveolar ridge → hard palate | T2 hard palate erosion; oroantral fistula |
| Anterior | Anterior maxillary wall → subcutaneous fat → cheek skin | T3 (subcutaneous), T4a (skin) |
| Lateral | Lateral wall → infratemporal fossa (ITF) → masticator space | T4a; trismus (pterygoid plate erosion); lateral spread |
| Lymphatic | Submandibular (Level I), upper jugular (Level II), retropharyngeal | Regional nodal metastasis; typically levels I–II for maxillary SCC |

**Pterygopalatine fossa (PPF) involvement** is a critical staging determinant. The PPF communicates:
- Anteriorly with the maxillary sinus (posterior wall)
- Superiorly via foramen rotundum to the middle cranial fossa
- Inferiorly via the greater palatine foramen to the hard palate
- Posteriorly via the pterygoid canal (vidian canal) to the foramen lacerum

MRI hallmark of PPF involvement: **obliteration of the normal fat signal on T1-weighted MRI** (the PPF normally appears bright on T1 due to fat; loss of this signal indicates tumour infiltration).

### Diagnostic Evaluation

#### History
- Duration, laterality, and character of nasal obstruction
- Epistaxis (frequency, volume)
- Visual symptoms (diplopia, proptosis, visual acuity change)
- Orbital pain, periorbital swelling
- Headache (skull base/intracranial)
- Trismus (pterygoid plate/masticator space invasion)
- Facial swelling/paraesthesia (infraorbital nerve V2)
- **Occupational history** — wood dust, nickel, formaldehyde exposure (mandatory)
- Tobacco and alcohol history

#### Examination
- Anterior rhinoscopy and rigid nasal endoscopy (0° and 30°) — identify tumour site, surface character, vascularity; assess for bilateral involvement
- Orbital examination: proptosis (Hertel exophthalmometry), diplopia, visual acuity, fundoscopy
- Facial sensation (V2 territory — cheek, upper lip, upper gingiva)
- Trismus (inter-incisal distance < 35 mm is significant)
- Palpation: cheek fullness, malar swelling, palatal expansion, cervical lymphadenopathy
- Oral examination: palatal expansion, alveolar erosion, oroantral fistula

#### Investigations

| Investigation | Modality | Key Information |
|---|---|---|
| CT PNS (with contrast) | Primary imaging modality | Bone erosion, cortical breakthrough, extent of sinus involvement, calcification (ENB), orbital floor status |
| MRI sinuses and skull base (with gadolinium) | Mandatory for all malignant cases | Soft tissue extent, perineural invasion (V2, V1), dural involvement, orbital fat invasion, PPF status (T1 fat obliteration), intracranial extension, brain involvement |
| PET-CT | Staging | Regional and distant metastasis detection; N0 neck assessment; baseline for response evaluation |
| CT chest | Staging | Pulmonary metastasis (ENB, ACC, melanoma particularly prone) |
| OPG (Orthopantomogram) | Dental | Alveolar, palatal, and mandibular involvement; pre-operative dental assessment |
| Nasal endoscopy + biopsy | Tissue diagnosis | Image-guided biopsy preferred; avoid blind biopsy of highly vascular lesions (ENB) |
| Ophthalmology review | If any orbital symptom | Formal visual acuity, fields, motility; baseline before orbital surgery or RT |

#### Role of Imaging in Follow-Up and Surveillance

- **Post-treatment MRI at 3 months**: establish new baseline; differentiate post-operative/post-RT changes from residual/recurrent disease
- **Gadolinium-enhanced MRI**: recurrence shows nodular enhancement or restricted diffusion on DWI; post-RT mucosal thickening shows linear, smooth enhancement and does not show restricted diffusion
- **PET-CT at 3 months post-treatment**: primary surveillance tool for complete response assessment and detection of distant failure; negative PET-CT at 12 weeks post-CRT has high negative predictive value
- **Annual MRI for at least 3 years**: standard for locoregional surveillance
- **CT chest yearly**: for ACC (late haematogenous metastases to lung 10–15 years post-treatment)

### Management Principles

#### Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Components

| Specialist | Role |
|---|---|
| Head and neck surgeon | Primary resection, maxillectomy, neck dissection |
| Neurosurgeon | Skull base extension, craniotomy for CFR or eCFR |
| Radiation oncologist | IMRT/proton planning, adjuvant or definitive RT |
| Medical oncologist | Chemotherapy (induction or concurrent) |
| Neuroradiologist | Imaging interpretation, staging, PPF/dural/orbital assessment |
| Ophthalmologist | Orbital involvement, visual outcomes, exenteration decision |
| Maxillofacial prosthodontist | Obturator planning, dental implants |
| Speech and language therapist | Pre- and post-operative assessment |
| Reconstructive surgeon | Free flap planning |

#### Treatment Algorithm by Stage

**Resectable disease (T1–T4a, selected N0–N+):**
- Primary surgery (maxillectomy ± craniofacial resection) → adjuvant RT ± chemotherapy
- Adjuvant RT: 60–66 Gy to primary site; extended fields for perineural invasion (V2 tract to skull base), N+ disease
- Concurrent chemotherapy (cisplatin 40 mg/m² weekly or 100 mg/m² 3-weekly) added for positive margins, nodal disease, or adverse features

**Unresectable disease (T4b, bilateral orbital apex, brainstem):**
- Definitive CRT: platinum-based chemotherapy + IMRT 66–70 Gy
- Induction chemotherapy (TPF or platinum-taxane) considered for response assessment and downstaging
- Palliative intent for poor performance status (PS ≥ 2) or patient refusal of aggressive therapy

**Induction chemotherapy strategy (bulky T4a/b):**
- 2–3 cycles induction (cisplatin + docetaxel ± 5-FU, or etoposide-cisplatin for ENB)
- Restaging with MRI/PET-CT
- If response (> 30% reduction): re-evaluate for surgery → adjuvant RT
- If no response: definitive CRT

**Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma (ACC) — special considerations:**
- Perineural spread is the hallmark: RT must include the entire V2 nerve tract (foramen rotundum to Gasserian ganglion)
- Lung metastases occur late (10–20 years); annual CT chest lifelong
- Proton beam therapy preferred for skull base ACC to reduce long-term cranial nerve toxicity
- Negative margins reduce locoregional recurrence; PNI is not a contraindication to curative surgery

**Sinonasal Undifferentiated Carcinoma (SNUC) — special considerations:**
- Highly aggressive; rapid growth; often presents at T4
- Concurrent CRT is primary treatment; role of surgery is debated; considered for induction responders
- Differentiation from high-grade neuroendocrine carcinoma (large cell NEC, small cell NEC) is critical as these are even more chemosensitive

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Sinonasal malignancies are rare tumours with a strong occupational link (wood dust → ITAC, nickel/chromium → SCC). Ohngren's line divides tumours into superstructure (poor access, worse prognosis) and infrastructure (better access) — a useful conceptual tool superseded by AJCC 8th edition staging. AJCC T4a (resectable advanced disease — orbital contents, pterygoid, cribriform, sphenoid/frontal sinus) and T4b (unresectable — orbital apex, dura, brain, CN beyond V2, nasopharynx) is the key staging distinction. Evaluation requires CT (bone), MRI with gadolinium (soft tissue, perineural, PPF obliteration, dural involvement), and PET-CT (staging). Management is multimodality: surgery plus adjuvant RT (60–66 Gy) for resectable disease; definitive CRT or induction chemotherapy followed by reassessment for T4b or unresectable disease. The pterygopalatine fossa is the key posterior gateway to the skull base, identified on MRI by T1 fat obliteration.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Wood dust (hardwoods) is an IARC Group 1 carcinogen for sinonasal adenocarcinoma** — an occupational history asking specifically about furniture making, carpentry, and shoe manufacturing is mandatory in any sinonasal tumour workup.
> 2. **Ohngren's line is a conceptual teaching aid**, not a formal staging system — but infrastructure vs. superstructure remains a clinically relevant concept for explaining prognosis to patients and examiners.
> 3. **The T4a/T4b distinction is the key resectability boundary in AJCC staging** — T4a (anterior orbit, skin, pterygoid, cribriform, sphenoid/frontal) is resectable with curative intent; T4b (orbital apex, dura, brain, V2-beyond, nasopharynx) is generally considered unresectable.
> 4. **Pterygopalatine fossa fat obliteration on T1 MRI** is the imaging hallmark of PPF tumour involvement — this indicates posterior extension toward skull base foramina and mandates expanded surgical or RT target volumes.
> 5. **MRI is superior to CT for soft tissue, dural, and perineural invasion** — CT is superior for cortical bone erosion; the complementary pair (CT + MRI) is mandatory in all sinonasal malignancies.
> 6. **Adenoid cystic carcinoma requires irradiation of the entire V2 nerve tract** to the Gasserian ganglion regardless of imaging findings, given the propensity for microscopic perineural spread well beyond radiological evidence.
> 7. **SNUC is among the most aggressive sinonasal malignancies** — it may be mistaken for SCC histologically; immunohistochemistry (positive cytokeratin, negative S100, NSE, CD45) is required; concurrent CRT is the treatment backbone.
> 8. **PET-CT at 12 weeks post-treatment** is the standard for complete response assessment; a negative PET has high NPV for residual disease and avoids unnecessary neck dissection.
> 9. **Regional nodal metastasis at presentation is uncommon (10–15%) in maxillary SCC** — but increases with T stage; elective neck treatment is not uniformly applied and remains centre-dependent.
> 10. **Proton beam therapy** is increasingly used for skull base and orbital-adjacent sinonasal malignancies to minimise late radiation toxicity (optic neuropathy, retinopathy, neurocognitive effects) while delivering adequate dose to the tumour.
