> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Musy, Reibel, and Levine (2002) published the first systematic multi-institutional review of multimodality treatment for SNUC, reporting improved survival with combined surgery, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy over single-modality treatment and establishing the principle that SNUC requires aggressive combined-modality therapy rather than surgery alone. Rischin et al. (2004) published a retrospective series from the Peter MacCallum Cancer Centre reporting promising 2-year overall survival of 64% with induction cisplatin–5-FU followed by concurrent chemoradiation in nine patients, demonstrating that sequencing induction chemotherapy before definitive local therapy was feasible and produced meaningful responses even in T4 disease. Amit et al. (2019) reported the landmark MD Anderson Cancer Center series of 95 patients with SNUC in which induction chemotherapy response (using cisplatin/carboplatin–etoposide or –docetaxel) guided subsequent treatment: responders proceeded to surgery plus adjuvant CRT achieving superior disease-specific survival, while non-responders received definitive chemoradiation, formalising induction chemotherapy as the preferred initial treatment strategy across all stages of SNUC.

## Definition

**Sinonasal Undifferentiated Carcinoma (SNUC)** is a highly aggressive, rare malignant epithelial tumour of the nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses characterised by:
- **Poorly differentiated cells** arranged in sheets, nests, or trabeculae
- **No squamous or glandular differentiation** identifiable on routine H&E
- **No neuroendocrine differentiation** by IHC (distinguishing it from neuroendocrine carcinoma)
- **High mitotic rate**, geographic necrosis, lymphovascular invasion

### WHO 5th Edition (2022) — Updated Classification
The WHO 2022 classification significantly restructures SNUC within the spectrum of **IDH-mutant and IDH-wildtype sinonasal carcinomas**:
- A proportion of tumours previously classified as SNUC harbour **IDH2 mutations** (particularly R172 hotspot) and are now categorised as **IDH-mutant sinonasal carcinoma**
- **SMARCB1 (INI1)-deficient sinonasal carcinoma** is now a distinct WHO entity — previously misclassified as SNUC
- **NUT carcinoma** has been formally separated from SNUC (NUT1 rearrangement)
- True SNUC (IDH-wildtype, SMARCB1-retained, NUT-negative) remains a diagnosis of exclusion

## Epidemiology

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Incidence | Rare — <1% of all sinonasal malignancies |
| Sex | M:F = 2:1 |
| Age | Peak 5th–7th decade |
| Race | No specific predilection |
| Aetiology | Unknown; no consistent occupational, viral, or environmental link |

## Histopathology and Immunohistochemistry

### Microscopic Features
- Undifferentiated cells: intermediate to large, round-to-polygonal nuclei, prominent nucleoli
- Arranged in **sheets, nests, ribbons, or trabeculae** — no glandular lumina, no intercellular bridges
- **Geographic necrosis:** large areas of ischaemic necrosis with viable peripheral rim
- **High mitotic rate:** >10 mitoses per 10 HPF
- **Lymphovascular invasion** common
- No squamous pearls, no keratinisation, no mucin, no rosettes, no peripheral palisading

### IHC Panel — Key Markers for SNUC

| Marker | SNUC | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **CK (pan-cytokeratin, CAM5.2, AE1/AE3)** | **Positive** | Confirms epithelial origin |
| **p63 / p40** | Variable (±) | Absent squamous differentiation; p40 consistently negative |
| **EMA** | Positive | |
| **CD56 / NSE / synaptophysin / chromogranin** | **Negative** | Distinguishes SNUC from neuroendocrine carcinoma |
| **NUT1 (NUT midline carcinoma Ab)** | **Negative** | Distinguishes from NUT carcinoma |
| **EBER / EBV ISH** | **Negative** | Distinguishes from nasopharyngeal undifferentiated carcinoma |
| **LCA (CD45)** | Negative | Distinguishes from large cell lymphoma |
| **INI1 (SMARCB1)** | **Retained (positive)** | INI1 loss defines SMARCB1-deficient carcinoma |
| **IDH2 R172** | Positive in ~20–30% | Now defines IDH-mutant sinonasal carcinoma in WHO 2022 |

## Differential Diagnosis — Key Poorly Differentiated Sinonasal Tumours

| Tumour | Key IHC Markers | Molecular | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SNUC** | CK+, NE markers−, NUT1−, INI1 retained | IDH2 mut in subset | Diagnosis of exclusion |
| **Olfactory neuroblastoma (ONB)** | CD56+, S100+ (sustentacular), chromogranin+ | NCAM | Lobular nests with sustentacular cells; Homer-Wright rosettes |
| **Large cell NEC** | CK+, CD56+, synaptophysin+, chromogranin+ | RB1, TP53 loss | NE markers positive; large cells, prominent nucleoli |
| **Small cell NEC** | CK+, CD56+, TTF-1+ (40%), synaptophysin+ | RB1, TP53 loss | Small cells, moulding, crush artefact |
| **NUT carcinoma** | NUT1+, p40+ | BRD4-NUT1 fusion | NUT1 IHC positive (diffuse nuclear); any age |
| **SMARCB1-deficient carcinoma** | INI1 (SMARCB1) **lost** | SMARCB1 deletion | INI1 loss on IHC; rhabdoid morphology |
| **Nasopharyngeal undiff. carcinoma** | CK+, EBER+ | EBV driven | EBER ISH positive; nasopharyngeal origin |
| **Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma** | LCA (CD45)+, CK− | BCL2/6/MYC | LCA+, epithelial markers negative |
| **Ewing sarcoma / PNET** | CD99+, FLI1+, CK− | EWSR1-FLI1 fusion | CD99 membranous positive; CK negative |

## Clinical Features

SNUC presents with **aggressive, rapid-onset symptoms** in contrast to the slow presentation of most sinonasal malignancies:
- **Unilateral nasal obstruction** — most common
- **Epistaxis** — often profuse
- **Facial pain / hypoaesthesia** — perineural involvement of CN V branches
- **Diplopia / proptosis** — orbital invasion (common at presentation)
- **Epiphora** — nasolacrimal duct obstruction
- **Headache** — skull base erosion
- **Trismus** — pterygoid/infratemporal fossa involvement
- **Anosmia** — cribriform plate / olfactory bulb involvement
- **Visual loss** — orbital apex / optic nerve compression

> **Key clinical fact:** SNUC is typically **large and locally advanced at presentation** — T3/T4 disease in >80% of patients. Bilateral sinonasal involvement and intracranial extension are not uncommon.

## Staging

SNUC uses the **AJCC 8th Edition staging for nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses (maxillary and ethmoid)** — there is no SNUC-specific staging system. Given the preponderance of ethmoid origin, the ethmoid/nasal cavity T-stage criteria most commonly apply.

## Management Protocol

### Multimodality Approach — Essential
Surgery alone produces uniformly poor outcomes. The current evidence-based protocol is:

```
INITIAL ASSESSMENT
        ↓
MRI skull base + CT PNS + PET-CT (staging)
Tissue diagnosis (biopsy with full IHC panel)
Multidisciplinary team (H&N surgery, radiation oncology, medical oncology, neuroradiology)
        ↓
INDUCTION CHEMOTHERAPY (all patients)
        ↓
RE-STAGING (CT/MRI at 2–3 cycles)
        ↓
RESPONDERS (≥PR by RECIST)          NON-RESPONDERS / UNRESECTABLE
        ↓                                       ↓
Surgery (craniofacial resection)     Definitive concurrent CRT
        ↓                             (60–66 Gy + cisplatin)
Adjuvant CRT
(60–66 Gy + concurrent cisplatin)
```

### Step 1: Induction Chemotherapy

| Regimen | Agents | Cycles | Response Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CE** (preferred) | Cisplatin 80 mg/m² day 1 + Etoposide 100 mg/m² days 1–3 | 2–3 | 60–70% |
| **CE (carboplatin)** | Carboplatin AUC5 + Etoposide | 2–3 | For cisplatin-ineligible patients |
| **TPF** | Docetaxel 75 mg/m² + Cisplatin 75 mg/m² + 5-FU 750 mg/m²/d × 4 | 2–3 | Some centres; data limited for SNUC specifically |

**Rationale for induction-first approach (Amit et al. 2019):**
1. High rate of occult micrometastatic disease — systemic treatment first
2. Response to IC predicts ultimate disease control
3. Downstaging may convert unresectable to resectable
4. Avoids immediate radical surgery in patients who may have rapidly progressive metastatic disease

### Step 2: Re-staging
- **CT and MRI** of primary after cycle 2–3
- Response assessment by **RECIST 1.1** criteria
- PET-CT for distant metastasis assessment

### Step 3a: Surgery (for Responders)

**Craniofacial Resection (CFR):**
- Standard for tumours involving anterior skull base / cribriform plate
- Aims for **R0 margins** — including dural margin if intracranial extension present
- Components:
  - Neurosurgery: bifrontal craniotomy, dural resection if involved, dural reconstruction
  - Head and neck surgery: endonasal or transnasal + facial component (total rhinectomy, orbital exenteration if periorbita breached)
- **Endoscopic craniofacial resection (eCFR):** for selected cases with favourable anatomy and experienced team; equivalent oncological outcomes with reduced morbidity

**Orbital exenteration:** only if periorbita is histologically involved on frozen section; not based on imaging alone.

### Step 3b: Adjuvant Concurrent Chemoradiation

- **Dose:** 60–66 Gy in 30–33 fractions
- **Technique:** IMRT with IGRT — spares contralateral eye, optic nerve, brainstem, chiasm
- **Concurrent chemotherapy:** Cisplatin 100 mg/m² every 3 weeks (or 40 mg/m² weekly) during RT
- **Target volumes:**
  - GTV: post-operative bed + residual disease
  - CTV: entire sinonasal cavity, skull base, anterior cranial fossa if dura involved
  - Elective nodal irradiation: retropharyngeal nodes, level IB–III

### Step 3c: Definitive Concurrent CRT (Unresectable / Non-Responders)

- **Dose:** 66–70 Gy concurrent cisplatin
- Salvage surgery if significant response occurs after definitive CRT — resection of residual disease if feasible
- **Response assessment:** PET-CT at 10–12 weeks post-CRT

### Immunotherapy — Emerging Role

- **PD-L1 expression** documented in 40–60% of SNUC; rationale for checkpoint inhibitor trials
- **Pembrolizumab** (anti-PD-1): case reports and early series showing durable responses in PD-L1+, TMB-high SNUC
- **Nivolumab:** similar early reports
- No Phase III data specific to SNUC; considered for recurrent/metastatic disease on clinical trial or compassionate-use basis
- **TMB (tumour mutational burden):** actionable if high (≥10 mut/Mb) — pembrolizumab FDA-approved for solid tumours regardless of histology (tumour-agnostic approval)

## Prognosis

| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| 5-year overall survival | **20–35%** (all stages combined) |
| T1–T2 disease | ~50–60% (rare presentation) |
| T3–T4 disease | ~15–30% |
| Distant metastasis rate | 30–40% at 5 years |
| Most common site of distant failure | Lung, brain, bone |
| Local recurrence | 40–60% without aggressive multimodality treatment |

> **Prognostic context:** SNUC has the **worst prognosis** among all sinonasal carcinomas — worse than ONB, NEC, and sinonasal SCC of comparable stage. Rapid initial progression is the norm; aggressive upfront multimodality treatment is the only approach associated with improved outcomes.

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> SNUC is a rare, highly aggressive poorly differentiated sinonasal carcinoma that is a diagnosis of exclusion — all neuroendocrine markers (CD56, synaptophysin, chromogranin), NUT1, INI1 loss, EBER, and squamous markers must be negative before diagnosing SNUC; the WHO 2022 classification has further separated IDH-mutant sinonasal carcinoma and SMARCB1-deficient carcinoma as distinct entities. Patients typically present with large T3–T4 tumours with orbital and skull base involvement, and single-modality treatment produces uniformly poor outcomes. The current standard protocol is induction chemotherapy (cisplatin-etoposide) first, with re-staging to guide subsequent treatment: responders proceed to craniofacial resection followed by adjuvant concurrent chemoradiation (60–66 Gy plus cisplatin), while non-responders or unresectable cases receive definitive concurrent CRT. The 5-year overall survival remains 20–35% even with aggressive multimodality treatment, and emerging immunotherapy data with pembrolizumab in PD-L1-positive disease is under active investigation.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. SNUC is a **diagnosis of exclusion** — the full IHC panel must exclude ONB, NEC (small and large cell), NUT carcinoma, SMARCB1-deficient carcinoma, lymphoma, and nasopharyngeal undifferentiated carcinoma before SNUC is diagnosed.
> 2. **IDH2 R172 mutation** identifies a distinct subset now classified as IDH-mutant sinonasal carcinoma (WHO 2022) — molecular testing is increasingly important.
> 3. **INI1 (SMARCB1) IHC** must be performed on all poorly differentiated sinonasal tumours: loss of INI1 staining defines SMARCB1-deficient sinonasal carcinoma, a newly recognised entity that is distinct from SNUC.
> 4. **NUT1 IHC** (diffuse nuclear staining) identifies NUT carcinoma — critical to test in any age group, particularly young patients with poorly differentiated sinonasal tumours.
> 5. **Induction chemotherapy first** is now the preferred approach for all stages of SNUC — not surgery first — based on Amit et al. (2019) and the MD Anderson institutional protocol.
> 6. **Cisplatin–etoposide (CE)** is the standard induction regimen for SNUC; response rates of 60–70% make this a meaningful staging tool that also treats occult micrometastases.
> 7. **Orbital exenteration** should be decided on intraoperative frozen-section histology of the periorbita, not on imaging alone — preserving the eye if periorbita is histologically clear is standard.
> 8. **PD-L1 expression in 40–60% of SNUC** provides a rationale for pembrolizumab — always test PD-L1 and TMB in recurrent/metastatic SNUC, as tumour-agnostic pembrolizumab approval applies to TMB-high solid tumours.
> 9. SNUC has a **30–40% rate of distant metastasis** at 5 years — PET-CT at diagnosis and during follow-up is essential to detect early pulmonary, hepatic, and bony metastases.
> 10. Despite aggressive treatment, **5-year OS remains 20–35%** — candid counselling about prognosis is mandatory, and enrolment in clinical trials (immunotherapy, targeted therapy for IDH2-mutant SNUC) should be actively pursued.
