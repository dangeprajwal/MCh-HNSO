> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> The original staging system for olfactory neuroblastoma was proposed by Kadish, Goodman, and Wang in 1976 based on a clinical analysis of 17 patients. Their three-stage classification — confined to the nasal cavity (Stage A), extending to the paranasal sinuses (Stage B), or beyond (Stage C) — established the framework still used today. Modified Kadish staging adds Stage D for cervical nodal or distant metastatic disease. Stage was shown to be an independent predictor of outcome in multiple subsequent analyses.
>
> The histological grading system introduced by Hyams in 1988 through the Armed Forces Institute of Pathology (AFIP) fascicle on upper respiratory tract tumours defined four grades based on lobular architecture, fibrillary stroma, rosette formation (Homer-Wright and Flexner-Wintersteiner), mitotic activity, and necrosis. Grade I and II tumours (well and moderately differentiated) carry a significantly better prognosis than Grade III and IV, and Hyams grade has become the principal intratumoural prognostic factor, guiding the addition of chemotherapy to the treatment regimen.
>
> Dulguerov, Allal, and Calcaterra published a meta-analysis and review in Lancet Oncology in 2001 encompassing 26 published series, demonstrating an overall 5-year survival of approximately 45% across all stages, but with considerable variation by stage and treatment modality; multimodality therapy (surgery plus radiotherapy) produced superior outcomes to either modality alone. Devaiah and Andreoli in 2009 analysed 361 patients from 49 published studies spanning 16 years, confirming that combined surgery and radiotherapy was superior, and specifically that endoscopic approaches were associated with comparable or improved survival versus open craniofacial resection in appropriately selected patients. Folbe et al. (2009) provided multicentre data on purely endoscopic endonasal resection across 23 patients at Miami and Pittsburgh, demonstrating feasibility and negative intraoperative margins in the majority of cases.

## Esthesioneuroblastoma (Olfactory Neuroblastoma)

### Definition and Histogenesis

Esthesioneuroblastoma (ENB), also called olfactory neuroblastoma (ONB), is a rare malignant neoplasm arising from the olfactory neuroepithelium of the upper nasal vault, cribriform plate, and superior nasal septum. It is classified as a neuroectodermal tumour with neuroendocrine differentiation, distinct from other sinonasal neuroendocrine tumours (sinonasal neuroendocrine carcinoma, large cell NEC, small cell NEC, SNUC). Histogenesis traces to the sustentacular (support) cells of the olfactory epithelium derived from neural crest, not from olfactory receptor neurons themselves.

Immunohistochemistry: positive for neuron-specific enolase (NSE), synaptophysin, chromogranin A, S100 (sustentacular cells at periphery of lobules). Negative for cytokeratin (distinguishes from SCC and SNUC), CD45 (lymphoma), and desmin (rhabdomyosarcoma).

### Epidemiology

- Bimodal age distribution: 2nd decade (10–20 years) and 6th decade (50–60 years)
- Slight male predominance (M:F approximately 1.2:1)
- Rare: approximately 3–6% of all sinonasal malignancies
- No established environmental aetiology (contrast with ITAC — wood dust, SNUC — no known cause)

### Staging Systems

#### Kadish Staging (1976) — Most Widely Used

| Stage | Definition | Approximate 5-year OS |
|---|---|---|
| A | Tumour confined to nasal cavity | ~75% |
| B | Tumour involving nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses | ~65% |
| C | Tumour extending beyond nasal cavity/sinuses — orbit, skull base, intracranial, cervical nodes, or distant metastasis | ~50% |

**Modified Kadish (Morita et al.)** adds Stage D for cervical lymph node or distant metastasis (separating nodal and distant disease from Kadish C orbital/skull base disease). Most contemporary series use the modified system.

#### Dulguerov (UCLA) TNM Staging (1992) — More Detailed

Based on CT and MRI findings, providing a TNM-based system analogous to other head and neck sites:

| Stage | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| T1 | Tumour involving nasal cavity and/or paranasal sinuses (excluding sphenoid), sparing the most superior ethmoidal cells |
| T2 | Tumour involving nasal cavity and/or paranasal sinuses (including sphenoid) with extension to or erosion of the cribriform plate |
| T3 | Tumour extending into orbit or protruding into anterior cranial fossa without dural invasion |
| T4 | Tumour involving brain |
| N0–N3 | As per standard nodal staging |
| M0–M1 | No/distant metastasis |

Dulguerov T staging correlates more precisely with resectability and has been validated as superior to Kadish staging in predicting locoregional recurrence. However, Kadish remains more widely cited in clinical practice.

### Hyams Grading (1988)

Histological grading defined in the Armed Forces Institute of Pathology (AFIP) fascicle on tumours of the upper respiratory tract and ear. The most important intratumoural prognostic factor.

| Feature | Grade I | Grade II | Grade III | Grade IV |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Architecture | Lobular | Lobular | Lobular | Sheets |
| Fibrillary stroma | Prominent | Present | Low/absent | Absent |
| Rosettes | Homer-Wright | Homer-Wright | Flexner-Wintersteiner rare | Absent |
| Mitoses | Absent | Rare | Moderate | High |
| Nuclear pleomorphism | Low | Moderate | Moderate | High |
| Necrosis | Absent | Absent | Present | Prominent |
| Calcification | May be present | May be present | Absent | Absent |

- **Grade I–II**: well-differentiated; favourable prognosis; surgery + RT often adequate
- **Grade III–IV**: poorly differentiated; aggressive behaviour; chemotherapy strongly indicated

Hyams grade III–IV corresponds closely to Kadish C/D disease in clinical behaviour and mandates multimodality intensification.

### Clinical Presentation

- **Unilateral nasal obstruction** — most common presenting symptom (may be gradually progressive over months)
- **Epistaxis** — often painless, recurrent
- **Anosmia** — characteristic but often overlooked by patient and clinician
- **Orbital symptoms** — proptosis, diplopia, periorbital swelling (orbital extension = Kadish C)
- **Headache and cranial neuropathies** — skull base/intracranial extension
- **Paraneoplastic syndromes** — ectopic ACTH secretion (Cushing syndrome), syndrome of inappropriate ADH (SIADH); rare but well-documented
- Cervical lymphadenopathy at presentation in approximately 5–8% of cases, but occult nodal disease in up to 30% of Kadish C/Hyams III–IV cases

### Diagnostic Workup

| Investigation | Purpose |
|---|---|
| CT PNS (with and without contrast) | Bony erosion, calcification (characteristic but not pathognomonic), extent of sinus involvement |
| MRI skull base with gadolinium | Intracranial extension, dural involvement, orbital invasion, perineural spread; "tram-track" peripheral enhancement on gadolinium MRI is characteristic of ENB lobules |
| Endoscopic biopsy | Tissue diagnosis — avoid trans-nasal biopsy without imaging (risk of profuse haemorrhage from highly vascular tumour) |
| PET-CT | Regional staging, distant metastasis (lungs, bone, liver) |
| CXR/CT chest | Lung metastases (ENB metastasises haematogenously) |
| Ophthalmology review | If proptosis, visual symptoms, or orbital involvement on imaging |
| Neuroendocrine workup | Urinary catecholamines if paraneoplastic symptoms; cortisol if Cushingoid features |

The "tram-track" sign on MRI — rim enhancement of peripheral tumour lobules with a central non-enhancing core — is considered characteristic of ENB and helps distinguish it from inverted papilloma, SCC, and lymphoma.

### Management

#### Overview: Multimodality is Standard

No randomised trials exist; evidence is from retrospective series and meta-analyses. The Devaiah and Andreoli meta-analysis (2009, 361 patients) established that combined surgery plus radiotherapy consistently outperforms either modality alone, with a 5-year disease-specific survival of ~65% for combined therapy versus ~50% for surgery alone and ~37% for radiotherapy alone.

#### Surgical Approaches

**Open Craniofacial Resection (CFR)**
- Historically the standard for any cribriform plate/skull base involvement
- Combined transcranial (bifrontal craniotomy) + transfacial (lateral rhinotomy or Weber–Ferguson) approach
- Allows wide en bloc resection of cribriform plate, olfactory bulbs, dura, and sinonasal tumour
- Indicated for: broad dural invasion, intracranial extension beyond dura, frontal lobe involvement

**Endoscopic Craniofacial Resection (eCFR)**
- Developed by Snyderman, Kassam, and colleagues at Pittsburgh; now the preferred approach at high-volume centres for selected cases
- Bimanual 4-hand technique with simultaneous endoscopic and neurosurgical capability
- Image-guided navigation is mandatory
- **Oncologically equivalent to open CFR** for cases without broad dural or brain parenchymal invasion (Devaiah & Andreoli 2009; Folbe et al. 2009; Howard et al. 2006)
- Advantages: no facial scar, no craniotomy, shorter recovery, lower CSF leak risk in experienced hands
- **Reconstruction**: nasoseptal flap (Hadad–Bassagaistegui flap, pedicled on posterior nasoseptal artery) for skull base defect — must be elevated at the start of the case before the tumour resection disrupts the pedicle territory

#### Staging-Based Surgical Strategy

| Kadish Stage | Dulguerov Stage | Surgical Approach |
|---|---|---|
| A | T1 | Endoscopic resection (EMM ± ethmoidectomy) |
| B | T2 | Endoscopic ± eCFR (cribriform involvement) |
| C (skull base/dura) | T3 | eCFR (preferred) or open CFR |
| C (intracranial extension > dura) | T4 | Open CFR ± cranioendoscopic combined |
| C (orbital) | T3 with orbit | Orbital periosteum preservation if periosteum intact; exenteration if fat invaded |

#### Radiotherapy

- **Standard**: 60–66 Gy to primary tumour bed, olfactory nerve tracts, and anterior skull base
- Include bilateral elective neck fields for Kadish C, Hyams III–IV
- Technique: IMRT (preferred for skull base sparing of optic nerves, optic chiasm, pituitary, brainstem)
- Timing: adjuvant RT starts 4–6 weeks after surgery when wound healed; concurrent not standard
- Proton beam therapy: advantageous for young patients and for skull base tumours (sharp Bragg peak, reduces late neurocognitive toxicity)
- RT alone: used for unresectable disease; 5-year OS approximately 30–40%

#### Chemotherapy

Indications: Hyams Grade III–IV, Kadish C/D, unresectable disease, or bulky tumour prior to definitive surgery (induction).

**Regimens:**
- **Etoposide + cisplatin** (most commonly used; based on small cell lung cancer analogy due to neuroendocrine histogenesis)
- **VAC regimen** — vincristine, doxorubicin (Adriamycin), cyclophosphamide — used particularly in paediatric patients and in some centres for Hyams III–IV
- **Induction chemotherapy**: for bulky Kadish C — 2–3 cycles of platinum-etoposide → restaging MRI → surgery/eCFR → adjuvant RT

Chemotherapy improves local control and possibly distant recurrence but has not shown a survival benefit in randomised data (no RCT exists).

### Neck Management

**Elective neck treatment** is the critical management decision specific to ENB:

- **Occult nodal metastasis rate**: approximately 20–30% for Kadish C and Hyams III–IV at presentation; delayed neck failure in untreated N0 neck occurs in up to 20% within 5 years
- **Recommended elective nodal irradiation**: bilateral levels I–III (retropharyngeal nodes included in some protocols) for Kadish C and Hyams III–IV
- **Elective neck dissection (END)**: Level I–III selective neck dissection is performed in N0 patients at high-risk institutions when surgical staging is preferred over elective radiation; evidence supports END reducing regional failure
- **Therapeutic neck dissection (ND)**: radical or modified radical ND for clinically or radiologically N+ disease; surgery followed by adjuvant RT to neck
- **Role of elective neck treatment for Kadish A/B, Hyams I–II**: controversial; some centres observe, others irradiate electively; most contemporary protocols include elective nodal irradiation for all Kadish B–C
- Late cervical nodal recurrence (> 5 years) is a well-documented behaviour of ENB — mandating long-term follow-up with clinical neck examination and interval MRI

### Prognosis and Follow-Up

| Factor | Favourable | Unfavourable |
|---|---|---|
| Kadish stage | A/B | C/D |
| Hyams grade | I/II | III/IV |
| Margins | Negative | Positive |
| Treatment | Surgery + RT | RT alone |
| Nodal status | N0 | N+ |

- Late recurrences (> 5 years) occur in ENB; minimum 10-year follow-up is recommended
- 5-year OS: Kadish A ~75%, Kadish B ~65%, Kadish C ~50%
- Local recurrence most common failure pattern; neck recurrence second
- Distant metastases (lung, bone, liver) in Hyams III–IV and Kadish D

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Esthesioneuroblastoma is a rare neuroectodermal malignancy arising from olfactory epithelium, presenting with unilateral nasal obstruction, epistaxis, and anosmia; Kadish C disease additionally manifests with orbital and skull base symptoms. Staging relies on Kadish (A–D) or Dulguerov TNM; Hyams histological grading (I–IV) is the most important intratumoural prognostic factor. Treatment is multimodality: surgery (endoscopic craniofacial resection for selected cases, open CFR for extensive disease) plus radiotherapy (60–66 Gy), with chemotherapy (etoposide-cisplatin or VAC) added for Hyams III–IV and Kadish C/D. Elective neck treatment (irradiation or selective ND of levels I–III) is essential for Kadish C and Hyams III–IV due to ~20–30% occult nodal metastasis rate. Late recurrences are characteristic of this tumour and mandate ten-year surveillance.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. ENB has a **bimodal age distribution** (2nd and 6th decades) — unlike most sinonasal malignancies which predominantly affect older adults; this unusual epidemiology is a classic examination point.
> 2. **Hyams grading is the principal histological prognostic factor** — Grade I/II (low) behaves indolently; Grade III/IV (high) is aggressive and mandates chemotherapy intensification regardless of Kadish stage.
> 3. **Modified Kadish Stage D** (cervical/distant metastasis) is distinguished from Stage C (orbital/skull base/intracranial) to avoid grouping disparate prognostic groups together.
> 4. The **"tram-track" peripheral rim enhancement** on gadolinium MRI is characteristic of ENB lobules and helps narrow the differential from SCC and lymphoma.
> 5. **Elective neck treatment is mandatory for Kadish C and Hyams III–IV** — 20–30% occult nodal metastasis in the untreated N0 neck justifies bilateral levels I–III irradiation or selective neck dissection.
> 6. **Endoscopic CFR is oncologically equivalent to open CFR** for cases without broad dural invasion or brain parenchymal involvement — Devaiah & Andreoli 2009 meta-analysis.
> 7. The **nasoseptal flap must be elevated at the start of the procedure** before any resection, to preserve the posterior nasoseptal artery pedicle as the workhorse for skull base reconstruction.
> 8. **Late recurrence is a hallmark of ENB** — recurrences at 10–15 years post-treatment are documented; lifelong annual surveillance is the standard.
> 9. **Ectopic ACTH** (Cushing syndrome) is the classic paraneoplastic manifestation of ENB — the only other common sinonasal tumour with neuroendocrine paraneoplastic features is neuroendocrine carcinoma.
> 10. **Etoposide + cisplatin** is the standard chemotherapy backbone for ENB (borrowed from small cell lung cancer protocols); VAC (vincristine, Adriamycin, cyclophosphamide) is preferred in paediatric patients.
