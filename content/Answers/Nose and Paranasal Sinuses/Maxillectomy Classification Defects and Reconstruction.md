> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Cordeiro and Santamaria (2000) from Memorial Sloan Kettering published the first systematic classification and reconstructive algorithm for maxillectomy defects in Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery, dividing defects into four types based on the extent of maxillary resection. Their algorithm recommended prosthetic obturation for limited defects, soft tissue free flaps for total maxillectomy, and composite tissue transfer for orbitomaxillary resections. This remains one of the most-cited frameworks for free flap planning in maxillary reconstruction.
>
> Brown and Shaw (2010) published in Lancet Oncology a revised classification specifically addressing the increasing complexity of midface reconstruction. Their two-axis system — vertical classes based on palatal involvement and horizontal subclasses based on orbital involvement — provides a comprehensive framework applicable to the full spectrum of maxillary resections from limited infrastructure to total orbitomaxillectomy. The publication emphasised that low defects not involving orbital adnexae can often be successfully managed with dental obturators, while class III–VI defects require free tissue transfer.

## Maxillectomy: Classification, Defects, and Reconstruction

### Anatomical Overview of the Maxilla

The maxilla is a paired bone forming the central skeletal framework of the midface. It houses the upper dentition, forms the hard palate (with the palatine bone), the floor and medial wall of the orbit, the lateral walls of the nasal cavity, and the anterior wall of the maxillary sinus. The infraorbital nerve (V2) exits through the infraorbital foramen 5–7 mm below the orbital rim. The pterygoid plates articulate posteriorly at the pterygomaxillary junction — the critical posterior osteotomy point in total maxillectomy.

Surgical planes of significance:
- **Ohngren's line** — an imaginary plane from the medial canthus to the angle of the mandible, dividing tumours into superstructure (above line — orbit, skull base — worse prognosis) and infrastructure (below line — palate, alveolus — better prognosis). Historically useful for prognostication; largely replaced by AJCC staging.
- **Pterygopalatine fossa (PPF)** — posterior gateway for tumour spread to skull base via V2 (foramen rotundum) and the greater and lesser palatine foramina.

### Types of Maxillectomy

| Type | Structures Removed | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Infrastructure maxillectomy | Hard palate, alveolar process, medial/inferior maxillary walls (below Ohngren's line) | T1–T2 infrastructure tumours, inverted papilloma |
| Subtotal maxillectomy | Infrastructure + anterior and lateral walls; orbital floor preserved | T2–T3 maxillary tumours without orbital floor involvement |
| Total (radical) maxillectomy | All six walls of the maxillary sinus; orbital floor removed but orbital contents preserved | T3 maxillary tumours with orbital floor involvement |
| Extended maxillectomy | Total maxillectomy + orbital exenteration; may include pterygoid plates, temporal fossa, or ethmoid sinuses | T4a–T4b with orbital contents involvement, extensive skull base disease |
| Medial maxillectomy | Medial wall, inferior turbinate, medial portion of orbital floor | Selected T2–T3 medial maxillary tumours (also performed endoscopically) |

### Brown and Shaw Classification (2010) — Primary Classification

The Brown and Shaw classification uses two axes: **vertical classes** describing the palatal/maxillary extent and **horizontal subclasses** describing orbital involvement.

#### Vertical Classes (Palatal/Maxillary Extent)

| Class | Definition | Typical Indication |
|---|---|---|
| I | Maxillectomy not causing an oronasal fistula (medial wall or infrastructure below level of floor of antrum) | Medial maxillectomy, very limited resection |
| II | Infrastructure maxillectomy: hard palate and inferior walls; does not include orbital floor | T1–T2 infrastructure SCC, inverted papilloma |
| III | Subtotal maxillectomy: hard palate + lower two-thirds of maxilla; orbital floor preserved | T2–T3 tumours sparing orbital floor |
| IV | Total maxillectomy: entire maxilla including orbital floor | T3 with orbital floor involvement (orbital contents preserved) |
| V | Orbitomaxillary resection: total maxillectomy + resection of orbital adnexae (contents preserved or exenterated) | T4a with orbital content involvement |
| VI | Total maxillectomy with orbital exenteration | T4b orbital apex, extensive orbital involvement |

#### Horizontal Subclasses (Orbital Involvement)

| Subclass | Definition |
|---|---|
| a | Orbital floor intact (no orbital involvement) |
| b | Orbital floor removed; orbital contents preserved (periosteum intact or stripped with fat preserved) |
| c | Orbital exenteration (total removal of orbital contents) |

Combined classification examples: Class IVb = total maxillectomy with orbital floor removal but orbital contents preserved; Class Vc = orbitomaxillary resection with exenteration.

### Cordeiro–Santamaria Classification (2000) — Alternative

| Type | Definition | Recommended Reconstruction |
|---|---|---|
| I | Limited maxillectomy (one or two walls except palate) | Local flap, ALT, or rectus |
| IIa | Subtotal maxillectomy preserving palate | Rectus abdominis or ALT |
| IIb | Subtotal maxillectomy including palate | Rectus + palatal closure or obturator |
| III | Total maxillectomy (all six walls, including palate) | Fibula free flap or rectus |
| IVa | Orbitomaxillary (III + lower two-thirds orbit, no exenteration) | Rectus abdominis |
| IVb | Orbitomaxillectomy (III + total orbital exenteration) | Rectus abdominis (volume) |

### Reconstruction Options by Defect Class

The reconstructive goals are: (1) separation of oral and nasal/antral cavities (watertight palatal seal); (2) support of orbital contents where relevant; (3) restoration of midfacial contour; (4) rehabilitation of dental function; (5) speech and swallowing competence.

#### Brown Class I–II (Small and Infrastructure Defects)

- **Prosthetic obturator**: the primary option for limited palatal defects. Fabricated by maxillofacial prosthodontist:
  - *Surgical obturator*: placed at the time of surgery, reduces aspiration and supports cheek flap
  - *Transitional obturator*: at 4–6 weeks once wound stable, allows wound inspection
  - *Definitive obturator*: at 3–6 months when wound matured; impressions for accurate fit
  - **Advantages**: allows ongoing wound surveillance (lift out and inspect), non-operative, modifiable as wound matures, speech and swallowing rehabilitation begins early
  - **Disadvantages**: daily removal and hygiene, comfort, retention (requires adequate alveolar ridge), dental implants needed for long-term stability
- **Buccal fat pad flap**: for small inferior maxillary defects; highly vascularised, rapid epithelialisation; useful for oro-antral closure after limited resection
- **Palatal island flap**: for anterior palatal defects; based on greater palatine vessels

#### Brown Class III–IV (Subtotal and Total Maxillectomy)

- **Fibula free flap** — gold standard for bony palatal reconstruction:
  - Provides vascularised bone (up to 25 cm) for neo-maxillary reconstruction
  - Multiple osteotomies to create a 3-dimensional "zygomaxillary" shape
  - Skin paddle for palatal lining; second skin paddle for external coverage if needed
  - Critical advantage: allows dental osseointegrated implants for definitive prosthetic rehabilitation
  - Virtual surgical planning (VSP) and patient-specific implants enhance accuracy
  - Disadvantage: technically demanding; long operative time; bilateral monitoring for donor site ischaemia
- **Radial forearm free flap (RFFF)** — for soft tissue palatal reconstruction:
  - Thin, pliable fasciocutaneous flap; excellent for palatal lining
  - No bony reconstruction; obturator still required for dental rehabilitation
  - Advantages: technically reliable, thin flap conforms to palatal contour, good sensate potential
  - Indication: elderly patients or where bone not required; Class III defects without dental rehabilitation goals
- **Anterolateral thigh (ALT) free flap** — for large soft tissue defects:
  - Based on descending branch of lateral circumflex femoral artery
  - Advantages: large skin paddle, can harvest with vastus lateralis muscle for volume, adjustable bulk
  - Ideal for Class IV–V defects requiring large soft tissue obliteration
  - Perforator anatomy variable — doppler preoperative marking essential
- **Temporalis muscle flap** (pedicled):
  - Based on deep temporal artery (branch of internal maxillary artery)
  - Provides bulk for IPF and infratemporal fossa obliteration; arc of rotation reaches palate and orbit
  - Limitations: limited reach, skull depression donor deformity, incomplete palatal coverage
  - Useful as a supplementary flap or when free tissue transfer is not feasible

#### Brown Class V–VI (Orbitomaxillary and Orbital Exenteration)

- **Rectus abdominis free flap** — workhorse for volume replacement after orbital exenteration:
  - Provides substantial muscle bulk to fill the orbital-maxillary dead space
  - Skin paddle for external lining; muscle fills intracranial dead space
  - Perforated fascia allows passive drainage
- **Temporoparietal fascia flap** (pedicled on superficial temporal artery):
  - Thin, well-vascularised; used to line socket or provide orbital volume supplement
  - Limited reach; typically combined with a split-thickness skin graft
- **Orbital prosthesis**: after exenteration; silicone prosthesis with osseointegrated implants (zygomatic implants for anchor points); good aesthetic outcomes with experienced prosthetist

### Palatal Reconstruction: Specific Considerations

The hard palate serves as the anatomical and functional barrier between the oral cavity and the nasal/antral space. Loss of palatal integrity results in:
- **Velopharyngeal incompetence (VPI)**: nasal air escape, hypernasal speech, nasal regurgitation
- **Oronasal fistula**: food/liquid passage into nasal cavity
- **Dental loss**: edentulous arch requiring rehabilitation

The sequence of palatal rehabilitation:
1. **Surgical obturator at time of surgery** — immediate phonation, swallowing, and wound support
2. **Speech pathology assessment** — velopharyngeal function assessment, nasal endoscopy, nasopharyngoscopy
3. **Transitional obturator at 4–6 weeks** — as wound shrinks and swelling resolves
4. **Definitive obturator or free flap reconstruction at 3–6 months** — when wound stable; decision based on defect size and patient factors

For free flap palatal reconstruction, the fibula provides both bony framework for implants and a skin paddle for watertight palatal closure. The RFFF provides thin, pliable tissue for palatal lining when bony support is not needed.

### Orbital Considerations in Maxillectomy

The decision to preserve or exenterate the orbit is a critical oncological and functional choice:

| Orbital Status | Recommended Management |
|---|---|
| Orbital floor erosion but periosteum intact | Remove orbital floor + reconstruct with titanium mesh; preserve orbital contents (Brown class IVa/b) |
| Periorbita breached; orbital fat not invaded | Periorbital stripping + orbital floor reconstruction; eye preservation possible |
| Orbital fat invaded by tumour | Orbital exenteration required for oncological clearance (Brown class Vc/VI) |
| Extraocular muscle involvement | Exenteration |
| Optic nerve or apex involvement (T4b) | Exenteration; consider palliative intent |

Orbital floor reconstruction options:
- **Titanium mesh** (0.4–0.6 mm): standard; allows visual inspection through mesh, supports orbital contents, prevents enophthalmos
- **Porous polyethylene (Medpor)**: alternative; tissue-integrating; less infection risk than metallic mesh
- **Autologous bone**: maxillary or calvarial bone graft; resorbs over time; not preferred in irradiated field

### Speech and Swallowing Rehabilitation

Following total or infrastructure maxillectomy, multidisciplinary rehabilitation is essential:
- **Speech pathologist**: VPI assessment, nasality evaluation, compensatory articulation therapy
- **Maxillofacial prosthodontist**: obturator design optimised for VP port closure
- **Dietitian**: caloric optimisation during early rehabilitation when swallowing compromised
- **Dental implantologist**: osseointegrated implants for obturator or denture retention (fibula free flap provides bony scaffold)

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Maxillectomy defects are classified by Brown and Shaw (2010) using a vertical axis (Classes I–VI, based on palatal/maxillary extent) and a horizontal subclass (a–c, based on orbital involvement); this is the standard contemporary classification. Small defects (Brown I–II) are rehabilitated with prosthetic obturators providing the advantage of wound surveillance, while larger defects (Brown III–IV) require free tissue transfer — the fibula free flap being the gold standard when bony palatal reconstruction and dental implants are goals. Orbital floor reconstruction uses titanium mesh when orbital contents are preserved; orbital exenteration is required only when tumour has breached the periorbita into orbital fat or extraocular muscles. Velopharyngeal incompetence management through a staged obturator sequence — surgical, transitional, definitive — is integral to functional rehabilitation.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Brown's classification uses two axes** — vertical (extent of palatal/maxillary resection, Class I–VI) and horizontal (orbital status, subclass a/b/c) — and knowing the combined class (e.g., IVb) conveys both the extent of bony resection and orbital management.
> 2. **Ohngren's line** (medial canthus to angle of mandible) divides tumours into superstructure (worse prognosis — orbit, skull base proximity) and infrastructure (better prognosis — palate, alveolus); historically important but modern AJCC staging supersedes it.
> 3. **The fibula free flap is the gold standard for bony palatal reconstruction** — it provides vascularised bone for 3D neo-maxillary reconstruction and a bony scaffold for osseointegrated dental implants, enabling prosthetic rehabilitation.
> 4. **Prosthetic obturators allow wound surveillance** — the ability to remove the prosthesis and inspect the raw surface is a key advantage over flap closure; this is particularly valuable in the first 1–2 years when recurrence risk is highest.
> 5. **Orbital periosteum is the oncological barrier** — periosteum intact = orbital contents can be preserved (titanium mesh floor reconstruction); periosteum breached with fat invasion = exenteration required.
> 6. **Velopharyngeal incompetence is the primary functional morbidity** of total maxillectomy; a staged obturator sequence (surgical → transitional → definitive) over 3–6 months optimises speech and swallowing outcomes.
> 7. **The ALT free flap** is preferred for large soft tissue defects (Class IV–V) where volume replacement is needed and bone is not required — adjustable bulk, large skin paddle, and versatile harvest make it ideal.
> 8. **Virtual surgical planning (VSP)** with patient-specific implants and 3D osteotomy guides has transformed fibula free flap reconstruction accuracy, particularly for three-dimensional maxillary reconstruction.
> 9. **Dental osseointegration requires adequate bone stock** — only the fibula free flap (and rarely the iliac crest free flap) provides sufficient vascularised bone depth (> 8 mm) to accept implants in the reconstructed maxilla.
> 10. **The temporalis muscle flap** (pedicled) is a reliable adjunct or salvage option for infratemporal fossa obliteration and limited palatal coverage when free tissue transfer is not feasible, but it leaves a temporal hollow at the donor site.
