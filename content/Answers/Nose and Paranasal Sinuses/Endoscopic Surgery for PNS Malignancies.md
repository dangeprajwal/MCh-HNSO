## Background and Rationale

Sinonasal malignancies were historically managed by open craniofacial resection (CFR), introduced by Ketcham in 1963, combining a transcranial and transfacial approach. While effective, open CFR carries significant morbidity: facial scarring (lateral rhinotomy, Weber–Ferguson incision), cerebrospinal fluid leak, meningitis, epiphora from lacrimal disruption, and 5–10% perioperative mortality in large series.

The endoscopic endonasal approach (EEA) was pioneered in the 1990s–2000s at centres in Brescia (Nicolai, Castelnuovo), Pittsburgh (Snyderman, Kassam), and Houston (Hanna). It offers:
- **Superior visualisation** of medial wall, skull base, orbit, and pterygopalatine fossa without facial incisions
- **Magnification and angled optics** (30°, 45°, 70°) allowing inspection behind anatomical corners
- **Reduced morbidity**: no facial scar, shorter hospitalisation, faster recovery
- **Oncologic equivalence** in appropriately selected tumours

## Patient Selection: The Castelnuovo–Nicolai Criteria

Patient selection is the **critical determinant** of endoscopic success. The Brescia group (Castelnuovo, Nicolai) established criteria refined over a decade of practice.

### Amenable to Endoscopic Resection

| Factor | Endoscopic Approach Suitable |
|---|---|
| Skull base involvement | Dura involved but brain not infiltrated |
| Orbital involvement | Periorbita intact, or periorbita involved but orbital fat not invaded |
| Histology | Intestinal-type adenocarcinoma (ITAC), esthesioneuroblastoma, low-grade tumours |
| Tumour extent | Limited to sinonasal cavity ± anterior skull base |
| Previous surgery | No prior open facial surgery precluding endoscopic access |
| Surgeon expertise | Experienced endoscopic skull base team with neurosurgical backup |

### Absolute Contraindications (Endoscopic Approach Not Appropriate)

1. **Dural invasion with parenchymal brain infiltration** — endoscopic dural resection without brain resection is feasible, but brain invasion demands open CFR with adequate cerebral margin
2. **Invasion of orbital fat (beyond periorbita)** — periorbital stripping is feasible endoscopically; true orbital fat invasion with extraocular muscle or optic nerve compromise requires orbital exenteration (best performed via open approach)
3. **Skin involvement** — direct anterior facial skin involvement requires en bloc resection of overlying skin, which is not achievable endoscopically
4. **Lateral extension into infratemporal fossa** — beyond the posterior maxillary wall into masticator space; poor endoscopic access
5. **Nasopharyngeal invasion** — extensive inferior spread with parapharyngeal involvement
6. **Inability to achieve negative margins endoscopically** — frozen section at margins must be available intraoperatively; if positive margins are anticipated in inaccessible locations, open approach is preferred

### Relative Contraindications

- Frontal sinus invasion beyond the frontal recess (may require Draf III / frontal sinus drill-out)
- Prior radiation causing scarring and poor tissue planes
- Extensive pterygopalatine fossa involvement (partial involvement can be addressed endoscopically)

## Histology-Driven Selection

Histology should guide the surgical decision, not merely anatomy:

| Histology | Preferred Primary Approach | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Intestinal-type adenocarcinoma (ITAC) | Endoscopic (EEA) | Woodworking/leather dust aetiology; ethmoid origin; excellent 5-yr DSS 94% in Nicolai series |
| Esthesioneuroblastoma (ONB) | EEA ± craniotomy (cranioendoscopic) | Hyams grade I–II respond well; Kadish C requires cranioendoscopic approach |
| Squamous cell carcinoma | EEA for selected T2–T3; open for T4 | Lower 5-yr DSS (60.7% Nicolai); higher recurrence risk |
| Sinonasal undifferentiated carcinoma (SNUC) | Combined chemoradiation ± surgery | Aggressive biology; surgery role limited |
| Mucosal melanoma | EEA for resectable disease | 5-yr DSS poor regardless of approach; margin status most important |
| Adenoid cystic carcinoma | EEA if resectable | Perineural spread; imaging to assess skull base foramina |
| NUT carcinoma / SMARCB1-deficient | Multimodal; surgery for debulking | Aggressive; chemotherapy-sensitive |

## Open vs. Endoscopic Approaches: Comparison

### Open Approaches (Historical Reference)

| Approach | Access Provided | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral rhinotomy | Medial maxilla, ethmoids, nasal cavity | T2–T3 medial maxillary tumours |
| Weber–Ferguson incision | Full maxilla, orbit, cheek | Total maxillectomy, T4 lesions |
| Midfacial degloving | Bilateral sinonasal access, no facial scar | Bilateral or midline tumours |
| Craniofacial resection (CFR) | Anterior skull base, cribiform plate | Skull base involvement, ONB |

### Endoscopic Approaches

| Approach | Access Provided | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Endoscopic medial maxillectomy (EMM) | Medial maxillary wall, ethmoids, inferior turbinate | T1–T2 medial maxillary tumours |
| Endoscopic ethmoidectomy and cribiform resection | Anterior ethmoids, cribiform plate, dura | ITAC, T2 ONB |
| Endoscopic craniofacial resection (eCFR, Snyderman) | Anterior skull base including dura | Anterior skull base malignancies, ONB Kadish B–C |
| Endoscopic total maxillectomy | Complete maxillary resection endoscopically | Selected T3 maxillary tumours; highly specialised centres |

### Oncologic Outcomes Comparison

From the Nicolai (2008) series of 184 patients:
- 5-yr **overall survival**: EEA 91.4% vs. open historical controls ~60–70%
- 5-yr **disease-specific survival** by histology: ITAC 94.4%, SCC 60.7%, ONB 85.7%
- Local recurrence rates comparable to open CFR for matched tumour stages

From Hanna et al. (2009) — 120 patients, MD Anderson:
- EEA alone: 93 patients (77.5%); cranioendoscopic: 27 patients (22.5%)
- 5-yr disease-specific survival 87%; 10-yr 80%
- Conclusion: endoscopic approach does not compromise oncologic outcomes in selected patients

## Endoscopic Medial Maxillectomy (EMM): Technique

EMM removes the medial wall of the maxillary sinus including the inferior turbinate, providing access to the maxillary sinus for tumours confined to its medial wall or inferior compartment.

### Instruments and Setup
- 0°, 30°, 45° rigid endoscopes (4 mm, 18 cm)
- Image-guided navigation (IGS) — mandatory for malignant cases
- Microdebrider, through-cutting forceps, powered drill with diamond burr
- Cottle elevator, Kerrison rongeurs

### Step-by-Step Technique

**Step 1 — Inferior turbinate resection**
- Submucosal infiltration with 1:100,000 adrenaline along inferior turbinate
- Incise anterior attachment of inferior turbinate with sickle knife or powered instrument
- Dislocate turbinate laterally; transect at posterior attachment
- Identify the nasolacrimal duct at its inferior meatal opening (nasolacrimal canal enters inferomedially)
- **Preserve nasolacrimal duct if uninvolved** — divide only if tumour extends to anterior medial wall

**Step 2 — Uncinate resection and middle meatal antrostomy**
- Standard uncinectomy with backbiting forceps or powered microdebrider
- Identify and widely open the maxillary ostium (middle meatal antrostomy)
- The natural ostium is located at the posterior fontanelle superior to the inferior turbinate attachment
- Extend antrostomy anteriorly and posteriorly to visualise the entire maxillary sinus floor and medial wall

**Step 3 — Anterior ethmoidectomy**
- Complete anterior and posterior ethmoidectomy to expose the skull base (fovea ethmoidalis), middle turbinate, and lamina papyracea
- Identify the basal lamella; remove to access posterior ethmoid cells if required
- This step is essential if tumour involves the ethmoid complex

**Step 4 — Medial maxillary wall resection**
- Identify the inferomedial angle of the maxillary sinus (junction of medial wall and floor)
- Using a powered drill with 3 mm cutting burr, thin the bone of the medial maxillary wall
- Through-cutting Blakesley forceps or Kerrison rongeurs to remove the thinned bone
- Extend resection from the nasolacrimal canal anteriorly to the posterior maxillary wall posteriorly
- Inferiorly, the floor of the nasal cavity forms the limit; the palate is preserved unless invaded
- This creates a wide communication between the nasal cavity and maxillary sinus (medial maxillectomy defect)

**Step 5 — Mucosal and subperiosteal resection**
- Tumour-involved mucosa is resected with a 5–10 mm mucosal margin
- If tumour abuts bone, drill the underlying bone to achieve clear osseous margins
- Frozen section margins: medial wall bone, nasolacrimal canal if involved, posterior wall if accessed
- For ITAC: the cribriform plate must be assessed; if involved, extend to endoscopic cribriform resection

**Step 6 — Haemostasis and reconstruction**
- Bipolar diathermy of ethmoidal arteries (anterior and posterior) at lamina papyracea
- Mucosal defects: allowed to granulate or lined with septal flap (nasoseptal flap, Hadad–Bassagaistegui) if dura exposed
- Absorbable haemostatic packing; dissolvable nasal splints

### Extent Variations

| Type | What Is Resected | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Type I EMM | Inferior turbinate + inferior meatal mucosa | Benign tumours of medial wall |
| Type II EMM | Medial wall + inferior turbinate | T1–T2 medial maxillary malignancies |
| Type III EMM (Total) | All walls except lateral + floor | T2–T3 maxillary malignancies; equivalent to Denker approach endoscopically |
| Type IVa | Orbital floor + medial wall | T3 with floor of orbit involvement |

## Endoscopic Craniofacial Resection (eCFR)

### Concept (Snyderman Modification)
Traditional CFR requires bifrontal craniotomy + transfacial approach. Snyderman and Kassam (2008) developed the **endoscopic craniofacial resection** — a purely transnasal, endoscopic removal of the cribriform plate, anterior skull base, olfactory bulbs, and involved dura, without a craniotomy in selected cases.

### Indications for eCFR
- T3 esthesioneuroblastoma (Kadish C without intracranial extension beyond dura)
- ITAC involving cribriform plate
- Sinonasal SCC with limited anterior skull base dural involvement
- ONB with intradural extension amenable to endoscopic margin

### Technique Principles
1. **Bilateral ethmoidectomy** — complete removal of all ethmoidal cells, exposing the skull base bilaterally
2. **Identification of the cribiform plate** — the cribiform plate sits medial to the fovea ethmoidalis; olfactory fibres pass through
3. **Circumferential osteotomy** — diamond burr drilling around the cribriform plate: posteriorly at planum sphenoidale, anteriorly at frontal sinus floor, laterally at fovea ethmoidalis
4. **Dural incision** — sharp dural incision circumferentially; if dura is invaded, an en bloc dural margin is taken
5. **Olfactory bulb resection** — both olfactory bulbs are resected with the specimen (anosmia is an accepted sequela)
6. **Reconstruction** — nasoseptal flap (Hadad–Bassagaistegui flap) is the workhorse; supplemented with free fat graft, dural substitute, and tissue glue; lumbar drain placed postoperatively for 3–5 days

### Combined Cranioendoscopic Approach
When intracranial extension exceeds what is safely managed endoscopically (e.g., orbital roof invasion, dural sinus involvement), a combined approach is used:
- **Neurosurgical component**: limited bifrontal or supraorbital craniotomy for intracranial resection
- **Endoscopic component**: transnasal resection of the sinonasal portion
- Both teams work simultaneously or sequentially ("two-team" approach)

## Reconstruction After Endoscopic Resection

| Defect | Preferred Reconstruction |
|---|---|
| Medial maxillary wall defect | Secondary intention / absorbable packing |
| Cribriform/anterior skull base defect | Nasoseptal flap (Hadad–Bassagaistegui) ± abdominal fat |
| Dural defect < 1 cm | Dural substitute + fibrin glue + nasoseptal flap |
| Dural defect > 1 cm | Inlay dural graft + onlay nasoseptal flap + lumbar drain |
| Total maxillectomy defect | Obturator prosthesis; rarely free flap endoscopically |

The **nasoseptal flap** (pedicled on the posterior nasoseptal artery, a branch of the sphenopalatine artery) is the most reliable vascularised reconstruction for skull base defects after endoscopic skull base surgery.

## Perioperative Management

### Preoperative
- **Staging**: MRI with gadolinium (soft tissue planes, dural/orbital/brain involvement); CT for bony anatomy
- **Nasal endoscopy + biopsy**: histologic confirmation mandatory before surgery
- **Ophthalmology assessment**: if orbital involvement suspected
- **Navigation planning**: load CT/MRI data; verify registration before incision
- **Multidisciplinary team meeting**: surgeon, radiation oncologist, medical oncologist, pathology, radiology

### Intraoperative
- Frozen section margin assessment is mandatory (medial, posterior, superior, deep margins)
- Haemostasis: anterior ethmoidal artery ligation/diathermy at skull base; sphenopalatine artery control
- Avoid monopolar near optic nerve and carotid; use bipolar at < 10 W

### Postoperative
- Lumbar drain 3–5 days if dura was opened
- Nasal saline irrigation from day 1 (mucociliary clearance)
- Endoscopic debridement at 1, 3, and 6 weeks post-op
- Adjuvant radiotherapy typically begins 4–6 weeks post-resection once wound healed
- Proton therapy preferred at skull base to spare brain and optic structures

---

## Adjuvant Treatment Integration

Endoscopic surgery in PNS malignancies is **rarely used alone**; it is part of a multimodal strategy:

- **Surgery + RT (PORT)**: standard for most resected sinonasal malignancies; 60–66 Gy to primary site
- **Surgery + CRT**: SCC with positive margins or N+ disease; cisplatin-based concurrent chemotherapy
- **Induction chemotherapy → surgery → RT**: investigational; used for SNUC and selected advanced ITAC
- **Proton therapy**: advantageous for skull base and orbital-sparing after eCFR; reduces late neurotoxicity

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **The correct question is not "can we do it endoscopically?" but "can we achieve negative margins endoscopically?"** — margin status is the dominant predictor of locoregional control; approach is secondary.
> 2. **Orbital periosteum (periorbita) involvement is not a contraindication to endoscopic resection** — the periorbita can be stripped endoscopically and the eye preserved; it is invasion of orbital fat that requires exenteration.
> 3. **ITAC is the ideal tumour for endoscopic resection** — it arises in the ethmoid/upper nasal cavity, is radiosensitive, and the Brescia series demonstrated 94% 5-year DSS with EEA + RT.
> 4. **Frozen section at the cribriform plate is mandatory** — a positive deep margin at the skull base dura is the most common reason for local failure in endoscopic skull base surgery.
> 5. **The nasoseptal flap must be elevated at the start of the case and preserved** — once the posterior nasal mucosa is violated during resection, the pedicle may be damaged and the flap unusable for skull base reconstruction.
> 6. **Image-guided navigation is not a substitute for anatomy** — navigation with CT/MRI fusion confirms boundaries, but registration error of 1–2 mm means the surgeon must continuously correlate landmarks with real-time anatomy.
> 7. **Anosmia is an expected and acceptable outcome of eCFR** — patients should be counselled preoperatively; for ITAC (woodworker's disease), smell is often already impaired at presentation.
