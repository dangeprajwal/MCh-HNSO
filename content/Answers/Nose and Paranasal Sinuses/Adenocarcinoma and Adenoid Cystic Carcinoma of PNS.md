## PART A: Sinonasal Adenocarcinoma

### Definition and Classification

Sinonasal adenocarcinoma encompasses a heterogeneous group of malignant epithelial tumours of the nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses with glandular differentiation. The **WHO 5th Edition (2022)** divides them into two broad categories based on histomorphology and aetiology:

| Category | Abbreviation | Aetiology | Primary Site |
|---|---|---|---|
| Intestinal-type adenocarcinoma | ITAC | Occupational (wood/leather dust) | Ethmoid > nasal cavity |
| Non-intestinal-type adenocarcinoma | NITAC | Sporadic | Maxillary > nasal cavity |

### Intestinal-Type Adenocarcinoma (ITAC)

#### Occupational Aetiology — IARC Group 1 Carcinogen
- **Hardwood dust (oak, beech)** is an **IARC Group 1 human carcinogen** specifically for sinonasal adenocarcinoma
- **Leather dust** (shoe and boot manufacture) — also Group 1 for sinonasal adenocarcinoma
- Relative risk for woodworkers (furniture, cabinet making): **500–900-fold** above background
- Latency period: typically **30–40 years** from first occupational exposure to diagnosis
- Mechanism: wood tannins (particularly beech/oak), fungal contaminants, and wood processing chemicals act as chemical carcinogens after mucociliary deposition in the ethmoid cleft
- **Mandatory occupational history** in all patients with ethmoid adenocarcinoma

#### WHO 2022 Histological Subtypes of ITAC

| Subtype | Features | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Papillary** | Well-formed papillae with fibrovascular cores; mild dysplasia | Best — 5-yr OS ~80% |
| **Colonic** | Most common (~40%); tubular/cribriform architecture identical to colorectal adenocarcinoma | Intermediate — 5-yr OS ~50% |
| **Solid** | Sheets of poorly differentiated cells; rare gland formation | Poor — 5-yr OS ~20% |
| **Mucinous** | Abundant extracellular mucin; signet-ring forms | Poor |
| **Mixed** | Two or more of the above | Intermediate |

#### Immunohistochemistry — Key Panel
| Marker | ITAC | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **CK20** | Positive | Lower GI-type expression |
| **CDX2** | Positive | Intestinal transcription factor; most reliable marker |
| **CK7** | Variable | Often positive (helps distinguish from metastatic colorectal) |
| **Villin** | Positive | Brush border protein |
| Exclude metastatic colorectal: | CK7+/CK20+/CDX2+ favours ITAC over metastatic | Clinical staging essential |

> **Critical diagnostic step:** All ITAC must be staged to exclude metastatic colorectal adenocarcinoma. Colonoscopy and abdominal CT are required before labelling a sinonasal glandular tumour as primary ITAC.

#### Molecular Features
- **KRAS mutations:** present in 40–60% of colonic-type ITAC (parallels colorectal cancer)
- **TP53 mutations:** associated with solid and mucinous subtypes
- **CDX2 expression:** driven by promoter hypomethylation; key pathogenic event
- No targetable driver mutation validated in prospective studies

#### Management of ITAC
1. **Surgery:** craniofacial resection (CFR) — open or endoscopic (eCFR) — is the cornerstone for resectable disease; aims at R0 margins including cribriform plate when dura is involved
2. **Adjuvant radiotherapy:** 60–66 Gy postoperatively, with concurrent cisplatin for high-risk features (positive margins, T4, dura involvement)
3. **Unresectable disease:** definitive CRT; response rates modest for solid/mucinous subtypes
4. **5-year OS by subtype:** papillary ~80%, colonic ~50%, solid ~20%

### Non-Intestinal-Type Adenocarcinoma (NITAC)

- Heterogeneous group; not linked to occupational exposure
- Subtypes: **tubular adenocarcinoma** (low-grade, well-differentiated), **papillary NITAC** (low-grade), **high-grade NOS**
- Low-grade NITAC: indolent behaviour, excellent prognosis after surgical excision
- High-grade NITAC: aggressive, resembles salivary-type adenocarcinoma NOS; treated as high-risk sinonasal malignancy
- IHC: CK7+, CDX2–, CK20–, mucin variable

## PART B: Sinonasal Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma (ACC)

### Definition and Origin

Sinonasal ACC arises from **submucosal minor salivary glands** lining the sinonasal mucosa. It is the **most common malignant minor salivary gland tumour** in the sinonasal region and shares identical molecular, histological, and behavioural features with ACC arising in major salivary glands.

### Sites of Origin

| Site | Frequency | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| Nasal cavity | Most common sinonasal site | Hard and soft palate also common |
| Maxillary sinus | Second | |
| Nasopharynx | Less common | Submucosal glands of nasopharyngeal mucosa |
| Ethmoid/sphenoid | Least common | |

### Histological Patterns

| Pattern | Architecture | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| **Cribriform** | Swiss cheese holes; classic | Intermediate |
| **Tubular** | Well-formed tubules | Best prognosis |
| **Solid** | Sheets of basaloid cells | Worst prognosis; >30% solid = high grade |

### Molecular Biology: MYB-NFIB Fusion

- **t(6;9)(q22–23;p23–24)** translocation → **MYB-NFIB chimeric fusion** present in **~85%** of ACC
- MYB: proto-oncogene transcription factor; deletion of its 3′-UTR removes miR-15a/16 and miR-150 binding sites → unregulated MYB overexpression
- NFIB: nuclear factor driving ACC-specific transcriptional programme
- Fusion is **diagnostic** and can be detected by FISH, RT-PCR, or RNA-seq
- Therapeutic implication: MYB is not directly druggable; downstream targets (CDK4/6, BET) are under clinical investigation

### Biological Behaviour

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Growth rate** | Slow; deceptively indolent on examination |
| **Perineural invasion (PNI)** | 50–90%; cardinal feature; responsible for pain, facial numbness, skip lesions |
| **Skip lesions** | Tumour cells along nerve sheaths far from gross tumour margin; necessitate wide nerve-bearing resection |
| **Lymph node metastasis** | Uncommon early; ~15–20% overall |
| **Distant metastasis** | Lung most common (>40% of patients develop pulmonary mets); liver, bone |
| **Timing of distant mets** | Late — often 5–15 years after initial treatment |
| **Local recurrence** | Common due to PNI and skip lesions |

### Perineural Spread Routes in Sinonasal ACC

The anatomical perineural pathway is critical for surgical planning and radiation target volume design:

```
Sinonasal tumour
        ↓ (palatine nerves)
Greater palatine foramen
        ↓
Pterygopalatine fossa (PPF)
        ↓ (CN V2 — infraorbital / posterior superior alveolar nerves)
Foramen rotundum
        ↓
Gasserian (trigeminal) ganglion
        ↓
Cavernous sinus → intracranial
```

Additional routes:
- Anterior ethmoidal nerve → anterior cranial fossa
- Vidian nerve (nerve of pterygoid canal) → cavernous sinus
- Olfactory nerve bundle → cribriform plate

### Imaging

| Modality | Key Findings |
|---|---|
| MRI T2 | **Low signal** — characteristic of ACC due to hyalinised/sclerotic stroma (opposite of most carcinomas); hypercellular solid pattern also low T2 |
| MRI T1+Gad | Perineural enhancement tracking named nerves; fat obliteration in PPF; cavernous sinus involvement |
| CT | Bone remodelling/erosion at skull base; widening of foramina (foramen rotundum, foramen ovale) suggesting perineural spread |
| MRI orbits | Orbital periosteum/extraconal fat involvement |

> **Radiological sign:** Fat obliteration in the **pterygopalatine fossa** on T1 MRI indicates perineural spread into PPF — a finding that upstages disease and mandates extended surgery or inclusion in the radiation target volume.

### Management

#### Surgery
- **Wide local resection tracking named nerves** — the operating principle; frozen-section confirmation of clear neural margins
- If PPF involved: **infratemporal fossa dissection** with removal of PPF contents and foramen rotundum tracking
- If cavernous sinus involved: typically unresectable; skull base surgery with palliative intent or definitive RT
- **Orbital exenteration** only if periorbita is breached (confirmed on MRI and intraoperative frozen section)
- Neck dissection: selective (level I–III) if clinically N+ or for access; elective neck dissection debated (low regional LN rate)

#### Adjuvant Radiotherapy
- **High-dose RT (≥66 Gy)** is mandatory post-resection — even with histologically clear margins, given PNI and skip lesion biology
- **Target volumes must include named nerve pathways:** PPF, foramen rotundum, trigeminal ganglion, and Meckel's cave as minimum
- **Particle therapy (proton/carbon ions):** preferred when technically feasible; carbon ion RT shows improved local control for sinonasal ACC at centres with access
- Definitive RT for unresectable disease: 70–74 Gy equivalent

#### Systemic Therapy
- **c-Kit (CD117) expression:** positive in >80% of ACC — initially exciting for targeted therapy
- **Imatinib (c-Kit inhibitor):** disappointing — phase II studies showed minimal response despite c-Kit positivity; expression does not correlate with kinase dependence
- **Lenvatinib, axitinib (VEGFR inhibitors):** partial responses in recurrent/metastatic ACC; phase II data
- **Clinical trials:** BET bromodomain inhibitors (targeting MYB downstream), notch inhibitors, CDK4/6 inhibitors
- **Chemotherapy for metastatic disease:** cisplatin-based regimens; modest response rates; primarily palliative

## PART C: HPV in Sinonasal Adenocarcinoma and ACC

### Current Evidence

| Tumour Type | HPV Evidence | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Sinonasal SCC** | HPV 16/18 in 20–40%; p16 overexpression in subset | Some p16+ sinonasal SCCs behave like HPV-driven oropharyngeal SCC; staging with AJCC 8th Edition HPV criteria does NOT apply to sinonasal SCC |
| **ITAC** | HPV not a significant aetiological factor | Wood/leather dust dominates; HPV testing not indicated |
| **NITAC** | Isolated case reports of HPV DNA; no systematic evidence | No clinical role for HPV testing |
| **Sinonasal ACC** | HPV DNA detected in 10–20% in some series | Unclear causality — likely passenger; MYB-NFIB fusion defines ACC irrespective of HPV status |

### Why HPV is Not Clinically Relevant in Sinonasal ACC
1. **MYB-NFIB fusion** is the defining molecular event — independent of viral status
2. HPV-positive sinonasal ACC cases do not show clinical behaviour different from HPV-negative cases
3. ACC lacks the tonsillar crypt epithelium (target for oropharyngeal HPV infection) — no analogous permissive epithelial niche in sinonasal mucosa
4. **Current international guidelines** do not recommend routine HPV testing in sinonasal ACC outside clinical trials

> **Examination answer key point:** HPV is causally linked to oropharyngeal SCC (tonsillar/BOT); in sinonasal ACC, HPV DNA may be detectable but is not aetiologically or therapeutically relevant. The MYB-NFIB fusion is the pathognomonic molecular signature of ACC.

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Sinonasal adenocarcinomas are divided into intestinal-type (ITAC) and non-intestinal-type (NITAC); ITAC is strongly and specifically linked to occupational hardwood (oak, beech) and leather dust exposure — an IARC Group 1 occupational carcinogen with a 500–900-fold relative risk — and the colonic subtype is the most common, histologically and immunohistochemically resembling colorectal adenocarcinoma (CDX2+, CK20+), requiring exclusion of metastatic disease before labelling as primary ITAC. Sinonasal ACC is the most common malignant minor salivary gland tumour in the sinonasal region and is defined by the MYB-NFIB fusion (t(6;9)), which drives MYB overexpression; its cardinal clinical features are perineural invasion (50–90%), skip lesions along named nerves, and late pulmonary metastasis. Management of sinonasal ACC requires wide surgery tracking perineural pathways through the pterygopalatine fossa and foramen rotundum, followed by high-dose adjuvant RT (≥66 Gy) covering named nerve routes; c-Kit expression is common but imatinib is not effective. HPV has no established causal role in sinonasal ACC; the MYB-NFIB fusion is the molecular hallmark and HPV testing is not indicated in routine clinical practice for ACC.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Hardwood dust (oak, beech)** is an IARC Group 1 carcinogen for sinonasal adenocarcinoma — a direct occupational causation that must be documented in the history and is medicolegally important.
> 2. **CDX2 and CK20 positivity** in a sinonasal glandular tumour must prompt colonoscopy and CT abdomen to exclude metastatic colorectal adenocarcinoma before diagnosing primary ITAC.
> 3. The **papillary subtype of ITAC** carries the best prognosis (~80% 5-year OS); the **solid subtype** the worst (~20%) — histological subtyping drives prognostication.
> 4. **Fat obliteration in the pterygopalatine fossa on T1 MRI** is the key imaging sign of perineural spread in sinonasal ACC — always review the PPF in any sinonasal mass with facial pain or numbness.
> 5. **Low T2 signal** in a sinonasal mass suggests ACC (hyalinised stroma) or lymphoma — most carcinomas are intermediate-to-high T2; ACC's low T2 is a useful MRI discriminator.
> 6. **Imatinib does not work in ACC** despite c-Kit positivity — c-Kit is a diagnostic IHC marker, not a therapeutic target in ACC.
> 7. The **MYB-NFIB fusion** is detectable by FISH on paraffin-embedded tissue and is both diagnostic and a potential future therapeutic target; FISH for MYB rearrangement should be requested when ACC is suspected but morphology is unusual.
> 8. **Perineural spread to cavernous sinus** from sinonasal ACC typically renders the tumour unresectable — the PPF and foramen rotundum are the surgical point of no return.
> 9. Sinonasal ACC has a **long natural history** — distant metastases (lung) occur 5–15 years after initial treatment; patients require lifetime follow-up with annual chest CT.
> 10. **HPV is not aetiologically relevant in sinonasal ACC** — the question "HPV association with sinonasal ACC" is a negative answer: MYB-NFIB fusion is the defining oncogenic event, not viral infection.
