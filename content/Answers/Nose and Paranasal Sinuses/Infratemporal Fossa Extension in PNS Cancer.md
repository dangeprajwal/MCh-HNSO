## Infratemporal Fossa Extension in Paranasal Sinus and Oral Cavity Cancer

### Surgical Anatomy of the Infratemporal Fossa

The infratemporal fossa (ITF) is a deep, irregular space medial to the ramus of the mandible and posterior to the maxilla, which connects the skull base to the parapharyngeal space inferiorly. It has no well-defined inferior boundary, opening directly into the parapharyngeal space.

#### Boundaries

| Wall | Structure |
|---|---|
| Superior (roof) | Greater wing of sphenoid, squamous part of temporal bone (infratemporal surface) |
| Medial | Lateral pterygoid plate, superior pharyngeal constrictor, tensor veli palatini |
| Lateral | Medial surface of the mandibular ramus (ascending ramus and coronoid process) |
| Anterior | Posterior wall of the maxillary sinus (infratemporal surface of maxilla) |
| Posterior | Styloid process, styloid muscles, carotid sheath |
| Inferior | Open — continuous with parapharyngeal space |

#### Communications
- **Pterygomaxillary fissure**: connects ITF to pterygopalatine fossa (PPF) anteriorly — critical corridor for tumour spread from maxillary sinus to ITF
- **Inferior orbital fissure**: connects PPF to orbit — route for orbital invasion
- **Foramen ovale**: V3 exits skull base at foramen ovale to enter ITF — route for perineural retrograde spread to middle cranial fossa
- **Foramen spinosum**: middle meningeal artery traverses — surgical landmark

#### Key Contents

| Structure | Surgical Relevance |
|---|---|
| Medial pterygoid muscle | Invaded in advanced maxillary sinus cancer and retromolar trigone cancer |
| Lateral pterygoid muscle | Invaded with pterygomaxillary fissure extension; attachment to condyle |
| Pterygoid venous plexus | Major source of haemorrhage during ITF dissection; anastomoses with cavernous sinus |
| Maxillary artery (internal maxillary artery, IMAX) | Enters ITF from ECA; gives off middle meningeal, inferior alveolar, buccal, sphenopalatine branches |
| Mandibular nerve (V3) | Enters ITF through foramen ovale; gives off: inferior alveolar (IAN), lingual, buccal, auriculotemporal nerves, and chorda tympani |
| Inferior alveolar nerve (IAN) | Enters mandibular canal at lingula; retrograde perineural spread from oral cavity cancer |
| Chorda tympani | Branch of facial nerve joining lingual nerve in ITF; taste fibres |
| Otic ganglion | Parasympathetic ganglion medial to V3 in ITF |

### Routes of Tumour Extension to the ITF

#### From Maxillary Sinus (PNS Cancer)
1. **Posterior wall of maxillary sinus → pterygomaxillary fissure → ITF**: the most direct route; T4a classification when posterior wall involved; T4b when pterygoid muscles are invaded
2. **Pterygoid plate erosion**: tumour erodes the lateral pterygoid plate and enters the medial ITF (pterygoid muscles)
3. **Inferior orbital fissure → orbit → ITF**: less direct; involves orbital invasion with secondary ITF infiltration
4. **Through floor of sphenoid sinus → sphenoid body → parasellar ITF**: advanced Stage IV disease

#### From Oral Cavity Cancer
1. **Retromolar trigone cancer**: direct posterior extension to the pterygopalatine raphe, medial pterygoid, and pterygomaxillary space
2. **Masticator space invasion**: retromolar trigone and posterior oral cavity tumours spread through the buccinator–pterygomandibular raphe into the masticator space, which overlaps anatomically with the ITF
3. **Mandibular ramus erosion**: cortical erosion of the ascending ramus exposes the ITF directly
4. **Perineural spread along inferior alveolar nerve**: tumour spreads retrograde along the IAN from the mandibular canal to the mandibular foramen (lingula) → into the ITF → retrograde along V3 → through foramen ovale → middle cranial fossa (perineural intracranial extension)
5. **Along lingual nerve**: less common; retrograde from floor of mouth to ITF

#### Staging Implications
- Maxillary sinus cancer: **T4a** = involvement of orbital floor, skin of cheek, pterygoid plates, infratemporal fossa, cribriform plate, floor/medial wall of orbit; **T4b** = orbital apex, dura, brain, middle cranial fossa, nasopharynx, clivus
- Oral cavity cancer (retromolar trigone, buccal mucosa): **T4a** = masticator space/pterygoid muscles = functionally unresectable or major surgery; **T4b** = masticator space with skull base or pterygoid plate invasion OR encasement of ICA

### Pre-operative Assessment

#### MRI (Primary Modality)
- Coronal and axial T1 fat-sat + gadolinium: assess pterygoid muscle signal change (replacement of normal muscle fat signal); enhance-enhancing tumour within ITF
- T1 along V3 nerve: enhancement tracking through foramen ovale into Meckel's cave = intracranial perineural spread; confirms T4b disease
- Cavernous sinus: enhancement or expansion = T4b (unresectable in most centres)
- Orbital fat: T2 fat-suppressed MRI to assess periorbitum integrity
- ICA: MRI angiography or CTA for circumferential encasement (>270°)

#### CT (Bone Algorithm)
- Pterygoid plate erosion pattern
- Foramen ovale enlargement (perineural spread marker)
- Skull base foraminal changes: widened foramen ovale or spinosum
- Mandible involvement: cortical erosion vs periosteal involvement only

#### CT Angiography / DSA
- ICA involvement: circumferential encasement >270° = unresectable (ICA cannot be sacrificed without reconstruction in most cases)
- **Balloon occlusion test (BOT)**: if ICA at risk of sacrifice during resection, BOT assesses cerebrovascular reserve — patient tolerates balloon inflation × 30 minutes with neurological monitoring; combined with SPECT or Xenon-CT for perfusion; if tolerates → ICA sacrifice may be survivable; if fails → revascularisation required (ECA–ICA bypass) before sacrifice

#### PET-CT
- Regional nodal staging (occult nodal disease in ~20% of T4 maxillary sinus cancer at presentation)
- Distant metastases (lung, bone, liver — most common sites)
- Assessment of response to induction chemotherapy

### Surgical Approaches to the Infratemporal Fossa

#### Approach 1: Total Maxillectomy with Deliberate ITF Dissection
**Most common approach for PNS cancer with limited ITF extension**
- Standard total maxillectomy (Weber-Ferguson incision; see Maxillectomy file) with deliberate posterior extension
- After removal of the maxilla, the posterior dissection is carried into the pterygomaxillary fissure under direct vision
- Pterygoid muscles identified; if invaded → resected as part of specimen (medial and/or lateral pterygoid)
- IMAX ligated in the pterygomaxillary fissure to reduce bleeding
- Pterygoid plates removed if invaded (osteotome or drill)
- This approach provides excellent access to the anterior and medial ITF
- **Limitation**: access to the superior ITF (foramen ovale, skull base, ICA) is restricted

#### Approach 2: Preauricular Subtemporal (Infratemporal) Approach
**For isolated or dominant ITF disease, including advanced JNA and skull base tumours**
- Skin incision: preauricular crease extending into temporal hairline (hemicoronal component)
- Subperiosteal dissection over temporal bone, zygomatic arch, and masseter
- **Zygomatic arch osteotomy**: arch divided anteriorly (near zygomaticomaxillary suture) and posteriorly (at temporal root); arch + masseter reflected inferiorly
- **Coronoid osteotomy** (optional): coronoid process divided with oscillating saw; temporalis muscle reflected — improves access to lateral ITF
- Exposure of the entire lateral and superior ITF: lateral pterygoid, V3, foramen ovale, middle meningeal artery, IMAX, mandibular condyle
- **Condyle resection** if invaded or for maximal exposure: resection of condylar head with TMJ disarticulation
- Reconstruction: zygomatic arch re-plated; condylar reconstruction with prosthesis or free fibula if condyle resected
- **Advantage**: wide access to ITF, skull base, and infratemporal surface of sphenoid without entering the cranial cavity

#### Approach 3: Transmandibular (Mandibulotomy) Approach
- Division of the mandibular ramus (ascending ramus osteotomy) or condyle resection with or without body osteotomy
- Swing the mandible inferiorly or laterally to expose the ITF and parapharyngeal space
- Major functional deficit: TMJ reconstruction, mandibular reconstruction required
- Indicated for massive oral cavity cancer with ITF and parapharyngeal space invasion

#### Approach 4: Fisch Infratemporal Fossa Approach (Types A–D)
- Primarily for lateral skull base tumours (paraganglioma, meningioma, neurofibroma)
- **Type A**: jugular foramen and ICA — facial nerve anterior transposition; most relevant for glomus jugulare
- **Type B**: ITF/clivus — zygomatic arch osteotomy, condyle displacement; access to ITF and anterior clivus
- **Type C**: anterior ITF and cavernous sinus — for tumours of the nasopharynx and cavernous sinus
- Type C is the variant most relevant to maxillary sinus cancer with intracranial ITF extension

#### Approach 5: Endoscopic-Assisted ITF Dissection
- For medial ITF and pterygopalatine fossa: endoscopic transmaxillary or transnasal approach to the PPF and medial ITF under endoscopic vision
- Limitations: pterygoid venous plexus makes haemostasis challenging; access to lateral ITF and condyle is limited
- Role: adjunct to open surgery for residual disease or surveillance biopsies

### Resectability Criteria in ITF-Involved PNS Cancer

| Feature | Resectable | Unresectable |
|---|---|---|
| Pterygoid muscle invasion | Yes (T4a) | — |
| Pterygoid plate erosion | Yes (T4a) | — |
| Lateral pterygoid root at skull base | Borderline (discuss at MDT) | Skull base involvement with fixed tumour |
| Foramen ovale involvement (perineural) | Yes (requires nerve sacrifice, T4) | Intracranial dura beyond Meckel's cave |
| ICA encasement | <180°: likely resectable with vascular prep | ≥270°: unresectable without bypass |
| Cavernous sinus involvement | Controversial — select centres attempt resection | Standard: unresectable; consider CRT ± bevacizumab |
| Bilateral pterygoid/ITF invasion | Unresectable (bilateral masticatory dysfunction) | — |
| Orbital apex involvement | Unresectable (optic nerve sacrifice unacceptable) | — |

---

### Reconstruction After ITF Resection

| Defect | Reconstruction |
|---|---|
| ITF dead space obliteration | Temporalis muscle transposition — reliable pedicled flap fills ITF dead space, reduces infection risk |
| Mandibular condyle resection | Total joint prosthesis (titanium condylar prosthesis) or free fibula osteocutaneous flap for complex mandibulectomy |
| Soft tissue skin/cheek defect | ALT (anterolateral thigh) free flap — thin and versatile; or pectoralis major pedicled flap |
| Cranial base dural defect | Pericranial flap, fascia lata, nasoseptal flap (if available) |
| Orbital floor (if resected) | Titanium mesh or porous polyethylene implant |

### Adjuvant Treatment

#### Radiation Therapy
- Post-operative RT is standard for T4 PNS and oral cavity cancer with ITF involvement
- **Target volumes** must encompass:
  - Primary tumour bed
  - Entire ITF (including pterygoid muscles, PTF, pterygoid plates)
  - **Perineural routes to skull base**: V3 from inferior alveolar nerve to foramen ovale; Meckel's cave if V3 MRI enhancement was present
  - Regional nodal levels (ipsilateral Ib, II, III, IV; bilateral if midline or near-midline tumour)
- Dose: 60–66 Gy to tumour bed (positive margin or ECE); 54–60 Gy to elective nodal volumes
- IMRT mandatory to spare contralateral parotid, spinal cord, brainstem

#### Chemotherapy
- Concurrent cisplatin (100 mg/m² q3 weeks × 3 cycles or weekly 40 mg/m²) with RT for positive margins, nodal disease, or adverse pathological features
- Histology-specific regimens if different tumour type (SNUC → platinum-etoposide; ACC → RT alone preferred)

---

### Prognosis

- T4a maxillary sinus SCC (including ITF invasion, pterygoid involvement): 5-year OS approximately **30–45%** with multimodality treatment (surgery + adjuvant CRT)
- T4b (cavernous sinus, ICA, skull base dura): 5-year OS approximately **10–20%** with CRT; surgery rarely curative
- Oral cavity cancer with masticator space/ITF invasion: 5-year OS approximately **25–35%** with multimodality treatment
- Perineural intracranial spread (V3 to Meckel's cave on MRI): significantly worsens prognosis; 5-year OS approximately **15–25%**

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The infratemporal fossa is a deep space medial to the mandibular ramus and posterior to the maxilla, containing the pterygoid muscles, pterygoid venous plexus, internal maxillary artery, and the mandibular nerve (V3) with its branches including the inferior alveolar nerve — which provides the principal perineural pathway for retrograde spread to the skull base and intracranial compartment through foramen ovale. ITF invasion constitutes T4a disease for maxillary sinus cancer when confined to pterygoid muscles and pterygomaxillary fissure. Pre-operative MRI defines pterygoid muscle involvement, V3 enhancement tracking to foramen ovale, and cavernous sinus involvement; CT angiography and balloon occlusion testing assess ICA encasement resectability. The most common surgical approach combines total maxillectomy with deliberate posterior dissection into the ITF, supplemented by a preauricular subtemporal approach when superior or lateral ITF access is required. Unresectable features include ICA circumferential encasement (>270°), bilateral ITF invasion, and cavernous sinus involvement at most centres. Post-operative RT must encompass the full ITF volume and perineural pathways to the skull base.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The pterygomaxillary fissure is the critical corridor connecting the posterior maxillary sinus to the ITF — widening or erosion on CT is the radiological sign of early ITF involvement in maxillary sinus cancer.
> 2. Foramen ovale enlargement on CT or V3 enhancement on gadolinium MRI indicates perineural intracranial spread — this changes staging to T4b and fundamentally alters the radiation target volume.
> 3. ICA encasement >270° on CT angiography is the principal unresectability criterion for ITF cancer; balloon occlusion testing is used to assess cerebrovascular reserve if ICA sacrifice is anticipated.
> 4. The temporalis muscle transposition flap is the workhorse for ITF dead space obliteration after maxillectomy — it prevents infection, CSF leak containment, and provides a vascularised bed for healing.
> 5. The preauricular subtemporal approach with zygomatic arch osteotomy provides the widest access to the lateral and superior ITF (foramen ovale, skull base, condyle) without requiring a full craniotomy.
> 6. Pterygoid venous plexus haemorrhage is the principal intraoperative hazard of ITF dissection — pre-operative embolisation (for vascular tumours) or meticulous bipolar coagulation is essential.
> 7. Post-operative RT target volumes must explicitly include the pterygoid muscles, PTF, foramen ovale, and Meckel's cave whenever ITF involvement or V3 perineural spread is demonstrated.
> 8. Bilateral ITF invasion renders the patient unresectable because bilateral pterygoid muscle resection causes permanent trismus and masticatory dysfunction incompatible with quality of life.
> 9. The masticator space (masseteric, pterygoid, infratemporal) is contiguous, and involvement of any one muscle compartment warrants systematic evaluation of the whole space — isolated pterygoid involvement is often the "leading edge" of a more extensive masticator space invasion.
> 10. 5-year OS for T4a maxillary sinus SCC with ITF involvement is approximately 30–45% with multimodality treatment — ITF involvement is therefore not a contraindication to resection but mandates complete MDT planning.
