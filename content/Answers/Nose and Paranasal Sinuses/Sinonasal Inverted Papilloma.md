> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Krouse (2000) published the first widely adopted staging system for sinonasal inverted papilloma, dividing disease into four categories (T1–T4) based on anatomical extent and presence of malignancy, which allowed meaningful comparison of outcomes between endoscopic and open surgical series and remains the standard classification referenced in all contemporary studies. Lawson, Kaufman, and Biller (2003) analysed 160 patients with inverted papilloma across a 20-year period, demonstrating that endoscopic resection achieves recurrence rates comparable to open approaches when the attachment site is fully addressed; the study also confirmed a 10–12% rate of synchronous or metachronous squamous cell carcinoma. Viitasalo et al. (2023) reported a population-based cohort showing that the cumulative incidence of malignant transformation in sinonasal inverted papilloma reaches 7.1% at 10 years, with HPV 16/18 infection and high-grade dysplasia identified as the strongest independent predictors of transformation, reinforcing the need for long-term endoscopic surveillance.

## Definition and Classification

**Sinonasal inverted papilloma (IP)** is a benign epithelial neoplasm derived from the **Schneiderian membrane** — the ectodermally derived respiratory epithelium lining the nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses. Its hallmark is an **endophytic (inverted) growth pattern** in which surface epithelium proliferates into the underlying stroma rather than outward, producing characteristic papillary projections that invert into the lamina propria.

### Three Types of Schneiderian Papilloma

| Type | Synonym | Proportion | Growth Pattern | Malignant Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Inverted** | Endophytic | 62% | Inverted into stroma | 5–15% |
| **Oncocytic** | Cylindrical cell | 18% | Mixed exo/endophytic | 4–17% (highest) |
| **Exophytic** | Fungiform / Septal | 20% | Outward from septum | <1% |

## Epidemiology

- **Sex:** M:F = 3:1
- **Age:** Peak in 5th–7th decade
- **Laterality:** Almost exclusively **unilateral**; bilateral disease suggests malignant transformation or synchronous separate lesions
- **Incidence:** 0.5–4 per 100,000 per year

## Aetiopathogenesis

### HPV Association
- HPV identified in **20–40%** of cases by PCR (lower by in-situ hybridisation)
- **Low-risk types (HPV 6, 11):** associated with recurrence risk
- **High-risk types (HPV 16, 18):** associated with **malignant transformation**; present in majority of IP-associated SCC
- Causality versus passenger status remains controversial; WHO 2022 acknowledges the association without confirming an obligate oncogenic role

### Other Aetiological Factors
| Factor | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Chronic sinusitis | Correlated in population studies; may share inflammatory milieu |
| Allergy / atopy | Possible but inconsistent association |
| Tobacco smoking | Increased risk; carcinogen-associated mutagenesis |
| Occupational chemical exposure | Wood dust, formaldehyde (some data) |
| Trauma / nasal surgery | Hypothetical epithelial displacement |

### Molecular Pathway to Malignancy
- **EGFR mutation / overexpression** — early change in dysplasia
- **p53 mutation** — found in high-grade dysplasia and IP-SCC
- **Ki-67 overexpression** — proliferative index correlates with grade
- **HPV 16/18 → E6/E7 oncoprotein** → p53 degradation and pRb inactivation
- **LOH 9p, 3p** — tumour suppressor loci lost in transformation

## Sites of Origin

| Site | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral nasal wall (middle meatus, uncinate process) | Most common primary site | Attachment here in ~70% |
| Maxillary sinus | Most common sinus involved | Often secondary spread from lateral wall |
| Ethmoid sinus | Second most common sinus | |
| Frontal sinus | Less common; endoscopic access challenging | |
| Sphenoid sinus | Least common; associated with worse prognosis |
| Nasal septum | Rare; when present, more often exophytic type |

## Malignant Transformation

- **Synchronous SCC** (present at time of IP diagnosis): 5–10%
- **Metachronous SCC** (arising during follow-up): 3–7%
- **Combined rate:** 5–15%
- **High-grade dysplasia / carcinoma in situ** in IP without invasion: 10–20%

### Predictors of Malignant Transformation
1. High-grade dysplasia on initial pathology
2. HPV 16 or HPV 18 positivity
3. p53 mutation
4. Elevated Ki-67 (>15%)
5. Krouse T3 or T4 disease (extensive disease at presentation)
6. Recurrent IP (each recurrence increases risk)

## Clinical Features

| Symptom | Frequency | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| Unilateral nasal obstruction | Most common | Gradual, progressive |
| Epistaxis | Common | Friable vascular stroma |
| Anosmia | Variable | With extensive nasal involvement |
| Rhinorrhoea | Common | Serous or mucoid |
| Facial pain / pressure | Sinus involvement | |
| Proptosis / epiphora | Orbital extension | Suggests T3–T4 |
| Visual loss | Orbital apex involvement | Suggests malignancy |
| Headache | Frontal/sphenoid involvement |

## Imaging

### CT (Computed Tomography)
- **Unilateral** enhancing polypoid soft-tissue mass
- **Bone remodelling** more common than frank erosion (pushed bone)
- Frank bone **erosion** raises suspicion for malignancy
- Calcification (dystrophic / osseous metaplasia) — uncommon but pathognomonic when present
- Maxillary sinus opacification with medial wall bowing
- **Focal hyperostosis** at the site of epithelial attachment — surgically important landmark

### MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)
- **T2 / T1 post-contrast:** **Convoluted cerebriform (columnar) pattern** — alternating bands of high and low signal — is **characteristic and pathognomonic**; reflects the inverted epithelial fronds separated by oedematous stroma
- Distinguishes IP from simple polyps (which lack the cerebriform pattern) and from inflammatory mucosal disease
- Essential for:
  - Defining orbital and skull base involvement
  - Detecting intracranial extension
  - Evaluating perineural spread if malignancy suspected
  - Post-operative surveillance for recurrence

### PET-CT
- Not routine for benign IP
- Indicated when malignant transformation is suspected (high FDG uptake favours SCC)

## Krouse Staging System (2000)

| Stage | Description | Typical Management |
|---|---|---|
| **T1** | Limited to nasal cavity (any one wall or portion); no sinus involvement | Endoscopic resection |
| **T2** | Extends to medial wall of maxillary sinus and/or ethmoid sinuses | Endoscopic medial maxillectomy ± anterior ethmoidectomy |
| **T3** | Involves lateral, inferior, anterior, or posterior walls of maxillary sinus; frontal or sphenoid sinus | EMM ± Draf III or open approaches for frontal/sphenoid |
| **T4** | Any extrasinonasal extension (orbit, skull base, intracranial) or malignancy (SCC) | Open craniofacial ± adjuvant therapy |

## Surgical Management

### Principle: Complete Excision of the Epithelial Attachment Site
The single most important factor predicting recurrence is failure to identify and completely excise the **mucosal attachment (stalk)** — which corresponds to the area of focal hyperostosis on CT.

### Endoscopic Medial Maxillectomy (EMM)
- **Current standard of care** for T1, T2, and selected T3 disease
- Components of EMM:
  1. Uncinectomy and maxillary antrostomy
  2. Resection of the medial maxillary wall (inferior turbinate, medial maxillary mucosa)
  3. Drilling of the bony attachment site
  4. Clearance of ipsilateral ethmoids if involved
- **Sturm classification** defines extent of endoscopic approach based on attachment site (nasal cavity, medial maxillary wall, full medial/lateral maxillary wall, frontal recess, sphenoid)

### Endoscopic Modified Medial Maxillectomy (EMMM) / Prelacrimal Approach
- Access to anterior, inferior, and lateral recesses of maxillary sinus
- Avoids skin incision; good access for T3 lateral wall disease

### Open Approaches (When Indicated)
| Approach | Indication |
|---|---|
| Caldwell-Luc | Lateral, inferior wall attachment not accessible endoscopically |
| Midfacial degloving | Extensive maxillary, bilateral, or recurrent disease |
| Lateral rhinotomy (with or without medial maxillectomy) | T3–T4 with orbital extension; failed endoscopic |
| Craniofacial resection | T4 with skull base or intracranial extension (in context of SCC) |

### Frontal Sinus IP
- **Draf IIb or Draf III (modified Lothrop):** for isolated frontal IP accessible endoscopically
- **Osteoplastic flap with or without obliteration:** for IP involving lateral frontal sinus recesses not accessible endoscopically

### Malignant Transformation (IP-SCC)
- Treat as primary **SCC of the paranasal sinus** regardless of origin in IP
- **Surgery:** craniofacial resection or endoscopic craniofacial resection for resectable disease
- **Adjuvant CRT:** for high-risk features (positive/close margins, perineural invasion, bone erosion, nodal disease)
- **Prognosis:** 5-year OS 50–70% for early-stage IP-SCC vs 20–40% for advanced disease

## Recurrence

| Variable | Data |
|---|---|
| Overall recurrence after endoscopic surgery | 10–20% |
| After open surgery | 8–15% (historically cited as lower) |
| Recurrence rate T1–T2 vs T3–T4 | T3–T4 approximately double the risk |
| Median time to recurrence | 18–36 months |
| Management | Re-excision (endoscopic preferred if feasible); assess for malignant transformation |

## Follow-Up Protocol

- **Nasal endoscopy:** 3 months, 6 months, 12 months, then annually for minimum 5 years
- **MRI:** if clinical or endoscopic suspicion of recurrence or malignancy
- **Biopsy:** any suspicious mucosal change at attachment site
- Long-term surveillance justified by late recurrence (up to 10+ years) and metachronous SCC risk

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Sinonasal inverted papilloma is a benign Schneiderian neoplasm with endophytic epithelial growth, occurring predominantly in middle-aged men, almost always unilateral, and arising most commonly from the lateral nasal wall with secondary maxillary sinus involvement. Its clinical significance lies in a 5–15% risk of synchronous or metachronous squamous cell carcinoma, driven by HPV 16/18 infection, p53 mutation, and high-grade dysplasia. Imaging hallmarks include focal bony hyperostosis at the attachment site on CT and the convoluted cerebriform pattern on MRI T2, which guides surgical planning. Krouse staging (T1–T4) stratifies extent of disease and guides the surgical approach, with endoscopic medial maxillectomy now the standard for T1–T3, and open or combined craniofacial approaches reserved for T4 or inaccessible disease. Recurrence occurs in 10–20% and mandates long-term endoscopic surveillance with a low threshold for re-excision and biopsy.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The **convoluted cerebriform pattern on MRI T2** is pathognomonic for inverted papilloma and reliably distinguishes it from inflammatory polyps — report its presence when describing an IP.
> 2. **Focal hyperostosis on CT** marks the epithelial attachment site and is the critical surgical landmark; drilling this bone is mandatory to prevent recurrence.
> 3. **HPV 16/18 positivity** in IP is the strongest molecular predictor of malignant transformation and justifies intensified surveillance.
> 4. **Synchronous SCC** is present in up to 10% of IP at presentation; all IPs must have representative biopsies before planning definitive surgery.
> 5. Krouse **T4 disease** by definition includes either extrasinonasal extension or malignancy — a T4 designation always prompts staging work-up and multidisciplinary discussion.
> 6. **Unilateral** nasal mass is the cardinal clinical feature; bilateral polyposis in the same patient is almost always inflammatory, not IP.
> 7. The **Caldwell-Luc approach** is no longer the gold standard — endoscopic medial maxillectomy provides equivalent or superior oncological control with reduced morbidity.
> 8. **Frontal sinus IP** carries the highest recurrence risk; Draf III (modified Lothrop) procedure is preferred to ensure complete attachment site visualisation and drilling.
> 9. After endoscopic resection, **nasal endoscopy at 3, 6, 12 months then annually for 5 years** is the minimum surveillance — late recurrence beyond 5 years and metachronous SCC both occur.
> 10. IP-associated SCC has a **better prognosis** than de novo sinonasal SCC, possibly due to earlier detection in surveilled patients and less deeply infiltrative growth pattern at time of diagnosis.
