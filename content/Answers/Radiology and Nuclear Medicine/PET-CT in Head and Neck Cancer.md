## Part A: Principles of 18FDG PET-CT

### Physical Basis

**PET (Positron Emission Tomography):**
- **Radiopharmaceutical:** 18-fluorodeoxyglucose (18FDG) — glucose analogue labelled with fluorine-18 (t½ = 110 minutes)
- **Mechanism:** 18FDG is taken up by cells via GLUT transporters (same as glucose); phosphorylated by hexokinase to FDG-6-phosphate — then **trapped** (cannot undergo further glycolysis, unlike glucose-6-phosphate)
- **Positron emission:** Fluorine-18 decays by positron emission; positron travels 1–2 mm then annihilates with an electron → **two 511 keV gamma rays** emitted at 180° to each other
- **Coincidence detection:** PET scanner detects simultaneous (coincident) 511 keV photons from the same annihilation event → generates a line of response → image reconstructed via filtered back-projection or iterative algorithms

**Metabolic basis of tumour FDG uptake:**
- Malignant tumours overexpress GLUT1, GLUT3 transporters and have elevated hexokinase II activity → increased FDG uptake
- **Warburg effect:** Tumour cells preferentially use aerobic glycolysis (even in presence of oxygen) → high glucose consumption → high FDG uptake

**Quantification — Standardised Uptake Value (SUV):**
- **SUV = (Activity in tissue [MBq/mL]) / (Injected dose [MBq] / Body weight [g])**
- SUVmax: maximum SUV in region of interest (most commonly used)
- Malignancy: typically SUVmax > 2.5 (but context-dependent)
- Not a binary threshold — overlaps between benign and malignant

**CT component:**
- Low-dose CT for **attenuation correction** of PET data (photon absorption in body)
- Provides anatomical localisation of PET findings
- Diagnostic-quality contrast CT can be added for full staging

## Part B: Accepted Indications of FDG-PET/CT in HNSCC

### 1. Pre-treatment Staging

| Application | Evidence | Advantage Over CT/MRI |
|-------------|---------|----------------------|
| **Nodal staging** | Sensitivity 80%, specificity 86% for nodal disease | Detects small/non-enlarged nodes with high metabolic activity |
| **Distant metastasis detection** | Sensitivity 90% for distant disease | Single whole-body scan; detects lung, bone, liver mets |
| **Unknown primary detection** | Detects primary in 25–50% of cases where CT/MRI negative | Direct metabolic imaging of small primaries in BOT, tonsil |
| **Second primary** | Detects synchronous second primary in 2–5% of HNSCC patients | CT misses metabolically active lesions without morphological change |

**Limitation in oral cavity:** High physiological FDG uptake in tongue muscles and floor of mouth reduces sensitivity for oral cavity primaries — **CT/MRI preferred** for oral cavity primary staging.

### 2. Unknown Primary Workup

- PET-CT identifies occult primary in **25–43%** of patients with metastatic neck node and negative CT/MRI
- Higher yield when combined with TORS exploration of BOT and tonsils
- **False negatives:** Small primaries <5 mm; high-grade or poorly vascularised tumours

### 3. Post-treatment Surveillance — THE KEY INDICATION

**PET-NECK Trial (Mehanna 2016) — pivotal study:**
- 564 patients with N2/N3 HNSCC treated with definitive CRT
- **Randomised:** PET-CT surveillance at 12 weeks post-CRT vs planned neck dissection
- **Primary endpoint:** 2-year overall survival

| Outcome | PET-CT Arm | Planned Neck Dissection Arm |
|---------|----------|---------------------------|
| 2-yr OS | 84.9% | 81.5% (HR 0.92, 95% CI 0.65–1.32 — NS) |
| Neck dissection rate | 54% (required after positive PET-CT) | 100% (mandatory) |
| Cost per patient | £1,492 less than neck dissection arm | — |
| Negative predictive value of PET-CT | 93% | — |

**Conclusion:** PET-CT at 12 weeks post-CRT is non-inferior to planned neck dissection for N2–N3 disease, reduces unnecessary surgery, and is cost-effective.

**Current practice:** Post-CRT PET-CT at **12–16 weeks** (not earlier — residual inflammation gives false positives)

**Interpretation:**
- **Complete metabolic response (CMR):** No FDG uptake above background → surveillance only
- **Partial metabolic response:** Residual uptake → MDT review; may proceed to salvage neck dissection
- **Progressive disease:** Increasing uptake → salvage treatment

### 4. Detection of Residual/Recurrent Disease

| Site | PET-CT Sensitivity | PET-CT Specificity |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Locoregional residual | 72–90% | 80–95% |
| Neck node residual | 85–93% | 88–95% |
| Distant metastasis | 88–96% | 92–98% |

**Optimal timing:** 12–16 weeks post-treatment (not <10 weeks — false positives from treatment inflammation and mucosal healing)

### 5. Surveillance (Follow-up)

- **Not recommended** for routine annual surveillance in asymptomatic patients (low yield, cost, radiation)
- Indicated for: symptom-directed investigation; rising tumour markers (NPC); clinical suspicion of recurrence

### 6. Larynx/Hypopharynx SCC

- Pre-CRT baseline PET for organ preservation candidates
- Post-CRT assessment at 12 weeks — response guides whether laryngectomy needed
- **SUVmax reduction >70%** from baseline generally indicates complete response
- Positive PET at 12 weeks → proceed to laryngectomy (salvage)

### 7. Oral Cancer PET-CT Role

- **Limited sensitivity** for primary (physiological uptake in tongue)
- **Nodal staging:** PET-CT superior to CT/MRI for occult nodal disease (N0 neck — SUVmax >2.5 in nodes = suspicious)
- **Distant metastasis:** Important in T4 oral SCC, bilateral N3 disease
- **Second primary:** Synchronous second primaries in aerodigestive tract
- **Overall:** CT/MRI remains primary imaging for oral cancer; PET-CT is additive for distant staging and second primary detection

## Part C: PET-MRI

### Technology

PET-MRI integrates:
- **MRI:** Superior soft tissue contrast, no ionising radiation, multiparametric sequences (DWI, DCE, T1/T2)
- **PET:** Functional/metabolic imaging with FDG
- **Simultaneous acquisition:** Unlike PET-CT (sequential), PET-MRI acquires both modalities simultaneously → shorter scan time, co-registration of functional and anatomical data

### Advantages Over PET-CT in HNC

| Advantage | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Superior soft tissue contrast** | Better delineation of tumour extent in tongue base, parapharyngeal space, orbit |
| **No additional radiation** | CT replaced by MRI; overall radiation dose reduced by ~50% vs PET-CT |
| **DWI + PET** | Combined metabolic (SUV) and diffusion (ADC) parameters predict response better than either alone |
| **Better for brain mets** | MRI far superior to CT for intracranial disease |
| **Perineural spread** | MRI detects perineural extension far better than CT |

### Current Role and Limitations

**Advantages:**
- Superior for BOT, nasopharynx, parapharyngeal space, skull base involvement
- DWI can distinguish post-RT fibrosis from recurrence (restricted diffusion in recurrence)

**Limitations:**
- Very long scan time (60–90 min) vs PET-CT (20–30 min)
- High cost; limited availability
- Patient comfort (claustrophobia, pacemakers contraindicated)
- MR attenuation correction less accurate than CT-based
- Not yet standard of care — PET-CT remains primary modality

## Part D: PET-Detected Thyroid Incidentalomas

### Definition

Focal thyroid FDG uptake detected incidentally on PET-CT performed for another indication (HNC staging, lymphoma staging, etc.).

### Prevalence and Significance

- Prevalence: **1.2–4%** of all PET-CT scans
- **Malignancy rate: 30–35%** (compared to 5–15% for incidental thyroid nodules on CT/USS)
- Higher malignancy rate than CT/USS incidentalomas — metabolically active nodules more likely malignant

### Management Algorithm

1. **Focal thyroid FDG uptake detected** on PET-CT
2. **USS thyroid** — characterise nodule (size, echogenicity, vascularity, calcification)
3. **Risk stratification:** TIRADS (ACR/EU-TIRADS) or BTA guidelines
4. **FNAC** (FNA cytology) if:
   - Solid nodule >10 mm with high-risk USS features
   - Any solid nodule >20 mm
   - Nodule causing symptoms
5. **Bethesda system** cytology classification (I–VI)
6. **Surgical excision:** Bethesda V (suspicious) or VI (malignant) → hemithyroidectomy or total thyroidectomy
7. **Observation:** Bethesda I–II (benign or non-diagnostic) with low-risk USS features

### Key Points on Incidentalomas
- **Diffuse thyroid FDG uptake** (not focal) = thyroiditis (Hashimoto's) — low malignancy risk; check TFTs and antibodies
- **Focal uptake** = must be investigated as per above algorithm — 30–35% malignancy rate
- Even in context of known malignancy (HNC), a thyroid incidentaloma must be evaluated — may represent a synchronous primary

## Part E: Imaging Comparison for Post-treatment Surveillance

| Modality | Sensitivity (Recurrence) | Specificity | Advantages | Limitations |
|----------|--------------------------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| **PET-CT** | 88–95% | 80–90% | Whole-body; functional | Cost; false +ve <12 wks; radiation |
| **CE-CT** | 60–75% | 70–80% | Widely available; fast | Cannot distinguish fibrosis from recurrence |
| **MRI** | 75–85% | 80–90% | Best soft tissue | Slow; expensive; artefacts from dental implants |
| **DWI-MRI** | 80–90% | 85–92% | Functional; no contrast | Motion artefacts; limited availability |
| **Clinical exam** | 50–70% | Variable | Cheap; immediate | Cannot assess deep structures |
| **Endoscopy** | 80–90% (mucosal) | High | Direct visualisation; biopsy | No deep assessment |

**Current consensus protocol (NCCN/ESMO):**
- Clinical exam every 1–3 months for 2 years, then every 6 months for years 3–5
- CT neck/chest (or PET-CT) at 12 weeks post-treatment for responders
- Imaging for symptomatic patients at any time
- Annual chest imaging for lung metastasis surveillance

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. 18FDG-PET works on the Warburg effect — tumour cells preferentially use aerobic glycolysis → elevated GLUT1/3 and hexokinase II → FDG trapped after phosphorylation
> 2. FDG-PET/CT is quantified by SUVmax (standardised uptake value); malignancy typically SUVmax >2.5 but not a binary threshold
> 3. **PET-NECK trial (Mehanna 2016):** PET-CT at 12 weeks post-CRT equivalent to planned neck dissection in N2–N3 disease; NPV 93%; saves 46% unnecessary neck dissections
> 4. Optimal post-treatment PET-CT timing: 12–16 weeks (not earlier — treatment inflammation gives false positives)
> 5. PET-CT identifies occult primary in 25–43% of unknown primary cases where CT/MRI negative
> 6. PET-CT limited for oral cavity primary staging (physiological tongue FDG uptake) — CT/MRI preferred
> 7. Focal thyroid incidentaloma on PET-CT: 30–35% malignancy rate; requires USS + FNAC per TIRADS
> 8. Diffuse thyroid FDG uptake = thyroiditis (benign); focal uptake = must investigate
> 9. PET-MRI superior soft tissue contrast + lower radiation vs PET-CT; not yet standard; limited availability
> 10. Post-treatment DWI-MRI: restricted diffusion (low ADC) in recurrent tumour; elevated ADC in post-RT fibrosis — helps distinguish recurrence from treatment change

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - "12 weeks post-CRT" is the magic number for PET-CT timing — earlier gives false positives; later may miss early salvage window
> - PET-NECK trial should be cited verbatim in any post-CRT neck management question: "Mehanna 2016, NEJM — PET-CT equivalent to planned neck dissection, saved 46% unnecessary surgeries"
> - A completely metabolic complete response (CMR) on PET-CT at 12 weeks post-CRT = surveillance only; do NOT operate — 93% NPV for residual disease
> - Thyroid incidentaloma: "focal FDG uptake = 30% malignancy risk — always USS + FNAC"; "diffuse uptake = thyroiditis — TFTs and antibodies"
> - PET-CT for unknown primary: combine with TORS exploration (tonsillectomy + BOT mucosectomy) for best detection rate
> - For laryngeal/hypopharyngeal SCC on organ preservation pathway, post-CRT PET-CT at 12 weeks guides whether laryngectomy is needed — SUVmax reduction guides clinical decision
