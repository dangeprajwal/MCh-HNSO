## Part A: Definition and Epidemiology

### Definition

**Cancer of Unknown Primary (CUP) / Unknown Primary Head and Neck Cancer:**
A metastatic head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) presenting as a **cervical lymph node**, where thorough clinical and radiological evaluation fails to identify the primary tumour site.

- Represents **3–5%** of all HNSCC
- Most commonly: **Level II/III lymph node** with squamous cell carcinoma histology
- After systematic workup, ~20–30% remain truly occult ("true CUP")

### Theories of Development

Two competing theories explain the neck node without identifiable primary:

**Theory 1: Occult Primary (Most Accepted)**
- A primary tumour exists somewhere in the upper aerodigestive tract but is too small to detect clinically or radiologically
- Most commonly arises in **Waldeyer's ring** (base of tongue, tonsil, nasopharynx, posterior pharyngeal wall)
- The primary may be within the mucosa of a palatine or lingual tonsil and not visible on standard examination
- **Evidence:** With systematic TORS exploration, 40–85% of "unknown primaries" are found in BOT/tonsil

**Theory 2: Branchial Cyst Malignant Transformation (Historical, Now Discredited)**
- **Volkmann (1882)** proposed the neck mass resulted from degeneration of a "branchiogenic cyst" into carcinoma — accepted until the 1940s
- **Martin and Mort (1940)** revisited in a study of 55 CUP patients — only 8 met Volkmann's criteria, effectively dismissing the hypothesis
- Martin's criteria: Location along anterior border of SCM, histology supporting branchial cleft origin, carcinoma arising in wall of cyst, 5-year follow-up showing no primary
- **Current consensus (Scott-Brown's, Harrison):** Essentially never proven — all such cases represent metastatic SCC from a small occult oropharyngeal primary; p16 positivity of the cystic node (not present in true branchial cysts) supports oropharyngeal origin

## Part B: Evaluation and Workup

### Initial Assessment

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| **History** | Tobacco, alcohol, HPV risk factors; duration of neck lump; dysphagia, odynophagia, ear pain (referred otalgia from oropharynx) |
| **Clinical examination** | Complete head and neck exam: scalp, face, oral cavity, oropharynx, hypopharynx (indirect laryngoscopy), nasopharynx (nasoendoscopy), ears, thyroid |
| **Flexible nasoendoscopy** | Essential first step in clinic — visualises nasopharynx, BOT, pyriform sinuses, larynx |

### Investigations

**Step 1: Narrow Band Imaging (NBI) Endoscopy in Clinic**
- Modified white light endoscopy filtering to 415 nm and 540 nm wavelengths
- Identifies neoangiogenesis (submucosal capillaries = brown; prominent vessels = cyan) → detects neoplastic tissue at earlier stage than conventional endoscopy
- Meta-analysis (Scott-Brown's): 115 patients with CUP — sensitivity 74.1%, specificity 94.1%
- Performed at initial outpatient nasoendoscopy; guides targeted biopsy

**Step 2: Cross-sectional Imaging**
- **CE-CT neck and chest** — characterise node (size, level, ECS), detect primary, assess distant disease
- **MRI neck** — superior for mucosal detail, BOT, parapharyngeal space, perineural spread

**Step 3: FDG-PET/CT — MUST BE DONE BEFORE PANENDOSCOPY AND BIOPSIES**
- Post-biopsy inflammatory response increases FDG tracer uptake → false positive results (Scott-Brown's)
- Detects occult primary in **25–43%** of cases where CT/MRI negative
- Whole-body: assesses distant metastases and second primaries
- Influences 20–30% of management decisions

**Step 4: HPV Testing of the Node**
- p16 IHC on FNA or core biopsy
- **p16-positive node** = almost certainly HPV+ oropharyngeal primary (BOT or tonsil) — guides directed workup and treatment
- p16-positive: 86–90% sensitivity, 94% specificity for HPV-related OPC

**Step 5: FNAC / Core Biopsy**
- Preferably **US-guided FNAC** of the neck node
- Core biopsy if FNAC non-diagnostic
- **Avoid excision biopsy** as first step — disrupts tissue planes, complicates definitive surgery
- Histology: SCC (most common), poorly differentiated carcinoma, adenocarcinoma

**Step 6: Examination Under Anaesthesia (EUA) + Panendoscopy**
| Procedure | Area Examined | Comment |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| Direct laryngoscopy | Larynx, hypopharynx, post-cricoid | Systematic visual inspection + random biopsies from suspicious areas |
| Nasopharyngoscopy | Nasopharynx and Rosenmuller's fossa | Bilateral biopsies from Rosenmuller's fossa and posterior nasopharynx |
| Oropharyngoscopy | BOT, vallecula, tonsils | Random biopsies from BOT mucosa |
| Oesophagoscopy | Oesophagus | If suspicious symptoms |
| Bronchoscopy | Tracheobronchial tree | If suspicious symptoms |

**Step 7: TORS/TLM for BOT and Tonsil Exploration**
- **Bilateral palatine tonsillectomy** — contralateral tonsil harbours primary in up to **10%** of cases; HPV tumours can be multifocal (Scott-Brown's); previous tonsillectomy remnants should also be excised
- **Tongue base mucosectomy (TBM)** — TORS-designed procedure sampling entire tongue base mucosa; identifies primary in **>50%** of patients who are PET-negative and have no tonsil primary; ~10% are contralateral foci (Scott-Brown's)
- TBM morbidity: bleeding, need for tube feeding, small risk of pharyngeal stenosis
- Overall: TORS increases primary detection rate from ~20% (panendoscopy alone) to 50–60% (panendoscopy + TORS) for PET-negative patients

### Diagnostic Algorithm — Level III Poorly Differentiated Carcinoma

```
Level III node, poorly differentiated carcinoma (FNA)
                     ↓
     p16 IHC on node specimen
                 ↙         ↘
         p16+               p16-
           ↓                  ↓
  HPV+ OPC likely       Consider ALL sites:
  Direct to BOT/         nasopharynx (EBV),
  tonsil TORS            thyroid, salivary gland,
  exploration            skin, lung, GI
           ↓                  ↓
      PET-CT              PET-CT + CT chest
  (whole body)            abdomen pelvis
           ↓                  ↓
    EUA + TORS          EUA + panendoscopy
  (BOT, tonsils)      (random biopsies all sites)
           ↓                  ↓
   Primary found?       Primary found?
      Yes → treat        No → "True CUP"
   site-specifically    → treat neck + mucosal RT
```

## Part C: Histological Subtypes and IHC

### Histological Classification

| Histology | Common Primaries | IHC Panel |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| **SCC (keratinising)** | Oral cavity, larynx, hypopharynx, skin | p40, CK5/6 |
| **SCC (non-keratinising)** | Oropharynx (HPV+), nasopharynx | p16, CK5/6, p40, EBV EBER |
| **Poorly differentiated carcinoma** | Any site; also NPC, NEC | p40, CK, synaptophysin, chromogranin |
| **Adenocarcinoma** | Salivary gland, thyroid, lung, GI | CK7/20, TTF-1, thyroglobulin, CEA, PAX8 |
| **Neuroendocrine carcinoma** | Larynx, Merkel cell** | Synaptophysin, chromogranin, CD56, CK20 |
| **Melanoma** | Skin, mucosa | S100, HMB45, Melan-A, SOX10 |
| **Lymphoma** | Lymph nodes | CD20 (B-cell), CD3 (T-cell), LCA |

### Key IHC Markers

| Marker | Positive in | Note |
|--------|------------|------|
| **p16** | HPV+ OPC (surrogate marker) | >70% nuclear/cytoplasmic staining = positive |
| **EBV EBER (ISH)** | NPC | Most sensitive marker for EBV association |
| **CK5/6, p40, p63** | SCC | Squamous differentiation markers |
| **TTF-1** | Thyroid carcinoma, lung adenocarcinoma | Helps distinguish primary |
| **Thyroglobulin** | Thyroid (well-differentiated) | Confirms thyroid origin |
| **CEA** | Colorectal, gastric | Excludes primary HNSCC |
| **CK7+/CK20−** | Salivary gland, lung adenocarcinoma | Adenocarcinoma panel |
| **CK7−/CK20+** | Colorectal adenocarcinoma | — |
| **Synaptophysin, Chromogranin** | Neuroendocrine tumours | LCNEC vs small cell |

## Part D: Management

### When Primary is Found
Treat according to primary site — standard site-specific management.

### When Primary Remains Occult (True CUP)

**ENT-UK Multidisciplinary Treatment Guidelines (2016) — per Scott-Brown's:**

| Stage | Surgery | Radiotherapy | Chemotherapy |
|-------|---------|-------------|-------------|
| T0N1M0 (no ECS) | SND or MRND | No, unless for mucosal sites | No |
| T0N1M0 (ECS present) | SND or MRND | Yes — to neck | Should be considered |
| T0N2M0 | SND or MRND | Yes — ipsilateral (consider contralateral) | Should be considered |
| T0N3M0 | MRND or RND | Yes — ipsilateral (consider contralateral) | Should be considered |

**Principle:** Treat the most likely occult primary site + neck, balancing comprehensive coverage against toxicity.

#### Surgery + Adjuvant Radiotherapy

**Indications:**
- Resectable node (N1–N2a without ENE)
- Good performance status
- Prefer surgery-first approach

**Steps:**
1. **Neck dissection:** Selective or modified radical neck dissection (ipsilateral, levels I–V)
2. **Adjuvant RT:** To **ipsilateral neck** (50–60 Gy) ± elective RT to bilateral neck and mucosal sites (Waldeyer's ring, nasopharynx, BOT)

#### Primary Radiotherapy (± Chemotherapy)

**Indications:**
- N2b–N3 disease (bilateral or large nodal disease)
- p16+ disease (HPV+ OPC — excellent prognosis with CRT)
- Inoperable disease
- Prefer organ preservation

**RT fields:**
- **Comprehensive mucosal RT:** Waldeyer's ring (BOT, tonsils, nasopharynx, pharyngeal walls) — prevents primary from manifesting in previously irradiated field
- **Ipsilateral neck** (N1–N2a) or **bilateral neck** (N2b/N3)
- **Dose:** 66–70 Gy to gross nodal disease; 50–60 Gy to elective areas

#### HPV-positive (p16+) Unknown Primary — Special Considerations

- Excellent prognosis (5-year OS ~80–90%)
- Most likely primary: BOT or tonsil
- TORS exploration mandatory before definitive treatment
- De-escalation trials ongoing (ECOG 3311) for post-TORS adjuvant RT

#### N3 / Unresectable Disease

- Primary CRT (cisplatin 100 mg/m² q3w + RT 70 Gy)
- Response assessment at 12 weeks (PET-CT)
- Salvage neck dissection for residual nodal disease
- Prognosis: 3-year OS 40–60% for N3 disease

### Role of TORS in Unknown Primary

**Diagnostic + therapeutic dual role:**
1. **Diagnostic:** Bilateral palatine tonsillectomy + BOT mucosectomy → identifies primary in 40–85%
2. **Therapeutic:** If primary found in tonsil/BOT, TORS simultaneously treats the primary (margins assessed on specimen)
3. **Avoids RT to full mucosal field:** Once primary identified and resected with TORS + negative margins, RT can be targeted to primary bed + neck only → less toxicity

**Nagel 2014:** TORS-identified primaries allowed reduction in RT field size in 56% of patients → less xerostomia, dysphagia.

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. CUP = cervical lymph node metastasis where thorough workup fails to identify primary; represents 3–5% of HNSCC
> 2. Two theories: (i) Occult primary (accepted — most likely Waldeyer's ring); (ii) Branchial cyst malignant transformation (discredited — diagnosis of exclusion)
> 3. Systematic workup: clinical exam → CE-CT → PET-CT → p16 IHC of node → EUA + panendoscopy → TORS exploration
> 4. PET-CT identifies primary in 25–43% of cases where CT/MRI negative
> 5. p16-positive node = HPV+ oropharyngeal primary in 86–90% — guide targeted TORS exploration of BOT and tonsils
> 6. TORS (bilateral tonsillectomy + BOT mucosectomy) increases primary detection from ~20% to 40–85%
> 7. True CUP treatment: neck dissection + RT to neck ± comprehensive mucosal RT (Waldeyer's ring)
> 8. p16+ CUP: excellent prognosis (~80–90% 5-yr OS); TORS-directed treatment allows field reduction
> 9. Avoid open excisional biopsy of neck node as first procedure — US-guided FNAC preferred
> 10. IHC panel: p16 (HPV), EBV EBER (NPC), CK5/6 (SCC), TTF-1/thyroglobulin (thyroid), synaptophysin/chromogranin (neuroendocrine)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - "Bilateral palatine tonsillectomy" is the single highest-yield procedure in unknown primary workup — do not just biopsy
> - A p16+ neck node from Level II/III = HPV+ OPC until proven otherwise; direct TORS to BOT and tonsils immediately
> - Martin's criteria for branchial cyst carcinoma are no longer accepted — treat all neck node SCCs as metastatic until proven otherwise
> - PET-CT + TORS together give highest primary detection rate — use both before declaring "true CUP"
> - N3 CUP with no primary found: treat like locally advanced OPC (CRT 70 Gy + cisplatin) + salvage ND for residual
> - Elective mucosal RT to Waldeyer's ring in true CUP prevents primary from emerging in an unirradiated site — reduces "delayed primary" appearance to <5%
