(2017) conducted the definitive systematic review and meta-analysis of 559 patients across 25 studies, confirming 100% technical success for both covered stenting and coil embolisation, and informing contemporary selection criteria.

## Part A — Carotid Blowout Syndrome (CBS)

### Definition

Carotid blowout syndrome (CBS) refers to haemorrhage from the carotid artery or its major branches resulting from tumour invasion, wound breakdown, infection, or radiation-induced vascular changes in the context of head and neck surgery and/or radiotherapy. It is among the most feared and immediately life-threatening complications in head and neck oncology. The term encompasses a spectrum from asymptomatic carotid exposure through to exsanguinating haemorrhage, and classification by stage directly guides urgency and modality of management.

### Classification (Powitzky / Shah Classification)

| Type | Description | Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Threatened CBS** | Carotid artery exposed in wound, pulsation visible or palpable; pseudoaneurysm on imaging | No active bleeding; high-risk anatomical finding |
| **Impending CBS** | Sentinel bleed — small herald haemorrhage (<200 mL), spontaneously arrests | Occurs days to weeks before major rupture; urgent intervention required |
| **Acute CBS** | Massive, life-threatening arterial haemorrhage | Rapidly fatal without immediate intervention; mortality 40–60% |

### Incidence and Risk Factors

**Incidence:**
- Overall post-neck dissection: 0.6%
- After salvage surgery in previously irradiated field: 3–4%
- After pharyngocutaneous fistula formation in irradiated neck: up to 15%

**Risk factors:**

*Surgery-related:*
- Radical neck dissection (sacrifice of sternocleidomastoid and internal jugular vein exposes carotid)
- Wound breakdown and carotid artery exposure
- Pharyngocutaneous fistula — saliva contacts carotid adventitia, causing enzymatic digestion of vessel wall
- Local wound infection (anaerobic organisms particularly destructive)
- Carotid involvement or encasement by recurrent tumour

*Radiation-related:*
- Prior external beam radiotherapy >60 Gy (endarteritis, adventitial necrosis)
- Re-irradiation — cumulative dose >100 Gy carries the highest risk
- Chemoradiation — microvascular injury accelerated by concurrent cisplatin
- Radiation-induced necrosis of overlying skin and mucosal tissue

*Patient-related:*
- Malnutrition (impaired wound healing)
- Diabetes mellitus, anaemia
- Prior carotid endarterectomy or carotid artery stenting
- Persistent or recurrent disease at carotid sheath

### Sentinel Bleed — Critical Warning Sign

- Defined as a self-limiting haemorrhage <200 mL from the neck wound or oral cavity
- Represents erosion through adventitia with tamponade by surrounding tissue
- May occur days to weeks before catastrophic rupture
- **Management of sentinel bleed: admit immediately; urgent CTA neck and angiography; ICU monitoring; plan endovascular intervention before major rupture**
- Failure to act on a sentinel bleed is the most preventable cause of CBS-related death

### Investigations for CBS Workup

| Investigation | Purpose |
|---|---|
| CTA neck with contrast | Localise bleed; identify pseudoaneurysm, exposed vessel, vessel wall integrity |
| Four-vessel cerebral angiography | Gold standard; assess collateral circulation; pre-procedure for endovascular planning |
| MRI neck | Tumour extent, carotid encasement, tissue viability (not in acute phase) |
| Wound swab and blood cultures | Guide antibiotic therapy; identify causative organism |
| FBC, coagulation screen, crossmatch | Baseline before intervention; guide transfusion |
| BOT with SPECT perfusion | Assess safety of PAO; stratify stroke risk prior to permanent occlusion |

### Immediate Management of Acute CBS

#### Step 1 — Airway
- **Intubation or emergency tracheostomy** is the first priority if blood is flooding the airway
- Awake fibreoptic intubation if partial airway compromise; RSI if complete obstruction
- Avoid manipulation of neck during laryngoscopy in active bleed

#### Step 2 — Haemostasis
- **Direct digital pressure** at the bleeding point — do not attempt blind clamping (risk of injuring adjacent cranial nerves, internal jugular vein, vagus nerve)
- Pack wound with haemostatic gauze (Surgicel/Kaltostat) if direct pressure insufficient
- Do NOT apply tourniquet or circumferential compression — risks venous engorgement and cerebral oedema

#### Step 3 — Resuscitation
- Large-bore IV access (×2), arterial line
- Massive transfusion protocol: packed red cells, FFP, cryoprecipitate (ratio 1:1:1)
- Reverse anticoagulation if applicable (protamine, vitamin K, idarucizumab)
- Target SBP 90–100 mmHg — permissive hypotension until haemostasis achieved
- Transfer to ICU/interventional radiology suite

#### Step 4 — Emergency Angiography
- Performed under general anaesthesia with direct pressure maintained
- Four-vessel cerebral angiography: bilateral carotid and vertebral arteries
- Assess: site of bleeding, collateral circulation (Circle of Willis patency), balloon occlusion test (BOT)

### Endovascular Management (Preferred)

**Balloon Occlusion Test (BOT):**
- Temporary occlusion of internal carotid artery under conscious sedation
- Assesses neurological tolerance for parent artery occlusion (PAO)
- If tolerated for 20–30 minutes without neurological deficit: PAO feasible
- Adjuncts: SPECT cerebral perfusion, transcranial Doppler, EEG monitoring

**Covered Stent Graft (First Choice — Flow Preservation):**
- Indication: adequate landing zones proximal and distal to bleed; acceptable vessel tortuosity; Circle of Willis patent but incomplete collateralisation
- Advantages: preserves antegrade flow; avoids stroke risk of PAO; immediate haemostasis
- Disadvantages: risk of endoleak; in-stent restenosis (higher in irradiated field); device infection risk in contaminated wound
- Example devices: Viabahn (Gore), iCAST (Atrium), Fluency Plus
- Rebleed rate: 20–30% in heavily irradiated fields (progressive vessel wall necrosis continues)

**Parent Artery Occlusion (PAO — Permanent):**
- Indication: positive BOT (collaterals adequate); vessel not suitable for stenting (tortuous, small calibre); severe contamination precluding implant
- Method: detachable coils or Amplatzer vascular plug proximal and distal to bleed
- Neurological complication rate: 20–25% (embolic or haemodynamic stroke)
- Higher stroke risk with ICA occlusion vs ECA occlusion

**Coil Embolisation (Pseudoaneurysm/Branch Vessel):**
- Preferred for pseudoaneurysm of external carotid branches (lingual, facial, superior thyroid)
- Lower stroke risk than ICA coiling
- Technical success >95%

### Surgical Management

Reserved for patients who fail endovascular management or in whom endovascular access is not available.

**Options:**
- **Direct repair/primary suture:** feasible only if vessel wall viable and wound uninfected; rarely possible in this context
- **Patch angioplasty or interposition graft:** synthetic (PTFE) or autologous vein graft; high failure/infection rate in irradiated field
- **Carotid ligation:** last resort; 30% stroke risk for internal carotid artery (ICA) ligation; lower for external carotid artery (ECA) ligation; preceded by BOT where time permits

### Palliative Context and Comfort Care

Many patients presenting with CBS have incurable, recurrent disease. Multidisciplinary discussion should precede any intervention to align management with goals of care:
- If endovascular intervention is appropriate and technically feasible: proceed as above
- If patient has end-stage disease and has declined aggressive intervention: dignified comfort measures including haemostatic dressings, dark-coloured towels to mask blood, anxiolytic/analgesic medications, and family preparation
- CBS in palliative care settings should be anticipated and a "CBS box" (midazolam, morphine, dark towels, airway suction) prepared in advance for home/hospice patients

### Prevention of CBS

1. **Wound management:** meticulous closure over neck dissection; avoid dead space; closed suction drainage
2. **Vascularised tissue coverage:** exposed carotid must be covered with viable muscle — pectoralis major myocutaneous flap (PMMC) or sternocleidomastoid muscle flap interposed between carotid and pharyngeal suture line at time of pharyngo-laryngectomy
3. **Early fistula management:** pharyngocutaneous fistulae require aggressive wound packing, nutritional support, and surgical closure; carotid exposure is an indication for urgent surgical coverage
4. **Nutritional optimisation:** enteral feeding via PEG or nasogastric tube; correct anaemia, hypoalbuminaemia
5. **Infection control:** wound swabs, targeted antibiotics, debridement of necrotic tissue
6. **Carotid artery stripping:** perioperative stripping of adventitia is associated with ischaemic necrosis — avoid

**Prophylactic carotid coverage at index surgery:**
Indications for prophylactic PMMC coverage at time of pharyngolaryngectomy or salvage neck dissection:
- Prior RT dose >60 Gy
- Re-irradiation planned
- Pharyngeal reconstruction with expected salivary leak risk
- Bilateral neck dissection with bilateral IJ sacrifice
- Presence of fistula or necrotic tissue at time of surgery

## Part B — Cervical Artery Disease and Post-Radiation Vasculopathy

### Radiation-Induced Vasculopathy — Pathophysiology

Ionising radiation causes a spectrum of vascular injury depending on dose, fractionation, and time from exposure. The carotid arteries lie within standard treatment volumes for oropharyngeal, laryngeal, and hypopharyngeal cancers, making this a near-universal late effect in long-term survivors.

| Time Post-RT | Pathological Change | Clinical Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Acute (weeks) | Endothelial swelling, thrombosis, endarteritis obliterans of vasa vasorum | Early occlusion (rare); inflammatory periarteritis |
| Subacute (months) | Intimal fibrosis, foam cell accumulation, smooth muscle proliferation | Accelerated atherosclerosis |
| Late (5–20 years) | Medial necrosis, adventitial fibrosis, calcification, plaque rupture | Symptomatic stenosis, TIA, ischaemic stroke |

Key features distinguishing radiation-induced atherosclerosis from de novo atherosclerosis:
- Occurs at younger age
- Affects atypical sites (carotid bifurcation and proximal ICA within radiation field)
- Involves longer segment of vessel
- Associated with fibrotic, poorly-collateralised surrounding tissue

### Incidence and Risk

- Carotid stenosis >50% within 10 years of RT: 30–50% of patients
- Stroke/TIA risk: 5–8× higher than age-matched controls not receiving RT
- Risk factors for significant stenosis: cumulative dose >50 Gy, carotid artery within treatment field, conventional (2 Gy/fraction) fractionation, concurrent smoking, hypertension, diabetes

### Surveillance Protocol

| Timepoint | Investigation |
|---|---|
| Baseline (pre-RT) | Carotid duplex USS |
| 2 years post-RT | Annual carotid duplex USS |
| Stenosis 50–70% | 6-monthly duplex; optimise cardiovascular risk factors |
| Stenosis >70% or symptomatic | CTA or MRA neck; neurovascular surgery/intervention referral |

### Carotid Endarterectomy (CEA) vs Carotid Artery Stenting (CAS) in Irradiated Field

| Parameter | CEA | CAS |
|---|---|---|
| Stroke/death rate (general) | 2–3% (NASCET data) | 3–5% (CREST data) |
| Stroke/death rate (post-RT) | 5–10% (increased) | 5–8% |
| Wound complications | High (skin/tissue fibrosis, delayed healing, CN injury risk) | Minimal (percutaneous) |
| Cranial nerve injury | Hypoglossal, marginal mandibular, vagus at risk | Not applicable |
| Restenosis rate | Low (5–10% at 5 years) | Higher in irradiated field (15–25%) |
| Preferred for | First-time stenosis, good surgical access, adequate tissue healing | Recurrent stenosis post-CEA, poor surgical candidate, hostile neck, contralateral ICA occlusion |
| Durability | Superior long-term | Inferior in irradiated necks due to neointimal hyperplasia |

**Current consensus:** CAS is preferred for radiation-induced carotid stenosis in most head and neck cancer patients due to hostile wound environment, fibrosis, and CN injury risk, despite higher restenosis rates. CEA remains preferred for low-risk patients with first-presentation high-grade stenosis and no prior neck surgery.

**NASCET and CREST trial context:**
- NASCET (1991): CEA beneficial for symptomatic stenosis >70%; number needed to treat = 6 at 2 years
- CREST (2010): No significant difference between CAS and CEA overall, but CAS had higher periprocedural stroke rate; CEA had higher MI rate — neither trial included irradiated-neck subgroup as a major stratification variable
- Subsequent irradiated-neck specific series show CAS to have acceptable periprocedural safety despite higher long-term restenosis in this population

### Vertebral Artery Injury During Neck Dissection

**Anatomy:** The vertebral artery enters the transverse foramen at C6 and is at risk during dissection of level IV (inferior to omohyoid) and level V.

**Recognition:** Bright red pulsatile bleeding from the transverse foramen — do NOT attempt blind clamping.

**Immediate management:**
1. Haemostatic packing (bone wax, haemostatic gauze) into transverse foramen
2. Maintain pressure and expedite transfer to angiography suite
3. Angiographic embolisation of vertebral artery at injury level
4. Note: bilateral vertebral artery injury is fatal; assess contralateral VA flow before embolising
5. If dominant VA injured and contralateral VA absent: covered stent may be required to preserve posterior circulation

### Subclavian Artery — Level IV Risk

- Left subclavian artery is at particular risk during left level IV and left level IVb (superior mediastinal) dissection
- Lies posterior to subclavian vein and thoracic duct; injury during ligature of thoracic duct can be catastrophic
- Management: direct pressure, vascular surgery involvement, possible median sternotomy extension
- Right subclavian artery is anomalously positioned (arteria lusoria) in ~0.5% — aberrant right subclavian artery passes posterior to oesophagus and is at risk during right level IV dissection; pre-operative imaging identifies this variant

### Lingual and Facial Artery Injury

- Lingual artery injury during level IB or level II dissection: usually managed by direct ligation; rarely haemodynamically significant
- Facial artery: identified at lower border of mandible and ligated at the time of supraomohyoid dissection; anomalous branching patterns should be anticipated
- External carotid artery (ECA) branches at the carotid bifurcation require individual identification and preservation or deliberate ligation during carotid body tumour resection and some radical neck dissections

### Internal Carotid Artery Involvement by Tumour

- Carotid encasement (>270° circumferential contact on CT): resection with carotid sacrifice historically associated with 40–50% stroke/death rate
- Modern approach: pre-operative BOT; if BOT-positive — carotid resection with or without reconstruction (PTFE interposition graft); if BOT-negative — surgery only in selected cases with cerebral protection
- Carotid reconstruction in irradiated field carries high infection and blowout risk; vascularised muscle coverage mandatory

### Medical Optimisation and Risk Factor Modification

Post-RT patients with cervical artery disease require aggressive cardiovascular risk factor management:

| Intervention | Target/Evidence |
|---|---|
| Antiplatelet therapy | Aspirin 75–100 mg daily (or clopidogrel if aspirin-intolerant) for stenosis >50% or symptomatic disease |
| Statin therapy | High-intensity statin (atorvastatin 40–80 mg); pleiotropic stabilisation of radiation-induced plaque |
| Blood pressure control | Target <130/80 mmHg; ACE inhibitors or ARBs preferred |
| Glycaemic control | HbA1c <7%; hyperglycaemia accelerates intimal hyperplasia |
| Smoking cessation | Synergistic vascular injury with RT; cessation reduces progression rate |
| Dietary and lifestyle | Mediterranean diet; supervised cardiac rehabilitation post-RT |

Anticoagulation (DOAC or warfarin) is generally reserved for concurrent AF or thromboembolic disease; antiplatelet therapy is preferred for radiation-induced carotid stenosis alone.

Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel) is used for 4 weeks post-CAS stent deployment, then single antiplatelet lifelong — analogous to coronary stenting protocol but with higher restenosis surveillance frequency in the irradiated neck.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Sentinel bleed is never trivial** — a small self-limiting neck haemorrhage in a post-RT/post-ND patient must be treated as an impending CBS until proven otherwise; admit, image, and plan endovascular intervention urgently.
> 2. **CBS type determines urgency, not management modality** — all three types (threatened, impending, acute) warrant endovascular evaluation; the difference is the timeline available.
> 3. **Endovascular-first is the standard of care** — covered stent graft preserves flow and is preferred over PAO when vessel anatomy permits; BOT is performed to stratify stroke risk before committing to PAO.
> 4. **Direct digital compression — not clamp** — blind clamping of the carotid in a contaminated, irradiated neck injures cranial nerves IX, X, XI and the internal jugular vein; finger pressure is safe and effective as a bridge.
> 5. **PMMC flap is the workhorse for prevention** — pectoralis major myocutaneous flap interposed between the carotid and a pharyngeal/cutaneous suture line reduces CBS risk from ~15% to <2% in high-risk reconstructions.
> 6. **Pharyngocutaneous fistula = carotid emergency** — saliva contains proteolytic enzymes (trypsin, elastase) that digest the carotid adventitia within days; fistula demands urgent surgical closure with vascularised coverage.
> 7. **Radiation-induced carotid stenosis is underdiagnosed** — 30–50% of patients develop >50% stenosis within 10 years of RT; annual duplex surveillance from 2 years post-treatment is the standard.
> 8. **CAS preferred over CEA in irradiated necks** — hostile soft tissue, fibrosis, and cranial nerve proximity make open surgery high-risk; however CAS has higher restenosis rates and patients need surveillance.
> 9. **Vertebral artery injury — pack, do not clamp** — packing the transverse foramen with bone wax buys time for angiographic embolisation; bilateral vertebral injury is uniformly fatal, so contralateral VA patency must be confirmed first.
> 10. **Re-irradiation (cumulative >100 Gy) is the highest-risk scenario** — the combination of impaired wound healing, necrosis, and progressive arterial wall fragility makes CBS prevention (vascularised flap coverage) essential at the time of salvage surgery.
