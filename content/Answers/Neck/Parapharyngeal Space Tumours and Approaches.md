>
> Carrau, Myers, and Johnson (1990) reported on the management of 68 parapharyngeal space tumours at the University of Pittsburgh, establishing the transcervical approach with cervico-parotid extension as the workhorse technique and demonstrating that the direction of internal carotid artery displacement on imaging reliably differentiates pre-styloid from post-styloid lesions — a concept that directly guides surgical planning. Olsen (1994) published the definitive review of 44 consecutive parapharyngeal space tumours, systematically describing the cervical-parotid approach in 35 patients and the cervical-parotid approach with midline mandibulotomy in 9 patients with large or skull base-extending lesions, providing the anatomical rationale and technical framework that remains the standard reference for approaching this space. Cohen, Burkey, and Netterville (2005) analysed 166 parapharyngeal space masses — 87% benign, 13% malignant — demonstrating that transcervical access with selective parotid extension suffices for the vast majority of lesions and that skull base involvement (41.6%) and vascular lesion characteristics (45.8%) are the key preoperative variables that determine the need for more extensive approaches. Kuet, Kasbekar, Masterson, and Jani (2015) performed the largest systematic review of parapharyngeal space tumours to date, analysing 1,293 cases from 25 years of literature and confirming that 82% of lesions are benign, that the most common primary lesion is pleomorphic adenoma (29%), and that certain imaging and clinical variables — deep lobe parotid origin, irregular margins, bone erosion, and infiltrative pattern — are independently associated with malignancy and guide the aggressiveness of surgical planning.

## Surgical Anatomy of the Parapharyngeal Space

### Definition and Shape
The parapharyngeal space (PPS) is an **inverted pyramid-shaped** potential space lying lateral to the pharynx in the deep face. It is the central hub of the deep neck — virtually all deep face and neck spaces communicate with it, making knowledge of its anatomy indispensable to head and neck surgery.

### Boundaries

| Wall | Structure |
|---|---|
| **Medial** | Superior pharyngeal constrictor muscle and buccopharyngeal fascia |
| **Lateral** | Medial pterygoid muscle, pterygoid fascia, and deep lobe of parotid gland |
| **Superior (base)** | Skull base — greater wing of sphenoid and petrous apex |
| **Inferior (apex)** | Posterior belly of digastric and hyoid bone |
| **Anterior** | Pterygomandibular raphe |
| **Posterior** | Prevertebral fascia and muscles |

### Styloid Process: The Central Anatomical Landmark
The styloid process, styloid musculature (styloglossus, stylohyoid, stylopharyngeus), and the stylomandibular ligament divide the PPS into two distinct compartments with different contents and therefore different tumour types:

---

## Pre-Styloid Compartment

### Contents
- Fatty areolar tissue (predominant constituent)
- Branches of the mandibular nerve (CN V3): auriculotemporal, inferior alveolar
- Internal maxillary artery and its branches
- Inferior alveolar nerve
- Minor salivary gland rests and ectopic salivary tissue
- Deep lobe of the parotid gland (which projects into this space)
- Ascending pharyngeal artery

### Tumours of the Pre-Styloid Space
Salivary tumours account for **40–50% of all PPS tumours** and originate predominantly in the pre-styloid compartment:

| Tumour | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Pleomorphic adenoma** | Most common PPS tumour overall; arises from deep parotid lobe or ectopic minor salivary tissue; accounts for ~40% of all PPS neoplasms |
| **Warthin tumour** | Second most common salivary tumour; parotid origin |
| **Mucoepidermoid carcinoma** | Most common malignant salivary tumour in PPS |
| **Adenoid cystic carcinoma** | Perineural spread; poor prognosis |
| **Carcinoma ex pleomorphic adenoma** | Malignant transformation of pre-existing PA |

---

## Post-Styloid Compartment

### Contents (Carotid Space)
The post-styloid PPS is synonymous with the **carotid space** and contains the principal neurovascular structures of the deep neck:

| Structure | Details |
|---|---|
| **Internal carotid artery (ICA)** | Traverses vertically; prone to displacement by tumours |
| **Internal jugular vein (IJV)** | Lateral to ICA |
| **CN IX** (Glossopharyngeal) | Exits jugular foramen; courses anteriorly |
| **CN X** (Vagus) | Lies posteriorly between ICA and IJV |
| **CN XI** (Accessory) | Exits jugular foramen |
| **CN XII** (Hypoglossal) | Crosses the carotid sheath at the level of C1 |
| **Sympathetic chain** | Posterior to carotid sheath; preganglionic fibres to the head |

### Tumours of the Post-Styloid Space
Neurogenic tumours predominate:

| Tumour | Origin | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Paraganglioma** | Chief cells of paraganglia; jugulotympanic or vagal | Highly vascular; blush on angiography; do not biopsy |
| **Schwannoma** | Schwann cells of CN IX, X, XI, XII, sympathetic chain | Most common: vagal schwannoma |
| **Neurofibroma** | Neural sheath | Associated with NF1 |
| **Carotid body tumour** | Carotid body at bifurcation | Strictly at bifurcation; not within PPS but closely related |

---

## Pathology Distribution

| Category | Proportion | Subtypes |
|---|---|---|
| **Benign** | ~80% | Salivary 40–50%; neurogenic 20%; vascular 5%; miscellaneous |
| **Malignant** | ~20% | Malignant salivary tumours; lymphoma; sarcoma; metastatic deposits; direct extension from adjacent structures |
| **Most common single tumour** | Pleomorphic adenoma | ~40% of all PPS tumours |

---

## Clinical Features

### Presenting Symptoms
- **Intraoral bulge:** Medial displacement of the tonsil and soft palate is the most consistent finding; the tonsil is pushed medially and the soft palate is deviated to the opposite side — a hallmark of PPS pathology
- **Neck mass:** A submandibular or upper neck swelling may be the sole presentation, particularly with post-styloid lesions
- **Dysphagia and muffled voice:** Oropharyngeal compression by large tumours
- **Snoring and obstructive symptoms:** Pharyngeal narrowing

### Cranial Nerve Deficit Signs
| Sign | CN Involved | Likely Tumour Origin |
|---|---|---|
| Hoarseness, dysphagia | CN X (vagus) | Vagal schwannoma or paraganglioma |
| Tongue deviation, dysarthria | CN XII (hypoglossal) | Post-styloid neurogenic |
| Shoulder weakness | CN XI (accessory) | Jugular foramen lesion |
| Glossopharyngeal neuralgia | CN IX | Jugular foramen lesion |
| **Horner syndrome** (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) | Sympathetic chain | Post-styloid lesion disrupting cervical sympathetics |
| **First bite syndrome** | Auriculotemporal nerve injury | Post-sympathectomy parotid spasm |
| **Trismus** | Pterygoid muscle or CN V3 involvement | Pre-styloid or infratemporal extension |

---

## Imaging Evaluation

### Computed Tomography (CT)
CT is the **first-line imaging** modality for PPS lesions. The key concept is the **direction of ICA displacement**, which definitively localises the tumour compartment:

| Finding | Pre-Styloid Tumour | Post-Styloid Tumour |
|---|---|---|
| **ICA displacement** | Posterolateral displacement of ICA | Anteromedial displacement of ICA |
| **Parapharyngeal fat** | Bowing and compression of fat medially and posteriorly | Fat displaced anteriorly |
| **Parotid relationship** | Contiguous with deep parotid lobe | Separate from parotid; fat plane may be present |
| **Enhancement** | Variable; salivary tumours enhance moderately | Intense blush = paraganglioma |

Additional CT assessment:
- Bone windows for skull base involvement, jugular foramen erosion, or mandibular invasion
- Calcification (rare; may suggest paraganglioma or calcified PA)
- Irregular margins or bone erosion suggesting malignancy

### MRI
MRI provides superior soft tissue characterisation and is complementary to CT:

| Sequence | Information |
|---|---|
| T1W | Fat signal; delineates fat planes; excellent for deep lobe parotid extension |
| T2W | High signal in PA (characteristic); intermediate in neurogenic tumours |
| Post-gadolinium T1W | Intense enhancement in paraganglioma; moderate in PA; perineural spread in ACC |
| MRA | Non-invasive vascular mapping; relationship of tumour to ICA |

**Characteristic MRI appearances:**
- **Pleomorphic adenoma:** T2 hyperintense, well-circumscribed, heterogeneous enhancement
- **Paraganglioma:** T2 intermediate with **salt-and-pepper** appearance (flow voids); avid enhancement
- **Schwannoma:** T2 bright, well-capsulated, moderate enhancement; may show central cystic degeneration

### Angiography
- **Conventional angiography or CT angiography** indicated for suspected vascular lesions (paraganglioma, carotid body tumour)
- Demonstrates tumour blush and precise vascular anatomy
- **Preoperative embolisation** (24–48 hours before surgery) reduces intraoperative blood loss in vascular lesions by 50–70%
- Balloon occlusion testing of the ICA if sacrifice of ICA is contemplated

### Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC)
- **Intraoral FNAC** is hazardous — risk of seeding, contamination, and haematoma in the PPS
- **Transcervical FNAC under CT guidance** is safe and provides tissue diagnosis
- Contraindicated for suspected paraganglioma (haemorrhage risk)
- Useful for lymphoma diagnosis (flow cytometry can be performed)

## Surgical Approaches to the Parapharyngeal Space

### Decision Framework
The choice of approach depends on:
1. Tumour size (< or > 3 cm)
2. Pre-styloid vs post-styloid location
3. Parotid deep lobe involvement
4. Skull base extension
5. Vascular involvement
6. Malignancy vs benignity

---

### 1. Transcervical Approach (Most Common)

**Indications:**
- Benign post-styloid lesions (neurogenic, paraganglioma without skull base extension)
- Small-to-medium pre-styloid lesions without deep parotid connection
- Lesions below the styloid process

**Incision:** Horizontal neck incision in a skin crease at the level of the hyoid, extended along the anterior border of the sternocleidomastoid

**Technique:**
1. Dissect through platysma; reflect skin flaps
2. Retract submandibular gland anterosuperiorly
3. Identify and protect CN XII crossing the carotid sheath
4. Ligate and divide the posterior belly of digastric if needed for superior access
5. Develop the plane between the carotid sheath (posterolateral) and the PPS mass (anteromedial)
6. Finger dissection and blunt enucleation of the tumour
7. Ligate and divide feeding vessels

**Advantages:** Excellent exposure of the carotid sheath; identifies CN IX–XII; avoids facial nerve dissection
**Limitations:** Limited superior exposure; cannot adequately address large pre-styloid tumours with deep parotid connection

---

### 2. Transparotid-Transcervical Approach

**Indications:**
- Pre-styloid tumours with demonstrated deep lobe parotid involvement
- Pleomorphic adenoma of the deep parotid lobe extending into PPS
- Any PPS tumour arising from or contiguous with the parotid gland

**Technique:**
1. Combine the transcervical incision with a transparotid incision (Blair or modified Blair)
2. Standard parotidectomy: identify the main trunk of CN VII at the tragal pointer
3. Dissect the superficial lobe from the facial nerve branches
4. Identify the deep lobe tumour as it passes between the mandibular ramus and the stylomandibular ligament
5. Divide the stylomandibular ligament to widen the stylomandibular tunnel and deliver the tumour
6. Combine with cervical dissection for inferior pole control

**Key Step:** Division of the **stylomandibular ligament** is the single most important manoeuvre to mobilise the deep lobe — without this step, the tumour cannot be delivered through the stylomandibular tunnel.

**Advantages:** Direct access to deep lobe; facial nerve identification and protection; oncologically sound for parotid-origin tumours
**Complications:** Facial nerve paresis/palsy (temporary 15–30%; permanent 2–5%); Frey syndrome (auriculotemporal nerve injury — parotid gustatory sweating)

---

### 3. Transoral Approach

**Indications (very limited):**
- Small, benign, medial pre-styloid lesions (rare indication)
- Selected minor salivary gland tumours with minimal lateral extension
- Not recommended as primary approach for most PPS tumours

**Technique:** Incision through the posterior tonsillar pillar or lateral pharyngeal wall; blunt dissection to reach the PPS

**Disadvantages:**
- Extremely limited access and poor visualisation
- Cannot control the carotid sheath or CN IX–XII
- Risk of wound contamination and infection
- High risk of incomplete resection
- Risk of haemorrhage without proximal vascular control
- Currently largely replaced by endoscopic-assisted or robotic transoral approaches for highly selected cases

---

### 4. Transcervical-Transmandibular Approach (Mandibulotomy / Mandibular Swing)

**Indications:**
- Large PPS tumours (> 5–6 cm) with skull base extension
- Malignant tumours requiring wide-field resection
- Recurrent tumours requiring extensive re-exploration
- Tumours requiring en bloc resection with mandibular involvement

#### Paramedian Mandibulotomy (Standard)

**Incision:** Lower lip split + chin incision + horizontal neck incision; creates a "mandibular swing" to open the oropharynx widely.

**Osteotomy site:** Paramedian — between the canine and the lateral incisor (to preserve the mental nerve and avoid the inferior alveolar nerve). The tooth is extracted preoperatively or the osteotomy is placed just medial to the mental foramen.

**Steps:**
1. Lower lip is split in the midline
2. Periosteal elevation to expose the mandibular body
3. Paramedian osteotomy using an oscillating saw or reciprocating saw
4. The mandibular segment is swung laterally, hinging on the temporomandibular joint
5. The floor of mouth, tongue base, and oropharynx are opened medially
6. Excellent panoramic access to the entire PPS from skull base to hyoid
7. Closure: plate fixation across the osteotomy; layered closure of floor of mouth; lip repair

**Alternative osteotomy sites:**
- **Midline mandibulotomy:** Between the two central incisors (one tooth extracted); simpler but may cause instability
- **Lateral mandibulotomy:** Posterior to the mental foramen; used when tumour extends to the angle

**Complications specific to mandibulotomy:** Osteotomy non-union; dental malocclusion; plate exposure; orocutaneous fistula; lip numbness (mental nerve); trismus

---

### 5. Infratemporal Fossa Approach (Fisch Type A)

**Indications:**
- Post-styloid tumours with jugular foramen involvement (glomus jugulare, glomus vagale extending superiorly)
- High post-styloid tumours at the skull base
- Tumours with intradural extension

**Key steps (Fisch Type A):**
1. Postauricular incision extended into the neck
2. Mastoidectomy and removal of the posterior external auditory canal
3. Anterior transposition of the facial nerve (out of the mastoid segment)
4. Subtotal petrosectomy exposing the jugular bulb and lower cranial nerves
5. Combined neck dissection for inferior tumour control
6. Packing of the Eustachian tube (to prevent CSF leak/meningitis)

**Trade-off:** Maximum skull base exposure with guaranteed conductive hearing loss and risk of facial nerve injury

---

### 6. Endoscopic-Assisted Approaches

- **Transoral robotic surgery (TORS)** has been applied to selected PPS tumours as an adjunct to transcervical dissection
- **Endoscopic transnasal** approaches for superior PPS lesions near the skull base (emerging; specialist centres)
- Role remains investigational for most PPS tumours

---

### Summary: Approach Selection

| Tumour Characteristics | Recommended Approach |
|---|---|
| Small–medium benign post-styloid (neurogenic, paraganglioma < 3 cm) | Transcervical alone |
| Pre-styloid with deep parotid lobe connection (pleomorphic adenoma) | Transparotid-transcervical |
| Pre-styloid without parotid involvement, < 3 cm | Transcervical alone |
| Large benign/malignant, skull base extension | Transcervical-transmandibular (mandibulotomy) |
| Post-styloid at jugular foramen (glomus jugulare) | Infratemporal fossa (Fisch Type A) |
| Selected small medial benign lesions | Transoral (selected cases only) |

## Complications of PPS Surgery

| Complication | Mechanism | Prevention/Management |
|---|---|---|
| **CN IX–XII palsy** | Traction, devascularisation, or inadvertent sectioning during dissection | Meticulous identification and dissection; neuromonitoring |
| **Frey syndrome** | Injury to auriculotemporal nerve → aberrant regeneration to sweat glands; sweating with eating | Botulinum toxin injections; antiperspirant; barrier techniques at closure |
| **First bite syndrome** | Sympathetic denervation of parotid → exaggerated first bite pain after sympathectomy during post-styloid dissection | Reassurance; usually self-limiting over 12–18 months; botulinum toxin can help |
| **Carotid artery injury** | Inadvertent arteriotomy or avulsion during tumour dissection | Pre-operative vascular mapping; proximal control before tumour dissection; vascular surgery backup |
| **Horner syndrome** | Sympathetic chain injury in post-styloid dissection | Ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis; no specific treatment; partial recovery in ~50% |
| **Haemorrhage** | Venous (IJV) or arterial (ICA, ECA) injury | Pre-operative embolisation for vascular lesions; careful haemostasis |
| **Pharyngocutaneous fistula** | Inadvertent pharyngotomy (if transoral or transmandibular approach) | Watertight closure; avoid contaminating the neck wound |
| **Trismus** | Pterygoid damage or fibrosis; mandibulotomy-related | Physiotherapy; early jaw exercises post-operatively |
| **Facial nerve injury** | During parotid dissection (transparotid approach) | Nerve monitoring; meticulous dissection; identify main trunk first |

## Malignant PPS Tumours

### Imaging Features Suggesting Malignancy
- **Irregular, poorly defined margins** (as opposed to well-circumscribed benign lesions)
- **Bone erosion** of the skull base, pterygoid plates, or mandible
- **Infiltrative pattern** with loss of fat planes
- **Perineural spread** along CN V3 (MRI: enhancement and enlargement of foramina)
- **Regional lymphadenopathy**
- **Heterogeneous internal architecture** with necrosis

### Management Principles for Malignant PPS Tumours
1. **Complete staging:** CT chest/abdomen, PET-CT for distant disease
2. **FNAC confirmation** (transcervical CT-guided) to guide treatment planning
3. **Surgery:** Wide-field resection with adequate margins; usually requires transmandibular or infratemporal approach
4. **Neck dissection:** Selective or comprehensive depending on primary tumour type and N-stage
5. **Adjuvant radiotherapy:** For positive margins, perineural invasion, angiolymphatic invasion, high-grade histology
6. **Skull base involvement:** May require neurosurgical collaboration; intracranial extension assessed pre-operatively with MRI

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
>
> 1. **ICA displacement direction is the key imaging sign:** Pre-styloid tumours displace the ICA **posterolaterally**; post-styloid tumours displace the ICA **anteromedially** — this single finding reliably localises the tumour and guides surgical planning.
> 2. **Never biopsy a pulsatile PPS mass** transorally — it may be a paraganglioma or aberrant ICA; always image first.
> 3. **Stylomandibular ligament division** is the critical step in the transparotid-transcervical approach — without it the deep lobe tumour cannot be delivered through the stylomandibular tunnel.
> 4. **First bite syndrome** (lancinating jaw pain on first bite of a meal) is a specific post-operative complication of post-styloid PPS surgery due to sympathetic denervation; it is largely self-limiting and should not be confused with recurrence.
> 5. **Pleomorphic adenoma** is the most common PPS tumour (~40%); it arises from the deep lobe of the parotid or ectopic minor salivary rests in the pre-styloid space; rupture during surgery seeds the field and leads to multifocal recurrence — avoid capsule rupture.
> 6. **Paragangliomas** in the PPS are highly vascular; pre-operative embolisation 24–48 hours before surgery reduces blood loss; conventional angiography is mandatory to map feeding vessels and define the tumour blush.
> 7. **Transcervical approach** is the workhorse for most benign post-styloid lesions and is adequate for the majority of PPS tumours (80%); the transparotid extension is added only when parotid deep lobe connection is confirmed.
> 8. **Mandibulotomy/mandibular swing** provides the widest access for large or skull base-extending PPS tumours; the paramedian osteotomy site (between lateral incisor and canine) is preferred to preserve the inferior alveolar nerve and mental nerve.
> 9. **Kuet systematic review (2015):** 82% benign, most common lesion is pleomorphic adenoma (29%); imaging variables most predictive of malignancy are irregular margins, bone erosion, and infiltrative pattern.
> 10. **Horner syndrome** preoperatively in a PPS mass indicates post-styloid sympathetic chain involvement — it helps localise the tumour to the post-styloid compartment and suggests neural origin.
