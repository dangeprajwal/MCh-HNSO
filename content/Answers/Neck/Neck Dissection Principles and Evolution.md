## Introduction

The cervical lymphatic system is the primary route of regional spread for squamous cell carcinoma of the head and neck. The management of neck metastases — whether clinically apparent or occult — has evolved from the morbid radical en bloc resection described by Crile in 1906 to the functionally sparing, level-based selective dissections that are today's standard. This evolution is underpinned by anatomical mapping of lymphatic drainage patterns, progressive understanding of oncological principles, and Level I trial evidence defining when and how the neck should be treated.

## Historical Evolution of Neck Dissection

### 1906 — Crile: Radical Neck Dissection

George Crile at the Cleveland Clinic described the **radical neck dissection (RND)**, reporting 132 cases in 1906 in JAMA. His procedure removed:
- All lymph node–bearing tissue of levels I–V
- Sternocleidomastoid muscle (SCM)
- Internal jugular vein (IJV)
- Spinal accessory nerve (CN XI)
- Submandibular gland and tail of parotid
- Cervical sensory nerves

Crile's core principle — **en bloc resection of the lymphatic compartment** — remains valid today. However, the price was significant: shoulder drop, facial oedema (bilateral IJV sacrifice), and cosmetic deformity.

### 1933 — Blair and Brown: Modification of Incisions

Blair and Brown described modifications to skin incision design, including the standard utility incisions still in use (MacFee, Schobinger), reducing wound complications and improving exposure.

### 1963 — Suárez: Anatomical Basis of Functional Dissection

Osvaldo Suárez of Córdoba, Argentina published "El problema de las metastasis linfáticas y alejadas del cáncer de laringe e hipofaringe" (*Rev Otorrinolaringol*. 1963;23:83–99) — describing the fascial envelope concept:

- The cervical lymphatics are enclosed within **fascial compartments** (investing, pretracheal, prevertebral fascia)
- En bloc removal of the fascial envelope containing lymph nodes could achieve oncological clearance **without sacrificing** SCM, IJV, or CN XI
- This concept was published in Spanish and initially unrecognised in the English-language literature; Suárez is considered the "often-forgotten father" of functional neck dissection (Ferlito 2004)

### 1967 — Bocca and Pignataro: Functional/Conservative Neck Dissection

Ettore Bocca and Oreste Pignataro from Ferrara, Italy published the landmark English-language paper in *Annals of Otology, Rhinology & Laryngology* (1967), building on Suárez's fascial principles:

- Coined the term **"conservation technique in radical neck dissection"**
- Preserved SCM, IJV, and CN XI in 843 patients — with equivalent regional control to classical RND
- This became the **Modified Radical Neck Dissection (MRND)** concept
- Demonstrated that functional preservation did not compromise oncological outcome when lymph nodes were not directly adherent to these structures

### 1988 — Byers: Selective Neck Dissection

Robert Byers at M.D. Anderson proposed that only the lymph node levels at highest risk for each primary site need to be removed:

- Oral cavity → levels I–III (supraomohyoid ND)
- Oropharynx/hypopharynx → levels II–IV
- Larynx → levels II–IV (lateral ND)
- Thyroid → level VI ± III–IV (central compartment ND)

This evolved into the **Selective Neck Dissection (SND)** concept — preserving not only SCM/IJV/CN XI but also lymph node groups not at risk.

### 1991 — American Academy of Otolaryngology Committee: Standardising Terminology

The AAO-HNS committee (Medina 1989; Robbins 1991) introduced standardised nomenclature:
- Radical ND
- Modified radical ND (MRND Types I, II, III)
- Selective ND with descriptor levels: e.g., SND (I–III), SND (II–IV), SND (VI)
- Extended ND

### 2002 — Robbins et al.: Level Classification Update (AAO-HNS/AHNS)

The pivotal 2002 consensus paper in *Arch Otolaryngol Head Neck Surg* introduced **sublevels** and codified anatomical boundaries:

| Level | Sublevel | Contents | Anatomical Boundaries |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | IA (submental) | Submental nodes | Bilateral anterior belly digastric, hyoid inferiorly |
| I | IB (submandibular) | Submandibular nodes, SMG | Anterior belly digastric, stylohyoid, body of mandible |
| II | IIA | Upper jugular nodes (anterior/medial to CN XI) | Skull base to hyoid; SCM ant, lateral, posterior — anterior to CN XI |
| II | IIB | Upper jugular nodes (posterior/lateral to CN XI) | Same vertical extent — posterior to CN XI |
| III | — | Mid-jugular nodes | Hyoid to cricoid; between SCM ant and lateral border sternohyoid |
| IV | — | Lower jugular nodes | Cricoid to clavicle; between SCM ant and lateral border sternohyoid |
| V | VA | Posterior triangle (upper) | Posterior triangle above transverse cervical vessels; to skull base |
| V | VB | Posterior triangle (lower) | Posterior triangle below transverse cervical vessels |
| VI | — | Central compartment | Hyoid to sternal notch; between carotid sheaths bilaterally (prelaryngeal, pretracheal, paratracheal) |
| VII | — | Superior mediastinal | Below sternal notch to innominate artery (added in 2008 consensus) |

### 2008 — Robbins et al.: Consensus Statement Refinements

The 2008 AAO-HNS consensus statement (*Arch Otolaryngol Head Neck Surg* 2008;134:536) refined:
- Boundaries between levels I/II and III/IV/VI
- Added **Level VII** (superior mediastinal nodes)
- Standardised pathological specimen submission protocol

## Types of Neck Dissection

### 1. Radical Neck Dissection (RND)

- Removes all 5 levels (I–V) + SCM + IJV + CN XI
- **Indication**: massive nodal disease with direct invasion of SCM, IJV, or CN XI; fixed nodal disease where structures cannot be separated
- Now rarely performed as primary surgery; reserved for salvage or direct invasion scenarios
- Morbidity: severe shoulder dysfunction (dropped shoulder, winging scapula), facial oedema (bilateral RND), cosmetic deformity

### 2. Modified Radical Neck Dissection (MRND)

- Removes all 5 levels (I–V) while **preserving one or more** of SCM, IJV, CN XI
- **MRND Type I**: preserves CN XI only
- **MRND Type II**: preserves CN XI + IJV
- **MRND Type III** (Bocca/functional): preserves CN XI + IJV + SCM
- **Indication**: clinically N+ neck (N1–N3) without direct invasion of preserved structures; elective treatment of high-risk N0 neck when bilateral levels needed
- MRND Type III functional equivalent to RND in terms of regional control for N1 and most N2 disease

### 3. Selective Neck Dissection (SND)

Preserves all three non-lymphatic structures (SCM, IJV, CN XI) and removes only the at-risk nodal levels:

| SND Type | Levels Dissected | Primary Site |
|---|---|---|
| Supraomohyoid (SOHND) | I–III | Oral cavity |
| Lateral ND | II–IV | Oropharynx, hypopharynx, larynx |
| Posterolateral ND | II–V | Cutaneous/parotid melanoma, parotid SCC |
| Central compartment ND | VI ± VII | Thyroid, subglottic larynx, cervical oesophagus |
| SND (I–IV) | I–IV | Oral cavity with high-risk features |

**Indication**: elective/prophylactic neck treatment in cN0 disease; pathological staging; well-selected cN+ (N1 without extranodal extension)

### 4. Extended Neck Dissection

- Removes one or more additional lymph node groups **or** non-lymphatic structures beyond standard RND
- Examples: parapharyngeal nodes, retropharyngeal nodes, carotid artery resection, skin, parotid gland
- Indicated when tumour directly involves these structures (e.g., Delphian node, retropharyngeal involvement in hypopharynx cancer, carotid encasement)

## Oncological Principles of Neck Dissection

### En Bloc Resection and Fascial Compartments

- The cervical fascia acts as a **natural oncological barrier** — lymph nodes and lymphatic channels lie within fascial envelopes
- En bloc removal of the fascial compartment (the Suárez principle) achieves clearance without piecemeal excision
- Cutting through or leaving positive fascial margins increases locoregional failure

### Lymphatic Compartment Dissection

- Lymph drainage of head and neck follows **predictable, hierarchical pathways**
- Level-based dissection removes the at-risk compartment while sparing uninvolved compartments
- Shah (1990): 1,081 patients with 1,119 neck dissections confirmed that **levels I, II, III** are at highest risk from oral cavity primaries; **levels II, III, IV** from oropharynx/hypopharynx/larynx

### Skip Metastasis

- Defined as metastasis to a non-first-echelon level with no disease in intervening levels
- Incidence: ~3–5% in oral cancer (e.g., Level IV positive with negative Level III)
- Higher in hypopharynx and subglottic larynx (Level IV/VI)
- Clinical relevance: argues against super-selective approaches without staging data; supports SLNB for unexpected drainage mapping

### Clearance Margins and Extranodal Extension

- Extranodal extension (ENE) — tumour extending beyond the lymph node capsule — is the single strongest predictor of locoregional failure and survival
- ENE upgrades pathological staging to pN3b in AJCC 8th edition
- ENE is an absolute indication for adjuvant chemoradiation (RTOG 9501 / EORTC 22931)

## Patterns of Cervical Metastasis by Primary Site

Based on Shah's landmark mapping study (1990) and subsequent selective dissection data:

| Primary Site | Primary Levels at Risk | Skip/Additional Levels |
|---|---|---|
| Oral tongue | I → II → III | Level IV rarely (skip ~3%); contralateral with crossing/near-midline |
| Floor of mouth | I → II → III | Bilateral Level I/II for central FOM lesions |
| Buccal mucosa | I → II → III | Rare Level IV/V |
| Retromolar trigone | II → III → IV | Level I in ~20% |
| Hard palate | I → II | Rare deep spread |
| Oropharynx (tonsil/BOT) | II → III | Level IV; contralateral II/III common |
| Hypopharynx | II → III → IV | Level VI (pyriform apex); retropharyngeal |
| Supraglottic larynx | II → III → IV | Level VI (subglottic extension) |
| Glottic larynx (T1-T2) | Low risk cN0; II–III if N+ | Level VI with subglottic spread |
| Nasopharynx (NPC) | II → V (bilateral) | Retropharyngeal (Level VII); Level V prominent |
| Thyroid | VI → III → IV | Level VII (superior mediastinal); Level I rare |
| Parotid/cutaneous | II → V (posterolateral) | Intraparotid nodes; Level I |

### Level IIB in Oral Cancer

- Level IIB (posterosuperior to CN XI) is at low risk (<10%) in oral cavity cancers without bulky Level IIA disease
- Dissecting Level IIB risks CN XI injury at the point of entry into SCM
- Many selective protocols spare Level IIB when Level IIA is clinically and radiologically negative

## Patterns of Invasion in Oral Cancer (Shah's Classification)

### Local Invasion Patterns

**1. Submucosal (Superficial) Spread**
- Tumour tracks submucosally beyond the visible mucosal margin
- Argues for frozen-section confirmation of mucosal and deep margins
- More common in buccal mucosa and floor of mouth

**2. Bone Involvement — Mandibular Invasion Patterns**
- *Erosive (pushing) pattern*: broad front of invasion with pressure resorption — lower grade, more amenable to marginal mandibulectomy
- *Infiltrative (permeative) pattern*: irregular infiltration through marrow spaces, perineural spread, higher grade — requires segmental mandibulectomy

**3. Depth of Invasion (DOI)**
- Defined as depth below the adjacent normal mucosal basement membrane to the deepest point of invasion
- DOI >5 mm: occult metastasis risk >20%; DOI >10 mm: risk >35%
- Incorporated into AJCC 8th edition T-staging (T1: DOI ≤5 mm; upstages with increasing DOI)
- DOI is the **single most important predictor** of cervical metastasis in oral cancer

**4. Perineural Invasion (PNI)**
- Tumour cells tracking along nerve sheaths (not necessarily within the nerve)
- Identified on H&E by tumour adjacent to epineurium or intraneural permeation
- Incidence: ~30–40% of oral SCC
- Associated with: ↑ locoregional recurrence, ↑ distant metastasis, ↑ occult nodal disease
- Absolute indication for adjuvant radiation
- Named nerves at risk: lingual nerve, inferior alveolar nerve (V3), facial nerve branches

**5. Lymphovascular Invasion (LVI)**
- Tumour cells within lymphatic or vascular spaces
- Detected on H&E ± D2-40 (podoplanin) immunostaining for lymphatics
- LVI significantly predicts occult nodal metastasis independent of T-stage
- Part of the histological risk assessment profile (along with DOI, PNI, pattern of invasion)

**6. WHO Pattern of Invasion (Bryne/Jakobsson Score)**
- Grade 1: well-defined pushing border (lowest risk)
- Grade 2: broad, irregular infiltrating cords
- Grade 3: small irregular islands and cords
- Grade 4: diffuse individual cell infiltration (highest risk — worst prognosis)
- Pattern of invasion grade correlates with occult metastasis rate and locoregional control

### Composite Risk Stratification for Cervical Metastasis

| Risk Factor | Low Risk | High Risk |
|---|---|---|
| DOI | <5 mm | >10 mm |
| T-stage | T1 | T3–T4 |
| Pattern of invasion | Grade 1–2 | Grade 3–4 |
| Perineural invasion | Absent | Present |
| Lymphovascular invasion | Absent | Present |
| Histological grade | Well differentiated | Poorly differentiated |

Composite high-risk profiles (multiple adverse features) carry >40% occult metastasis risk, regardless of primary site or T-stage.

## Elective vs Therapeutic Neck Dissection — Evidence

### The 20% Rule

Weiss et al. (1994) established that **elective neck treatment is indicated when occult metastasis risk exceeds 20%** — below this threshold, morbidity of treatment outweighs benefit.

### D'Cruz RCT — NEJM 2015 (Level I Evidence)

- **Design**: Prospective RCT, Tata Memorial Hospital
- **Patients**: 596 cT1-T2 cN0 oral SCC
- **Arms**: Elective ND (END, n=245) vs Therapeutic ND — neck treated only on clinical recurrence (TND, n=251)
- **Results**:
  - Overall survival at 3 years: END **80.0%** vs TND **67.5%** (HR 0.64, p=0.01)
  - Disease-free survival: END **69.5%** vs TND **45.9%** (p<0.001)
  - Occult metastasis in END specimens: **30%**
  - Neck recurrence in TND arm: 45.9%
- **Conclusion**: END is the standard of care for T1-T2 cN0 oral cancer; therapeutic ND alone is no longer acceptable

### When Observation May Still Be Considered

- Superficial tumours (DOI <3–4 mm)
- Favourable subsites: hard palate, superficial buccal mucosa
- Occult metastasis risk truly <15%
- Requires meticulous ultrasound-guided follow-up every 6–8 weeks
- Following the D'Cruz trial, the threshold for elective treatment has effectively dropped below the 20% classical threshold

## Micrometastasis, Isolated Tumour Cells, and Clinical Significance

| Finding | Size | Staging | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| Macrometastasis | >2 mm | pN+ | Standard adjuvant decision-making |
| Micrometastasis | 0.2–2 mm | pN1(mi) | Consider adjuvant RT; same prognosis implications as macrometastasis |
| Isolated tumour cells (ITC) | ≤0.2 mm | pN0(i+) | Clinical significance debated; most centres observe unless other adverse features |

Micrometastases detected on enhanced step-serial sectioning + immunohistochemistry (IHC with pan-cytokeratin) carry intermediate prognosis. Current evidence does not mandate adjuvant treatment for ITC alone, but MDT discussion is warranted.

## Quality Metrics in Neck Dissection

### Level IIB Sparing

- Routine Level IIB dissection risks CN XI injury at the posterior triangle entry point
- Level IIB involvement in cN0 oral cavity cancer: <10%
- Most centres spare IIB when Level IIA is negative on clinical exam and imaging — reduces shoulder morbidity without compromising regional control
- Mandatory when Level IIA is positive (direct lymphatic continuity)

### CN XI Preservation and Shoulder Function

- CN XI injury produces: dropped shoulder, winging scapula, chronic shoulder pain, reduced abduction
- Functional neck dissection (MRND Type III or SND) preserves CN XI in virtually all selective cases
- Shoulder dysfunction even with CN XI anatomical preservation (traction injury) occurs in 10–30% — physiotherapy essential
- MRND Type I (sacrifice IJV + SCM, preserve XI): better shoulder outcomes than classical RND

### Chyle Leak and Thoracic Duct

- Left-sided low neck dissection (Level IV) risks thoracic duct injury
- Prevention: dissect deep to omohyoid; ligate all fatty tissue at the confluence of left IJV and subclavian vein
- Management: conservative (medium-chain triglyceride diet, pressure dressing) vs re-exploration and ligation

## Post-CRT Neck Management

For clinically N+ patients receiving definitive chemoradiation:
- PET-CT at 10–12 weeks post-CRT: residual disease vs complete response
- Complete PET response (Deauville 1–3): observation; planned neck dissection abandoned (Mehanna NEJM 2016 — PET-CT guided watchful waiting non-inferior to planned ND)
- Incomplete response or progressive disease on PET: salvage neck dissection (MRND or SND depending on residual bulk)
- Salvage neck dissection post-CRT carries higher complication rates (wound breakdown, chyle leak, fistula)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Crile's 1906 en bloc principle remains valid — what has evolved is how broadly and which non-lymphatic structures must be sacrificed.
> 2. The Suárez fascial envelope concept (1963) is the anatomical justification for functional ND — the fascia, not the structures within it, is the oncological barrier.
> 3. DOI is the most powerful predictor of cervical metastasis in oral cancer — a 10 mm DOI oral tongue tumour has >35% occult metastasis risk regardless of T-stage.
> 4. Level IIA and Level IIB are separated by CN XI — dissecting IIB unnecessarily risks the nerve; always assess IIA first.
> 5. Pattern of invasion (Grade 3–4) and LVI are adverse histological features that independently predict occult metastasis and should prompt elective neck treatment even when DOI is borderline.
> 6. The D'Cruz NEJM 2015 trial settled the END vs observation debate — END improves overall survival by ~13% at 3 years in T1-T2 cN0 oral cancer.
> 7. NPC is unique — it metastasises primarily to Level II and Level V (posterior triangle), and bilateral involvement is present in >50% at diagnosis; RND is rarely appropriate.
> 8. Thyroid cancer metastasises first to Level VI (central compartment) before lateral levels — central compartment dissection is the first surgical step for nodal disease.
> 9. Perineural invasion along named nerves (lingual, inferior alveolar) may produce skull base spread without clinical nodes — MRI perineural tracking is mandatory before staging as N0.
> 10. For post-CRT neck management, PET-CT at 10–12 weeks has transformed practice — a complete metabolic response avoids planned neck dissection in ~80% of patients (Mehanna 2016).
