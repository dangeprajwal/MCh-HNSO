## Part A: Biology, Genetics, and Epidemiology

### Paraganglion Cells and Neural Crest Origin

Paragangliomas arise from paraganglion cells — specialised neuroectodermal cells derived from the neural crest that migrate during embryogenesis to form chemoreceptor and baroreceptor organs distributed along the great vessels and cranial nerves. Shah's Chapter 14 describes these cells as belonging to the diffuse neuroendocrine system (DNES), closely related to chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla. Head and neck paragangliomas are predominantly non-chromaffin (parasympathetic) and rarely functional (secretory).

### Incidence and Hereditary Disease

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Incidence** | 1 in 30,000 — 100,000; overall rare |
| **Most common site** | Carotid body (~60% of head and neck paragangliomas) |
| **Sex predilection** | Female predominance in sporadic cases |
| **Sporadic vs hereditary** | ~70% sporadic; ~30% hereditary (familial) |
| **Bilateral CBT** | ~5% sporadic; ~30-40% in hereditary cases |
| **Multifocal (synchronous)** | Up to 10% overall; 25-50% in SDH mutation carriers |
| **Malignancy rate** | 5-10% for carotid body tumour; ~30-40% for SDHB mutations |

### Genetic Mutations — Succinate Dehydrogenase (SDH)

The majority of hereditary paragangliomas are caused by germline mutations in genes encoding subunits of succinate dehydrogenase (SDH), a mitochondrial enzyme complex:

| Gene | Inheritance | Key Features |
|------|------------|--------------|
| **SDHD** (11q23) | Paternal imprinting (autosomal dominant, paternally transmitted) | Multifocal head and neck paragangliomas; low malignancy risk |
| **SDHB** (1p36) | Autosomal dominant | Higher malignancy risk (30-40%); extra-adrenal paragangliomas; thoracic/abdominal disease |
| **SDHC** (1q21) | Autosomal dominant | Predominantly head and neck; gastric stromal tumours (Carney-Stratakis) |
| **SDHA** (5p15) | Autosomal dominant | Rarest; pheochromocytoma risk |
| **MAX, TMEM127, VHL** | Various | Pheochromocytoma-paraganglioma syndrome overlap |

Shah's Chapter 14 emphasises: **genetic testing for SDH mutations should be offered to all patients** with head and neck paraganglioma, particularly those with bilateral tumours, multifocal disease, family history, or age <40 years. SDHB mutation status is the most important predictor of malignancy.

## Part B: Sites and Anatomy

### Sites of Head and Neck Paragangliomas

| Site | Origin | Eponym | Features |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| **Carotid body** | Carotid bifurcation chemoreceptor | Glomus caroticum | Most common; subadventitial at bifurcation |
| **Vagal paraganglioma** | Ganglia of the vagus nerve | Glomus vagale | Second most common; high CN morbidity |
| **Jugulotympanic** | Jugular bulb / middle ear | Glomus jugulare / tympanicum | Most common overall; skull base extension |
| **Aortopulmonary** | Aortopulmonary bodies | — | Rare; mediastinal location |
| **Laryngeal** | Superior laryngeal paraganglia | — | Very rare |

### Carotid Body Anatomy

The carotid body (Zuckerkandl body) is a small oval chemoreceptor organ, approximately 3-6 mm in diameter in its normal state, situated in the adventitia of the posterior medial surface of the common carotid artery bifurcation. Key anatomical points relevant to surgery:

- **Location:** Nestled at the bifurcation of the common carotid artery (CCA) into the external carotid artery (ECA) and internal carotid artery (ICA)
- **Attachment:** Adherent to the adventitia of the carotid bifurcation via Mayer's ligament — a fibrous band that anchors the tumour and must be divided early in surgery
- **Blood supply:** Predominantly from the ascending pharyngeal artery (a branch of the ECA) and small branches from the carotid bifurcation
- **Nerve supply:** Hering's nerve (branch of glossopharyngeal nerve CN IX) — the sinus nerve of the carotid body; also sympathetic fibres from the superior cervical ganglion
- **Function:** Peripheral chemoreceptor sensing oxygen tension, CO₂, and pH — triggers respiratory adjustments

## Part C: Clinical Presentation

### Carotid Body Tumour

The typical presentation is a **slowly enlarging, painless pulsatile lateral neck mass** at the level of the carotid bifurcation (angle of the jaw, anterior to sternocleidomastoid). Key clinical features:

| Sign/Symptom | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Fontaine sign** | Horizontal mobility of the mass (can be moved side to side but NOT vertically — tethered to carotid bifurcation) — pathognomonic |
| **Pulsatility and bruit** | Transmitted pulsation from the carotid; bruit audible on auscultation in ~50% |
| **Duration** | Often present for years — slow-growing tumours (median doubling time 4-7 years) |
| **Hypertensive crises** | Rare; only if functional (secretory) tumour — <3% of head and neck paragangliomas |
| **Cranial nerve deficits** | Late feature with large tumours: vagus (hoarseness, dysphagia), hypoglossal (tongue deviation), sympathetic (Horner syndrome) |
| **Dysphagia/airway compromise** | Large tumours displacing the pharynx or larynx |

### Vagal Paraganglioma

Vagal paragangliomas arise from paraganglia of the vagus nerve, most commonly from the nodose ganglion. They present as:
- **Painless parapharyngeal mass** pushing the tonsil medially (medial displacement of the tonsil and lateral pharyngeal wall)
- **Vocal cord palsy** (unilateral) — earliest and most consistent finding due to involvement of the recurrent laryngeal nerve or vagus itself; often present at diagnosis
- Higher cranial nerve morbidity than carotid body tumours — sacrifice of the vagus nerve at surgery is frequently unavoidable
- Higher rate of contralateral vagal palsy risk makes bilateral vagal paragangliomas particularly challenging

## Part D: Evaluation and Investigations

### Initial Assessment

All patients require:

1. **Complete clinical examination** — neck, cranial nerves (IX-XII), Horner syndrome, indirect laryngoscopy (vocal cord mobility), auscultation for bruit
2. **Urinary/plasma catecholamines** — 24-hour urinary catecholamines, metanephrines, and VMA; plasma fractionated metanephrines — mandatory to exclude functional tumour before surgery or embolisation (hypertensive crisis risk)

### Imaging

| Modality | Findings | Role |
|---------|---------|------|
| **CT Angiography (CTA)** | Lyre sign (splaying of ICA and ECA by the mass at the bifurcation); intense enhancement; relationship to carotid vessels for Shamblin classification | First-line; surgical planning |
| **MRI** | "Salt and pepper" appearance — multiple flow voids (pepper) on T2 with foci of high signal haemorrhage (salt); T2 bright mass; gadolinium enhancement | Tissue characterisation; soft tissue detail; cranial nerve involvement |
| **Doppler Ultrasound** | Highly vascular mass at carotid bifurcation; colour flow confirms vascularity; evaluates internal architecture | Useful screening tool; guided biopsy is contraindicated |
| **Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA)** | Gold standard for vascular anatomy; "tumour blush" at bifurcation; identifies feeding vessels; performed at same session as embolisation | Pre-embolisation; large/complex tumours |
| **Functional imaging** | See below | Multifocal/hereditary disease workup |

**The Lyre Sign (Lira Sign):** On CTA or DSA, the ICA and ECA are splayed apart by the mass at the bifurcation, resembling the strings of a lyre — a characteristic finding highly suggestive of carotid body tumour.

### Functional Imaging for Multifocal/Hereditary Disease

| Modality | Tracer | Use |
|---------|--------|-----|
| **Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT** | ⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE (somatostatin receptor ligand) | Most sensitive (>90%); preferred for head and neck paragangliomas; detects multifocal and occult lesions |
| **¹²³I-MIBG scintigraphy** | Meta-iodobenzylguanidine | Lower sensitivity than Ga-DOTATATE for head and neck; useful for abdominal/thoracic disease |
| **¹⁸F-DOPA PET/CT** | Fluorine-18 DOPA | High sensitivity for head and neck paragangliomas; particularly useful in SDHD mutation carriers |
| **¹⁸F-FDG PET/CT** | Fluorodeoxyglucose | Less specific; more useful for SDHB-related malignant disease |

Shah's Chapter 14 states: somatostatin receptor imaging (particularly ⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE) has become the **preferred functional imaging** for head and neck paragangliomas given superior sensitivity compared with MIBG.

### Genetic Testing

- Offer to all patients, particularly: bilateral CBT, age <40, family history, multifocal disease, prior paraganglioma or pheochromocytoma
- Panel: SDHB, SDHC, SDHD (minimum); extended panel includes SDHA, MAX, TMEM127, VHL
- Positive result: screen first-degree relatives; screen for synchronous lesions (CTA neck/chest/abdomen); ophthalmology referral if VHL

## Part E: Shamblin Classification

The Shamblin classification, proposed in 1971, stratifies carotid body tumours based on their relationship to the carotid vessels and predicts surgical difficulty and risk of vascular injury:

| Type | Description | Surgical Difficulty | CN Injury Risk | Stroke Risk | % of Cases |
|------|-------------|--------------------|--------------:|------------|-----------|
| **Type I** | Small tumour, minimally attached to adventitia of carotid bifurcation; easily dissected from vessels | Low | Low | <2% | ~25% |
| **Type II** | Moderate-sized tumour, partial encasement of the carotid vessels; adherent but can be dissected with careful technique (subadventitial plane) | Moderate | Moderate (5-10%) | 5% | ~50% |
| **Type III** | Large tumour, complete encasement and adherence to the ICA and CCA; carotid vessel reconstruction often required | High | High (10-20%) | 10-20% | ~25% |

**Arya et al. (2008)** defined MRI criteria for Shamblin classification:
- Type I: circumferential contact with ICA <180°
- Type II: 180-270° contact with ICA
- Type III: >270° contact (near-complete encasement)

**Modified Shamblin (Luna-Ortiz, 2005)** added Type IIIb (encasement with carotid arterial wall involvement requiring resection and reconstruction) to better stratify operative planning.

## Part F: Preoperative Investigations for Shamblin III Paraganglioma

Shamblin III tumours completely encase the carotid vessels and carry significant risks of stroke (10-20%), cranial nerve injury (10-20%), and need for vascular reconstruction. A comprehensive preoperative workup is mandatory:

### Mandatory Investigations

1. **CT Angiography (neck + chest + abdomen)** — cross-sectional imaging of the entire sympathoadrenal axis to exclude synchronous paraganglioma (given hereditary associations)
2. **24-hour urinary catecholamines, VMA, metanephrines / plasma fractionated metanephrines** — exclude functional pheochromocytoma or paraganglioma (surgery/anaesthesia risk if unrecognised)
3. **Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) with preoperative embolisation** — maps feeding vessels; reduces intraoperative blood loss; performed 24-48 hours before surgery
4. **[[Answers/General/Balloon Occlusion Test|Balloon Occlusion Test (BTO)]]** — most critical for Shamblin III; ICA sacrifice or clamping may be unavoidable; BTO with ⁹⁹ᵐTc-HMPAO SPECT determines tolerance to ICA occlusion and stratifies stroke risk; guides need for vascular reconstruction
5. **Functional imaging (⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT or ¹⁸F-DOPA PET/CT)** — exclude synchronous paragangliomas that would alter surgical plan or staging
6. **Genetic counselling and SDH mutation panel** — bilateral/hereditary phenotype; SDHB mutation significantly alters malignancy risk assessment

### Additional Investigations

7. **Indirect laryngoscopy / videostroboscopy** — document pre-existing vocal cord palsy (vagal involvement); critical for bilateral tumours or vagal paraganglioma
8. **MRI neck with gadolinium** — soft tissue detail, cranial nerve relationship, assessment of skull base extension
9. **Anaesthesia consultation** — planning for possible intraoperative carotid clamping, systemic heparinisation, neuromonitoring (SSEP/EEG), cell salvage
10. **Vascular surgery co-management** — availability of saphenous vein graft or prosthetic graft (PTFE) for ICA reconstruction

### Rationale for Balloon Occlusion Test in Shamblin III

Shah's Chapter 14 describes: in tumours that completely encase the carotid artery (Shamblin III), the internal carotid artery may need to be temporarily or permanently occluded. Stell and Maran's Chapter 14 states: temporary balloon occlusion testing to assess cerebral perfusion may be indicated in certain large carotid paragangliomas to determine whether the patient will tolerate common carotid clamping or internal carotid artery sacrifice. This test can evaluate whether a patient will tolerate ICA sacrifice (high-flow bypass may be needed if the test is failed).

## Part G: Surgical Management

### Operative Principles

Surgery is the treatment of choice for carotid body tumours in fit patients. The fundamental principle is **subadventitial (Gordon-Taylor) dissection** — developing a plane within the adventitia of the carotid vessels rather than outside the tumour, preserving the arterial wall.

**Gordon-Taylor technique (subadventitial dissection):**
- Incision anterior to SCM along carotid sheath
- Early control of CCA, ECA, and ICA — proximal and distal vascular control before tumour manipulation
- **Mayer's ligament** (fibroadventitial attachment at the bifurcation) is divided early — reduces blood loss by interrupting the principal arterial supply
- Dissection proceeds in the subadventitial plane using sharp dissection and bipolar diathermy — the tumour is peeled off the carotid wall
- Avoid excessive traction on cranial nerves (particularly CN IX, X, XII)
- ECA ligation may be required when the tumour is inseparable from ECA branches; ECA sacrifice is generally safe
- ICA must be preserved or reconstructed whenever possible

### Approach by Shamblin Type

| Type | Surgical Approach |
|------|------------------|
| **Type I** | Subadventitial dissection; straightforward; ECA sacrifice rarely needed |
| **Type II** | Subadventitial dissection; temporary carotid clamping with heparinisation; ECA ligation often needed |
| **Type III** | Subadventitial dissection attempted; vascular reconstruction usually required; ICA resection and interposition graft (saphenous vein or PTFE); preoperative embolisation and BTO mandatory |

### Vascular Reconstruction

When the ICA must be resected:
- **Reversed saphenous vein interposition graft** — gold standard; autologous; best long-term patency
- **PTFE prosthetic graft** — used if vein unavailable; comparable results
- **Primary end-to-end anastomosis** — occasionally possible if sufficient arterial length; tension-free anastomosis essential

### Cranial Nerve Preservation

The most common cranial nerve injuries (in order of frequency):
1. **Hypoglossal nerve (CN XII)** — crosses the surgical field; gentle retraction; avoid coagulation near nerve
2. **Vagus nerve (CN X)** — runs within the carotid sheath; at risk in large tumours; sacrifice may cause hoarseness + dysphagia
3. **Accessory nerve (CN XI)** — at risk during neck dissection if concurrent
4. **Glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX)** — Hering's nerve division may cause baroreceptor dysfunction
5. **Sympathetic chain** — Horner syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis)
6. **Facial nerve marginal mandibular branch** — rare; large tumours extending superiorly

### High Carotid Extension — Approaches

For tumours extending to the skull base (high ICA involvement):
- **Mandibular subluxation** — anteriorly dislocate the mandible to gain access to the parapharyngeal space and high ICA
- **Styloidectomy** — removal of the styloid process and stylohyoid/stylomandibular ligaments to increase access
- **Mastoidectomy/petrosectomy** — for very high extension (glomus vagale with jugular involvement)
- **Anterior vertical ramus osteotomy** — rare; reserved for extreme superior extension

## Part H: Preoperative Embolisation

### Rationale

Carotid body tumours are highly vascular, receiving blood supply primarily from:
- Ascending pharyngeal artery (main feeder)
- Direct small branches from the carotid bifurcation adventitia
- Occasionally: occipital, external carotid branches

Preoperative embolisation reduces intraoperative blood loss, shortens operative time, and may improve subadventitial dissection planes by inducing tumour fibrosis.

### Technique and Timing

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Method** | Superselective transarterial embolisation via femoral artery access; microcatheter navigated into feeding vessels |
| **Embolic agents** | PVA particles (most common), Onyx, Gelfoam; coils for proximal ECA feeders |
| **Timing** | Surgery within **24-48 hours** of embolisation — to prevent collateral vessel recruitment |
| **Post-embolisation** | Steroids to reduce inflammatory tumour swelling; analgesics |

### Benefits and Risks

| Benefits | Risks |
|---------|-------|
| Reduced intraoperative haemorrhage | Cerebral embolisation via ECA-ICA anastomoses (~1%) |
| Shorter operative time | Cranial nerve palsy (transient, oedema-related) |
| Improved dissection planes | Tumour swelling and pain post-procedure |
| Potential tumour shrinkage | Arterial dissection at puncture site |

Jackson et al. (2015) meta-analysis confirmed significantly reduced estimated blood loss with preoperative embolisation for carotid body paragangliomas. Shah's Chapter 14 states: preoperative embolisation is required for **larger carotid body tumours** — particularly Shamblin II and all Shamblin III cases.

## Part I: Radiation Therapy

### Indications

Radiation therapy is NOT standard first-line treatment for resectable carotid body tumours in fit patients; surgery remains preferred. RT is indicated in:

1. **Inoperable tumours** — Shamblin III with positive BTO (inadequate collateral), extensive skull base involvement, or surgically unresectable disease
2. **Elderly or medically unfit patients** — where surgical morbidity is disproportionate
3. **Bilateral synchronous CBTs** — to avoid bilateral carotid surgery risk (bilateral vagus nerve sacrifice)
4. **Vagal paraganglioma in the only hearing ear or contralateral vagal palsy** — where CN morbidity of surgery is prohibitive
5. **Residual or recurrent disease** — after incomplete resection
6. **Refusal of surgery**

### Modalities

| Modality | Dose | Outcomes |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Conventional EBRT** | 45-55 Gy in 25-28 fractions | 90-95% local disease control (growth arrest); rarely induces complete tumour regression |
| **Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS) — Gamma Knife / CyberKnife** | 12-15 Gy single fraction | High local control (>90%); preferred for smaller, discrete lesions; low cranial nerve toxicity |
| **Stereotactic Body RT (SBRT)** | 20-25 Gy in 3-5 fractions | Emerging data; similar local control to SRS |

**Important:** RT achieves **growth arrest** rather than tumour cure — tumour mass persists on imaging but ceases to grow. True regression occurs in a minority. Radiation does not eliminate malignant potential.

## Part J: Vagal Paraganglioma

Vagal paragangliomas (glomus vagale) arise from the nodose or jugular ganglion of the vagus nerve and lie in the parapharyngeal space along the neurovascular bundle:

| Feature | Vagal Paraganglioma |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Presentation** | Painless parapharyngeal mass; pharyngeal fullness; medial displacement of tonsil and lateral pharyngeal wall |
| **CN palsy** | Vocal cord palsy (vagus involvement) is present at diagnosis in 20-30% — predates surgery |
| **Imaging** | Displaces ICA anteromedially (unlike CBT which splays ICA/ECA); MRI shows salt and pepper; no lyre sign |
| **DSA** | Tumour blush; ascending pharyngeal artery feeder |
| **Surgery** | Higher morbidity than CBT — sacrifice of the vagus nerve is often unavoidable; post-op hoarseness + aspiration risk in most cases |
| **Wait-and-scan** | Increasingly favoured in elderly patients, those with contralateral vagal palsy, or bilateral tumours — given slow growth and high surgical CN morbidity |
| **RT** | Strong indication for vagal paraganglioma in elderly/bilateral/contralateral vagal palsy — excellent local control |

Shah's Chapter 14 states: vagal paragangliomas are unique in that they are often associated with preoperative vagal nerve deficits, and surgery virtually always results in at least temporary, and often permanent, vagal nerve paralysis — the decision to resect must weigh the certain operative morbidity against the natural history of slow tumour growth.

## Part K: Malignant Paraganglioma

### Diagnostic Criteria

Malignancy in paraganglioma **cannot be determined by histology alone** — there are no reliable histological criteria distinguishing benign from malignant lesions. Malignancy is defined solely by:
- **Metastasis** to non-paraganglion sites (lymph nodes, liver, bone, lung) — the only accepted criterion

### Risk Factors for Malignancy

| Factor | Malignancy Risk |
|--------|----------------|
| SDHB mutation | 30-40% |
| Extra-adrenal site (thoracic/abdominal) | Higher risk than head and neck |
| Carotid body tumour | 5-10% |
| Jugulotympanic | <5% |
| Large tumour size, local invasion | Associated feature |

### Prognosis and Treatment (Lee et al., 2002)

- Overall 5-year survival: **59.5%**
- Regionally confined disease: **76.8%**
- Distant metastasis: **11.8%**
- Treatment: complete surgical resection if feasible; adjuvant EBRT for positive margins; systemic therapy for metastatic disease

### Systemic Therapy for Metastatic Disease

| Agent | Mechanism | Evidence |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Sunitinib** | VEGFR/PDGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor | Phase II data; modest response rates |
| **Temozolomide** | Alkylating agent (DNA methylation) | Active particularly in SDHB-mutated tumours |
| **PRRT (¹⁷⁷Lu-DOTATATE)** | Peptide receptor radionuclide therapy | Emerging; requires SSTR expression; growing evidence |
| **¹³¹I-MIBG** | Targeted radionuclide therapy | Palliative; secretory tumours |
| **Sunitinib + temozolomide** | Combination | Phase II activity in SDH-mutated PGLs |

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Fontaine sign is pathognomonic** for carotid body tumour — the mass moves horizontally but NOT vertically, because it is tethered to the carotid bifurcation; this distinguishes it from a mobile lymph node or branchial cyst
> 2. **Never biopsy** a suspected carotid body tumour in the clinic — uncontrollable haemorrhage; diagnosis is made by imaging alone (CT/MRI); biopsy is absolutely contraindicated
> 3. **The lyre sign on CTA/DSA** — splaying of the ICA and ECA at the carotid bifurcation — is the classic imaging finding; if present, the diagnosis is essentially confirmed
> 4. **Exclude phaeochromocytoma before any intervention** (surgery, embolisation, biopsy) — failure to do so risks life-threatening hypertensive crisis on the table; 24-hr urinary catecholamines and plasma metanephrines are mandatory
> 5. **Shamblin III requires a BTO** — preoperative balloon occlusion test with SPECT (Shah's recommendation) is mandatory before surgery for Shamblin III to determine ICA sacrifice tolerance and guide the need for vascular reconstruction
> 6. **Genetic testing changes management** — SDHB mutation carriers have 30-40% malignancy risk and require full-body functional imaging; SDHD carriers have high multifocal risk; offer testing to all patients under 40 or with bilateral/multiple tumours
> 7. **Surgery within 24-48 hours of embolisation** — the critical timing rule; delay beyond 48 hours allows collateral vessel recruitment and negates the benefit; administer steroids to counter post-embolisation tumour swelling
> 8. **Malignancy cannot be diagnosed histologically** — only metastasis to a non-paraganglion site establishes malignancy; apparent cellular atypia, mitoses, or perineural invasion are not diagnostic on their own
> 9. **RT achieves growth arrest, not cure** — for inoperable or elderly patients, external beam RT achieves 90-95% local control (stable disease); true tumour regression is uncommon; malignant potential is not eliminated
> 10. **Vagal paraganglioma — the CN morbidity is near-certain at surgery** — pre-existing vocal cord palsy in 20-30% at diagnosis; vagus sacrifice virtually inevitable; always document vocal cord status preoperatively and consider wait-and-scan or RT for elderly/bilateral cases to avoid obligate dysphonia and aspiration
