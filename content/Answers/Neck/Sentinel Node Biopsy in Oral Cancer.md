## Introduction

Sentinel node biopsy (SNB) identifies and removes the first-echelon (sentinel) lymph node(s) draining a primary tumour. The sentinel node (SN) concept holds that if the SN is histologically free of metastasis, the remainder of the regional nodal basin is almost certainly negative, sparing the patient an elective neck dissection (END) and its associated morbidity.

Originally established in cutaneous melanoma (Morton 1992) and breast cancer (Giuliano 1994), SNB was adopted for oral and oropharyngeal SCC during the late 1990s and validated through a series of increasingly robust multicentre trials culminating in the SENT trial (2015) and the Senti-MERORL RCT (2020). The procedure is now endorsed by NICE (UK, 2016) and recommended in major international guidelines including NCCN for selected T1-T2 cN0 oral cavity SCC.

## Rationale

### The Problem of Occult Nodal Metastasis

- Early oral cavity SCC (T1-T2) carries a **20-30% risk of occult nodal metastasis** despite a clinically and radiologically N0 neck
- Failure to treat the cN0 neck results in neck recurrence in ~30% of observed patients, with markedly reduced salvage rates
- The D'Cruz NEJM trial confirmed that **END confers superior survival** over watchful waiting and therapeutic ND in T1-T2 node-negative tongue cancer (OS 80% vs 67.5% at 3 years)

### The Problem with Universal Elective Neck Dissection

- END subjects **70-80% of patients** with no occult disease to an unnecessary operation
- END morbidity: shoulder dysfunction (spinal accessory nerve injury), wound complications, lymphoedema, prolonged hospital stay, scar
- SNB offers a **selective approach**: only patients with positive SN undergo completion neck dissection, sparing negative-SN patients the morbidity of full END

### Where SNB Fits

| Strategy | Indication | Risk | Morbidity |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Observation (watchful waiting)** | cN0 only | 30% neck failure | Low initially; high salvage failure |
| **Elective neck dissection** | All cN0 | Overtreats 70-80% | Shoulder, wound, scar |
| **Sentinel node biopsy** | T1-T2 cN0 | FNR ~14% | Low if SN negative |

## Patient Selection

### Indications

- **T1 or T2** primary tumour (≤4 cm)
- **Clinically and radiologically cN0 neck** (clinical exam + CT/MRI ± PET-CT)
- **Squamous cell carcinoma** of oral cavity (all subsites) or oropharynx
- No previous ipsilateral neck surgery or radiotherapy
- Patient fit for general anaesthesia and neck exploration

### Contraindications

| Contraindication | Rationale |
|---|---|
| T3-T4 primary | Occult metastasis rate >40%; END preferred |
| Clinically or radiologically N+ neck | SNB role unproven in N+ disease |
| Previous neck surgery or RT | Disrupted lymphatic channels alter drainage patterns |
| Distant metastases (M1) | Palliative setting; SNB does not change management |

### Special Considerations

- **Floor of mouth (FOM) tumours**: shine-through phenomenon (see below) — higher false-negative rate; requires SPECT-CT or ICG modification
- **Midline tumours**: bilateral lymphoscintigraphy and bilateral SNB required; may detect contralateral unexpected drainage
- **Multiple primary sites**: each primary must be injected separately

## Technique of Sentinel Node Biopsy

### Overview of Dual-Agent Protocol (Shah, 5th Ed)

SNB uses two complementary agents — a radiolabelled colloid and a visible dye — combined with intraoperative gamma probe guidance. The dual-agent approach maximises identification rate to >99%.

### Step 1: Preoperative Lymphoscintigraphy

**Radioisotope preparation:**
- Tracer: **Technetium-99m (Tc-99m) sulphur colloid** (USA) or **Tc-99m nanocolloid** (Europe)
- Dose: 20-40 MBq divided into 4 peritumoral injection sites
- Timing: ideally the day before or 2-4 hours before surgery
- Particle size: smaller particles (nanocolloid) migrate more rapidly and allow earlier imaging

**Imaging protocol:**
- Dynamic gamma camera imaging at 5, 15, and 60 minutes after injection
- Delayed static images at 2-3 hours if needed
- SPECT-CT (single photon emission CT fused with CT): increasingly used to provide **3D anatomical localisation** of sentinel nodes — particularly valuable in floor-of-mouth tumours and posterior oral cavity sites
- Skin surface marked over "hot spots" with a permanent marker or radioactive marker placed by nuclear medicine technician to guide incision placement

**Interpretation:**
- All "hot" foci on lymphoscintigraphy represent potential sentinel nodes
- First-appearing nodes = true sentinel nodes (first-echelon)
- Multiple sentinel nodes may be identified — **all must be retrieved**
- Nodes appearing only on delayed imaging may represent second-echelon nodes

### Step 2: Patent Blue Dye (Intraoperative)

**Preparation:**
- Agent: **Patent blue V** (Europe) or **isosulfan blue / Lymphazurin 1%** (USA)
- Volume: 1-2 mL total; injected in 4 quadrants circumferentially around tumour margin, submucosally
- Timing: **5-10 minutes before incision** in the neck (allows transit time to regional nodes)

**Intraoperative technique:**
- Blue-stained afferent lymphatic channels are identified in the neck dissection flap
- Followed to their terminus at the first blue-stained lymph node = sentinel node
- Blue staining fades within 30-45 minutes — surgeon must act promptly after injection
- Anaphylactoid reaction to blue dye occurs in ~1-2%: premedicate with antihistamine/corticosteroid; ensure resuscitation available

### Step 3: Intraoperative Gamma Probe

**Probe characteristics:**
- Collimated handheld gamma probe with digital display (counts per 10 seconds)
- Background count measured in the operative field away from the primary site

**Thresholds for sentinel node designation (Shah):**
- **In vivo rule**: any node with count rate **>3 times background** = sentinel node
- **Ex vivo rule**: after excision, SN count must be **>10 times any remaining non-sentinel node** in the basin
- After each SN excision, probe re-applied to residual basin — if residual activity **>10% of the hottest excised SN** ex vivo count, further exploration is required

### Step 4: Sentinel Node Excision

1. Neck incision planned based on pre-marked hot spots and anticipated lymphatic levels
2. Subplatysmal flaps raised; lymphatic channels followed to SN
3. Both the blue-stained node and the "hottest" radioactive node are excised (they should be the same node — if not, both are taken)
4. Additional nodes meeting in vivo 3:1 threshold are also excised
5. Primary tumour resected via transoral approach in the same operation
6. Pathological assessment: labelled, oriented, and sent for enhanced processing (see below)

### Learning Curve

- Minimum **10 proctored cases** required before independent SNB
- Technical proficiency generally requires **≥30 cases**; false-negative rate stabilises after 30 cases
- Multidisciplinary requirement: trained head and neck surgeon + nuclear medicine physician + specialist head and neck pathologist
- NICE guidance (UK): SNB for oral cancer should be performed only in centres with appropriate expertise and case volume

## Pathological Processing of Sentinel Nodes

### Why Standard Frozen Section is Inadequate

- Routine frozen section has **sensitivity of only 25-50%** for micrometastases in sentinel nodes
- Cutting artefact and tissue loss during cryostat sectioning destroys material available for permanent section
- **AVOID relying on frozen section alone** for intraoperative decision-making

### Step-Serial Sectioning with Immunohistochemistry (IHC)

| Step | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Bisection** | Node bisected through hilum |
| **Step-serial sections** | Sections cut at **150-200 μm intervals** through entire node |
| **H&E staining** | Each level stained and examined |
| **IHC staining** | Pan-cytokeratin (AE1/AE3) on alternate sections — identifies occult SCC cells |
| **Turnaround** | 24-72 hours (rush permanent section) |

### Classification of Findings

| Category | Size | TNM | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Macrometastasis** | >2 mm | pN1 | Completion neck dissection (ND) |
| **Micrometastasis** | 0.2-2 mm | pN1(mi) | Completion ND recommended |
| **Isolated tumour cells (ITC)** | ≤0.2 mm | pN0(i+) | Controversial; discuss in MDT — completion ND in most centres |
| **Negative** | No tumour | pN0 | Observation — no completion ND |

- Step-serial + IHC detects an additional **10-15% of micrometastases** missed by standard single-level H&E processing of neck dissection specimens

## Key Trial Evidence

### SENT Trial — Schilling et al. (2015)

The **Sentinel European Node Trial** is the largest prospective validation study of SNB in oral cancer.

| Parameter | Result |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Prospective observational; 14 European centres |
| **Patients** | 415 patients; T1-T2 cN0 oral/oropharyngeal SCC |
| **SN identification rate** | 99.5% |
| **Occult metastasis rate** | 23% (SN positive) |
| **SN only positive node** | 80% of positive cases |
| **Sensitivity** | 86% |
| **NPV** | 95% |
| **False-negative rate** | 14% |
| **3-year DSS** | 94% |
| **Conclusion** | SNB is a reliable, safe staging technique for the cN0 neck in T1-T2 oral SCC |

### ACOSOG Z0360 — Ross/Civantos et al.

- American multicenter trial (140 patients; T1-T2 cN0 oral SCC)
- Validated dual-agent (Tc-99m + blue dye) technique in a North American setting
- Confirmed predominant drainage to Levels I-III; identified unexpected drainage to Levels IV-V in a small subset
- Planar lymphoscintigraphy and SLNB showed good but imperfect correlation in Levels II and III
- Established the technical basis for the ACOSOG multi-institutional protocol

### Alkureishi et al. 2010 (5-Year Follow-up, European Multicenter)

- 227 SNB procedures across 6 European centres; 134 T1/T2 N0 patients
- 79 patients had SNB as the **sole staging procedure** (no completion ND if SN negative)
- Confirmed long-term safety and reliability of SNB-directed management
- Identified the **floor-of-mouth shine-through** problem as a recurrent technical challenge
- False-negative rate consistent with published literature (~14%)

### D'Cruz et al. (NEJM 2015) — Impact on SNB Adoption

- **Design**: Prospective RCT; 596 patients; T1-T2 lateralised oral SCC (primarily tongue); tata Memorial Hospital, Mumbai
- **Randomisation**: END (n=276) vs therapeutic ND on recurrence (n=320)
- **Primary endpoint**: overall survival
- **Results**:
  - 3-year OS: END **80.0%** vs therapeutic ND **67.5%** (HR 0.64; 95% CI 0.45-0.92; P=0.01)
  - DFS: **69.5% vs 45.9%** (P<0.001)
- **Impact on SNB**: This trial demonstrated that *not* treating the cN0 neck surgically is inferior — it strengthened the argument for prophylactic nodal management (whether END or SNB) over watchful waiting
- SNB is now positioned as an alternative to END (not observation), allowing selective pathological staging before committing to completion dissection

## False-Negative Rate: Causes and Implications

### Definition

False-negative SNB = negative sentinel node but metastasis present elsewhere in regional nodes (regional recurrence after negative SNB)

### SENT Trial False-Negative Rate: 14%

This means 14% of patients with occult nodal metastasis had a false-negative SNB result.

### Causes of False-Negative Results

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Shine-through** | FOM tumours: radiotracer from primary obscures adjacent Level I SNs on lymphoscintigraphy and with gamma probe |
| **Skip metastasis** | Tumour bypasses the true SN and deposits in second-echelon node |
| **Technical failure** | SN not identified intraoperatively; inadequate gamma probe technique |
| **Pathological sampling error** | Micrometastasis in SN missed even with step-serial sectioning |
| **Multiple drainage pathways** | Not all SNs harvested; residual basin activity not checked |
| **Prior biopsy/manipulation** | Altered lymphatic anatomy |

### Site-Specific Considerations

**Floor of mouth (FOM) — Shine-through phenomenon:**
- Primary site is immediately adjacent to Level I-II nodes
- Radiotracer injected peritumorally migrates only 1-2 cm — distance between primary and SN is minimal
- Gamma probe in the neck detects radioactivity from the primary injection site ("shine-through"), masking the SN signal
- Solutions: SPECT-CT fusion imaging for anatomical separation; ICG fluorescence (no radiation-based shine-through); subtraction scintigraphy; shielding the primary with a lead apron during probe use

**Bilateral drainage:**
- Midline and near-midline tumours (floor of mouth, anterior tongue, lower lip) may drain bilaterally
- Bilateral lymphoscintigraphy and bilateral SNB required in these cases
- Bilateral positive SN necessitates bilateral completion ND

**Multiple sentinel nodes:**
- Mean 2-3 SNs per patient in most series
- All nodes meeting the in vivo 3:1 threshold must be excised — removing only the "hottest" node risks false-negative result

## Completion Neck Dissection

### Indications

| Finding | Action |
|---|---|
| Macrometastasis (>2 mm) | Completion selective or modified radical ND ipsilaterally |
| Micrometastasis (0.2-2 mm) | Completion ND — same management as pN1 |
| ITC (≤0.2 mm) | MDT discussion; most centres favour completion ND |
| Extracapsular spread (ECS) | Completion ND + adjuvant chemoradiation |
| Negative SN | No completion ND; observation with regular neck surveillance |

### Timing

- **Two-stage approach**: SNB first; await permanent section (24-72 hours); completion ND as a second procedure if SN positive
- **Single-stage approach**: SNB with intraoperative frozen section (limited sensitivity); complete ND at same sitting if positive — reduces need for second anaesthetic but frozen section may miss micrometastases
- Most centres use the **two-stage approach** given the limitations of frozen section for sentinel nodes

## Comparison with Elective Neck Dissection

| Parameter | Elective Neck Dissection | Sentinel Node Biopsy |
|---|---|---|
| **Evidence level** | Level I (D'Cruz NEJM 2015) | Level I (Senti-MERORL RCT 2020) |
| **Neck control** | 90-95% | ~91% (Senti-MERORL) |
| **Overall survival** | Equivalent | Equivalent |
| **Morbidity** | Shoulder dysfunction, wound complications | Lower; confined to SN excision incision |
| **Shoulder function** | Worse at 6 months (Garrel RCT) | Significantly better at 6 months |
| **Overtreats** | 70-80% (no occult disease) | Only patients with positive SN undergo ND |
| **Identifies unexpected drainage** | No | Yes (skip levels, contralateral nodes) |
| **Enhanced pathology** | Routine H&E only | Step-serial + IHC — detects more micrometastases |
| **Cost** | Single operation cost | SNB cost + second ND if positive |
| **Availability** | Universal | Requires nuclear medicine, gamma probe, specialist pathology |

## Current Guideline Endorsement

| Organisation | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| **NICE (UK)** | Endorsed SNB for T1-T2 cN0 oral cavity SCC in centres with appropriate expertise (2016) |
| **NCCN** | SNB listed as acceptable option for selected cN0 early oral cavity SCC |
| **EANM** | Published joint practice guidelines for radionuclide lymphoscintigraphy in oral/oropharyngeal SCC |
| **European consensus** | SENT trial and Senti-MERORL provide sufficient Level I evidence for routine adoption |

### Limitations in Low-Resource Settings

- Requires gamma camera for preoperative lymphoscintigraphy (often unavailable in district hospitals)
- Tc-99m radioisotope supply chain dependent on nuclear pharmacy and short half-life (6 hours)
- Specialist head and neck pathologist for step-serial IHC processing essential
- Learning curve of ≥30 cases necessitates case concentration in high-volume centres
- ICG fluorescence (no radioisotope) is an emerging alternative that may reduce infrastructure requirements

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The **dual-agent protocol** (Tc-99m + patent blue V + gamma probe) achieves a sentinel node identification rate of 99.5% — using blue dye alone or radioisotope alone significantly reduces accuracy
> - **Never rely on frozen section alone** for sentinel node assessment — sensitivity for micrometastases is only 25-50%; always use step-serial sectioning with pan-cytokeratin IHC on permanent sections
> - The **in vivo 3:1 rule** and **ex vivo 10:1 rule** are thresholds for sentinel node designation; after each SN excision, re-check residual basin — if residual activity >10% of the hottest excised node, continue exploring
> - **Floor-of-mouth tumours** are the highest-risk site for false-negative SNB due to shine-through: consider SPECT-CT imaging and/or ICG fluorescence to overcome this; lead shielding of the primary during probe use may also help
> - **Multiple sentinel nodes** (mean 2-3 per patient) are the rule, not the exception — all nodes meeting the threshold must be harvested, not just the single "hottest" node
> - The **D'Cruz NEJM trial** (2015) did NOT test SNB vs END — it tested END vs watchful waiting; SNB is positioned as an alternative to END, not to observation
> - **Micrometastases detected only on IHC** alter staging from pN0 to pN1(mi) and mandate completion neck dissection in most current protocols; their prognostic significance is distinct from isolated tumour cells (ITC ≤0.2 mm)
> - SENT trial false-negative rate of **14%** means SNB does not achieve zero neck failure — patients with negative SNB still require regular neck surveillance (clinical exam + USS) for at least 2 years
> - **Learning curve**: false-negative rates are higher in a surgeon's first 10-15 cases; NICE mandates a supervised learning phase of at least 10 cases; proficiency is generally accepted after ≥30 procedures
> - **Bilateral drainage** (midline and near-midline tumours) requires bilateral lymphoscintigraphy and bilateral neck exploration — unilateral SNB in a midline tumour risks missing contralateral occult disease
