## A. Second Primary Malignancies in Head and Neck Cancer

### Definitions and Diagnostic Criteria

A **second primary tumour (SPT)** is a new independent malignancy arising in a patient previously diagnosed with a primary cancer. The **Warren-Gates criteria (1932)** define an independent second primary as:
1. Each tumour histologically confirmed as malignant
2. Each tumour separated by normal non-neoplastic mucosa (geographically distinct)
3. The second tumour cannot be a metastasis of the first

**Temporal classification:**
- **Synchronous second primary**: diagnosed within 6 months of the index cancer (some definitions use simultaneous presentation or <6 months)
- **Metachronous second primary**: diagnosed more than 6 months after the index cancer
- Synchronous SPTs are more likely to be within the head and neck or upper aerodigestive tract; metachronous SPTs most commonly affect the lung

### Pathophysiology — Field Cancerization

**Slaughter et al. (1953)** coined the term **field cancerization** to explain why HNC patients develop multiple independent primaries:

- Chronic carcinogen exposure (tobacco smoke, alcohol, HPV) affects a wide mucosal field simultaneously
- The entire upper aerodigestive tract epithelium is repeatedly exposed, producing **widespread premalignant genetic alterations** (p53 mutations, loss of heterozygosity, epigenetic silencing)
- This "condemned mucosa" contains multiple independent clones capable of malignant transformation
- Clinically visible tumour represents only one of many foci with carcinogenic potential
- Explains why resection with clear margins is followed by new primary tumours at distant mucosal sites

**Molecular refinement (Braakhuis 2003):** A single stem cell clone with early p53 mutations may spread across the mucosa, with individual sub-clones acquiring additional mutations independently — the "clonal patch" model. This explains why some "second primaries" share clonal ancestry and others are truly independent.

### Incidence

| Category | Rate |
|---|---|
| Synchronous second primaries at presentation | 3–8% of HNC patients |
| Lifetime risk of metachronous second primary | 10–35% |
| Annual risk after treatment of index cancer | 3–7% per year |
| Deaths attributable to second primaries in cured HNC | 20–25% |
| Lung as most common metachronous second primary site | ~30–40% of all metachronous SPTs |

### Common Sites for Second Primary Malignancy

**Within head and neck (synchronous preference):**
- Contralateral or adjacent oropharyngeal/oral cavity mucosa
- Hypopharynx (especially in alcohol/tobacco patients)
- Oesophagus (alcohol-driven field effect — squamous SCC)

**Distant sites (metachronous preference):**
- **Lung** — most common metachronous SPT; SCC or adenocarcinoma; accounts for >30% of metachronous SPTs
- Oesophagus — SCC; common in high alcohol/tobacco exposure
- Bladder — tobacco-related transitional cell carcinoma
- Another HNC site (oral cavity, larynx, oropharynx)

### Risk Stratification

| Risk Factor | Effect on Second Primary Risk |
|---|---|
| Active smoking (>20 pack-years) | 3–5× increased risk |
| Heavy alcohol (>6 units/day) | Additive with tobacco; especially oesophageal SPC |
| HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer | Lower second primary risk (~10–15%); lung second primary risk reduced |
| Prior radiation to head and neck | Increased risk of radiation-induced second primaries (thyroid, salivary, sarcoma) at 10–20 years |
| Fanconi anaemia/Li-Fraumeni | Very high SPT risk; early surveillance essential |

**HPV-positive OPC patients** have a significantly lower second primary risk compared to tobacco/alcohol-driven HNC because the field cancerization mechanism (carcinogen-driven widespread mucosal damage) is absent. However, they are not immune — tobacco co-exposure negates the protective effect.

### Surveillance and Screening Protocols

**Annual clinical examination:**
- Full head and neck endoscopic examination every 3–6 months for 2 years, then 6-monthly to 5 years, then annually

**Lung second primary surveillance:**
- **Low-dose CT chest (LDCT)** annually: recommended for HNC survivors with >20 pack-year tobacco history; comparable to lung cancer screening protocols (NLST criteria)
- Standard CXR is insufficient — low sensitivity for early lung SCC/adenocarcinoma

**Oesophageal surveillance:**
- **Upper GI endoscopy** for high-risk patients (heavy alcohol + tobacco, hypopharyngeal primary): Lugol's chromoendoscopy to detect synchronous oesophageal SCC
- Annual or biennial endoscopy in very high-risk patients (hypopharynx/postcricoid primary — synchronous oesophageal SCC in 5–10%)

**PET-CT:**
- Not used for routine surveillance of second primaries
- Indicated when symptomatic, rising tumour markers, or clinical suspicion of recurrence vs second primary

**Panendoscopy at diagnosis:**
- For all new HNC diagnoses: triple endoscopy (laryngoscopy + oesophagoscopy + bronchoscopy) was standard; now replaced in most centres by CT/PET-CT to detect synchronous second primaries and distant disease

### Impact on Prognosis and Management

- Second primaries account for **20–25% of deaths** in patients who are otherwise cured of their index HNC
- Index cancer prognosis often good (T1–T2 treated successfully), but second primary may be detected at advanced stage
- Detection of synchronous lung second primary at HNC diagnosis complicates staging (M1 vs synchronous primary) — multidisciplinary decision
- Smoking cessation post-treatment is the single most effective intervention for reducing second primary risk

### Chemoprevention

- **13-cis-retinoic acid (isotretinoin)**: RCT (Hong et al.) showed reduction in second primaries in locally advanced HNC survivors; however, toxicity (mucocutaneous, hepatic, teratogenicity) limited clinical uptake; not standard of care
- **Vitamin E/beta-carotene**: no proven benefit; beta-carotene increased lung cancer risk in smokers (ATBC trial)
- **HPV vaccination**: role in primary prevention of HPV-related HNC; potential reduction of HPV-related second primaries; no proven benefit in established cancer survivors

## B. Thyroglossal Duct Cyst

### Embryology

- Thyroid gland arises from **endodermal proliferation at the foramen caecum** (junction of anterior 2/3 and posterior 1/3 of the tongue, between the 1st and 2nd pharyngeal arches)
- During weeks 4–7 of gestation, the thyroid descends from the foramen caecum inferiorly, passing anterior to the hyoid bone and reaching its final pretracheal position (week 7)
- The **thyroglossal duct** is the epithelial channel connecting the descending thyroid to the foramen caecum
- Normally involutes completely by **week 10**; persistence → thyroglossal duct cyst (TDC)
- The intimate relationship with the hyoid bone is created as the duct passes through or around the developing hyoid anlage — this is the anatomical basis for the **Sistrunk procedure**

### Epidemiology

- Most common congenital neck mass: 2/3 of all congenital anterior midline neck masses
- 70% present before age 30; can present at any age
- Equal sex distribution
- No consistent inheritance pattern; sporadic

### Location Distribution

| Location | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Subhyoid (infrahyoid) | 60% |
| At level of hyoid | 25% |
| Suprahyoid | 10% |
| Intralingual (at foramen caecum) | 2–3% |
| Intrathoracic/substernal | Rare (<1%) |

Note: TDCs may deviate slightly laterally (usually to the left) as they descend — but remain within 2 cm of the midline. A true lateral cyst in the posterior triangle is not a TDC.

### Clinical Features

- **Smooth, well-defined midline or near-midline cystic neck mass**
- Classically moves **upward with both swallowing AND tongue protrusion** (due to attachment to foramen caecum via the fibrous duct remnant through or around the hyoid bone)
- Non-tender unless infected
- Soft, fluctuant; transillumination positive if large
- Size: usually 1–3 cm; may enlarge after upper respiratory infection (URTI)
- May present acutely infected (50% present at some point with infection) — warm, tender, erythematous swelling; may form abscess

**Complications:**
- **Infection**: most common complication; may lead to rupture → cutaneous thyroglossal fistula
- **Fistula formation**: post-infection drainage or spontaneous rupture → chronic draining sinus/fistula in midline neck; requires Sistrunk procedure for definitive management
- **Malignant transformation**: 1–2% of TDCs; almost exclusively **papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC)** arising in ectopic thyroid tissue within the cyst wall; rarely squamous cell carcinoma; average age 40 years; usually excellent prognosis if confined to cyst

### Investigations

**Ultrasound (mandatory first investigation):**
- Confirms cystic nature (anechoic, thin-walled)
- **Critical**: identifies normal thyroid gland in normal position — ectopic thyroid tissue within the TDC is the only functioning thyroid in ~1% of patients (surgical removal would cause hypothyroidism)
- Detects internal echoes (infected/haemorrhagic cyst) or solid components (malignancy)

**Thyroid function tests:**
- Performed routinely; essential if US does not identify normal thyroid (ectopic thyroid)
- Normal in most cases

**Fine-needle aspiration cytology (FNAC):**
- Indicated when US shows solid component, thick walls, calcification, or suspicious features
- TDC fluid typically: turbid, mucoid; cytology shows columnar/squamous epithelial cells, colloid material
- FNAC to exclude malignancy before surgery if clinically suspicious

**CT/MRI:**
- For large cysts, recurrent disease, or suspected malignancy — defines extent and relationship to surrounding structures
- Not routine for uncomplicated TDC

### Sistrunk Procedure (1920) — Standard Surgical Treatment

Walter Sistrunk described the definitive operation in 1920, recognising that simple cyst excision had an unacceptably high recurrence rate (~30–50%).

**Principles:**
1. **Cyst excision** with intact cyst wall (rupture increases recurrence)
2. **Resection of the central body of the hyoid bone** — the duct invariably passes through or immediately adjacent to the hyoid; leaving hyoid remnant leaves residual duct
3. **Core of tongue base tissue** from the hyoid to the foramen caecum — the duct branches extensively in the tongue base and simple linear excision misses lateral branches

**Surgical steps:**
- Transverse skin crease incision over the cyst
- Dissect cyst free, maintaining integrity; identify and preserve strap muscles
- Follow the cyst superiorly to the hyoid — divide hyoid bone on either side of the midline, removing the central body (1–1.5 cm segment)
- Continue superior dissection through the tongue base musculature as a 5–7 mm core up to the foramen caecum
- Suture tongue musculature closed; layered wound closure

**Outcomes:**
- Recurrence rate: **3–5%** with Sistrunk procedure vs 30–50% with simple cyst excision alone
- Recurrence is most commonly due to incomplete removal of duct branches in the tongue base or failure to remove the hyoid body
- Re-do Sistrunk for recurrence: technically challenging (scar, distorted anatomy) but feasible; recurrence rate after second Sistrunk ~15%

### Management of Malignancy in Thyroglossal Duct Cyst

If incidental PTC is found on histology of Sistrunk specimen:
- **Small PTC (<1 cm, confined to cyst wall, no extrathyroidal extension)**: Sistrunk alone may be sufficient; thyroid US to assess gland
- **PTC >1 cm, multifocal, or gross extrathyroidal extension**: total thyroidectomy + radioactive iodine (RAI) ± neck dissection if nodal disease
- Prognosis is excellent — PTC in TDC behaves no differently from intrathyroidal PTC of equivalent stage
- SCC in TDC: requires more aggressive approach (extended resection + neck dissection + post-operative RT)

## C. Blood Supply to Neck Skin and Neck Incision Planning

### Blood Supply to Neck Skin

The neck skin receives its blood supply from **musculocutaneous perforators and direct cutaneous arteries** arising from several vascular territories:

**Anterior triangle:**
- **Submental branch of the facial artery** — supplies submental and submandibular skin
- **Superior thyroid artery branches** — anterior midline neck
- Perforators through **platysma** from the submental and superior thyroid vessels

**Posterior triangle:**
- **Occipital artery** (branch of external carotid) — posterior and upper posterior triangle
- **Transverse cervical artery** (branch of thyrocervical trunk from subclavian) — lower posterior triangle and supraclavicular skin
- **Posterior auricular artery** — retroauricular and posterior scalp skin

**Lateral neck / overlap zone:**
- **External jugular vein tributaries** carry venous drainage but associated arterial perforators from facial and transverse cervical territories
- **Supra-scapular artery** — lower lateral neck and shoulder skin

**Platysma as the key anatomical layer:**
- Platysma muscle carries a rich **subdermal plexus** on its deep surface, supplied by musculocutaneous perforators from the facial, submental, and superior thyroid vessels
- Platysma elevation in the subplatysmal plane preserves the subdermal vascular plexus and maintains blood supply to overlying skin flaps
- Supra-platysmal dissection (above the muscle) devascularises the skin — avoided in neck dissection

### Principles of Neck Incision Design

**1. Avoid trifurcation (three-point junction):**
- The point where three skin flap edges meet has the lowest blood supply — ischaemic necrosis and wound breakdown
- Classic trifurcating designs (Y-incision, Martin's T-incision) risk corner necrosis
- Minimise acute angles; use broad curves

**2. Avoid crossing the carotid sheath:**
- Incision crossing directly over the carotid artery risks wound breakdown → carotid exposure → carotid rupture (especially in post-RT patients)
- Transverse limbs of the MacFee incision avoid this risk by design

**3. Adequate exposure:**
- Incision must provide access to all levels to be dissected and the primary site (oral cavity, larynx, etc.)
- Single incision for unilateral ND; bilateral/apron design for bilateral ND or total laryngectomy + bilateral ND

**4. Cosmesis:**
- Transverse incisions in natural skin creases (Langer's lines) heal with better scars than vertical or oblique incisions
- Incisions should be placed in or parallel to skin creases where possible

**5. Post-radiation skin:**
- Radiation-induced **endarteritis** reduces subdermal blood supply → wound breakdown risk
- Avoid incisions crossing RT scar (telangectatic, thin skin)
- Prefer transverse MacFee design in irradiated necks
- Preoperative Doppler assessment of skin blood flow in heavily irradiated patients
- Two-layer tension-free closure essential; consider pectoralis major myocutaneous (PMMC) flap for anticipated skin deficiency or carotid coverage

**6. Incisions through previous scars:**
- Previous neck incision scars alter vascularity — plan new incisions to avoid placing trifurcation points adjacent to prior scar
- Where previous MacFee scar exists, re-use same incision lines if possible

### Classic Neck Incisions

| Incision | Design | Advantages | Disadvantages | Best Indication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **MacFee** | Two parallel horizontal transverse incisions (upper at hyoid level, lower at cricoid level) | Best blood supply — no flap undermining at corners; no trifurcation; excellent in post-RT neck | Long scars; less cosmetic than single incision; limited upper exposure for parotid | Post-RT neck; bilateral ND; preferred modern standard |
| **Modified Schobinger** | Horizontal upper incision + vertical descending limb posteriorly (hockey-stick) | Good access; lower trifurcation risk than classic Y | Some corner risk at junction; more visible scar | Unilateral neck dissection with posterior triangle access |
| **Apron (bilateral MacFee / bilateral transverse)** | Upper horizontal incision from mastoid to mastoid + lower horizontal incision | Wide bilateral access for total laryngectomy + bilateral ND | Longer operating time; bilateral skin elevation | Total laryngectomy + bilateral ND |
| **Martin (Y or T-incision)** | Vertical midline + bilateral horizontal limbs | Wide access; used historically | Trifurcation in midline — carotid exposure risk; high wound breakdown rate | Historical; largely abandoned |
| **Conley** | Posterior oblique incision from mastoid tip to clavicle | Good posterior triangle access | Limited anterior triangle access; not suitable for unilateral ND alone | Posterior triangle tumours; parotidectomy + ND combined |

### Post-Radiation Neck Incision Management

- **Delay elective surgery** by minimum 6 weeks post-RT to allow some tissue recovery
- **Doppler USS** preoperatively to map residual skin perforators and plan incision
- **Single transverse MacFee incisions**: preferred — minimises undermined skin bridges
- **Two-layer closure**: platysma + skin closed separately; reduces tension and improves healing
- **PMMC flap**: planned coverage if extensive skin deficiency anticipated, or carotid protection in high-risk salvage cases
- **Wound breakdown management**: small areas — conservative (negative pressure wound therapy, dressings); carotid exposure — emergency pedicled PMMC or free flap coverage

### Platysma Preservation

- Preserving the **platysma during skin flap elevation** (dissecting in the subplatysmal plane) maintains subdermal plexus integrity
- Platysma elevation keeps the blood supply attached to the skin flap
- In salvage/post-RT necks, careful subplatysmal dissection is the most important technical step for avoiding skin flap necrosis
- Platysma also provides a **waterproof fascial layer** over the carotid sheath — acts as second barrier if skin breakdown occurs

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Warren-Gates criteria (1932) define a second primary: histologically confirmed, separated by normal mucosa, not a metastasis — all three criteria must be met to diagnose an independent SPT.
> 2. Field cancerization (Slaughter 1953) explains why tobacco and alcohol users develop multiple HNC primaries — the entire aerodigestive mucosa is in a state of precancerous genetic instability.
> 3. The most common metachronous second primary in HNC patients is lung cancer (~30–40%); annual LDCT chest is recommended for heavy smokers post-treatment.
> 4. HPV-positive OPC patients have a lower second primary risk than tobacco/alcohol-driven HNC — the field effect mechanism is absent when no carcinogen-induced field damage exists.
> 5. The pathognomonic clinical feature of a thyroglossal duct cyst is elevation with tongue protrusion (not just swallowing) — this differentiates it from other midline neck masses (dermoid, lymph node).
> 6. Always confirm normal thyroid in normal position by ultrasound before operating on a TDC — in ~1% of cases, the cyst contains the only functioning thyroid tissue.
> 7. The Sistrunk procedure mandates removal of the central hyoid body — leaving the hyoid bone is the most common cause of TDC recurrence (recurrence drops from >40% to <5% with hyoid removal).
> 8. Malignancy in a TDC is almost always papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC) — suspect if solid components on US, calcification, or FNAC shows papillary architecture; prognosis excellent if confined to cyst.
> 9. The MacFee double-transverse incision is the preferred approach for post-radiation neck dissection — it avoids trifurcation, avoids crossing the carotid, and preserves maximum skin blood supply.
> 10. Subplatysmal skin flap elevation is the critical technical principle in neck dissection — it preserves the subdermal vascular plexus and protects both the skin flap and the carotid sheath.
