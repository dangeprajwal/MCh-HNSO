## Part A — Paediatric Neuroblastic Tumours of the Neck (Q16)

### Classification: The Neuroblastic Tumour Spectrum

Neuroblastic tumours arise from **primitive neural crest cells** of the sympathetic nervous system and represent a biological spectrum from malignant to benign:

| Tumour | Differentiation | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| **Neuroblastoma** | Undifferentiated / poorly differentiated | Malignant; most common in infancy |
| **Ganglioneuroblastoma** | Intermediate; mixed mature and immature | Intermediate malignant potential |
| **Ganglioneuroma** | Fully mature ganglion cells; Schwann stroma | Benign; typically in older children |

The **International Neuroblastoma Pathology Classification (INPC / Shimada system)** classifies tumours as favourable or unfavourable histology based on the degree of differentiation, Schwannian stromal content, mitosis-karyorrhexis index (MKI), and patient age.

### Cervical Neuroblastoma

- Accounts for **1–5% of all neuroblastomas**; the adrenal medulla is the most common primary site (35–40%)
- Cervical tumours arise from the **cervical sympathetic chain** (superior cervical ganglion or middle cervical ganglion)
- Represents the **most favourable anatomical subsite** — cervical location is associated with lower INRG risk and better prognosis

### INRG Staging System

The **International Neuroblastoma Risk Group (INRG) Staging System** (2009) uses pre-operative imaging rather than surgical findings:

| Stage | Definition |
|---|---|
| **L1** | Localised tumour; no image-defined risk factors (IDRFs); confined to one body compartment |
| **L2** | Localised tumour; one or more IDRFs present |
| **M** | Distant metastatic disease (except MS) |
| **MS** | Metastatic disease in children <18 months confined to skin, liver, and/or bone marrow (<10%) — "special" stage with potential for spontaneous regression |

### Image-Defined Risk Factors (IDRFs) in the Neck

IDRFs predict surgical difficulty and risk of incomplete resection:

| IDRF | Cervical Relevance |
|---|---|
| Encasement or abutment of carotid artery or jugular vein | Common in cervical neuroblastoma |
| Encasement of brachial plexus roots | Cervicothoracic junction tumours |
| Intraspinal extension (>⅓ of spinal canal, with cord compression) | Dumbbell extension through neural foramina |
| Encasement of trachea or oesophagus | Large cervical or mediastinal extension |
| Extension into the skull base | Superior cervical ganglion tumours |

### Clinical Features

#### Local Features (Cervical Mass)
- **Firm, non-tender mass** deep to the sternocleidomastoid (SCM)
- Mass in the posterior triangle or along the carotid sheath
- Rapid growth; may be large at presentation

#### Horner Syndrome
Involvement of the **cervical sympathetic chain** produces the classic triad:
- **Ptosis** (partial; levator palpebrae superioris spared — unlike CN III palsy)
- **Miosis** (small pupil)
- **Anhidrosis** (absent sweating ipsilaterally on the face)
- **Heterochromia iridis** — lighter iris on the affected side (in congenital or long-standing sympathetic disruption)

#### Signs of Systemic/Metastatic Disease
- **Periorbital ecchymosis ("raccoon eyes")** -- retroorbital metastasis with periorbital haemorrhage; pathognomonic of metastatic neuroblastoma
- **Proptosis** -- retroorbital mass
- **Subcutaneous nodules** ("blueberry muffin" in MS stage)
- **Hepatomegaly** -- massive in MS stage (Pepper syndrome)
- **Bone pain / limping** -- skeletal metastases
- **Dancing eyes / dancing feet** (opsoclonus-myoclonus-ataxia syndrome) -- paraneoplastic

### Investigations

#### Biochemical
- **Urine catecholamines:** 24-hour urine VMA (vanillylmandelic acid) and HVA (homovanillic acid) — elevated in >90% of neuroblastomas; diagnostic
- **Serum LDH** -- elevated in high-risk disease; adverse prognostic marker
- **Serum ferritin** -- elevated in metastatic disease; adverse prognostic marker
- **NSE (neuron-specific enolase)** -- elevated; correlates with tumour burden

#### Imaging
- **CT neck/chest/abdomen/pelvis** -- defines primary tumour, IDRFs, lymph node involvement, hepatic and osseous metastases
- **MRI** -- superior soft tissue delineation; intraspinal extension (mandatory if dumbbell tumour suspected)
- **MIBG scan (¹²³I-meta-iodobenzylguanidine)** -- functional imaging; taken up by noradrenergic tissue; whole-body staging of neuroblastoma with very high sensitivity (>90%); also used to assess response and guide MIBG therapy

#### Pathological
- **Core needle biopsy** of the primary mass (CT-guided or open)
- **Bone marrow biopsy** (bilateral iliac crests) -- mandatory for staging
- **FISH (Fluorescence In Situ Hybridisation):** Detection of MYCN amplification, 1p deletion, 11q aberration, 17q gain

### Risk Stratification

The **INRG risk groups** integrate staging, age, histology, and molecular markers:

| Risk Group | Features | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Very Low / Low** | L1 or L2; favourable histology; no MYCN amplification; age <18 months (MS without MYCN) | Surgery alone ± observation |
| **Intermediate** | L2; unfavourable histology; or M disease <18 months without MYCN | Surgery + chemotherapy (4–8 cycles) |
| **High** | M stage >18 months; any stage with MYCN amplification; L2/M with unfavourable features | Induction chemo + surgery + consolidation (HDC-ASCR) + maintenance |

#### Key Molecular Markers

| Marker | Significance |
|---|---|
| **MYCN amplification** | Most important adverse marker; >10 copies; present in 25% high-risk NB; poor prognosis (<40% 5-yr OS) |
| **ALK mutation/amplification** | Targetable with crizotinib/lorlatinib; poor prognosis |
| **1p deletion** | Associated with MYCN amplification; adverse |
| **11q aberration (segmental)** | Independent adverse prognostic marker in non-MYCN-amplified disease |
| **Ploidy (DNA index)** | Hyperdiploidy/triploid favourable (especially in infants); diploid/tetraploid unfavourable |

### Neck-Specific Surgical Considerations

- **Goal:** Complete resection without amputation of vital structures
- **Cervical sympathetic chain involvement:** Attempt to preserve where possible; sacrifice accepted when necessary for complete resection of L1 tumours without IDRFs
- **Carotid encasement (IDRF):** Upfront surgery not recommended; induction chemotherapy to reduce IDRFs first
- **Brachial plexus preservation:** Paramount; permanent neurological deficit unacceptable if avoidable
- **Dumbbell tumours:** Combined neurosurgery and head-and-neck approach; decompression of spinal cord is the priority

### Treatment Summary

| Risk Group | Approach |
|---|---|
| **Low-risk** | Surgery alone; observation for MS with spontaneous regression potential |
| **Intermediate-risk** | Surgery + 4–8 cycles carboplatin/etoposide/cyclophosphamide/doxorubicin |
| **High-risk** | Induction chemo (6 cycles COJEC/N5-N6) → surgery → consolidation (Busulfan-Melphalan + autologous SCR) → local RT to primary site → maintenance (dinutuximab + isotretinoin/13-cis-retinoic acid) |

### Prognosis

| Group | 5-Year Overall Survival |
|---|---|
| All stages combined | ~75% |
| Localised cervical disease (L1) | >90% |
| Intermediate risk | 85–90% |
| High risk | 40–50% |
| MYCN-amplified | <40% |
| MS stage (infants, no MYCN) | >85% (spontaneous regression) |

## Part B — Olfactory Neuroblastoma (Esthesioneuroblastoma) — Role of Neck Dissection (Q37)

### Definition and Origin

**Olfactory neuroblastoma (ONB)**, also called **esthesioneuroblastoma**, is a malignant neural crest tumour arising from the **olfactory neuroepithelium** of the cribriform plate. It is a rare tumour accounting for 3–6% of all sinonasal malignancies.

- **Origin:** Olfactory receptor cells (bipolar neurons) of the olfactory mucosa lining the roof of the nasal cavity, superior septum, and superior turbinate
- Bimodal age distribution: peaks at 11–20 years and 50–60 years
- No sex predominance

### Staging Systems

#### Kadish Staging (Original, 1976)
| Stage | Extent |
|---|---|
| **A** | Confined to the nasal cavity |
| **B** | Extends to the paranasal sinuses |
| **C** | Extends beyond the nasal cavity and sinuses (orbit, skull base, intracranial, dura) |

#### Modified Kadish (Morita, 1993)
| Stage | Extent |
|---|---|
| A, B, C | As above |
| **D** | Cervical lymph node or distant metastasis |

#### Hyams Histological Grading
| Grade | Features | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | Well differentiated; lobular architecture; fibrillary stroma; no mitoses | Favourable |
| **II** | Moderately differentiated; pseudorosettes (Homer Wright); rare mitoses | Favourable |
| **III** | Poorly differentiated; true rosettes (Flexner-Wintersteiner); frequent mitoses; necrosis | Unfavourable |
| **IV** | Undifferentiated; sheets of cells; no rosettes; prominent mitoses; necrosis | Unfavourable |

Higher Hyams grade correlates with worse prognosis and higher rates of cervical and distant metastasis.

### Clinical Features

- **Unilateral nasal obstruction** -- most common presenting symptom (>90%)
- **Epistaxis** -- unilateral; recurrent
- **Anosmia** -- due to direct involvement of olfactory epithelium
- **Proptosis / periorbital swelling** -- orbital extension (Kadish C)
- **Headache** -- intracranial extension
- **Visual disturbance / diplopia** -- cavernous sinus or orbital apex involvement
- **Neck mass** -- cervical lymphadenopathy (Kadish D)

### Imaging

- **CT (bone windows):** Bony erosion of cribriform plate; skull base destruction; calcification within tumour; paranasal sinus opacification
- **MRI (gadolinium-enhanced):** Superior delineation of intracranial, dural, or orbital extension; characteristic "dumbbell" configuration with waist at cribriform plate; cysts at the intracranial tumour margin are characteristic
- **PET-CT:** Staging; distant metastasis assessment; response evaluation; not sensitive for small cervical nodal disease

### Cervical Lymph Node Metastasis in ONB

This is the most examinable aspect of ONB management:

| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| **Incidence at presentation** | 5–20% of all cases |
| **Delayed cervical metastasis** | Up to 20–30% develop neck disease months to years after primary treatment |
| **Most common nodal level** | Level II (jugulodigastric), followed by Level I and III |
| **Bilateral nodal disease** | Uncommon; <5% at presentation |
| **Kadish C disease** | Higher risk of nodal metastasis (~30%) |
| **High Hyams grade** | Significantly higher nodal metastasis risk |

The **delayed and late onset of cervical metastasis** (sometimes 5–10 years after primary treatment) is a defining biological characteristic of ONB that directly informs the management of the N0 neck.

### Role of Neck Dissection in Olfactory Neuroblastoma

#### The Central Question
Given the 20–30% risk of delayed cervical metastasis, should the **clinically N0 neck** be treated electively?

#### N+ Neck: Therapeutic Neck Dissection
- **Therapeutic neck dissection is mandatory** for clinically or radiologically positive cervical nodes
- Level II–IV selective neck dissection is the standard; bilateral if bilateral nodes present
- Combined with adjuvant radiotherapy to the neck
- Outcome with therapeutic neck dissection: 5-year control of neck disease ~50–60%

#### N0 Neck: Elective Treatment — Options and Evidence

| Strategy | Details | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Observation** | No treatment of N0 neck; salvage at relapse | High rate of salvage-failure; supported only in Kadish A/B, low Hyams grade |
| **Elective Neck Dissection (END)** | Selective ND (levels II–IV) at time of primary surgery | Allows pathological staging; low false-negative rate; recommended for Kadish C, high Hyams grade |
| **Elective Neck Irradiation (ENI)** | Inclusion of bilateral or ipsilateral neck nodal basins in the RT field | Most widely practised; avoids surgical morbidity; levels I–IV included in the radiation field |
| **Combined (END + RT)** | Radical approach; reserved for high-risk patients or those with pathologically positive nodes found on END | Maximal nodal control |

#### Current Recommendations for N0 Neck

- **Kadish A / Hyams Grade I–II:** Observation acceptable; close surveillance with annual MRI/CT of neck for minimum 5 years
- **Kadish B–C / Hyams Grade III–IV:** Elective neck treatment is recommended — either **elective neck irradiation** (preferred) or **elective neck dissection** (if pathological staging needed or RT not planned)
- **Shah (5th Ed) recommendation:** For advanced ONB (Kadish C), elective bilateral level II–IV neck dissection or bilateral elective neck irradiation should be performed given the documented risk of delayed cervical relapse
- **The Dulguerov meta-analysis (2001)** demonstrated that combined craniofacial resection + RT achieved the best outcomes and supports inclusion of elective neck treatment in advanced disease

### Primary Treatment of ONB

#### Surgical Approach
**Craniofacial Resection (CFR)** — the standard of care for resectable ONB:
- Combined **transcranial + transfacial** approach
- Resects the cribriform plate en bloc with the olfactory bulbs, dura, anterior skull base, and nasal septum
- Achieves clear margins at the skull base — the most important determinant of local control
- Mortality <5% in experienced centres; morbidity: CSF leak, meningitis, frontal lobe injury, wound infection

**Endoscopic Skull Base Surgery:**
- Purely endoscopic endonasal approach increasingly adopted
- Comparable oncological outcomes to open CFR in selected cases
- Lower morbidity (no craniotomy scar, shorter hospital stay, faster recovery)
- Preferred for Kadish A–B and selected Kadish C without intracranial invasion

#### Radiation Therapy
- **Adjuvant RT** (55–60 Gy) is indicated for all Kadish B–C tumours after surgery
- **IMRT** allows better sparing of optic apparatus, brain, and pituitary
- Primary RT (without surgery) for unresectable disease
- **Elective neck RT:** 45–50 Gy to bilateral levels II–IV for N0 neck in advanced disease

#### Chemotherapy
- **Cisplatin-based chemotherapy** (cisplatin + etoposide or cisplatin + ifosfamide) used in:
  - Neoadjuvant setting for locally advanced / unresectable disease
  - Adjuvant treatment for high-grade (Hyams III–IV) tumours
  - Metastatic / recurrent disease
- Response rates 50–70% to neoadjuvant chemotherapy
- No randomised trials — evidence is from retrospective series

### Outcomes

| Kadish Stage | 5-Year OS |
|---|---|
| A | ~90% |
| B | ~70–75% |
| C | ~50–55% |
| D (nodal/distant) | ~25–30% |
| Overall (all stages) | ~62–78% |

- **Hyams Grade I–II:** 5-year OS 80–90%
- **Hyams Grade III–IV:** 5-year OS 40–50%
- **Recurrence:** Local recurrence 30–40%; cervical relapse 20–30%; distant metastasis 10–20%
- Monitoring: Annual MRI of skull base and neck for minimum 10 years (late recurrence well documented)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Neuroblastoma staging uses INRG system** (L1, L2, M, MS) based on **pre-operative imaging IDRFs**, not surgical findings — key distinction from older INSS (International Neuroblastoma Staging System) which required surgical assessment
> 2. **Cervical neuroblastoma = most favourable prognosis** among all anatomical sites; localised cervical L1 tumours have >90% 5-year OS
> 3. **Horner syndrome in a child** with a neck mass is pathognomonic of cervical sympathetic chain involvement — most commonly neuroblastoma until proven otherwise
> 4. **"Raccoon eyes"** (periorbital ecchymosis) = retroorbital metastases; pathognomonic of metastatic neuroblastoma; not to be confused with trauma
> 5. **MYCN amplification** is the most critical adverse molecular marker in neuroblastoma — reduces 5-year OS to <40% regardless of stage; drives treatment intensification to high-risk protocol
> 6. **ONB is the only sinonasal tumour** with a bimodal age distribution (11–20 years and 50–60 years); all other sinonasal cancers increase with age
> 7. **Delayed cervical metastasis in ONB** can occur 5–10 years after primary treatment — annual neck surveillance (MRI/CT) for at least 10 years is mandatory; this unique biology justifies elective neck treatment at the time of primary surgery
> 8. **Hyams grade independently predicts prognosis** in ONB — Grades I–II are favourable (OS ~80–90%); Grades III–IV are unfavourable (OS ~40–50%) and mandate elective neck treatment and adjuvant chemotherapy consideration
> 9. **Kadish staging vs Hyams grading:** Kadish = anatomical extent (guides resectability); Hyams = histological differentiation (guides adjuvant treatment and neck management) — both must be reported
> 10. **Endoscopic craniofacial resection** has achieved oncological equivalence to open CFR in selected patients (Kadish A–B, selected C without broad dural invasion), with lower morbidity — now the preferred approach in high-volume skull base centres
