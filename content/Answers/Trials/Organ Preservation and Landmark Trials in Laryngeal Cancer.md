## Historical Context

Before 1991, the standard treatment for locally advanced (stage III/IV) laryngeal cancer was **total laryngectomy with postoperative radiation therapy**. This approach achieved good locoregional control but permanently eliminated the natural voice and required a tracheostoma. The VA Laryngeal Study transformed practice by proving the larynx could be preserved without compromising survival.

## Part A: Landmark Trials in Laryngeal Organ Preservation

### Timeline Overview

| Year | Trial | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| 1991 | VA Laryngeal Cancer Study | Induction chemo + RT equivalent survival to surgery; 64% larynx preservation |
| 1996 | EORTC 24891 (Lefebvre) | Extended organ preservation to hypopharyngeal cancer |
| 2003 | RTOG 91-11 (Forastiere) | Concurrent CRT superior to induction CRT and RT alone for larynx preservation |
| 2007 | TAX 324 (Posner) | TPF superior to PF as induction chemotherapy in LA-HNSCC |
| 2009 | GORTEC 2000-01 (Pointreau) | TPF superior to PF for larynx preservation specifically |
| 2013 | RTOG 91-11 10-year update | Concurrent CRT best for preservation; non-cancer deaths elevated |

---

### 1. VA Laryngeal Cancer Study (Department of Veterans Affairs, 1991)

**Wolf et al., NEJM 1991**

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III trial |
| **Population** | 332 patients, stage III–IV laryngeal SCC (glottic and supraglottic) |
| **Arm A (Induction)** | Cisplatin 100 mg/m² + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/d × 5d; 2 cycles → response assessment → RT (66–76 Gy) in responders; non-responders → salvage laryngectomy + postoperative RT |
| **Arm B (Surgery)** | Total laryngectomy + postoperative RT (50–70 Gy) |
| **Primary endpoint** | Overall survival |

**Key Results:**
- 2-year OS: **68% (both arms)** — no significant difference
- Larynx preservation at 2 years: **64%** in the chemotherapy arm
- Response to first 2 cycles predicted larynx preservation
- Higher rate of local recurrence in the chemotherapy arm (compensated by surgical salvage)
- Trend toward fewer distant metastases in the chemotherapy arm

**Significance:**
- Established the **proof of concept** for organ preservation without compromising survival
- Introduced the concept of **chemoselection** (response to induction chemotherapy as a surrogate for radiosensitivity)
- Led directly to the RTOG 91-11 trial design

---

### 2. EORTC 24891 Trial (Lefebvre, 1996)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III |
| **Population** | 202 patients, stage II–IV resectable hypopharyngeal cancer |
| **Arm A** | Induction cisplatin/5-FU (2–3 cycles) → complete/partial responders: definitive RT; non-responders: surgery + postoperative RT |
| **Arm B** | Immediate surgery (total laryngectomy + partial pharyngectomy) + postoperative RT |
| **Primary endpoint** | Overall survival |

**Key Results:**
- No significant difference in OS (median ~25 months both arms)
- Larynx preservation: **42% at 3 years**, 35% at 5 years
- 10-year update confirmed equivalent survival; functional larynx retained in most survivors

**Significance:** Extended the organ-preservation concept from laryngeal to **hypopharyngeal** cancer; provided basis for the standard that resectable hypopharyngeal cancer can undergo organ-preservation attempt.

---

### 3. RTOG 91-11 Trial (Forastiere 2003, 2013)

**The Definitive Three-Arm Trial for Larynx Preservation**

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III, three-arm |
| **Population** | 547 patients, stage III–IV (T2–4, N0–N2c; excluded T1, and T4 with massive involvement) glottic/supraglottic laryngeal SCC |
| **Arm A (Induction)** | Cisplatin/5-FU × 3 cycles → RT (70 Gy) in responders; surgery in non-responders |
| **Arm B (Concurrent)** | Cisplatin 100 mg/m² on days 1, 22, 43 + RT (70 Gy) |
| **Arm C (RT alone)** | RT (70 Gy) alone |
| **Primary endpoint** | Laryngeal preservation rate |

**Key Results (2003 publication):**

| Endpoint | Induction + RT | Concurrent CRT | RT Alone |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2-yr laryngeal preservation | 72% | **84%** | 67% |
| Locoregional control | 61% | **78%** | 56% |
| 2-yr OS | 76% | 74% | 75% (NS) |
| Distant metastasis | Lowest | Intermediate | Highest |

**10-Year Update (2013 publication):**

| Endpoint | Induction + RT | Concurrent CRT | RT Alone |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10-yr larynx preservation | 63.5% | **81.7%** | 63.8% |
| 10-yr OS | 27.5% | 28.9% | 31.5% (NS) |
| Non-cancer deaths | Lower | **Highest** | Lowest |

**Critical Findings:**
1. Concurrent CRT is **superior to both other arms for laryngeal preservation**
2. **Overall survival was NOT different** between the three arms at 10 years
3. Concerning signal: **more non-cancer-related deaths** in the concurrent arm (possibly aspiration pneumonia, late dysphagia, malnutrition)
4. The paradox: best larynx preservation but no survival advantage

**Criticisms:**
- Excluded T1 tumours and bulky T4 (>1 cm cartilage invasion)
- No systematic QOL or functional outcome data collected
- Non-cancer death signal raises concern about "preserved but non-functional larynx"

---

### 4. TAX 324 (Posner, 2007)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III |
| **Population** | 501 patients with locally advanced HNSCC (multiple sites, including larynx) |
| **Arm A (TPF)** | Docetaxel 75 mg/m² + cisplatin 100 mg/m² + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/d × 4d → CRT (weekly carboplatin × 7 + RT 70–74 Gy) |
| **Arm B (PF)** | Cisplatin 100 mg/m² + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/d × 5d → CRT |

**Key Results:**
- Median OS: **71 months (TPF) vs 30 months (PF)** — significantly superior
- 3-year OS: 62% vs 48%
- PFS and overall response rate significantly better with TPF
- TPF had more neutropenia; PF had more mucositis

**Significance:** Established TPF as the preferred induction chemotherapy regimen when induction is used.

---

### 5. GORTEC 2000-01 (Pointreau, 2009)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III (larynx/hypopharynx specific) |
| **Population** | 213 patients, stage III/IV laryngeal or hypopharyngeal SCC |
| **Arm A** | TPF × 3 cycles → RT (if ≥50% response) in responders; surgery in non-responders |
| **Arm B** | PF × 3 cycles → RT (if ≥50% response) in responders; surgery in non-responders |
| **Primary endpoint** | Larynx preservation at 3 years |

**Key Results:**
- 3-year larynx preservation: **70.3% (TPF) vs 57.5% (PF)** — p = 0.03
- 5-year OS: similar between arms (60.4% TPF vs 65.5% PF — NS)
- Confirmed TPF is the superior induction regimen for organ preservation

---

### 6. TREMPLIN Trial (Lefebvre, 2013)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Population** | Stage III–IV laryngeal/hypopharyngeal SCC; only patients responding to TPF induction |
| **Comparison** | RT + cisplatin (100 mg/m² × 2) vs RT + cetuximab (weekly) as concurrent therapy |
| **Primary endpoint** | Larynx preservation at 3 months after CRT |

**Key Results:**
- Larynx preservation at 3 months: **95% (cisplatin) vs 93% (cetuximab)** — comparable
- Cisplatin had better disease control at 18 months (larynx function)
- Cetuximab was not superior, but was better tolerated in terms of acute mucositis

**Significance:** Informed the ongoing discussion about induction TPF → subsequent cisplatin vs cetuximab CRT.

## Part B: T3N0M0 Carcinoma Larynx — Evaluation and Management

**Q9 (TMH 2019):** Management of a 40-year-old with T3N0M0 carcinoma larynx

### Definition of T3 Laryngeal Cancer
- **Glottic T3:** Tumour limited to larynx with vocal cord fixation, and/or invasion of paraglottic space, and/or minor thyroid cartilage erosion (inner cortex)
- **Supraglottic T3:** Invades postcricoid area, pre-epiglottic space, paraglottic space, and/or minor thyroid cartilage erosion

### Pre-treatment Evaluation

| Assessment | Components |
|---|---|
| **History** | Duration of hoarseness, dysphagia, dyspnoea; smoking/alcohol history; performance status |
| **Fibreoptic laryngoscopy** | Extent of tumour, vocal cord mobility, subglottic extension |
| **CT scan neck + chest** | Cartilage involvement, paraglottic space, lymph nodes, lung metastases |
| **MRI larynx** | Superior to CT for pre-epiglottic space involvement; thyroid cartilage involvement |
| **PET-CT** | For N0 disease — to assess occult nodal disease; remote metastases |
| **Stroboscopy** | Vocal cord vibration assessment if appropriate |
| **Speech and swallow assessment** | Pre-treatment baseline |
| **Nutritional assessment** | Weight loss, BMI; consider PEG if malnourished |
| **Multidisciplinary team** | Head and neck surgeon, radiation oncologist, medical oncologist, speech therapist |

### Management Algorithm for T3N0M0 Larynx

```
T3N0M0 Laryngeal Cancer
        │
        ├── Assess Suitability for Organ Preservation
        │         │
        │         ├── SUITABLE (no massive T4, adequate pulmonary reserve, good performance status)
        │         │       │
        │         │       ├── Preferred: Concurrent CRT (cisplatin 100mg/m² d1,22,43 + RT 70Gy)
        │         │       │              ─ Standard of care based on RTOG 91-11
        │         │       │
        │         │       └── Alternative: Conservation Surgery (if experienced centre)
        │         │              ─ Supracricoid laryngectomy with CHEP or CHP
        │         │              ─ Laser cordectomy (select glottic T3)
        │         │              ─ Followed by adjuvant RT if adverse features
        │         │
        │         └── NOT SUITABLE (poor pulmonary reserve, massive cartilage invasion, patient preference)
        │                 │
        │                 └── Total Laryngectomy + Postoperative RT
```

### Evidence Base for T3 Management

| Option | Evidence | Larynx Preservation Rate | 5-yr OS |
|---|---|---|---|
| Concurrent CRT (cisplatin + RT 70 Gy) | RTOG 91-11 | 84% at 2 years | ~55–60% |
| Induction TPF → CRT | GORTEC 2000-01 | 70% | ~60% |
| Supracricoid laryngectomy (CHEP) | Multiple series | 100% (functional larynx) | ~65–70% |
| Total laryngectomy + postoperative RT | Historical standard | 0% | 60–65% |

**For T3N0M0 specifically:** Concurrent CRT is the preferred non-surgical option based on RTOG 91-11. In centres with expertise, supracricoid laryngectomy (CHEP for glottic T3) offers functional larynx preservation with potentially better swallowing outcomes than CRT.

## Part C: MARCH Meta-analysis — Altered Fractionation

**Q16 (TMH 2022):** MARCH meta-analysis and indications/pros/cons of altered fractionation

### Background

The MARCH (Meta-Analysis of Radiotherapy in Carcinomas of Head and neck) Collaborative Group published a series of meta-analyses examining whether altered fractionation schedules improve outcomes over conventional fractionation in head and neck cancer.

### Meta-analysis Overview

| Study | Publication | Trials included | Patients |
|---|---|---|---|
| MARCH (original) | Lacas, Radiother Oncol 2017 (updated) | 34 randomised trials | 11,969 patients |
| Comparison | Altered fractionation (hyperfractionation or accelerated) vs conventional RT (once daily, 1.8–2 Gy per fraction) | — | — |

### Key Findings (MARCH Updated 2017)

| Endpoint | Conventional RT | Altered Fractionation | Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5-year OS | Reference | +3.4% absolute | HR 0.92 (95% CI 0.86–0.97), p = 0.003 |
| Locoregional control | Reference | +6.4% absolute | p < 0.001 |
| Hyperfractionation benefit | +8% locoregional control | Most beneficial subtype | — |
| Accelerated fractionation | +2% locoregional control | Less benefit than hyperfractionation | — |
| Late toxicity | — | Modestly increased late mucositis/xerostomia | No increased late dysphagia |

### Types of Altered Fractionation

#### 1. Hyperfractionation
- **Definition:** More fractions (>1 per day), smaller dose per fraction, **same overall treatment time**, increased total dose
- **Rationale:** Exploits the radiobiological advantage of smaller fractions in reducing late normal tissue injury while maintaining tumour control; allows higher total dose
- **Example:** 1.2 Gy × 2 fractions/day (6 hr apart) = 80–81 Gy in 7 weeks
- **RTOG 90-03:** Hyperfractionation (81.6 Gy) and accelerated fractionation with concomitant boost both improved locoregional control vs conventional RT; hyperfractionation best for locoregional control

#### 2. Accelerated Fractionation
- **Definition:** Same dose per fraction, same total dose, but **shorter overall treatment time** (reducing treatment time reduces tumour repopulation)
- **Rationale:** Reduces overall treatment time to minimise accelerated tumour repopulation between fractions
- **Example:** Pure acceleration: 2 Gy × 3 fractions/week (6 fractions in 5 days); concomitant boost: standard fractions + PM boost in last 12 days
- **CHART (Continuous Hyperfractionated Accelerated Radiation Therapy):** 54 Gy in 36 fractions over 12 days (3 × 1.5 Gy/day) — improved locoregional control in laryngeal cancer (Dische 1997)

#### 3. Accelerated Fractionation with Reduced Total Dose
- Shorter overall time with modest dose reduction to limit late toxicity
- Less commonly used; may compromise total dose

### Indications for Altered Fractionation

| Indication | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Locally advanced laryngeal/hypopharyngeal cancer (if no concurrent chemo) | Hyperfractionation provides significant OS benefit |
| Patients unfit for concurrent chemotherapy | Altered fractionation as a substitute for CRT when cisplatin is contraindicated |
| Early/intermediate stage HNC | Hyperfractionation achieves comparable or better results than CRT in some series |
| High-volume tumour with likely accelerated repopulation | Accelerated regimens reduce repopulation advantage |

### Pros and Cons of Altered Fractionation

| Aspect | Hyperfractionation | Accelerated Fractionation |
|---|---|---|
| **Advantages** | Higher biological dose; better locoregional control and OS | Reduced tumour repopulation; shorter treatment duration |
| **Disadvantages** | Twice-daily visits (6 hr minimum interval); logistically complex | Increased acute mucositis; may not allow adequate recovery |
| **Late toxicity** | Similar to conventional; slightly increased late xerostomia | Higher acute mucositis but similar late toxicity |
| **Utility in CRT era** | Being replaced by concurrent cisplatin-based CRT | Still useful when chemo is contraindicated |

**Key limitation of MARCH:** Most trials were conducted before routine IMRT — the absolute benefits of altered fractionation may be smaller when IMRT is used, as IMRT itself reduces toxicity.

## Part D: Swallowing Outcomes After CRT for Laryngeal Cancer

**Q17 (TMH 2022):** Evaluation of swallowing outcomes after CRT for laryngeal cancers — instruments and results from landmark studies

### Why Swallowing Assessment Matters

Dysphagia is the most functionally significant late complication of CRT for laryngeal cancer. It can manifest as:
- **Aspiration** (silent or symptomatic) — leading to pneumonia
- **Reduced pharyngeal clearance** — requiring multiple swallows
- **Stricture/stenosis** of the pharyngoesophageal segment
- **PEG dependency** — inability to maintain oral nutrition

The RTOG 91-11 non-cancer deaths signal (10-year update) is attributed in part to aspiration-related complications from a **"preserved but non-functional larynx"**.

### Assessment Instruments

#### A. Subjective / Patient-Reported Outcome Instruments

| Instrument | Full Name | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **MDADI** | MD Anderson Dysphagia Inventory | 20-item self-report; 4 subscales (global, emotional, functional, physical); score 0–100 (higher = better swallowing); validated in HNC; most commonly used in de-escalation trials |
| **SWAL-QOL** | Swallowing Quality of Life questionnaire | 44-item; 11 subscales; comprehensive patient-reported swallowing QOL; validated in dysphagia |
| **PSS-HN** | Performance Status Scale for Head and Neck Cancer | 3 domains: normalcy of diet, understandability of speech, public eating; widely used in HNC trials |
| **EORTC QLQ-H&N35** | EORTC Head and Neck Cancer Specific Module | Part of EORTC QOL battery; includes swallowing and nutrition subscales |
| **VHI** | Voice Handicap Index | Self-report of voice function; 30 items; used alongside swallowing measures |
| **EAT-10** | Eating Assessment Tool | 10-item screening; quick dysphagia screen |

#### B. Objective Instrumental Assessment

| Instrument | Full Name | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **FEES** | Fibreoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing | Direct visualisation of pharynx/larynx; assesses bolus control, penetration/aspiration; penetration-aspiration scale (PAS); no radiation; can be done at bedside |
| **MBS / VFSS** | Modified Barium Swallow / Videofluoroscopic Swallowing Study | Dynamic X-ray; gold standard for aspiration detection; quantifies swallow timing; identifies anatomical dysfunction; requires X-ray facilities; Rosenbek PAS used |
| **High-resolution manometry** | — | Quantifies pharyngoesophageal pressure; used in research |

#### C. Functional Outcome Scales

| Scale | Application |
|---|---|
| **Functional Oral Intake Scale (FOIS)** | 7-level ordinal scale (1 = nothing by mouth → 7 = full oral diet); widely used |
| **Performance Status Scale — Normalcy of Diet** | 8-level scale (0 = nothing orally → 100 = no restriction) |

### Landmark Studies on Swallowing After CRT

#### 1. Hutcheson et al. (2012) — Late Dysphagia after Radiotherapy-Based Treatment

- **Design:** Prospective study, 208 HNC patients (majority laryngeal/pharyngeal)
- **Key finding:** Swallowing dysfunction can develop or worsen **years after treatment** ("late dysphagia")
- Severity correlates with radiation dose to pharyngeal constrictors and larynx
- Rate of aspiration pneumonia highest in those with late dysphagia
- **Clinical implication:** Need for long-term swallowing surveillance beyond 12 months post-treatment

#### 2. Logemann et al. (2008) — Swallowing in the First Year After Radiation and CRT

- **Design:** Longitudinal study; FEES and MBS assessments at baseline, 1 month, 3 months, 12 months
- **Key finding:** Peak swallowing dysfunction at **3 months** post-CRT; limited recovery by 12 months
- CRT worse than RT alone for swallowing at all timepoints
- **Clinical implication:** Intensive rehabilitation needed during and immediately after CRT

#### 3. Eisbruch et al. (2004) — Dysphagia/Aspiration-Related Structures (DARS)

- Identified DARS: **pharyngeal constrictors**, **supraglottic larynx**, and **glottic larynx**
- Radiation dose to these structures predicted late swallowing dysfunction
- Established the foundation for **DARS-sparing IMRT** — prospective dose-volume constraints for swallowing structures
- **Clinical implication:** When designing IMRT plans, limiting dose to inferior pharyngeal constrictors (<50 Gy mean), supraglottic larynx, and glottic larynx reduces late dysphagia

#### 4. RTOG 91-11 10-Year Update (Forastiere, 2013)

- Non-cancer deaths significantly higher in concurrent CRT arm
- Attributed partly to aspiration-related complications (pneumonia) from a preserved but non-functional larynx
- **Clinical implication:** Larynx preservation does not equal functional larynx; swallowing rehabilitation is critical

#### 5. Swallowing Outcomes in ORATOR Trial (Palma, 2020)

- Primary endpoint was MDADI at 1 year
- RT arm had significantly better MDADI (84.3 vs 74.4, p = 0.042)
- Challenged the assumption that TORS automatically yields better functional outcomes than RT

### Dysphagia Risk Stratification: Predictors of Poor Swallowing after CRT

| Risk Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Higher radiation dose to PCM/pharyngeal constrictors | Direct dose-response relationship with dysphagia |
| Tumour site (hypopharynx > oropharynx > larynx) | Hypopharyngeal tumours have worst dysphagia |
| T stage (higher T) | More extensive radiation field needed |
| Neck dissection (bilateral) | Disruption of swallowing musculature |
| Weight loss >5% pre-treatment | Baseline nutritional compromise |
| Concurrent cisplatin | Additive neurotoxicity to CRT-related fibrosis |
| Poor baseline swallowing function | Worse post-treatment trajectory |
| Lack of rehabilitation | Absence of swallowing exercises increases fibrosis risk |

### Swallowing Rehabilitation Strategies

| Strategy | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Prophylactic swallowing exercises** (pre-treatment) | Maintains muscle function; reduces fibrosis; improves post-treatment recovery |
| **Mendelsohn manoeuvre** | Prolongs laryngeal elevation; improves CP opening |
| **Shaker exercise** | Strengthens suprahyoid muscles; improves UES opening |
| **Effortful swallow** | Increases pharyngeal pressure; improves clearance |
| **Chin tuck** | Reduces aspiration risk by narrowing laryngeal inlet |
| **Head turn** | Directs bolus to contralateral side (used with unilateral pharyngeal weakness) |
| **Diet modification** | Texture modification; thickened fluids for aspiration risk |
| **PEG placement** | For inability to maintain adequate oral nutrition; also used prophylactically in selected high-risk patients |
| **Botulinum toxin injection** | For CP spasm/dysfunction |
| **CP myotomy** | For UES dysfunction — surgical option for selected patients |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. VA Laryngeal Cancer Study (1991): First proof that organ preservation is feasible with equivalent survival — 64% larynx preservation with induction chemo + RT vs surgery; this is the foundational trial
> 2. RTOG 91-11 (Forastiere 2003): Three-arm trial — concurrent CRT achieves 84% larynx preservation (superior to induction 72% and RT alone 67%); overall survival same across all three arms
> 3. RTOG 91-11 10-year update (2013): Best larynx preservation with concurrent CRT, but highest non-cancer deaths — the "preserved but non-functional larynx" paradox
> 4. TPF is the preferred induction chemotherapy regimen for organ preservation (TAX 324, GORTEC 2000-01) — superior to PF for OS and larynx preservation
> 5. MARCH meta-analysis: Altered fractionation improves 5-year OS by 3.4% and locoregional control by 6.4% vs conventional RT; hyperfractionation has the greatest benefit
> 6. Indications for altered fractionation: patients unfit for cisplatin-based CRT, locally advanced disease when chemo is contraindicated
> 7. T3N0M0 larynx management: concurrent CRT (RTOG 91-11 standard) OR supracricoid laryngectomy (CHEP) in experienced centres; total laryngectomy reserved for poor organ preservation candidates
> 8. Swallowing instruments: MDADI (patient-reported, most used in trials), PSS-HN, SWAL-QOL (subjective); FEES and Modified Barium Swallow/VFSS (objective); FOIS (functional scale)
> 9. Landmark swallowing studies: Logemann (2008) — peak dysfunction at 3 months; Hutcheson (2012) — late dysphagia can worsen years after treatment; Eisbruch (2004) — DARS-sparing IMRT reduces dysphagia
> 10. Concurrent CRT reduces distant metastases (RTOG 91-11) while organ preservation with induction chemo reduces distant failure more than RT alone

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The "paradox" of RTOG 91-11: concurrent CRT gives best larynx preservation rate but NOT the best overall survival (all three arms equivalent at 10 years) — this distinction is critical for the exam
> - CHART is a specific accelerated fractionation schedule (54 Gy in 36 fractions, 3×/day for 12 days) that improved locoregional control in laryngeal cancer but is logistically very demanding
> - MARCH meta-analysis shows altered fractionation benefits most when concurrent chemotherapy is NOT used; when CRT is used, it is generally the preferred approach over altered fractionation alone
> - The non-cancer deaths in the RTOG 91-11 concurrent CRT arm (10-year update) is thought to reflect aspiration pneumonia from a preserved but dysfunctional larynx — a critical late toxicity concern
> - MDADI score: higher is better (100 = normal swallowing); the ORATOR trial used MDADI as its primary endpoint; a clinically meaningful difference is typically ≥5–8 points
> - Pre-treatment prophylactic swallowing exercises are now standard practice at major centres — the evidence shows they reduce late dysphagia and PEG dependency
> - The "selection for salvage" principle: when CRT fails (persistent or recurrent disease), salvage total laryngectomy remains possible in ~15–20% — this is why OS is equivalent to primary surgery despite higher local failure rates with organ preservation
