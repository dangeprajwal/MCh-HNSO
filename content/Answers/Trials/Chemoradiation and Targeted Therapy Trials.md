## Part A: Phase I Clinical Trials — Significance in Oncology

**Q3 (AMRITA 2017): Explain the significance of Phase I clinical trials in oncology**

### Definition and Purpose

A Phase I trial is the **first-in-human study** of a new anticancer agent. Its primary objective is not to demonstrate efficacy but to:
1. Determine the **maximum tolerated dose (MTD)**
2. Characterise the **dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs)**
3. Establish the **pharmacokinetic (PK) profile** (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion)
4. Define the **recommended Phase II dose (RP2D)**

### Design Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Population** | 15–30 patients; typically advanced/refractory solid tumours where no standard therapy exists |
| **Starting dose** | Based on preclinical toxicology (typically 1/10th of the LD₁₀ in the most sensitive animal species) |
| **Dose escalation** | Traditional 3+3 design (cohorts of 3; expand to 6 if DLT occurs); newer accelerated titration, modified continual reassessment method (mCRM), Bayesian optimal interval (BOIN) designs |
| **Endpoints** | MTD, DLT rate, PK parameters, pharmacodynamics (target engagement biomarkers) |
| **DLT definition** | Grade ≥3 non-haematological toxicity or grade 4 haematological toxicity within the first treatment cycle |
| **MTD definition** | One dose level below the dose at which >33% of patients experience DLT |

### 3+3 Design Algorithm

```
Start at dose level 1 (3 patients)
    │
    ├── 0 DLTs → escalate to next dose level
    ├── 1 DLT → expand cohort to 6 patients
    │       ├── ≤1/6 DLTs → escalate
    │       └── ≥2/6 DLTs → STOP; previous dose = MTD
    └── ≥2 DLTs in initial 3 → STOP; previous dose = MTD
```

### Significance of Phase I Trials

1. **Safety gateway:** Phase I is the essential safety filter before exposing larger populations to a new agent; prevents widespread harm
2. **Dose selection:** Identifies the RP2D — the dose used in all subsequent phase II/III development; an incorrect dose selection at phase I can doom an effective drug to failure
3. **PK characterisation:** Understanding how the drug is absorbed, metabolised, and excreted informs dosing schedules, drug interactions, and special population adjustments (renal/hepatic impairment)
4. **Biomarker development:** Modern phase I trials incorporate pharmacodynamic endpoints (e.g., target inhibition in tumour biopsies, circulating tumour DNA) to establish proof-of-mechanism
5. **Tumour type selection:** "Expanded cohort" phase I trials now enrol disease-specific cohorts to generate early efficacy signals and identify the tumour types most likely to respond
6. **First therapeutic benefit:** Even though phase I is not designed for efficacy, some patients (particularly in targeted therapy trials where patient selection is biomarker-driven) achieve durable responses at phase I
7. **Combinability studies:** In HNC, phase I trials test novel agents combined with existing standards (e.g., novel immunotherapy + standard CRT) to establish safe dosing before phase III

### Evolution: From Traditional to Modern Phase I Design

| Design | Feature | Advantage |
|---|---|---|
| Traditional 3+3 | Rigid rule-based; cohorts of 3 | Simple; widely accepted |
| Continual Reassessment Method (CRM) | Bayesian model-based | More accurate MTD estimation |
| BOIN | Interval-based Bayesian | Easy to implement; good operating characteristics |
| Accelerated titration | Single patient/cohort until DLT; then 3+3 | Fewer patients at subtherapeutic doses |
| Drug-drug combination phase I | 2D dose escalation (dose matrix) | Complex; needed for combinations |

## Part B: Design and Conduct of Clinical Trials

**Q4 (AMRITA 2018): Describe the design and conduct of clinical trials**

### Trial Phases Overview

| Phase | Primary Objective | Population | Key Design Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase I** | Safety, MTD, PK | 15–30 patients (advanced cancer) | Dose escalation; 3+3 or Bayesian |
| **Phase II** | Preliminary efficacy | 30–100 patients | Single-arm or randomised; ORR primary endpoint |
| **Phase III** | Definitive efficacy vs standard | Hundreds to thousands | RCT; OS or PFS primary endpoint |
| **Phase IV** | Post-marketing surveillance | Large real-world population | Long-term safety; rare adverse events |

### Key Design Elements for Phase III Trials

#### 1. Randomisation
- Eliminates selection bias; ensures balance of known/unknown confounders
- Methods: simple, block, stratified (by HPV status, T stage, institution)
- **Centralised randomisation** preferred over local (reduces allocation prediction)

#### 2. Blinding
| Level | Description | HNC applicability |
|---|---|---|
| Open-label | Neither party blinded | Most HNC trials (surgery, RT — cannot blind) |
| Single-blind | Patient blinded | Possible with identical-appearing placebo |
| Double-blind | Both blinded | Gold standard for drug trials; difficult in HNC due to visible toxicity profiles (cetuximab rash) |

#### 3. Endpoints in HNC Trials

| Endpoint | Definition | Used in |
|---|---|---|
| **Overall survival (OS)** | Time to death from any cause | RTOG 91-11, CheckMate-141, KEYNOTE-048 |
| **Progression-free survival (PFS)** | Time to progression or death | Phase II, immunotherapy trials |
| **Locoregional control (LRC)** | Time to locoregional failure | RT trials (PARSPORT, MARCH) |
| **Laryngeal preservation rate** | Proportion retaining larynx | Organ preservation trials |
| **Objective response rate (ORR)** | CR + PR rate | Phase II, R/M setting |
| **Patient-reported outcomes (PRO)** | QOL, xerostomia, dysphagia | PARSPORT (xerostomia), ORATOR (MDADI) |

#### 4. Non-Inferiority Trial Design
- Used when the new treatment is expected to be "not unacceptably worse" than standard
- Requires a **pre-specified non-inferiority margin (δ)** based on clinical judgment and statistical principles
- Larger sample sizes needed than equivalence trials
- Critical in HNC: RTOG 1016 (non-inferiority of cetuximab vs cisplatin — failed); PATHOS (ongoing)

#### 5. Key Statistical Concepts

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Power (1-β)** | Probability of detecting a true difference if it exists; typically 80–90% |
| **Type I error (α)** | Probability of false positive; typically 0.05 (two-sided) |
| **Type II error (β)** | Probability of false negative; typically 0.10–0.20 |
| **Hazard ratio (HR)** | Ratio of event rates between arms in time-to-event analysis; HR<1 favours experimental arm |
| **Intent-to-treat (ITT)** | Primary analysis including all randomised patients regardless of adherence |
| **Per-protocol (PP)** | Analysis of patients who completed treatment as planned; supportive only |
| **Interim analysis** | Pre-planned analysis by DSMB; can recommend early stopping for efficacy, futility, or safety |

## Part C: MACH-NC Meta-analysis

**The MACH-NC (Meta-Analysis of Chemotherapy in Head and Neck Cancer)**

| Publication | Pignon et al., Lancet 2000; updated Blanchard, Radiother Oncol 2011 |
|---|---|
| **Trials included** | 93 randomised trials; 17,346 patients |
| **Comparison** | Adding chemotherapy to locoregional treatment vs locoregional treatment alone |
| **Primary endpoint** | Overall survival |

**Key Results:**

| Chemotherapy Type | 5-yr OS Benefit | HR |
|---|---|---|
| Concurrent chemotherapy | **+6.5% absolute** (most significant) | 0.81 |
| Induction chemotherapy | +2.4% (smaller benefit) | 0.96 (NS in updated) |
| Adjuvant chemotherapy | No significant benefit | — |

**Findings:**
- **Concurrent cisplatin-based CRT** provides the greatest OS benefit (+6.5% at 5 years)
- Benefit is consistent across subsites, stages, and ages ≤70 years
- Induction chemotherapy has a smaller OS benefit, primarily reducing distant metastases
- Adjuvant chemotherapy provides no significant overall survival advantage

**Clinical implication:** Concurrent cisplatin-based CRT is the standard of care for locally advanced HNSCC based on MACH-NC. This applies to laryngeal, pharyngeal, and oral cavity cancers undergoing definitive radiotherapy.

## Part D: PARSPORT Trial

**Q7 (AMRITA 2020): PARSPORT Trial**

### Full Name
**Parotid-Sparing Intensity Modulated versus Conventional Radiotherapy in Head and Neck Cancer** (Nutting et al., Lancet Oncology 2011)

### Trial Design

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Phase III open-label randomised controlled trial |
| **Population** | 94 patients with pharyngeal carcinoma (oropharynx/hypopharynx); stage II–IVB; requiring bilateral neck irradiation |
| **Arm A** | IMRT (intensity-modulated radiotherapy): 65 Gy in 30 fractions (primary tumour) with bilateral parotid sparing |
| **Arm B** | Conventional RT (3D-CRT): 65 Gy in 30 fractions, opposed lateral fields ± anterior neck field |
| **Primary endpoint** | Grade ≥2 xerostomia at 12 months (RTOG/EORTC criteria) |
| **Concurrent chemo** | Allowed if clinically indicated (not a study variable) |
| **Institutions** | UK multicentre (Medical Research Council trial) |

### Key Results

| Endpoint | IMRT | Conventional RT | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grade ≥2 xerostomia at 12 months | **38%** | **74%** | 0.003 |
| Grade ≥2 xerostomia at 24 months | 29% | 83% | <0.001 |
| Unstimulated salivary flow (>25% of baseline) | 49% | 29% | 0.03 |
| Disease control | Comparable | Comparable | NS |
| Overall survival | Comparable | Comparable | NS |

**Mean parotid doses:**
- IMRT: 23.8 Gy (contralateral), 38.5 Gy (ipsilateral)
- Conventional RT: 63.5 Gy (contralateral), 63.3 Gy (ipsilateral)

### Conclusions
1. IMRT **significantly reduces grade ≥2 xerostomia** at 12 and 24 months (38% vs 74%)
2. The benefit persists and increases over time (29% vs 83% at 24 months)
3. No compromise in disease control — IMRT does not sacrifice oncological outcomes
4. **PARSPORT established IMRT as the standard of care** for pharyngeal cancers requiring bilateral neck irradiation

### Clinical Implications
- IMRT is now the global standard for all head and neck cancers where bilateral neck irradiation is required
- Parotid mean dose constraint: contralateral parotid ≤24 Gy (aiming for >25% salivary function preservation — Eisbruch threshold)
- The "quality of life co-primary endpoint" approach of PARSPORT influenced subsequent trial design in HNC

## Part E: PARADIGM Trial

**Q2 (AMRITA 2016): PARADIGM trial in head and neck cancers**

### Full Name
**Phase III Trial of Docetaxel-Based Induction Chemotherapy with or without Cetuximab, Followed by Chemoradiotherapy (PARADIGM)**

Cohen EEW et al., Lancet 2014; doi: 10.1016/S0140-6736(14)61490-X

### Background
Following the success of cetuximab in head and neck cancer (Bonner trial, EXTREME trial), the hypothesis was that adding cetuximab to induction chemotherapy (TPF) and to subsequent CRT could improve outcomes in locally advanced HNSCC.

### Trial Design

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III |
| **Population** | 145 patients with stage III–IVB HNSCC (oropharynx, hypopharynx, larynx, oral cavity) |
| **Arm A (TPF + CRT)** | Docetaxel 75 mg/m² + cisplatin 100 mg/m² + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/d × 4d → concurrent CRT (weekly carboplatin + RT 70–74 Gy) |
| **Arm B (DCC + CETUX-RT)** | Docetaxel 75 mg/m² + cisplatin 75 mg/m² + cetuximab 250 mg/m² weekly (DCC) → cetuximab + RT (70–74 Gy) |
| **Primary endpoint** | 3-year overall survival |

### Key Results

| Endpoint | TPF + CRT | DCC + Cetuximab-RT | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3-year OS | **73%** | **78%** | 0.77 (NS) |
| 3-year PFS | 67% | 67% | NS |
| CR rate to induction | 49% | 52% | NS |
| Grade ≥3 mucositis | 35% | 46% | — |

### Conclusions
- **No significant difference** in OS or PFS between the two arms
- The trial was **underpowered** (accrued only 145 of a planned 330 patients — closed early due to poor accrual)
- **Negative trial:** Adding cetuximab to induction chemotherapy and replacing cisplatin with cetuximab in CRT does not improve outcomes
- Cetuximab-based induction followed by cetuximab-RT is **not superior** to TPF induction followed by cisplatin CRT

**Significance:** PARADIGM, along with DeCIDE, suggested that induction chemotherapy (with or without cetuximab) does not significantly improve OS over direct CRT, and cetuximab is not a valid substitute for cisplatin in the curative setting — conclusions later confirmed by RTOG 1016 and De-ESCALaTE.

## Part F: Bonner Trial — Cetuximab + Radiotherapy

**Bonner JA et al., NEJM 2006**

### Trial Design

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III |
| **Population** | 424 patients, stage III–IVB HNSCC of oropharynx, hypopharynx, or larynx |
| **Arm A** | High-dose RT alone (70 Gy conventional, 72–76.8 Gy hyperfractionation, or 72 Gy accelerated) |
| **Arm B** | Cetuximab (loading 400 mg/m², then 250 mg/m² weekly × 7–8 weeks) + same RT |
| **Primary endpoint** | Locoregional control duration |

### Key Results

| Endpoint | RT alone | Cetuximab + RT |
|---|---|---|
| Median locoregional control | 14.9 months | **24.4 months** (p = 0.005) |
| Median OS | 29.3 months | **49.0 months** (p = 0.03) |
| 5-year OS | 36.4% | **45.6%** |
| Grade ≥3 acneiform rash | 0% | 17% |
| Grade ≥3 infusion reaction | 0% | 3% |
| Grade ≥3 mucositis | 52% | 56% (NS) |

### Conclusions
- Cetuximab + RT significantly improved locoregional control and overall survival vs RT alone
- **First targeted therapy to improve OS** in locally advanced HNSCC
- **No increase in mucositis** with cetuximab (vs cisplatin which markedly increases mucositis)
- The cetuximab acneiform rash correlated with clinical benefit — a pharmacodynamic biomarker

**Limitations and subsequent context:**
- Trial compared cetuximab-RT to RT **alone** (not to cisplatin-CRT) — the relevant clinical question
- Subsequent RTOG 0522 showed adding cetuximab to cisplatin-CRT did NOT improve outcomes
- RTOG 1016 and De-ESCALaTE showed cetuximab-RT is **inferior** to cisplatin-CRT in HPV+ OPSCC
- Current role of cetuximab + RT: limited to patients who are **unfit for cisplatin** (renal impairment, ototoxicity) — even here, weekly carboplatin may be preferred

## Part G: CheckMate-141 and KEYNOTE-048 — Immunotherapy

### CheckMate-141 (Ferris et al., NEJM 2016)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III |
| **Population** | 361 patients with R/M HNSCC, platinum-refractory (progressed within 6 months of platinum) |
| **Arm A** | Nivolumab 3 mg/kg IV q2 weeks |
| **Arm B** | Investigator's choice: methotrexate, docetaxel, or cetuximab |
| **Primary endpoint** | Overall survival |

**Key Results:**
- Median OS: **7.5 months (nivolumab) vs 5.1 months (investigator's choice)** — p = 0.01; HR 0.70
- 1-year OS: 36% vs 17%
- ORR: 13.3% vs 5.8%
- Grade ≥3 AEs: 13.1% vs 35.1% — nivolumab significantly less toxic

**Conclusions:** Nivolumab is the standard of care for platinum-refractory R/M HNSCC; first immunotherapy approval in HNC.

### KEYNOTE-048 (Burtness et al., Lancet 2019)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III, 3-arm |
| **Population** | 882 patients with untreated R/M HNSCC (first-line); all tumour types |
| **Arm A** | Pembrolizumab monotherapy (200 mg q3 weeks) |
| **Arm B** | Pembrolizumab + platinum (cisplatin or carboplatin) + 5-FU |
| **Arm C (control)** | Cetuximab + platinum + 5-FU (EXTREME regimen) |
| **Primary endpoints** | OS in PD-L1 CPS ≥20, CPS ≥1, and all patients |

**Key Results:**

| Comparison | Population | Pembrolizumab ± CT vs EXTREME: OS |
|---|---|---|
| Pembrolizumab monotherapy vs EXTREME | CPS ≥20 | **14.9 vs 10.7 months** (HR 0.61; p < 0.001) |
| Pembrolizumab monotherapy vs EXTREME | CPS ≥1 | **12.3 vs 10.3 months** (HR 0.78; p = 0.0086) |
| Pembrolizumab monotherapy vs EXTREME | All patients | Trend; NS (for CPS <1) |
| Pembrolizumab + CT vs EXTREME | All patients | **13.0 vs 10.7 months** (HR 0.77; p = 0.0034) |

**Conclusions:**
1. Pembrolizumab monotherapy: new standard for PD-L1 CPS ≥1 first-line R/M HNSCC
2. Pembrolizumab + platinum-5FU: new standard for all PD-L1 expressions (all-comers)
3. EXTREME is no longer first-line standard in R/M HNSCC (superseded by KEYNOTE-048)
4. PD-L1 CPS score guides choice: CPS ≥20 → pembrolizumab monotherapy; CPS <1 → pembrolizumab + CT

## Part H: RADPLAT Regime

**Q11 (TMH 2020): RADPLAT regime for management of advanced cancer**

### Principle
RADPLAT (**RADiotherapy with PLATinum** intra-arterially) combines supradose intra-arterial (IA) cisplatin with simultaneous IV sodium thiosulfate (STS) and concurrent conventional RT.

**Pharmacological basis:**
- IA delivery achieves **5–15× higher tumour cisplatin concentration** vs equivalent IV dose
- The **regional advantage (R) = 1 + (Cl_total / Q_regional)**: for cisplatin, R can reach 5–15
- Simultaneously administered IV sodium thiosulfate (STS) neutralises cisplatin in the systemic venous circulation, sharply limiting nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, and neuropathy

### RADPLAT Protocol (Robbins Protocol)

| Component | Dose and Schedule |
|---|---|
| **IA cisplatin** | 150 mg/m² IA (via selective catheterisation of the external carotid artery or its branches feeding the tumour) — 4 weekly cycles |
| **IV sodium thiosulfate** | 9 g/m² IV bolus, then 12 g/m² over 6 hours (simultaneous with cisplatin) |
| **Concurrent RT** | 70 Gy in 35 fractions, conventional fractionation |
| **Pre-treatment** | IV hydration, antiemetics, mannitol |
| **Catheterisation** | Selective catheterisation via femoral or radial approach; angiography confirms tumour-feeding branch |

### Clinical Results (Robbins et al., 2000 — 213 patients)

| Endpoint | Result |
|---|---|
| Complete response rate (primary site) | **88%** |
| 2-year locoregional control | ~72% |
| 5-year OS (estimated) | ~50% |
| Grade ≥3 neurological toxicity | ~15% |
| Osteoradionecrosis | ~8% |
| Cisplatin-related nephrotoxicity | Low (STS neutralisation effective) |
| Ototoxicity | Low (STS neutralisation) |

### Indications
- **Primary indication:** Organ-preservation approach for advanced HNSCC (stage III–IV) — particularly where organ preservation is the primary goal
- Unresectable or borderline resectable HNSCC
- Patients wishing to avoid surgery
- Particularly used for: advanced oral cavity, oropharynx, hypopharynx, and larynx cancers

### Advantages and Disadvantages

| Advantage | Disadvantage |
|---|---|
| Extremely high tumour drug concentrations (supradose) | Technically demanding — requires interventional radiology expertise |
| Reduced systemic toxicity (nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity) with STS | Neurological complications (stroke, cranial nerve palsy) ~15% |
| High complete response rate (88%) | Available only at specialised centres |
| Comparable to IV CRT for locoregional control | Expensive; requires angiography suite |
| Effective organ preservation | No randomised phase III trial comparing RADPLAT to standard IV CRT |
| Allows dose escalation beyond IV cisplatin safe doses | — |

### Limitations of Evidence
- No randomised phase III trial comparing RADPLAT to standard IV cisplatin CRT exists
- Reporting bias: most data from Robbins' group at the University of Tennessee
- Complication rate (neurological) may limit widespread adoption

## Part I: Selumetinib Trial

**Q6 (AMRITA 2020): Selumetinib trial**

### Background
Selumetinib (AZD6244) is a **selective, non-ATP competitive inhibitor of MEK1/2** — components of the RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK (MAPK) signalling pathway, which is frequently activated in HNSCC.

**Rationale in HNSCC:**
- HRAS, KRAS, NRAS mutations occur in ~5–15% of HNSCC
- RAS activation → MEK → ERK → cell proliferation; MEK inhibition blocks this downstream pathway
- Preclinical studies showed selumetinib reduces cell proliferation and induces apoptosis in HNSCC cell lines with activated RAS/MAPK signalling

### Phase I/II Trials of Selumetinib in HNSCC

#### Selumetinib Phase II in HNSCC (Ahn et al., 2012)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Phase II single-arm |
| **Population** | 38 patients with R/M HNSCC, ≥1 prior platinum-based therapy |
| **Dose** | Selumetinib 75 mg orally twice daily (continuous dosing) |
| **Primary endpoint** | Objective response rate (ORR) |

**Key Results:**
- ORR: **2 confirmed PRs (5.3%)**; median duration not reached
- Disease control rate (CR + PR + SD): 42.1%
- Median PFS: 2.0 months
- Median OS: 5.6 months

**Conclusions:**
- Selumetinib as a **single agent** has minimal activity in unselected R/M HNSCC
- The low ORR indicates that MEK inhibition alone is insufficient — likely due to feedback activation of alternative signalling pathways (PI3K/AKT)
- **Rationale for combinations:** selumetinib + PI3K inhibitors, selumetinib + EGFR inhibitors, or biomarker-selected populations (RAS-mutant HNSCC) may be more promising

### Selumetinib in Thyroid Cancer
In contrast to HNSCC, selumetinib has shown clinically meaningful activity in **BRAF/RAS-mutant radioiodine-refractory differentiated thyroid cancer** — the SELECT trial demonstrated its ability to re-differentiate thyroid cancer cells and increase radioiodine uptake. However, this is a separate application from HNSCC.

### Clinical Significance of the Selumetinib Trial in HNSCC
- Demonstrated the **challenge of targeting RAS/MAPK in HNSCC** — the pathway is not dominant in most tumours
- Highlighted the importance of **biomarker-selected patient populations** for targeted therapy trials
- Informed subsequent trial designs incorporating RAS mutation status as a selection criterion
- Established the expected activity level (~5%) that selumetinib combinations need to surpass to demonstrate meaningful benefit

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. MACH-NC: Concurrent cisplatin-based CRT provides +6.5% absolute OS benefit at 5 years vs RT alone — the most effective chemotherapy schedule; induction and adjuvant chemo provide less benefit
> 2. Bonner trial (2006): Cetuximab + RT vs RT alone — first targeted therapy to improve OS in LA-HNSCC; median OS 49 vs 29 months; no increase in mucositis
> 3. PARSPORT (2011): IMRT vs conventional RT — Grade ≥2 xerostomia 38% vs 74% at 12 months; disease control comparable; IMRT established as standard of care
> 4. PARADIGM trial (2014): Adding cetuximab to induction TPF and replacing cisplatin with cetuximab in CRT showed no OS benefit; trial closed early due to poor accrual — negative result
> 5. RADPLAT: IA cisplatin 150 mg/m² × 4 weekly + IV sodium thiosulfate + RT 70 Gy; 88% complete response rate; reduced systemic toxicity; no randomised comparison to IV CRT
> 6. CheckMate-141: Nivolumab improved OS (7.5 vs 5.1 months) in platinum-refractory R/M HNSCC — first immunotherapy approval in HNC; lower toxicity than standard chemotherapy
> 7. KEYNOTE-048: Pembrolizumab ± chemotherapy vs EXTREME; pembrolizumab monotherapy is new standard for CPS ≥1 first-line R/M HNSCC; EXTREME is no longer standard first-line
> 8. Phase I trials: primary goal is MTD and DLT characterisation, not efficacy; 3+3 dose escalation design; RP2D established for phase II; modern trials incorporate PK and pharmacodynamic endpoints
> 9. Selumetinib (MEK1/2 inhibitor): single-agent ORR of only 5.3% in unselected R/M HNSCC — minimal activity; biomarker-selected populations (RAS-mutant) may benefit; active in BRAF-mutant thyroid cancer
> 10. Non-inferiority trial design requires a pre-specified non-inferiority margin (δ) and is used when the new treatment offers advantages (less toxicity, better QOL) but may have similar efficacy; sample sizes are larger than superiority trials

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - PARSPORT's primary endpoint was **xerostomia** (a patient-reported outcome) rather than OS — this was innovative at the time and reflects HNC's focus on functional outcomes in addition to survival
> - The PARADIGM trial is often misquoted — it tested a cetuximab-based induction then cetuximab-RT vs TPF induction then cisplatin-CRT; it was NEGATIVE and underpowered
> - Distinguish the Bonner trial (cetuximab + RT vs RT **alone** — positive) from RTOG 0522 (cetuximab added to cisplatin-CRT — negative) and RTOG 1016 (cetuximab-RT vs cisplatin-CRT — negative; cetuximab inferior)
> - RADPLAT requires selective catheterisation of the external carotid artery system — the main risks are neurological (stroke, cranial nerve palsy) from inadvertent catheterisation of internal carotid branches
> - KEYNOTE-048 uses PD-L1 CPS (combined positive score) not TPS for patient selection; CPS ≥20 = pembrolizumab mono; CPS ≥1 = pembrolizumab mono (non-inferior); all-comers = pembrolizumab + chemo
> - Selumetinib has a role in thyroid cancer (radioiodine re-sensitisation) but minimal role in HNSCC as monotherapy — distinguish these two applications carefully
