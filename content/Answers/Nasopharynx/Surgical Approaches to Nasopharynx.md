## Anatomy of the Nasopharynx

### Boundaries
| Wall | Landmark |
|---|---|
| Superior | Body of sphenoid, basiocciput (clivus) |
| Posterior | Anterior arch of C1–C2; prevertebral muscles (longus capitis) |
| Inferior | Soft palate (imaginary horizontal plane) |
| Anterior | Posterior choanae (junction with nasal cavity) |
| Lateral | Fossa of Rosenmüller (pharyngeal recess); Eustachian tube orifice; Levator veli palatini muscle |

### Key Anatomical Relationships
- **Fossa of Rosenmüller** (pharyngeal recess): the most common site of NPC origin; posterolateral recess adjacent to Eustachian tube
- **Rouvière's node**: first-echelon retropharyngeal lymph node; lies between C1 transverse process and jugular vein; site of earliest nodal spread in NPC
- **Internal carotid artery (ICA)**: ascends immediately lateral to nasopharynx, enters carotid canal at foramen lacerum — critical surgical landmark
- **Foramen lacerum**: fibrocartilage-filled; the ICA crosses it superiorly; violated in T4 NPC
- **Foramen ovale, rotundum, spinosum**: posterolateral skull base foramina lateral to nasopharynx

## Surgical Indications

| Indication | Preferred Approach |
|---|---|
| Diagnostic biopsy of NPC | Transnasal endoscopic |
| Recurrent/residual NPC after RT | Endoscopic nasopharyngectomy |
| Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma (Stage I–II) | Transnasal endoscopic ± Denker |
| JNA Stage III | Endoscopic + midfacial degloving / lateral rhinotomy |
| JNA Stage IV / ICA involvement | Infratemporal fossa (Fisch Type C/D) |
| Benign tumours (Tornwaldt cyst, chordoma — small) | Transnasal endoscopic |
| Extensive midline skull base lesions | Transmaxillary (Le Fort I) |
| Lateral skull base extension | Infratemporal fossa approach |

## Surgical Approaches: Detailed Description

### 1. Transnasal Endoscopic Approach

**Indications:** Diagnostic biopsies; JNA Stages I–II; Tornwaldt cysts; small recurrent NPC (rT1)

**Setup:**
- General anaesthesia, orotracheal intubation
- Topical vasoconstrictors (oxymetazoline) + submucosal adrenaline injection
- 0° and 30° rigid endoscopes (4 mm or 2.7 mm)
- Image guidance (neuronavigation) recommended for skull base work

**Technique:**
1. Bilateral transnasal access; wide sphenoidotomies if posterior nasopharynx or skull base exposure needed
2. Middle turbinate lateralisation or partial resection for access
3. For biopsy of NPC: visualise fossa of Rosenmüller; biopsy forceps or cup forceps; multiple samples (6 minimum); send for histology + EBER-ISH
4. For nasopharyngectomy (recurrent NPC): see dedicated section below

**Advantages:** Minimal morbidity, no external scar, direct visualisation, no palate division, day-case possible for biopsy

**Limitations:** Limited lateral access; ICA at risk if parapharyngeal extension; requires experienced endoscopic surgeon

---

### 2. Transpalatal Approach (Trotter's Operation / Soft Palate Splitting)

**Indications:** Biopsy or limited resection of nasopharyngeal tumours; limited access to posterolateral wall; historical use for NPC biopsy (now superseded by endoscopic)

**Technique:**
1. Orotracheal intubation + Boyle-Davis mouth gag
2. Midline incision through soft palate from uvula to hard palate junction
3. Lateral relaxing incisions if needed (creates bipedicled flap)
4. Hard palate may be fractured or resected (Trotter's modification) for wider exposure
5. Direct visualisation of nasopharynx through palatal window
6. Closure: meticulous layered repair of soft palate (oral, muscular, nasal layers); if hard palate resected, obturation prosthesis or pedicled flap

**Advantages:** Wide midline exposure; no external scar; can visualise both lateral walls

**Complications:**
- Velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI) — most significant (10–20%)
- Palatal fistula — if closure breaks down
- Eustachian tube injury — otitis media with effusion
- Palatal necrosis (rare)
- Trismus — if pterygoid plates involved

**Current role:** Largely superseded by endoscopic approaches for biopsy; may still be used in combination for complex resections

---

### 3. Infratemporal Fossa Approach (Fisch Classification)

**Fisch Type A (Transotic):** Petrous bone; not primarily nasopharyngeal

**Fisch Type B:** Middle cranial fossa, clivus, anterior skull base; nasopharynx accessible

**Fisch Type C:** Infratemporal fossa, pterygopalatine fossa, nasopharynx, ICA management

**Fisch Type D:** Anterior infratemporal fossa; anterior cranial fossa

#### Type C Approach (Most Relevant to Nasopharynx):

**Indication:** JNA Stage IIIB–IVA; recurrent NPC with lateral parapharyngeal extension; pterygopalatine fossa tumours

**Technique:**
1. Pre-auricular incision extending into temporal hairline
2. Temporalis muscle elevation
3. **Mandibular condylectomy or anterior dislocation** of TMJ — key step to expose infratemporal fossa
4. Division of internal maxillary artery and middle meningeal artery
5. Exposure of ICA (horizontal petrous segment) — mandatory for control
6. Resection of pterygoid plates and access to nasopharynx/PPF
7. Closure: temporalis muscle flap to obliterate dead space; reconstruction of TM joint (cartilage spacer or prosthesis)

**Complications:** Facial nerve injury, mandibular condyle morbidity (trismus, malocclusion), ICA injury, CSF leak, conductive hearing loss, temporal lobe retraction injury

---

### 4. Transmaxillary Approach (Le Fort I Osteotomy)

**Indication:** Large midline nasopharyngeal tumours requiring bilateral simultaneous exposure; selected skull base lesions (craniopharyngiomas, clival chordomas)

**Technique:**
1. Bicoronal or gingival incision
2. Le Fort I osteotomy — horizontal cut through bilateral maxillary walls, nasal septum, pterygoid plates
3. Maxilla mobilised inferiorly ("down-fracture") on bilateral palatine vessels
4. Excellent bilateral exposure of nasopharynx, clivus, upper cervical spine
5. Closure: rigid fixation (mini-plates) to restore maxillary position; dental occlusion must be re-established

**Advantages:** Bilateral wide exposure; no palate splitting; preserves palatal function
**Complications:** Tooth devitalisation, VPI (rare if palate intact), malocclusion, avascular necrosis of maxilla, oronasal fistula

---

### 5. Endoscopic Nasopharyngectomy (Ng's Approach)

**Indications:**
- Recurrent NPC (rT1 or rT2) after definitive RT
- No ICA encasement (relative contraindication; inoperable if bilateral)
- Performance status ECOG 0–1
- EBV DNA guided (detectable but not markedly elevated = recurrent, not distant spread)
- MRI skull base: defines extent, parapharyngeal involvement, ICA proximity

**Pre-operative Assessment:**
- MRI with gadolinium (skull base protocol)
- PET-CT (exclude distant metastases)
- Plasma EBV DNA (confirm locoregional pattern)
- Angiography ± balloon occlusion test (if ICA proximity)
- Dental assessment (post-RT patients at risk of osteoradionecrosis)

**Surgical Technique (Ng's Modified Endoscopic Nasopharyngectomy):**
1. Nasal decongestion; neuronavigation setup
2. **Wide bilateral middle meatal antrostomies** — posterior ethmoidectomies and wide sphenoidotomies
3. Choanal resection (posterior nasal septum) to create bilateral working corridor
4. Mucosal incision at superior nasopharynx (vault) with adequate margins (1 cm)
5. Dissection of **posterior nasopharynx wall** off prevertebral fascia
6. Lateral wall dissection: Eustachian tube resected on involved side; torus tubarius removed
7. **Parapharyngeal dissection** (Ng's key step): dissection between nasopharynx and parapharyngeal fat
8. **ICA identification and isolation** — mandatory; ICA courses immediately lateral
9. En bloc or piecemeal resection with mucosal and submucosal margin (tumour + periosteum)
10. Haemostasis; packing; healing by secondary intention or fat graft

**Oncological Outcomes:**
- rT1: 5-yr OS ~70–73%; 5-yr disease-specific survival ~80%
- rT2: 5-yr OS ~50–55%
- rT3 or ICA encasement: high morbidity, poor outcomes — not recommended

**Complications:**
- ICA injury (most feared, potentially fatal — 1–2%)
- Palate necrosis (post-RT vascularity impaired)
- Osteoradionecrosis of skull base (clivus)
- CSF leak
- Eustachian tube dysfunction → severe otitis media with effusion

---

### 6. Combined Approaches

**Transnasal + transoral:** Bimanual endoscopic technique for midline tumours
**Transpterygoid (endoscopic):** Middle cranial fossa access via pterygoid process; used for lateral recess recurrences
**Transorbital endoscopic:** Emerging; allows orbital roof approach to superior nasopharynx and cavernous sinus

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The transnasal endoscopic approach is the workhorse for NPC biopsy and most benign nasopharyngeal tumours. The transpalatal (Trotter's) approach, while now largely historic for NPC, is still examinable — key steps are midline soft palate division, palate repair in layers, and risk of VPI. The infratemporal fossa (Fisch Type C) approach is used for advanced JNA and lateral skull base tumours requiring ICA control. For recurrent NPC after RT, **endoscopic nasopharyngectomy (Ng's approach)** offers 5-yr OS of ~70% for rT1 and ~50% for rT2; ICA identification and parapharyngeal dissection are the critical technical steps. Pre-operative MRI, PET-CT, and plasma EBV DNA are essential before any salvage surgery to exclude distant metastases and define surgical boundaries.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The fossa of Rosenmüller (pharyngeal recess) is the most common site of NPC origin — ensure biopsies specifically target this area.
> 2. Rouvière's node (first-echelon retropharyngeal node) is the earliest site of nodal spread in NPC; not palpable clinically but detectable on MRI.
> 3. The ICA ascends immediately lateral to the nasopharynx — ICA injury during endoscopic nasopharyngectomy (~1–2%) is the most feared complication; pre-operative angiography is mandatory if proximity <7 mm.
> 4. The most significant complication of the transpalatal (Trotter's) approach is velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI) — incidence 10–20%; prevented by careful layered closure.
> 5. Fisch Type C infratemporal fossa approach requires mandibular condylectomy or anterior TMJ dislocation to expose the infratemporal fossa — trismus and malocclusion are expected post-operative morbidities.
> 6. Endoscopic nasopharyngectomy is contraindicated if there is ICA encasement bilaterally or if the patient is ECOG >2 (post-RT patients are fragile).
> 7. Le Fort I (transmaxillary) approach provides the widest bilateral exposure without palate splitting — preferred for large midline clivus/nasopharynx lesions.
> 8. Post-RT nasopharynx has impaired vascularity and healing — flap coverage or fat grafting reduces healing complications in salvage nasopharyngectomy.
> 9. For JNA, the surgical approach is determined by Andrews staging: endoscopic for Stage I–II; lateral rhinotomy or infratemporal fossa for Stage III–IV.
> 10. Neuronavigation (image-guided surgery) is strongly recommended for all endoscopic skull base procedures on the nasopharynx to avoid ICA and skull base injury.
