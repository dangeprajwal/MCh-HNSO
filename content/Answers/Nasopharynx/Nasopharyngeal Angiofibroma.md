## Overview and Epidemiology

- **Full name:** Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma (JNA)
- **Nature:** Benign but locally aggressive fibrovascular tumour; NOT malignant but causes significant morbidity via local destruction
- **Demographics:**
  - Almost exclusively **adolescent males** (peak age 13–17 years)
  - Rare in females (if diagnosed in female, consider androgen receptor analysis)
  - Accounts for ~0.5% of all head and neck tumours
  - Incidence: ~1 in 150,000 males

---

## Aetiology and Pathology

### Origin
- Most widely accepted: **posterolateral nasal wall at the sphenopalatine foramen / junction of sphenopalatine foramen and pterygoid process at the roof of the pterygopalatine fossa**
- Alternative proposed origins: periosteum of basisphenoid, Eustachian tube fibrocartilage, embryological vascular hamartoma

### Pathology
- **Gross:** Firm, rubbery, purple-red to tan mass; no true capsule; pseudocapsule of compressed surrounding tissue
- **Histology:** Two components:
  1. **Fibrous stroma:** Dense fibrous tissue, stellate/spindled fibroblasts, collagen bundles; lacking vascular smooth muscle → inability to contract → profuse bleeding when cut
  2. **Vascular component:** Irregular, thin-walled, endothelium-lined vascular channels of varying calibre, lacking smooth muscle in walls
- **Androgen receptors:** Strongly expressed in stromal fibroblasts → testosterone-driven growth (explains adolescent male predilection)
- **PDGFR-β, VEGF:** Overexpressed → angiogenesis; rationale for targeted therapy in recurrent/unresectable JNA

### Hormonal and Molecular Biology
- Androgen receptors: JNA responds to testosterone → growth during puberty, occasional regression post-puberty
- Spontaneous regression reported in adults (~25% partial regression after puberty)
- This forms the basis for historical use of pre-operative anti-androgen therapy (flutamide) — now abandoned as it didn't reduce intraoperative bleeding effectively

---

## Blood Supply

### Primary
- **Internal maxillary artery** (branch of ECA) via **sphenopalatine artery** = dominant supply in Stage I–II

### Secondary (Advanced Stages)
- Ascending pharyngeal artery (ECA branch)
- Facial artery (ECA)
- Descending palatine artery
- **ICA branches** (accessory meningeal, vidian artery branches): develop in advanced intracranial extension (Stage IVA–B)
- Bilateral ICA supply makes pre-operative embolisation incomplete and increases surgical risk

---

## Clinical Features

### Hallmark Triad
1. **Adolescent male**
2. **Progressive unilateral nasal obstruction** (months to years)
3. **Spontaneous and/or recurrent epistaxis** (unilateral; can be massive)

### Additional Features by Stage
- **Cheek swelling:** Pterygopalatine fossa / infratemporal fossa extension
- **Proptosis:** Orbital extension
- **Palatal bulge:** Inferior pterygoid plate erosion
- **Otitis media with effusion:** Eustachian tube compression
- **Anosmia:** Nasal cavity obstruction
- **Headache, diplopia, visual loss:** Intracranial extension (advanced)
- **Trismus:** Infratemporal fossa / pterygoid muscle invasion

> **BIOPSY IS CONTRAINDICATED** in the clinic — highly vascular; risk of catastrophic haemorrhage. Diagnosis is radiological (CT + MRI); surgical biopsy intraoperatively if diagnosis uncertain.

---

## Radiology

### CT Findings
- **Intensely enhancing mass** at pterygopalatine fossa on contrast CT
- Widening of sphenopalatine foramen (pathognomonic on axial CT)
- **Holman-Miller sign (Antral sign):** Anterior bowing / displacement of posterior antral wall (posterior wall of maxillary sinus) — classic radiological sign
- Bone remodelling (expansion) rather than bone destruction (distinguishes JNA from malignancy)
- Pterygoid plate erosion in Stage II+

### MRI Findings
- **T1:** Isointense to muscle
- **T2:** Heterogeneous; interspersed flow voids (rapidly flowing vessels) — "**salt and pepper**" pattern (pepper = flow voids; salt = fibrous stroma)
- **Post-contrast:** Intense, heterogeneous enhancement
- MRI is superior for defining intracranial extension, cavernous sinus, and ICA proximity

### Angiography
- Not for diagnosis; performed **24–72 hours before surgery** in conjunction with embolisation
- Defines arterial feeders, enables superselective embolisation
- Confirms ICA involvement in advanced stages
- Essential before surgery for all stages

---

## Staging: Andrews Classification (1989 Modified)

| Stage | Extent |
|---|---|
| **Stage I** | Limited to nasopharynx / posterior nasal cavity; bone destruction negligible / limited to sphenopalatine foramen |
| **Stage II** | Extension into pterygopalatine fossa or maxillary, ethmoid, or sphenoid sinus with bone destruction |
| **Stage IIIA** | Extension into orbit or infratemporal fossa WITHOUT intracranial involvement |
| **Stage IIIB** | Extension into orbit or infratemporal fossa WITH intracranial extradural (parasellar) involvement |
| **Stage IVA** | Extensive intracranial extradural involvement; lateral to cavernous sinus |
| **Stage IVB** | Cavernous sinus, optic chiasm, or pituitary fossa involvement (ICA encasement) |

> Other staging systems used: Fisch & Mattox; Radkowski et al. Andrews is most widely used in exams and practice.

## Pre-operative Management

### Pre-operative Embolisation

**Purpose:** Reduce intraoperative blood loss (from ~1.5 L to 250–350 mL)

**Timing:** **24–72 hours before surgery** (not >72 hours — risk of reperfusion via collaterals)

**Technique:**
- Superselective catheterisation of feeding vessels via femoral artery approach
- Embolic agents: **PVA (polyvinyl alcohol) particles** (most common), gelfoam, Onyx (liquid embolic for ICA-fed portions)
- ECA branches embolised preferentially; ICA branches cannot be safely embolised (risk of stroke)
- If ICA supply confirmed → consider balloon occlusion test; surgery requires ICA-proximal control

**Complications of embolisation:** Contrast nephropathy, post-embolisation fever/pain syndrome, retrograde migration of embolic material (rare), cranial nerve palsy (facial nerve branch of ECA embolised)

## Surgical Approaches by Stage

### Stage I–II: Endoscopic Resection (Preferred)
- **Standard of care** for Stages I–IIIa in experienced centres
- **Technique:**
  1. Bilateral nasal endoscopy; 0° and 30° scopes
  2. Middle meatal antrostomy; wide sphenoidotomy
  3. **Identification of sphenopalatine foramen** — the origin; cauterisation / ligation of sphenopalatine artery early in dissection (reduces bleeding)
  4. **Medial maxillectomy** (if needed): removes posterior antral wall for pterygopalatine fossa access
  5. Tumour dissection from pterygoid plates, posterior nasopharynx
  6. **Complete mucoperiosteal removal** from sphenoid floor — prevents recurrence from residual mucosal component
  7. Haemostasis; packing; healing by secondary intention
- **Outcomes:** Recurrence 10–15% (vs 10–20% open); significantly lower morbidity (no facial scar, no palatal fistula)

### Stage II with Maxillary Extension: Denker Approach (Endoscopic Denker)
- Resection of anterior maxillary wall (canine fossa) to widen pterygopalatine fossa access endoscopically
- Maintains endoscopic approach while accessing wider infratemporal area

### Stage IIIA: Endoscopic ± Lateral Rhinotomy / Midfacial Degloving
- Midfacial degloving: sublabial incision, subperiosteal dissection of midface; no facial scar; excellent exposure of anterior and lateral nasopharynx
- Lateral rhinotomy (Weber-Fergusson approach): hemifacial incision; widely used historically; good infratemporal fossa access

### Stage IIIB–IVA: Infratemporal Fossa Approach (Fisch Type C)
- Required when significant intracranial extension or extensive infratemporal fossa involvement
- Mandibular condylectomy/anterior dislocation; ICA proximal control
- Combined neurosurgical + ENT/skull base team
- See [[Answers/Nasopharynx/Surgical Approaches to Nasopharynx|Surgical Approaches to Nasopharynx]]

### Stage IVB (ICA Encasement): Radiotherapy
- Surgery is extremely high risk when ICA is encased bilaterally or cavernous sinus invaded
- **External beam RT (30–36 Gy in 18–20 fractions):** achieves tumour control in 80–90% of Stage IV JNA
- Risk: radiation-induced sarcoma (RIS) — especially in young patients; latency 5–10 years; osteosarcoma, fibrosarcoma most common
- **RT reserved for IVB or unresectable recurrences** — avoided in younger adolescents if at all possible

---

## Recurrence

### Incidence and Causes
- **Recurrence rate:** 10–25% overall; lower with endoscopic resection in experienced hands
- **Common causes:** Incomplete removal at pterygopalatine fossa / sphenoid sinus; residual mucosal foci; inadequate margins around sphenopalatine foramen

### Surveillance
- CT/MRI at **3 months post-resection** (baseline); then every 6–12 months × 2 years
- Enhancement on CT = residual/recurrent tumour; biopsy if uncertain

### Management of Recurrence
- **Endoscopic re-operation** (for resectable recurrences): preferred; similar success rates as primary surgery
- **Open re-operation** (infratemporal fossa, transmaxillary): for lateral or intracranial recurrences
- **Radiotherapy (30–36 Gy):** for unresectable recurrences or repeated recurrences in older patients
- **Anti-angiogenic therapy (bevacizumab):** experimental; case reports of response in multiply-recurrent IVB JNA

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> JNA is a benign but locally aggressive fibrovascular tumour of the sphenopalatine foramen, occurring almost exclusively in adolescent males (androgen receptor-positive stroma). The hallmark clinical triad is adolescent male + unilateral nasal obstruction + recurrent epistaxis. The pathognomonic CT sign is the **Holman-Miller sign** (anterior bowing of posterior antral wall); MRI shows "salt and pepper" pattern (fibrous stroma + flow voids). Staging by Andrews classification (I–IV); **pre-operative embolisation 24–72 hours before surgery** reduces blood loss from ~1.5 L to 250–350 mL. **Endoscopic resection** is standard for Stage I–IIIa; open infratemporal fossa approach for Stage IVA; **RT alone for Stage IVB** (ICA encasement) with risk of radiation-induced sarcoma. Recurrence rate is 10–25%; early detection by 3-month post-operative CT/MRI.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. JNA is the most common benign nasopharyngeal tumour — and the most common cause of massive epistaxis in adolescent males; always suspect it before considering biopsy.
> 2. **BIOPSY IS CONTRAINDICATED in clinic** — catastrophic haemorrhage risk; diagnosis is based on CT + MRI; biopsy only intraoperatively after embolisation and surgical control.
> 3. The **Holman-Miller sign** (anterior bowing of posterior antral wall on CT) is pathognomonic of JNA in the pterygopalatine fossa.
> 4. **"Salt and pepper" pattern on MRI** = fibrous stroma (salt/white on T1) interspersed with flow voids from large vessels (pepper/dark on T1/T2) — this describes the heterogeneous T2 appearance of JNA.
> 5. Pre-operative embolisation must be performed **24–72 hours before surgery** — not earlier (collateral reperfusion) and not the same day (insufficient time for ischaemia).
> 6. The sphenopalatine artery (branch of internal maxillary artery) is the primary feeder — identified at the sphenopalatine foramen; coagulated/clipped early in endoscopic dissection.
> 7. Andrews Stage IVB (ICA encasement) is treated with radiotherapy, not surgery — the risk of ICA rupture makes surgical resection prohibitive; but RT carries radiation-induced sarcoma risk in young patients.
> 8. **Androgen receptors** are expressed in JNA stromal fibroblasts — explains male predominance and occasional spontaneous regression after puberty; historically anti-androgens were tried but abandoned.
> 9. Recurrence (10–25%) most commonly arises from residual tumour at the sphenoid floor or pterygopalatine fossa — meticulous removal of mucoperiosteum at the sphenopalatine foramen is essential.
> 10. PDGFR-β and VEGF are overexpressed in JNA — providing rationale for anti-angiogenic therapy (bevacizumab) in refractory/multiply-recurrent Stage IV disease.
