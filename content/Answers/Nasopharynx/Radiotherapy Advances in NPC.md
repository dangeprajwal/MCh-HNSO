## Historical Evolution of Radiotherapy in NPC

### Timeline of Technological Advances

| Era | Technique | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| 1960s–1980s | 2D conventional RT (parallel-opposed fields) | Rectangular fields, high dose to salivary glands, spinal cord |
| 1990s | 3D conformal RT (3D-CRT) | Improved conformality but still limited sparing ability |
| 2000s | Intensity-modulated RT (IMRT) | Steep dose gradients; reduced xerostomia, ototoxicity |
| 2010s | VMAT (Volumetric Modulated Arc Therapy) | Faster delivery; equivalent dosimetric outcomes to step-and-shoot IMRT |
| 2010s | IGRT (Image-guided RT) | Daily CBCT-based corrections; reduces geometric uncertainty |
| 2020s | Proton beam therapy | Superior dose distribution, especially for T3–T4 and re-irradiation |
| Future | MR-guided RT (MRgRT) | Real-time soft tissue visualisation; adaptive replanning per fraction |

## IMRT in Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma

### Why NPC Uniquely Benefits from IMRT

The nasopharynx is surrounded by critical structures that are dose-limiting:
- Parotid glands (lateral) — xerostomia
- Brainstem and spinal cord (posterior)
- Cochleae — sensorineural hearing loss
- Optic nerves and chiasm (anterior/superior)
- Temporal lobes — radiation necrosis (late effect)
- Pituitary/hypothalamus — endocrine dysfunction

IMRT's ability to create **concave high-dose volumes** and **steep dose gradients** makes it ideally suited for NPC, where the target wraps around the brainstem and spinal cord.

### Dose Prescription: Simultaneous Integrated Boost (SIB) Protocol

| Volume | Definition | Dose (SIB) |
|---|---|---|
| GTV-P (Gross Tumour Volume — Primary) | Macroscopic primary tumour on MRI/PET | **70 Gy / 33 fractions** (2.12 Gy/fr) |
| CTV-1 (High-risk CTV) | GTV-P + 5 mm margin + involved nodes | **63–66 Gy / 33 fractions** |
| CTV-2 (Elective CTV) | High-risk uninvolved nodal regions | **54–56 Gy / 33 fractions** |

> SIB delivers all three dose levels simultaneously in each daily fraction — more radiobiologically efficient than sequential boost and reduces overall treatment time.

### Dosimetric Advantages Over 2D RT

| Outcome | 2D Conventional RT | IMRT |
|---|---|---|
| Xerostomia (Grade 2+) | 70–80% | 30–40% |
| Locoregional control (5-yr) | 60–70% | 80–90% |
| Trismus | 20–30% | 10–15% |
| Temporal lobe necrosis | 5–10% | 2–3% |
| Sensorineural hearing loss | 30–50% | 15–25% |

Key study: **Lee et al. (2002, IJROBP)** demonstrated significant reduction in xerostomia with IMRT in NPC; parotid mean dose <26 Gy preserves function in >50% of cases.

### QUANTEC Dose Constraints for NPC

| Organ at Risk | Constraint | Toxicity Endpoint |
|---|---|---|
| Brainstem | Max dose ≤54 Gy (D0.03cc ≤60 Gy) | Brainstem necrosis |
| Spinal cord | Max dose ≤45 Gy (D0.03cc ≤50 Gy) | Radiation myelopathy |
| Optic nerve / chiasm | Max ≤54 Gy | Radiation optic neuropathy |
| Cochlea (each) | Mean ≤45 Gy | Sensorineural hearing loss |
| Parotid gland (each) | Mean ≤26 Gy (or contralateral mean ≤20 Gy) | Grade 2 xerostomia |
| Temporal lobe | D1cc ≤65 Gy; max ≤72 Gy | Temporal lobe necrosis |
| Mandible | Max ≤70 Gy | Osteoradionecrosis |

## Chemotherapy Integration: Evidence Review

### Concurrent Chemotherapy (Established Standard)

**INT 0099 (Al-Sarraf 1998) — the pivotal trial:**
- Cisplatin 100 mg/m² (days 1, 22, 43) concurrent with RT, then adjuvant PF × 3 cycles
- 3-yr OS: 78% (CRT arm) vs 47% (RT alone) — HR 0.48 (p<0.001)
- Limitation: predominantly Caucasian patients (61%), many keratinizing NPC; results may overestimate benefit in endemic Asian NPC
- Despite limitations, established concurrent CRT as global standard

**Preferred regimen:**
- Cisplatin **100 mg/m² every 3 weeks × 3 cycles** (standard): high complete response rate
- Cisplatin **40 mg/m² weekly × 6–7 cycles** (alternative): better tolerance, comparable efficacy in meta-analyses

### Adjuvant Chemotherapy Post-CRT: Evidence Against

**NPC-0501 (Chen et al. 2012):**
- Adjuvant CF (cisplatin + fluorouracil) × 3 cycles after CRT vs observation
- No improvement in failure-free survival or OS
- High rates of non-compliance (40–50% of patients did not complete all 3 cycles)
- **Conclusion:** Adjuvant chemotherapy after CRT is NOT recommended as standard (except in specific protocols)

**Rationale for failure of adjuvant approach:**
- Cumulative toxicity after CRT limits drug delivery
- Timing of occult micrometastasis may be too advanced by treatment end
- Induction — given upfront — reaches micrometastases when tumour burden is lowest

### Induction Chemotherapy: Evidence For

**Sun et al. 2016 (NEJM) — TPF induction:**
- Stage III–IVB NPC; induction TPF × 3 → CRT vs CRT alone
- 3-yr FFS: 80.0% vs 72.0% (HR 0.68; p=0.034)
- Grade 3–4 toxicity: higher in induction arm but manageable
- Validated in Chinese endemic (non-keratinizing) NPC

**Zhang et al. 2019 (NEJM) — GP induction (preferred):**
- Stage III–IVB NPC; induction GP × 3 → CRT vs CRT alone
- 3-yr FFS: 85.3% vs 76.5% (HR 0.51; p<0.001)
- Grade 3–4 leukopenia: 47% vs 28% — manageable; less mucositis than TPF
- GP was better tolerated than TPF (no alopecia, less GI toxicity)
- **Current preferred induction regimen for locoregionally advanced NPC**

### Current Treatment Algorithm (2023 Consensus)

```
Stage I (T1N0):          RT alone (70 Gy, IMRT)
Stage II (T1N1/T2):      Concurrent CRT (cisplatin weekly)
Stage III–IVA:           Induction GP × 3 → Concurrent CRT
                          (or TPF × 3 → CRT per Sun 2016)
Stage IVB (M1):          Systemic therapy (GP ± pembrolizumab)
                          + locoregional RT (if oligometastatic)
```

## Advanced Radiotherapy Techniques

### Proton Beam Therapy
- Physical advantage: **Bragg peak** — deposits dose at defined depth, minimal exit dose
- Advantages for NPC: superior sparing of brainstem, spinal cord, cochlea, temporal lobes, parotid glands vs photon IMRT
- Clinical scenarios:
  - **T3–T4 disease** with complex proximity to brainstem/ICA
  - **Paediatric NPC** — reducing long-term neurocognitive and endocrine toxicity
  - **Re-irradiation** — cumulative dose to critical structures is lowest with protons
- Current status: no randomised data showing OS superiority over IMRT; cost and access remain barriers
- See also: [[Answers/Radiotherapy and Chemotherapy/IMRT and IGRT in Head and Neck Cancer|IMRT and IGRT]]

### Adaptive Radiotherapy
- **Problem:** NPC tumours and parotid glands change in size/position during the 6–7 week RT course (tumour shrinks, parotid migrates medially)
- **Solution:** Adaptive replanning — repeat CT/MRI mid-treatment (typically at week 3–4) to re-contour and replan
- **Benefit:** Maintains target coverage while reducing doses to shrinking parotids and shifting cord
- **CBCT-based daily IGRT:** Cone-beam CT before each fraction detects set-up errors (translational and rotational); corrects for isocenter shifts
- Standard of care in high-volume centres; reduces geometric miss

### SBRT/SRS for Recurrence
- **Stereotactic body RT (SBRT)** for isolated small-volume local or nodal recurrences after prior IMRT
- Dose: 8–10 Gy × 5 fractions (biologically equivalent dose comparable to 60–66 Gy conventional)
- Key benefit: concentrates re-irradiation dose while limiting cumulative dose to brainstem/cord
- See [[Answers/Nasopharynx/Recurrent NPC Management|Recurrent NPC Management]] for full salvage algorithm

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> IMRT is the gold standard for NPC radiotherapy, delivering SIB with three-dose-level prescriptions (70/63/56 Gy in 33 fractions) that simultaneously boost the primary and treat elective nodes. Its key advantage is parotid-sparing (mean <26 Gy), reducing Grade 2+ xerostomia from ~75% with 2D RT to ~35% with IMRT. **Critical QUANTEC constraints:** brainstem max 54 Gy, spinal cord 45 Gy, cochlea mean <45 Gy. For chemotherapy: INT 0099 established concurrent CRT; adjuvant chemotherapy failed in NPC-0501 due to poor compliance and no OS benefit. The two landmark NEJM trials — **Sun 2016 (TPF induction)** and **Zhang 2019 (GP induction)** — both showed improved failure-free survival with induction → CRT vs CRT alone; GP is preferred due to better tolerability. Proton beam therapy is emerging for complex T3–T4 tumours, paediatric cases, and re-irradiation.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. IMRT SIB in NPC uses three simultaneous dose levels in 33 fractions: 70 Gy (GTV), 63 Gy (high-risk CTV), 56 Gy (elective) — memorise these doses for exam questions on dose prescription.
> 2. Parotid gland mean dose constraint is <26 Gy per QUANTEC; keeping contralateral parotid mean <20 Gy preserves salivary function.
> 3. INT 0099 (Al-Sarraf 1998) is the pivotal CRT trial but included predominantly Caucasian patients — its benefit magnitude may be overestimated for endemic Asian UCNT.
> 4. Adjuvant chemotherapy (NPC-0501) failed because 40–50% of patients couldn't complete all cycles post-CRT; this is why induction (given upfront) is now preferred.
> 5. Zhang 2019 GP regimen (gemcitabine + cisplatin) is preferred over TPF for induction in NPC because of better tolerability (less mucositis, no alopecia, less GI toxicity).
> 6. The Bragg peak of proton therapy means minimal exit dose — especially valuable for NPC with posterior proximity to brainstem and spinal cord.
> 7. Adaptive RT replanning at week 3–4 is important in NPC because parotid glands migrate medially as tumour shrinks, increasing incidental dose if the original plan is used.
> 8. Temporal lobe necrosis is the most feared late complication of NPC RT — constraint is D1cc ≤65 Gy; manifests as white matter changes, cognitive decline, seizures years post-RT.
> 9. SBRT for recurrent NPC (8–10 Gy × 5) can deliver BED equivalent to 60–66 Gy in fewer fractions, sparing adjacent critical structures from cumulative dose.
> 10. EBV DNA clearance is the key response biomarker post-RT: undetectable at 6–8 weeks = complete response; persistent EBV DNA = residual disease requiring further investigation.
