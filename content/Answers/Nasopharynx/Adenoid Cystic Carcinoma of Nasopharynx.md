## Overview

### Site of Origin in Nasopharynx
- ACC of the nasopharynx is **rare** — ACC primarily affects major and minor salivary glands
- In the nasopharynx, ACC arises from **minor salivary gland rests** (seromucous glands) scattered in the nasopharyngeal submucosa
- Accounts for a small fraction of nasopharyngeal malignancies (<5%)
- Important differential from NPC — completely different biology, treatment, and prognosis

### Comparison with NPC

| Feature | NPC (UCNT) | ACC of Nasopharynx |
|---|---|---|
| EBV association | Virtually 100% (Type 3) | **None** |
| Radiosensitivity | Highly radiosensitive | **Radioresistant** |
| Primary treatment | RT ± CRT | **Surgery (radical resection)** |
| Perineural invasion | Rare | **Universal (~80–100%)** |
| Distant metastasis timing | Early (within 2–3 years) | **Late (decades later)** |
| Most common distant site | Lung, bone, liver | **Lung (50–60%)** |
| Chemotherapy response | Good (cisplatin-based) | **Poor (no standard regimen)** |
| Nodal metastasis | Common at presentation (~70%) | **Uncommon at presentation (~15–20%)** |
| Neck dissection | Elective bilateral RT | Only for clinically positive nodes (selective ND) |

## Histopathology

### Three Architectural Patterns

#### 1. Cribriform Pattern (Swiss-cheese pattern) — Most Common (~50–60%)
- Tumour cells arranged in nests with multiple cylindrical punched-out spaces (pseudocysts) containing myxoid/hyaline matrix
- Basement membrane material fills the spaces
- Cells: two populations — luminal ductal cells (inner) + abluminal myoepithelial cells (outer)
- **Best prognosis** among the three patterns

#### 2. Tubular Pattern (~15–20%)
- Small tubular structures lined by two cell layers
- More organised, less solid areas
- **Intermediate prognosis**

#### 3. Solid Pattern (~20–25%) — Most Aggressive
- Sheets and nests of basaloid tumour cells with minimal intervening matrix
- No cribriform or tubular architecture
- High mitotic rate, necrosis
- >30% solid component = worse prognosis; **>50% solid = worst prognosis**
- Independently associated with higher recurrence rate and distant metastasis

### Perineural Invasion (PNI) — The Pathological Hallmark
- Present in **80–100%** of ACC cases
- Tumour cells track along nerves for centimetres beyond the gross tumour edge
- **Consequences:**
  - Skip lesions (tumour deposits discontinuous with main mass)
  - Positive surgical margins even with apparently clear gross resection
  - Late recurrence (decades) from dormant perineural spread
  - Cranial nerve dysfunction (cranial nerves V, VII most affected)
- Mandates **wide perineural margins** along named cranial nerve branches in surgical planning
- Dictates postoperative RT to nerve pathways even with clear margins

### Immunohistochemistry
- **CK7+, CK5/6+:** Epithelial
- **SMA (smooth muscle actin)+, S-100+, calponin+:** Myoepithelial component
- **MYB+:** Expressed in ~90% of ACC (including MYB-NFIB fusion and non-fusion cases)
- **c-KIT (CD117)+:** Expressed in ~90%; prognostic but not predictive (imatinib ineffective)
- **EBV-ISH negative** — key differentiator from NPC (EBER-ISH negative)

## Molecular Biology

### MYB-NFIB Fusion Oncogene

**Mechanism:**
- **t(6;9)(q22–23;p23–24)** chromosomal translocation
- **MYB** (chromosome 6q23): proto-oncogene encoding a transcription factor; regulates cell cycle entry, differentiation, apoptosis
- **NFIB** (chromosome 9p24): transcription factor (Nuclear Factor I B)
- **Fusion:** MYB fused to NFIB → deletion of MYB 3'-UTR regulatory region → **MYB overexpression** without the normal post-translational degradation signals
- **Present in ~50–60%** of ACC (may be undetected in additional cases due to cryptic translocations)
- **Effect:** MYB overexpression drives transcription of anti-apoptotic genes, cell cycle entry genes (E2F, CDK4), NOTCH target genes

### Additional Molecular Alterations
- **NOTCH1 mutations:** Found in ~17% of ACC, predominantly in **solid variant**; activating NOTCH1 mutations correlate with worse prognosis
- **FGFR1/FGFR2 alterations:** Present in 10–15%; targetable (rationale for lenvatinib, which inhibits VEGFR + FGFR)
- **TP53 mutations:** Rare in typical ACC; more common in high-grade transformation
- **PIK3CA mutations:** ~8–10%; targetable
- **BRCA1/2 mutations:** ~5%; rationale for PARP inhibitors (olaparib trials)
- **CREBBP/EP300 (histone acetyltransferases):** Frequently mutated; epigenetic dysregulation

### High-Grade Transformation
- ~10% of ACC undergo **high-grade transformation (HGT)** — also called **ACC-ex-PLGA** or dedifferentiation
- Features: loss of cribriform architecture, increased mitoses, necrosis, loss of MYB expression
- Much worse prognosis than conventional ACC; may resemble high-grade SCC or undifferentiated carcinoma
- Treated more aggressively (surgery + RT + consider chemotherapy)

## Clinical Features

### Presentation
- **Nasopharyngeal mass** on endoscopy: submucosal, often covered by intact mucosa (unlike NPC which frequently ulcerates)
- **Nasal obstruction** (unilateral or bilateral)
- **Epistaxis:** Less common than NPC; may be first symptom
- **Cranial nerve deficits** (from perineural spread): diplopia (VI), facial numbness (V2/V3), facial weakness (VII), deafness/tinnitus (VIII)
- **Deep-seated pain:** Characteristic of perineural spread along trigeminal branches
- **Middle ear effusion** (Eustachian tube involvement)
- **Neck nodes:** Present in only ~15–20% at diagnosis (vs ~70% in NPC) — ACC is NOT a primarily nodal disease

### Natural History — "Slow but Relentless"
- Grows slowly but continuously; does NOT truly plateau
- **Local recurrence** even after apparently complete resection — decades later
- **Distant metastases** may appear 10–20 years after primary treatment
- Despite slow growth, pulmonary metastases may remain indolent for years (watched approach acceptable if asymptomatic)

## Treatment

### Surgery — Primary Modality

**Principle:** Maximal surgical resection with wide margins, including margins along nerves

**Approach for Nasopharyngeal ACC:**
- Determined by size and extent: endoscopic (limited), Le Fort I (midline), infratemporal fossa (lateral extension)
- **Perineural spread assessment:** pre-operative MRI with gadolinium; identify involved cranial nerve branches (foramen ovale for V3, foramen rotundum for V2)
- **Skull base resection** if T3–T4 involvement: craniofacial resection with neurosurgical team
- **Frozen section margins** intraoperatively — but perineural spread may produce positive margins despite best efforts
- **Selective neck dissection:** Only for clinically (N+) or radiologically positive nodes; not elective bilateral (unlike NPC)

### Postoperative Radiotherapy (PORT) — Mandatory

- **Dose:** 60–66 Gy in 30–33 fractions
- **Indications:** All ACC (given universal positive/close margins and perineural invasion)
- **RT fields must include:**
  - Primary tumour bed
  - Named nerve pathways involved (e.g., V3 → foramen ovale → Gasserian ganglion → trigeminal cistern)
  - Skull base foramina
- **Fast neutron RT** (where available): higher relative biological effectiveness (RBE) for radioresistant ACC; improves local control; limited availability
- **IMRT:** Preferred technique; allows nerve tract coverage while sparing critical structures

### Systemic Therapy

**Conventional chemotherapy:**
- No standard regimen; ACC is largely chemoresistant
- Cisplatin + doxorubicin or vinorelbine: modest response rates (~15–30% partial responses); no OS benefit established
- Used palliatively for symptomatic or rapidly progressive distant metastases

**Targeted therapy (under investigation):**
- **Lenvatinib** (multikinase — VEGFR1-3, FGFR1-4, PDGFR, KIT, RET): Phase II activity in ACC; ORR ~15–20%; stable disease in additional 40–50%
- **Olaparib** (PARP inhibitor): For BRCA-mutated ACC; phase II trials ongoing
- **Axitinib** (VEGFR inhibitor): Phase II activity
- **Imatinib** (c-KIT inhibitor): Despite c-KIT expression, imatinib showed NO activity (c-KIT not mutated / amplified); abandoned
- **Notch inhibitors** (BMS-986115, AL101): Targeting NOTCH1-activated solid-variant ACC

**Immunotherapy:**
- Pembrolizumab and nivolumab: Limited activity in ACC (low TMB, low PD-L1 typically); single-arm trials show modest ORR (~5–10%); not standard of care
- **Combination with chemotherapy**: Preliminary data; no phase III available

## Prognosis

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| 5-yr OS (all stages) | ~60–70% |
| 10-yr OS | ~40% (late relapses pull down long-term OS) |
| 20-yr OS | ~25–30% |
| 5-yr OS — Cribriform/tubular | ~70–80% |
| 5-yr OS — Solid pattern | ~50–55% |
| Local recurrence (10-yr) | ~40–50% |
| Distant metastasis (10-yr) | ~30–40% (lung predominant) |

**Indolent pulmonary metastases:** ~50% of patients with lung metastases survive >5 years from time of metastasis detection — watchful waiting appropriate for asymptomatic, slow-growing lesions; systemic therapy for progressive/symptomatic disease

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> ACC of the nasopharynx arises from minor salivary gland rests and is fundamentally different from NPC: it is **NOT EBV-associated**, **NOT radiosensitive**, and does NOT respond to standard CRT. The **MYB-NFIB fusion oncogene** (t(6;9)) is the molecular hallmark, driving MYB overexpression. Histologically, three patterns (cribriform > tubular > solid); solid pattern (>30%) is the most aggressive. **Perineural invasion is universal** — explains skip lesions, positive margins, and late recurrence decades post-treatment. Primary treatment is **radical surgery + PORT (60–66 Gy)** with RT covering perineural pathways. Neck dissection is only for clinically positive nodes (not elective). **No effective systemic therapy** — lenvatinib, olaparib (for BRCA+), and axitinib are under evaluation. Despite 5-yr OS ~65%, late relapses bring 20-yr OS to ~25%; long-term follow-up (>10 years) is mandatory.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The **MYB-NFIB fusion** (t(6;9)) is pathognomonic of ACC — present in ~60%; even MYB-NFIB-negative ACC overexpresses MYB by other mechanisms (MYB expression nearly universal).
> 2. ACC is **NOT EBV-associated** — EBER-ISH negative; this is the single most important distinguishing feature from NPC on exam questions.
> 3. **Perineural invasion is virtually universal in ACC** — dictates wide perineural margins, named nerve tract RT coverage, and explains late recurrences.
> 4. The **solid pattern** (>30–50% solid component) carries the worst prognosis among ACC histological subtypes; correlates with NOTCH1 mutations and more aggressive behaviour.
> 5. Despite c-KIT (CD117) positivity in ~90% of ACC, **imatinib showed no activity** — c-KIT is not mutated or amplified; expression alone does not predict response to c-KIT inhibitors.
> 6. ACC produces **late distant metastases** (decades after primary treatment) — unlike NPC which tends to metastasize early; long-term (10–20 year) follow-up is essential.
> 7. **Indolent pulmonary metastases** in ACC may be watched without treatment if asymptomatic and slow-growing; ~50% survive >5 years from lung metastasis diagnosis.
> 8. Elective **bilateral neck irradiation is NOT indicated** in ACC (unlike NPC) — neck dissection only for clinically positive nodes; ACC has low risk of occult nodal disease (~15–20%).
> 9. **Fast neutron RT** (available at select centres) improves local control in ACC due to higher RBE for radioresistant tumour — used as boost or definitive treatment for unresectable disease.
> 10. The **10-yr OS of ~40%** vs 5-yr OS of ~65% in ACC demonstrates late relapse pattern — patients with apparently favourable 5-yr outcomes must continue long-term surveillance for local and distant recurrence.
