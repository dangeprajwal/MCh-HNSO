## Overview of Recurrent NPC

### Definitions
- **Local recurrence (rNPC):** Recurrence at the primary nasopharyngeal site after completion of definitive RT
- **Regional recurrence (rN):** New or enlarging neck nodes after treatment
- **Distant recurrence (M1):** Metastatic spread to lung, bone, liver, or brain after initial treatment
- **rT staging (AICC 2010 / Ho's classification):** rT1–rT4 mirrors primary T staging; used to select salvage modality

### Epidemiology
- 15–30% of NPC patients develop locoregional or distant recurrence after definitive treatment
- Pattern of recurrence:
  - **Local alone:** ~30%
  - **Nodal alone:** ~20%
  - **Locoregional:** ~15%
  - **Distant (with or without locoregional):** ~30%
- **Median time to recurrence:** Local ~24–36 months; distant ~18–24 months
- **Distant sites:** Lung (most common, ~60%), bone (40%), liver (30%), brain (<10%)

### Detection and Diagnosis
- **EBV DNA rising** on surveillance: perform MRI skull base + PET-CT within 2–4 weeks
- **Nasopharyngoscopy + biopsy:** mandatory to confirm histological recurrence before salvage
- **PET-CT:** Distinguishes recurrence from post-RT fibrosis (FDG avid = recurrence; non-avid = fibrosis); also detects occult distant metastases before committing to local salvage
- **MRI skull base protocol:** gadolinium-enhanced; distinguishes recurrence from radiation necrosis; diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) can help distinguish (ADC value)

## Management of Local Recurrence (rNPC)

### Risk Stratification for Salvage

| Factor | Favourable | Unfavourable |
|---|---|---|
| rT stage | rT1–rT2 | rT3–rT4 |
| ICA status | Not involved | Encasement |
| Interval from prior RT | >12 months | <6 months |
| Volume of recurrence | Small (≤15 cc) | Large (>15 cc) |
| Performance status | ECOG 0–1 | ECOG ≥2 |
| EBV DNA level | Low / undetectable | High (>10,000) |

---

### Option 1: Endoscopic Nasopharyngectomy (Salvage Surgery)

**Best for:** rT1–rT2 limited recurrences without ICA encasement

**Pre-operative workup:**
- MRI with gadolinium (skull base protocol) — define resection extent
- PET-CT — exclude distant metastases
- Plasma EBV DNA — confirm locoregional pattern
- Angiography ± balloon occlusion test if ICA <7 mm from tumour

**Surgical technique (Ng's approach — see [[Answers/Nasopharynx/Surgical Approaches to Nasopharynx|Surgical Approaches to Nasopharynx]] for full description):**
- Wide bilateral sphenoidotomies + choanal resection
- Mucosal incisions with 1 cm margins
- Parapharyngeal dissection
- ICA identification and preservation
- En bloc or piecemeal removal; periosteum included

**Oncological outcomes:**

| Stage | 5-yr OS | 5-yr DSS |
|---|---|---|
| rT1 | ~70–73% | ~80% |
| rT2 | ~50–55% | ~60% |
| rT3 | ~25–35% | ~35% |

**Complications:**
- ICA injury (1–2%) — potentially fatal haemorrhage
- Palatal necrosis — impaired post-RT vascularity
- Osteoradionecrosis of clivus
- CSF leak (dural breach)
- Severe otitis media / Eustachian tube dysfunction

---

### Option 2: Re-irradiation (Intensity-Modulated Re-irradiation, IMRT)

**Indications:** rT2–rT3 recurrences unsuitable for surgery; post-surgical recurrence; salvage when surgery declined

**Challenges:**
- Prior full-dose RT (70 Gy) limits cumulative dose to critical structures
- Risk of serious late toxicity increases with cumulative dose:
  - Temporal lobe radionecrosis
  - Brainstem necrosis
  - Radiation-induced carotid blowout
  - Osteoradionecrosis of clivus/mandible

**Re-irradiation protocol:**
- Dose: **60–66 Gy** in 30–33 fractions (conventional fractionation IMRT)
- Concurrent cisplatin sometimes added (though toxicity is high)
- Minimum interval from prior RT: **6 months** (preferably ≥12 months)
- Strict constraint reductions: brainstem max ≤45 Gy (cumulative), spinal cord ≤40 Gy total

**Outcomes:** 5-yr OS 25–40% for rT2; significantly worse for rT3–rT4

---

### Option 3: Stereotactic Body Radiotherapy (SBRT / CyberKnife / Tomotherapy)

**Rationale:** High biological effective dose delivered in few fractions to small recurrence volume, concentrating dose away from previously irradiated critical structures

**Protocol:**
- Typically **8–10 Gy × 5 fractions** (total 40–50 Gy; BED ~72–100 Gy at α/β = 10)
- Alternatively 5 × 5 Gy (SBRT with more conservative BED) for very small lesions
- Highly conformal; real-time image guidance with CBCT or fiducial tracking

**Best indications:**
- Small volume (≤15–20 cc) isolated local recurrence
- Previously irradiated patients with high cumulative brainstem/cord dose (limits conventional re-RT)
- Isolated nodal recurrence (SRS/SBRT to small residual node)

**Outcomes:** 2-yr LC 60–75%; 2-yr OS 50–65% for selected rT1–rT2; better toxicity profile than conventionally fractionated re-RT for small volume disease

## Management of Regional Recurrence (Neck)

### Isolated Neck Recurrence (rN)
- Confirm with PET-CT (exclude distant metastases and primary site recurrence)
- **Radical neck dissection** (modified or radical depending on extent) — standard for resectable rN
  - Post-RT tissue is fibrotic; increased risk of wound breakdown, carotid exposure, chylous fistula
  - Selective neck dissection insufficient — residual nodes in post-RT field require comprehensive level dissection
- **Re-irradiation** for unresectable rN or when surgery declined: IMRT re-RT to involved nodes ± concurrent cisplatin

### Post-CRT Residual Neck Nodes
- PET-CT at **10–12 weeks** post-CRT (NPC-specific recommendation)
- If PET-positive residual node: proceed to salvage neck dissection
- If PET-negative: observe; serial imaging; repeat PET if EBV DNA rises

## Management of Distant Metastases (Stage IVB / M1)

### First-Line Systemic Therapy

**GP Regimen (Zhang et al. 2016, Lancet Oncology — current standard):**
- Gemcitabine 1000 mg/m² IV D1 and D8 + Cisplatin 80 mg/m² IV D1; every 3 weeks × 6 cycles
- **ORR: 64%** (vs 42% for PF); **mPFS: 7.0 months** (vs 5.6 months for PF)
- Preferred for all patients with good performance status (ECOG 0–1)

**GP + Pembrolizumab (KEYNOTE-789, 2023 — emerging standard):**
- Pembrolizumab 200 mg IV + GP × 6 cycles → pembrolizumab maintenance × 35 cycles
- Phase III: PFS HR 0.51 (95% CI 0.37–0.69; p<0.001) in overall population
- Clinically meaningful; regulatory approvals pending in some jurisdictions
- Now considered the preferred first-line for recurrent/metastatic NPC in guidelines (NCCN 2024)

### Second-Line and Beyond

**Pembrolizumab monotherapy (KEYNOTE-122):**
- For platinum-pretreated recurrent/metastatic NPC
- ORR ~26%; durable responses in PD-L1 CPS ≥1
- Generally better tolerated than chemotherapy (avoid cumulative nephrotoxicity)

**Nivolumab:**
- AtTEnd trial ongoing; nivolumab + chemotherapy in first-line

**Other options:**
- Capecitabine (oral; some activity as maintenance or 2nd line)
- S-1 (tegafur/gimeracil/oteracil) — active in Asian trials
- Docetaxel (2nd–3rd line)
- Afatinib (EGFR inhibitor): NPC shows EGFR overexpression; modest activity

### Locoregional RT in Oligometastatic Disease
- For 1–3 distant metastases (SBRT) with controlled locoregional disease
- May improve PFS in selected oligometastatic patients (retrospective data)
- No randomised trial in NPC specifically

## Role of EBV DNA in Recurrence Monitoring

| Time Point | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Rising EBV DNA during surveillance | Probable recurrence — initiate MRI + PET-CT |
| Persistently undetectable post-salvage | Complete response — continue standard surveillance |
| Declining but not undetectable post-RT | Partial response — close imaging follow-up |
| EBV DNA cleared post-salvage surgery | Confirms complete resection — favourable prognosis |

**Key principle:** EBV DNA rises typically **2–4 months before clinical evidence** of recurrence — use it as a trigger for imaging, not as standalone diagnostic.

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Recurrent NPC affects 15–30% of patients; detection relies on rising plasma EBV DNA (precedes clinical recurrence by 2–4 months), MRI skull base, and PET-CT. For local recurrences: **endoscopic nasopharyngectomy** (Ng's approach) achieves 5-yr OS ~70% for rT1 and ~50% for rT2; ICA proximity (<7 mm) is a relative contraindication. **Re-irradiation** (IMRT 60–66 Gy or SBRT 8–10 Gy × 5) is used when surgery is not feasible, with major concerns for temporal lobe necrosis and carotid blowout. For nodal recurrence: radical neck dissection is standard. For metastatic disease: **GP regimen** (Zhang 2016, Lancet Oncology) is standard first-line with 64% ORR; **GP + pembrolizumab (KEYNOTE-789)** is the emerging standard; pembrolizumab monotherapy is second-line (KEYNOTE-122, ORR 26%).

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Rising plasma EBV DNA during surveillance is the most sensitive indicator of recurrence — triggers MRI + PET-CT before clinical symptoms appear (lead time 2–4 months).
> 2. PET-CT at 10–12 weeks post-CRT is essential to guide neck management — PET-positive residual nodes → proceed to neck dissection.
> 3. Endoscopic nasopharyngectomy for rT1 achieves ~70–73% 5-yr OS — comparable to results of re-irradiation for rT1 but with lower late toxicity risk.
> 4. ICA encasement is a near-absolute contraindication to salvage nasopharyngectomy — risk of intraoperative ICA rupture; re-irradiation or palliative CRT preferred.
> 5. Re-irradiation (IMRT) minimum recommended interval from prior RT: 6 months (preferably ≥12 months) to allow normal tissue recovery.
> 6. SBRT (8–10 Gy × 5) for small volume local recurrences reduces cumulative dose to brainstem and spinal cord compared to conventionally fractionated re-RT — preferred for lesions ≤15 cc.
> 7. **GP regimen** (gemcitabine + cisplatin) is the standard first-line for recurrent/metastatic NPC (ORR 64%) — superior to the older PF (5-FU + cisplatin) regimen.
> 8. **KEYNOTE-789** (GP + pembrolizumab) improved PFS (HR 0.51) in first-line metastatic NPC; this is likely to become the new standard when fully adopted.
> 9. Temporal lobe necrosis is the most dreaded late complication of NPC re-irradiation — manifests as white matter changes, cognitive decline, seizures; dose constraint D1cc ≤45 Gy in re-RT setting.
> 10. Post-RT neck dissection is technically challenging — fibrosis, poor wound healing, carotid artery exposure risk; pre-operative nutritional optimisation and hyperbaric oxygen (if available) improve outcomes.
