## Introduction

Proton beam therapy (PBT) is an advanced form of external beam radiation that exploits the unique physical properties of protons --- specifically the Bragg peak --- to deliver highly conformal radiation to the target while minimising dose to surrounding normal tissues. In head and neck cancer, where critical structures (brainstem, spinal cord, optic apparatus, parotid glands, cochleae, pharyngeal constrictors) are in close proximity to tumour volumes, proton therapy offers a compelling dosimetric advantage over conventional photon-based techniques. The publication of the Frank et al. phase III trial (2025) has transformed proton therapy from a theoretically superior modality to one with level 1 evidence supporting improved survival and reduced toxicity in oropharyngeal cancer.

## Physics of Proton Therapy

### The Bragg Peak

- Protons are positively charged particles with mass approximately **1836 times** that of an electron
- Unlike photons (which deposit dose exponentially with depth), protons deposit the **majority of their energy at a specific depth** --- the **Bragg peak**
- Beyond the Bragg peak, dose drops to **virtually zero** (sharp distal fall-off)
- This property allows precise targeting of deep-seated tumours while sparing tissues distal to the target

### Spread-Out Bragg Peak (SOBP)

- A single Bragg peak is too narrow to cover most clinical target volumes
- **SOBP** is created by superimposing multiple Bragg peaks of different energies (range modulation)
- The SOBP provides a uniform dose across the entire depth of the target volume
- Trade-off: the entrance dose in an SOBP field is higher than a single pristine Bragg peak

### Relative Biological Effectiveness (RBE)

- Proton RBE is conventionally assigned a value of **1.1** relative to photons
- This means 1 Gy (RBE) of protons is biologically equivalent to 1.1 Gy of photons
- The RBE of 1.1 is applied **uniformly** in clinical practice, though in reality RBE varies with:
  - **LET** (linear energy transfer) --- increases at end of range near the Bragg peak
  - **Dose per fraction** --- higher RBE at lower doses per fraction
  - **Tissue type** --- late-responding tissues may have higher RBE
- **Clinical implication**: potential for increased biological effect at the distal edge, particularly relevant for structures immediately beyond the target (e.g., brainstem, temporal lobes)

### Linear Energy Transfer (LET)

- Protons are **low-LET** radiation for most of their path (similar to photons)
- LET increases sharply at the **distal edge** of the Bragg peak (end-of-range effect)
- Elevated LET at the distal edge may cause **higher-than-expected biological damage**
- This is a source of uncertainty and is actively investigated in clinical and preclinical studies

### Distal Edge Uncertainty

- The exact position of the Bragg peak is subject to **range uncertainty** (typically +/- 3.5% of range + 1 mm)
- Sources of uncertainty:
  - CT number-to-stopping power conversion
  - Patient setup variability
  - Anatomical changes (weight loss, tumour shrinkage, mucositis)
  - Respiratory and swallowing motion
- **Clinical consequence**: safety margins are added, and beams are generally not aimed directly at a critical structure in the distal direction

## Delivery Techniques

### Passive Scattering

- **First-generation** proton delivery technique
- A broad proton beam is shaped using **scatterers**, **range modulators**, and **patient-specific apertures and compensators**
- Advantages: robust, simple, long clinical track record
- Limitations: limited ability to create concave dose distributions, higher neutron contamination from beam-line hardware, less conformal than scanning techniques

### Uniform Scanning

- Intermediate technique: a narrow beam is scanned across the field in a uniform pattern
- Still uses patient-specific compensators for distal conformity
- Reduced neutron contamination compared to passive scattering

### Pencil Beam Scanning (PBS) / Intensity-Modulated Proton Therapy (IMPT)

- **State-of-the-art** delivery technique and current standard for HNC
- A narrow proton beam (pencil beam, ~5-10 mm sigma) is magnetically steered across the target in three dimensions
- Each pencil beam has individually optimised energy (depth) and intensity (weight)
- **Advantages**:
  - No patient-specific hardware (apertures/compensators) required
  - Ability to create highly conformal, **concave dose distributions** (analogous to IMRT)
  - Can achieve **simultaneous integrated boost** (SIB) delivery
  - Minimal neutron contamination
- **Limitations**: more sensitive to range uncertainties and inter-fraction anatomical changes

### Robustness Optimisation

- Standard IMPT optimisation may produce plans that are highly sensitive to uncertainties
- **Robust optimisation** explicitly accounts for:
  - Setup uncertainty (typically +/- 3 mm)
  - Range uncertainty (typically +/- 3.5%)
- Produces plans that maintain target coverage and OAR sparing across multiple uncertainty scenarios
- **Minimax optimisation**: optimises the worst-case scenario across all perturbation combinations
- Essential for HNC due to complex anatomy and proximity to critical structures

## Dosimetric Advantages over Photon Therapy

### Reduced Integral Dose

- Proton therapy reduces the **integral dose** (total energy deposited in the patient) by **50-60%** compared to IMRT
- This translates to reduced low-dose bath to normal tissues
- Particularly relevant for reducing:
  - Risk of radiation-induced secondary malignancies
  - Bone marrow suppression (important during concurrent chemotherapy)
  - Fatigue and overall treatment burden

### Organ-at-Risk (OAR) Sparing

| OAR | Dosimetric Advantage | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Parotid glands** | Mean dose reduction 30-60% | Reduced xerostomia |
| **Submandibular glands** | Significant sparing, especially contralateral | Preserved resting salivary flow |
| **Oral cavity** | Mean dose reduction 50-70% | Reduced mucositis, taste alteration |
| **Pharyngeal constrictors** | Superior and middle constrictor sparing | Reduced dysphagia |
| **Cochleae** | Mean dose often <10 Gy | Preserved hearing |
| **Brainstem** | Reduced Dmax | Reduced neurotoxicity risk |
| **Optic nerves/chiasm** | Reduced Dmax and mean dose | Preserved vision |
| **Temporal lobes** | Reduced V40, V50 | Reduced temporal lobe necrosis |
| **Spinal cord** | Reduced Dmax | Reduced myelopathy risk |
| **Bone marrow** | Reduced low-dose exposure | Less neutropenia with concurrent chemo |
| **Thyroid gland** | Reduced mean dose | Less hypothyroidism |

### Comparative Planning Studies (Holliday 2016)

- Case-matched analysis: IMPT vs IMRT for oropharyngeal cancer
- IMPT significantly reduced mean doses to:
  - Contralateral parotid: **0.36 Gy vs 24.6 Gy** (p < 0.001)
  - Oral cavity: **8.1 Gy vs 30.6 Gy** (p < 0.001)
  - Larynx: **2.6 Gy vs 19.8 Gy** (p < 0.001)
- Target coverage was equivalent between modalities

## Clinical Indications

### 1. Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma (NPC)

- **Strongest established indication** for proton therapy in mucosal HNC (prior to Frank trial)
- NPC requires bilateral neck irradiation with high doses (70 Gy) to a centrally located primary
- Critical structures at risk: brainstem, temporal lobes, optic apparatus, cochleae, parotid glands
- IMPT advantages:
  - Significant reduction in temporal lobe dose (reduces temporal lobe necrosis from 5-10% to <2%)
  - Better cochlear sparing (hearing preservation)
  - Reduced brainstem and optic pathway doses
  - Lower integral dose despite bilateral treatment fields

### 2. Sinonasal and Skull Base Tumours

- **Established indication** with longest track record (>30 years of experience)
- Tumour types: chordoma, chondrosarcoma, esthesioneuroblastoma, adenoid cystic carcinoma, sinonasal SCC
- Weber et al. (2016): 7-year LC for chordomas 71%, chondrosarcomas 94%
- Advantages:
  - Ability to deliver high doses (>70 Gy RBE) while respecting brainstem, optic nerve, and chiasm tolerances
  - Critical for chordomas/chondrosarcomas that require 74-78 Gy (RBE) for optimal local control
  - Sharp distal fall-off protects brain parenchyma

### 3. Oropharyngeal Cancer

- **Now supported by level 1 evidence** (Frank et al. 2025, Lancet)
- Phase III trial (440 patients, 21 US sites):
  - 5-year OS: **90.9% (IMPT) vs 81.0% (IMRT)** --- IMPT non-inferior and numerically superior
  - Feeding tube dependence: **27% vs 40%** (p < 0.05)
  - Severe lymphopenia (grade 3+): **76% vs 89%** (p < 0.05)
  - Weight loss >5%: **76% vs 86%** (p < 0.05)
- Mechanism of survival benefit: likely related to reduced severe lymphopenia (preserved immune function)
- Unilateral neck irradiation (well-lateralised tonsil cancers): IMPT can nearly eliminate dose to contralateral structures

### 4. Re-Irradiation

- Previously irradiated patients have limited normal tissue tolerance
- Romesser et al. (2016): multi-institutional proton re-irradiation series (92 patients)
  - 12-month locoregional control: **74.9%**
  - 12-month OS: **65.2%**
  - Grade 3+ acute toxicity: **9.8%**
  - No treatment-related deaths
- IMPT advantages in re-irradiation:
  - Reduced dose to previously irradiated critical structures (especially brainstem, spinal cord)
  - Lower cumulative integral dose
  - Potentially reduced risk of carotid blowout syndrome and osteoradionecrosis

### 5. Paediatric Head and Neck Cancer

- **Strongest general indication** for proton therapy regardless of site
- Children are most vulnerable to:
  - Growth disturbance from irradiation of developing tissues
  - Endocrine dysfunction (pituitary, thyroid)
  - Neurocognitive impairment
  - Secondary malignancy induction (lifetime risk significantly higher)
- Proton therapy is considered **standard of care** for paediatric tumours wherever available
- Common paediatric HNC: rhabdomyosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, nasopharyngeal carcinoma

### 6. Unilateral Neck Irradiation

- Well-lateralised tumours (early tonsil, parotid, skin cancers) requiring ipsilateral-only treatment
- IMPT can virtually eliminate dose to contralateral neck structures
- Particularly beneficial for preserving contralateral parotid and submandibular gland function

### Other Indications

- **Periorbital tumours**: lacrimal gland carcinoma, eyelid carcinoma --- eye preservation
- **Salivary gland malignancies**: adenoid cystic carcinoma with perineural spread along skull base
- **Cutaneous SCC with extensive perineural invasion**: following nerve pathways to skull base

## Evidence Base

### Retrospective Series and Planning Studies

- Multiple single-institution series have demonstrated favourable toxicity profiles
- Comparative planning studies consistently show dosimetric superiority of IMPT over IMRT
- **Limitation**: selection bias (patients referred for proton therapy may differ systematically)

### Randomised Controlled Trials

| Trial | Design | Status | Primary Endpoint |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Frank et al. (NCT01893307)** | Phase III, IMPT vs IMRT, OPC (n=440) | **Published 2025** | PFS (non-inferiority) |
| **NRG-HN007** | Phase II/III, IMPT vs IMRT, OPC (n=300+) | Accruing | 2-year composite toxicity |
| **TORPEdO** (UK) | Phase III, IMPT vs IMRT, OPC | **Published 2026** | Patient-reported swallowing |

### Frank et al. Phase III Trial --- Key Details

- **Design**: multicentre, open-label, non-inferiority phase 3
- **Population**: stage III-IVB oropharyngeal SCC (predominantly HPV-positive)
- **Treatment**: IMPT (n=221) vs IMRT (n=219), with concurrent cisplatin
- **Primary endpoint**: progression-free survival (non-inferiority margin HR 1.45)
- **Key results**:
  - PFS: IMPT non-inferior to IMRT (HR 0.70, 95% CI 0.48-1.02)
  - OS: 5-year 90.9% vs 81.0% (HR 0.51, 95% CI 0.31-0.85)
  - Grade 3+ adverse events during treatment: **85% vs 95%**
  - Severe lymphopenia: **76% vs 89%**
  - Gastrostomy tube use: **27% vs 40%**
- **Significance**: first level 1 evidence establishing IMPT as a new standard-of-care option for OPC

## Toxicity Reduction

### Acute Toxicity

| Toxicity | IMPT Advantage | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Mucositis** | Reduced grade 3+ (less oral cavity dose) | Comparative planning studies |
| **Dermatitis** | Reduced entrance dose spread | Clinical observation |
| **Dysphagia** | Significant reduction | Frank 2025 (31% vs 49% grade 3+) |
| **Weight loss** | Less >5% weight loss | Frank 2025 (76% vs 86%) |
| **Feeding tube dependence** | Significant reduction | Frank 2025 (27% vs 40%) |
| **Neutropenia** | Reduced grade 3+ | Bone marrow sparing, less lymphopenia |
| **Nausea** | Reduced severity | Lower integral dose |

### Late Toxicity

| Toxicity | IMPT Advantage | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Xerostomia** | Significant reduction | Parotid/submandibular sparing |
| **Dysphagia** | Reduced late stricture/stenosis | Pharyngeal constrictor sparing |
| **Hypothyroidism** | Reduced incidence | Lower thyroid gland dose |
| **Hearing loss** | Preserved hearing | Cochlear dose often <10 Gy |
| **Temporal lobe necrosis** | Reduced from 5-10% to <2% (NPC) | Temporal lobe dose reduction |
| **Osteoradionecrosis** | Potentially reduced | Lower mandibular dose |
| **Trismus** | Potentially reduced | Reduced pterygoid muscle dose |
| **Secondary malignancy** | Theoretically reduced | Lower integral dose, less neutron scatter with PBS |

### Immunological Benefit

- The Frank trial highlighted **severe lymphopenia** as a key differentiator
- IMRT causes more extensive bone marrow irradiation (cervical vertebrae, mandible, skull base)
- Lymphocytes are exquisitely radiosensitive (D0 ~2 Gy)
- Severe lymphopenia during chemoradiation is an independent adverse prognostic factor
- Reduced lymphopenia with IMPT may preserve anti-tumour immune surveillance, contributing to the observed survival benefit

## Carbon Ion Therapy

### Physics and Radiobiology

- Carbon ions are **heavier** than protons (mass ~12x proton)
- Sharper Bragg peak and **reduced lateral penumbra** compared to protons
- **Higher RBE**: 2-5 (compared to 1.1 for protons)
- Higher LET throughout the beam path, with further increase at the Bragg peak
- Greater biological effectiveness against **radioresistant tumours** (hypoxic cells, slow-growing tumours)
- Reduced oxygen enhancement ratio (OER) --- more effective against hypoxic tumour cells

### Indications in HNC

| Tumour Type | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Adenoid cystic carcinoma** | Radioresistant, slow-growing, perineural spread |
| **Mucosal melanoma** | Radioresistant, high LET overcomes resistance |
| **Sarcomas** (skull base) | Radioresistant, requires high biological dose |
| **Chordoma/chondrosarcoma** | Requires very high dose, radioresistant |
| **Locally advanced/recurrent SCC** | When maximal biological dose is needed |

### Limitations

- **Very limited availability**: ~15 centres worldwide (Japan: NIRS/QST; Germany: HIT Heidelberg; Italy: CNAO; China: SPHIC Shanghai)
- **No centres in India** (as of 2026)
- Higher cost than proton therapy
- Greater biological uncertainty (variable RBE)
- Less clinical evidence compared to proton therapy
- Risk of increased late toxicity due to higher RBE in normal tissues

## Limitations of Proton Therapy

### Cost

- Proton therapy facility construction: **USD 100-200 million** (vs USD 3-5 million for a linear accelerator)
- Single-room compact systems: USD 25-40 million (reducing cost barrier)
- Treatment cost per patient: 2-3 times photon-based IMRT
- Cost-effectiveness depends on demonstrated clinical benefit (now supported by Frank trial)

### Range Uncertainty

- CT Hounsfield unit-to-proton stopping power conversion introduces systematic uncertainty
- Anatomical changes during 6-7 week treatment course:
  - Weight loss (common in HNC, up to 10-15% body weight)
  - Tumour shrinkage
  - Parotid gland shrinkage
  - Mucosal swelling (mucositis)
- **Adaptive re-planning** may be required (typically 1-3 times during treatment)
- Dual-energy CT and proton CT are emerging solutions for improved stopping power estimation

### Motion Sensitivity

- PBS is particularly sensitive to **intra-fraction motion**:
  - Swallowing
  - Breathing
  - Mandibular movement
- **Interplay effect**: interaction between beam scanning pattern and organ motion can create dose hot/cold spots
- Mitigation strategies: robust optimisation, motion management, repainting

### Facility Availability

- Globally: ~120 proton therapy centres operational (2025)
- Limited access in low- and middle-income countries
- Long wait times at some centres
- Need for multidisciplinary expertise in proton treatment planning

### Lack of Mature Prospective Data (Diminishing Limitation)

- Historically, the major criticism was lack of randomised evidence
- Frank et al. 2025 provides the first phase III evidence of clinical benefit
- Additional RCTs (NRG-HN007, TORPEdO) are expected to further define the role of proton therapy
- Long-term follow-up data (>10 years) still maturing

## Indian Context

### Available Facilities

| Facility | Location | Status | Technology |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Apollo Proton Cancer Centre** | Chennai | Operational since 2019 | PBS/IMPT |
| **TMC-ACTREC National Hadron Beam Therapy Facility** | Kharghar, Navi Mumbai | Operational since August 2023 | PBS/IMPT (3 gantry rooms, 360-degree rotation) |

- **Apollo Proton Cancer Centre**: India's first and South Asia's first proton therapy centre; has treated >2500 patients from 147 countries
- **TMC-ACTREC**: first government proton therapy facility in India; treated 541 patients in first two years; 65% received subsidised treatment, 27% treated free of cost
- **Planned centres**: additional facilities under development in Hyderabad and Gurugram (expected by 2028)

### Access Considerations

- Limited to two operational centres (Chennai and Navi Mumbai) for a population of 1.4 billion
- Significant **geographical, financial, and logistical barriers** for most Indian patients
- TMC-ACTREC offers subsidised treatment through government and trust funding
- No carbon ion therapy facility in India (patients must travel abroad --- Japan, Germany)
- Growing expertise in proton treatment planning and delivery at both centres
- **Patient selection** is critical given limited capacity --- cases with maximal dosimetric benefit should be prioritised (NPC, skull base, paediatric, re-irradiation)

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Bragg peak** is the fundamental physical advantage --- protons deposit maximum dose at a defined depth with virtually no exit dose, unlike photons
> 2. **RBE of 1.1** is the clinically used value for protons, but actual RBE increases at the distal edge due to rising LET --- this is a source of biological uncertainty
> 3. **Pencil beam scanning (PBS/IMPT)** is the current state-of-the-art delivery technique, enabling concave dose distributions analogous to photon IMRT but with reduced integral dose
> 4. **Frank et al. 2025 (Lancet)** is the landmark phase III RCT: IMPT achieved 5-year OS 90.9% vs 81.0% for IMRT in oropharyngeal cancer, with significantly reduced toxicity --- this is now level 1 evidence
> 5. **Severe lymphopenia reduction** with IMPT (76% vs 89%) may explain the survival benefit through preserved anti-tumour immune surveillance
> 6. **Strongest indications**: nasopharyngeal carcinoma, skull base chordoma/chondrosarcoma, paediatric HNC, re-irradiation, and oropharyngeal cancer
> 7. **Re-irradiation** with proton therapy is feasible with acceptable toxicity (Romesser: 12-month OS 65%, grade 3+ toxicity <10%)
> 8. **Carbon ion therapy** offers higher RBE (2-5) for radioresistant tumours (adenoid cystic carcinoma, mucosal melanoma) but has very limited global availability and no centre in India
> 9. **Major limitations**: cost, range uncertainty, anatomical changes during treatment (weight loss), and need for adaptive re-planning
> 10. **India has two operational proton centres** --- Apollo Chennai (since 2019) and TMC-ACTREC Navi Mumbai (since 2023) --- patient selection prioritising maximum dosimetric benefit is essential given limited capacity
