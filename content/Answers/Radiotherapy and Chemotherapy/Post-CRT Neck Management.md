## Introduction

Concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CRT) is the standard organ-preserving treatment for locally advanced head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC), particularly oropharyngeal, hypopharyngeal, and laryngeal cancers. Following CRT, management of the previously node-positive neck has been one of the most debated topics in head and neck oncology. Historically, planned neck dissection (ND) was performed routinely 6-8 weeks after CRT, regardless of response. However, the recognition that **50-70% of patients achieve a complete pathological response** in the neck has led to a paradigm shift towards response-adapted, PET-CT-guided surveillance strategies.

## Rationale for Change in Practice

### Complete Pathological Response Rates

- **50-70%** of patients with N2-N3 disease achieve complete pathological response (pCR) in the neck after CRT
- In planned ND specimens, **only 20-30%** harbour viable tumour cells
- This means **70-80% of planned neck dissections are unnecessary**, exposing patients to surgical morbidity without therapeutic benefit
- The pCR rate correlates with:
  - Initial N stage (higher pCR in N1-N2a than N2c-N3)
  - Primary site (oropharynx > hypopharynx > larynx)
  - HPV/p16 status (HPV+ tumours have higher pCR rates, approaching 80-90%)
  - Radiation dose to the neck (>60 Gy improves pCR)

### Overtreatment with Routine Planned Neck Dissection

- Planned ND carries significant morbidity: wound complications (15-25%), chyle leak (2-5%), cranial nerve XI injury (5-10%), carotid exposure (3-5%)
- Post-CRT surgical fields are fibrotic, with loss of normal tissue planes increasing operative difficulty and complication rates
- Regional control rates with observation alone in complete responders exceed **95%**
- The PET-NECK trial demonstrated that surveillance is non-inferior to planned ND, with the added benefit of fewer complications and lower cost

## Clinical Assessment of the Neck Post-CRT

### Timing of Assessment

- **8-12 weeks post-CRT** is the standard interval for initial response assessment
- Earlier assessment (<8 weeks) risks false-positive findings due to inflammatory changes
- The 12-week time point balances tumour regression kinetics with the need for timely salvage if required

### Clinical Examination

- **Palpation**: assess for residual palpable nodes, fixation, size, and consistency
- Post-CRT changes (fibrosis, induration, oedema) may mimic residual disease
- Clinical examination alone has a **sensitivity of only 60-70%** and a **specificity of 50-60%** for detecting residual disease
- Serial examinations improve accuracy but are insufficient as the sole assessment modality

### Imaging Modalities

| Modality | Sensitivity | Specificity | NPV | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CT/MRI | 70-80% | 50-65% | 75-85% | Anatomical assessment, residual mass measurement |
| PET-CT | 85-95% | 85-95% | >95% | Gold standard for response assessment |
| Ultrasound + FNA | 70-80% | 90-95% | 85-90% | Targeted assessment of suspicious nodes |

## Role of PET-CT in Post-CRT Neck Management

### The PET-NECK Trial (Mehanna et al. 2016)

This landmark multicentre, randomised Phase III non-inferiority trial compared PET-CT-guided surveillance with planned ND in patients with N2-N3 HNSCC treated with CRT.

**Design:**
- 564 patients from 37 UK centres (2007-2012)
- Randomised 1:1 to PET-CT surveillance (n=282) vs planned ND (n=282)
- PET-CT performed 12 weeks post-CRT
- In the surveillance arm, ND performed only if PET-CT showed incomplete or equivocal response

**Key Results:**
- **2-year overall survival**: 84.9% (surveillance) vs 81.5% (planned ND)
- **Hazard ratio for OS**: 0.92 (95% CI 0.65-1.32), confirming non-inferiority
- Neck dissections performed: **54/282** (19%) in surveillance arm vs **221/282** (78%) in planned ND arm
- Surgical complications: 22 in surveillance arm vs 85 in planned ND arm
- **Cost saving of GBP 1,492 per patient** with PET-CT surveillance
- Quality of life similar between groups
- No difference in regional recurrence rates

**Conclusion:** PET-CT-guided surveillance is non-inferior to planned ND, results in significantly fewer operations and complications, and is more cost-effective.

### Optimal Timing of PET-CT

- **12 weeks (3 months) post-CRT** is the consensus optimal timing
- Earlier scanning (6-8 weeks) yields higher false-positive rates due to:
  - Post-radiation inflammatory FDG uptake
  - Reactive lymphadenopathy
  - Granulation tissue metabolic activity
- Scanning beyond 16 weeks risks delayed detection of residual disease
- The 12-week interval allows sufficient time for:
  - Tumour cell death and apoptosis to complete
  - Inflammatory changes to subside
  - FDG uptake to normalise in responding tissues

### Interpretation Criteria

#### Hopkins Criteria (5-Point Scale)

| Score | Description | Management |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | No residual FDG uptake | Surveillance |
| 2 | Uptake less than internal jugular vein | Surveillance |
| 3 | Uptake equal to internal jugular vein | Equivocal — repeat scan or biopsy |
| 4 | Uptake greater than internal jugular vein | Suspicious — consider ND |
| 5 | Markedly increased uptake with new foci | Highly suspicious — ND recommended |

#### Deauville-Like Scoring (Adapted from Lymphoma)

- Uses mediastinal blood pool and liver as reference structures
- Score 1-3: complete metabolic response (CMR) — surveillance
- Score 4-5: incomplete metabolic response (IMR) — further investigation or ND

#### Negative Predictive Value (NPV)

- The NPV of PET-CT at 12 weeks is **>95%** (range 94-98% across studies)
- This means a negative PET-CT reliably excludes residual viable disease
- The high NPV is the primary justification for omitting planned ND in PET-negative patients
- Positive predictive value (PPV) is lower (50-60%) due to post-treatment inflammation, necessitating confirmatory biopsy or repeat imaging for positive scans

## Indications for Planned Neck Dissection Post-CRT

Despite the shift towards PET-CT surveillance, certain clinical scenarios still warrant planned ND:

### Absolute Indications

1. **Biopsy-proven residual disease** — core needle biopsy or FNA confirming viable tumour
2. **Progressive disease on imaging** — enlarging nodes or new nodal disease
3. **PET-CT showing incomplete metabolic response** — Hopkins score 4-5, persistent focal FDG avidity above background

### Relative Indications

4. **Initial N3 disease (>6 cm)** — higher residual disease rates (30-40%), though PET-CT can still guide management
5. **Residual mass >1 cm** on CT/MRI with equivocal PET findings
6. **Matted or fixed nodes at presentation** — higher risk of extranodal extension and residual disease
7. **Equivocal PET-CT** (Hopkins score 3) — consider repeat scan at 4-6 weeks or ultrasound-guided FNA
8. **Primary site persistence or recurrence** — concurrent neck dissection recommended

### Situations Where ND May Be Safely Omitted

- Complete metabolic response on PET-CT (Hopkins 1-2)
- N1-N2a disease with complete clinical and radiological response
- HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer with complete response (pCR rates >80%)

## Surgical Considerations for Post-CRT Neck Dissection

### Timing of Surgery

- **10-12 weeks post-CRT** is the optimal window
- Earlier surgery (<8 weeks): tissue oedema, poor wound healing, difficult dissection planes
- Later surgery (>16 weeks): progressive fibrosis makes dissection technically more challenging
- Goguen et al. demonstrated that ND performed at 12 weeks or later does not adversely affect complication rates or survival outcomes

### Extent of Dissection

#### Selective Neck Dissection (SND)

- **Preferred approach** for residual disease limited to identifiable levels
- CT-guided selective and superselective ND feasible (Goguen et al. 2010)
- Dissection confined to levels harbouring pre-treatment disease and levels with residual abnormalities
- Adequate for most post-CRT scenarios with limited residual disease

#### Modified Radical Neck Dissection (MRND)

- Reserved for:
  - Bulky residual disease involving multiple levels
  - Extranodal extension on imaging
  - Disease encasing or abutting the internal jugular vein or sternocleidomastoid muscle
  - Failure to identify clear tissue planes intraoperatively

#### Superselective Neck Dissection

- Emerging concept: dissection of only the involved level(s) based on post-CRT imaging
- Supported by data showing negative computed tomography accurately predicts pathologic complete response at uninvolved levels
- Reduces morbidity while maintaining oncologic adequacy

### Surgical Planes and Technical Challenges Post-RT

Post-CRT surgical fields present unique challenges:

- **Fibrosis**: loss of normal fascial planes, difficulty identifying neurovascular structures
- **Carotid adherence**: tumour or fibrotic tissue may be densely adherent to the carotid sheath; careful subadventitial dissection required
- **Venous fragility**: internal jugular vein walls may be friable, increasing risk of haemorrhage
- **Skin flap viability**: irradiated skin has impaired vascularity; wider flaps, gentle handling, and avoidance of trifurcation point tension are essential
- **Cranial nerve XI identification**: fibrosis makes nerve identification difficult; intraoperative nerve monitoring may be beneficial

### Complication Rates Post-CRT Neck Dissection

| Complication | Incidence | Prevention/Management |
|---|---|---|
| Wound dehiscence/breakdown | 15-25% | Tension-free closure, vascularised tissue coverage |
| Chyle leak | 2-8% | Meticulous ligation of thoracic duct tributaries (Level IV) |
| Cranial nerve XI injury | 5-12% | Careful identification, nerve monitoring |
| Carotid exposure/blowout | 2-5% | Pectoralis major flap coverage if carotid exposed |
| Marginal mandibular nerve injury | 3-8% | Identify and preserve; fibrosis increases risk |
| Haematoma/seroma | 5-10% | Adequate drainage, meticulous haemostasis |
| Pharyngocutaneous fistula | 3-5% | Avoid if concurrent pharyngeal surgery |

- Overall complication rate: **25-40%** (higher than for ND without prior CRT)
- Radical ND after CRT carries significantly higher complications than selective ND (Goguen et al. 2010)

## Pathological Findings in Planned Neck Dissection Specimens

### Viable Tumour Rates

- **20-30%** of planned ND specimens contain viable tumour cells
- This varies significantly by initial N stage:
  - N1: 10-15% viable tumour
  - N2a-b: 15-25% viable tumour
  - N2c: 25-35% viable tumour
  - N3: 30-45% viable tumour
- **Treatment effect** is commonly seen: extensive fibrosis, necrosis, giant cell reaction, and xanthogranulomatous inflammation surrounding residual tumour nests

### Correlation with Initial N Stage and Response

- Higher initial N stage predicts higher residual disease rates
- Complete clinical response does NOT always equate to pathological complete response
  - 8-15% of clinical complete responders harbour occult viable disease
- Conversely, residual CT abnormalities do NOT always indicate viable tumour
  - Up to 70% of persistent radiological abnormalities represent fibrosis or necrosis without viable tumour

### Histopathological Patterns

- **Complete response**: fibrosis, foreign body giant cells, no viable tumour cells
- **Microscopic residual**: scattered tumour nests within extensive treatment effect
- **Macroscopic residual**: grossly identifiable tumour deposits, often with necrotic centres
- **Extranodal extension**: may persist even in partial responders; important prognostic factor

## Surveillance Protocols Post-CRT

### For Patients Under Observation (No ND)

#### Year 1-2

- **Clinical examination**: every 4-6 weeks for the first year, every 6-8 weeks in year 2
- **PET-CT**: baseline at 12 weeks; repeat only if clinical concern
- **CT or MRI neck**: at 3-4 months and 12 months post-CRT; further as indicated
- **Ultrasound with FNA**: for any clinically or radiologically suspicious node

#### Year 3-5

- **Clinical examination**: every 3-4 months
- **Imaging**: CT or MRI annually or as clinically indicated
- **Thyroid function tests**: annually (post-radiation hypothyroidism occurs in 20-50%)

#### Beyond 5 Years

- Annual clinical examination
- Imaging only if clinically indicated
- Continued attention to late radiation effects (xerostomia, fibrosis, carotid stenosis, second primary)

### Ultrasound-Guided FNA for Suspicious Nodes

- **Indications**: new or enlarging node on examination or imaging, equivocal PET findings
- **Technique**: real-time ultrasound guidance, 22-25 gauge needle, multiple passes
- **Sensitivity**: 85-95% when performed by experienced operator
- **Role**: avoids unnecessary ND for equivocal findings; confirms disease before committing to salvage surgery

## Special Scenarios

### HPV-Positive Oropharyngeal Cancer

- **Excellent response to CRT**: pCR rates of 80-90% in HPV+ OPC
- **Lower ND rates**: PET-CT surveillance is particularly effective; fewer patients require salvage ND
- **Cystic nodal metastases**: may persist radiologically as fluid-filled structures even after tumour sterilisation; PET-CT helps distinguish viable disease from sterile cysts
- **De-escalation context**: ongoing trials (ECOG-ACRIN E3311, PATHOS) exploring reduced-intensity treatment; post-CRT neck management follows the same PET-CT surveillance paradigm
- **Caution**: despite excellent prognosis, HPV+ patients should still undergo standard surveillance protocols; late recurrences (>3 years) can occur

### Residual Cystic Nodes

- Post-CRT cystic nodes pose a diagnostic challenge
- PET-CT may show low FDG avidity even with viable tumour within cyst walls
- **Management options**:
  - Serial imaging (ultrasound or MRI) to assess size trajectory
  - Ultrasound-guided FNA of cyst wall/solid component
  - Excisional biopsy if concern persists
  - ND if biopsy confirms viable disease
- Stable or shrinking cystic nodes without FDG avidity can be safely observed

### N1 Disease

- Patients presenting with N1 disease have the highest pCR rates (85-90%)
- PET-CT surveillance is strongly favoured over planned ND
- Regional recurrence rate in observed N1 complete responders is <3%
- Planned ND adds morbidity without meaningful survival benefit in this group

### N3 Disease

- Represents the highest-risk category for residual disease (30-45%)
- PET-CT surveillance is still appropriate if complete metabolic response is achieved
- However, **lower threshold for ND** should be maintained:
  - Equivocal PET findings should prompt biopsy or ND rather than continued observation
  - Residual mass >1 cm warrants further investigation even if PET is equivocal
- Some centres advocate planned ND for all N3 disease regardless of PET response, though evidence increasingly supports PET-guided management even in this subgroup

### Salvage Neck Dissection for Recurrence

- **Timing**: as soon as recurrence is confirmed
- **Extent**: comprehensive ND (levels I-V) with inclusion of involved structures
- **Outcomes**: 5-year OS after salvage ND is 30-50%, depending on extent of recurrence
- **Complications**: higher than primary post-CRT ND due to double-treated tissues
- Consider concurrent free flap reconstruction if extensive resection anticipated

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - **The 12-week rule**: PET-CT performed at 12 weeks post-CRT has the highest accuracy (NPV >95%); earlier scanning increases false-positive rates and may lead to unnecessary neck dissections
> - **PET-NECK changed practice**: this trial provides Level I evidence that PET-CT surveillance is non-inferior to planned ND, with 80% fewer operations in the surveillance arm and significant cost savings
> - **NPV trumps PPV**: the strength of PET-CT lies in its negative predictive value (>95%); a negative scan reliably excludes disease, but a positive scan requires confirmation (PPV only 50-60%)
> - **N stage matters**: pCR rates range from 85-90% (N1) to 55-60% (N3); the threshold for ND should be lower with increasing initial N stage
> - **HPV+ OPC is different**: these patients have the highest pCR rates (80-90%) and the lowest rates of planned ND required; PET-CT surveillance is particularly effective
> - **Selective ND is sufficient**: when post-CRT ND is indicated, selective or superselective ND based on CT-guided mapping is oncologically adequate and reduces morbidity compared with comprehensive ND
> - **Beware residual cystic nodes**: in HPV+ OPC, persistent cystic nodes may represent sterile cysts rather than viable disease; serial imaging and targeted FNA help differentiate
> - **Carotid protection**: always plan for pectoralis major or free flap coverage if the carotid artery is expected to be exposed during post-CRT ND
> - **Complications are higher post-CRT**: expect wound complications in 15-25%, chyle leak in 2-8%, and CN XI injury in 5-12%; counsel patients accordingly and use meticulous surgical technique
> - **Surveillance is not passive**: PET-CT-guided observation requires a structured follow-up protocol with clinical examination every 4-6 weeks in year 1, imaging at defined intervals, and a low threshold for ultrasound-guided FNA of suspicious findings
