## Introduction

The integration of systemic chemotherapy with radiation therapy (chemoradiation, CRT) represents one of the most significant advances in the management of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC). CRT has become the standard of care for locally advanced, unresectable disease; for organ preservation in laryngeal and hypopharyngeal cancers; and as adjuvant therapy in high-risk resected tumours. This answer covers the biological rationale, evidence base, specific regimens, site-specific applications (particularly hypopharyngeal cancer), toxicity, and emerging approaches.

---

## Rationale for Concurrent Chemoradiation

### Biological Basis of Radiosensitization

The therapeutic advantage of concurrent CRT derives from **spatial cooperation** (chemotherapy treats micrometastases beyond the radiation field) and **radiosensitization** (chemotherapy enhances radiation-induced tumour cell kill within the field). The mechanisms of radiosensitization are agent-specific:

**Platinum compounds (cisplatin, carboplatin):**
- Formation of DNA interstrand and intrastrand crosslinks (platinum-DNA adducts)
- Inhibition of DNA damage repair pathways (particularly nucleotide excision repair and homologous recombination)
- Cell cycle redistribution — accumulation of cells in the radiosensitive G2/M phase
- Generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) that amplify radiation-induced oxidative damage
- Cisplatin adducts act as "radiation damage fixers," converting sublethal radiation damage into lethal lesions

**5-Fluorouracil (5-FU):**
- S-phase-specific cytotoxicity via inhibition of thymidylate synthase
- Radiosensitization through depletion of intracellular thymidine pools
- Incorporation into RNA causing impaired RNA processing
- Maximal effect when 5-FU exposure coincides with radiation (continuous infusion preferred)

**Cetuximab (anti-EGFR monoclonal antibody):**
- Blockade of epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) signalling
- Inhibition of radiation-induced accelerated repopulation (EGFR activation is a key driver of repopulation after radiation)
- Impairment of DNA double-strand break repair via downregulation of DNA-PKcs
- Cell cycle arrest in G1 phase
- Inhibition of tumour angiogenesis through reduced VEGF expression

**Taxanes (docetaxel, paclitaxel):**
- Stabilization of microtubules causing mitotic arrest (G2/M block)
- G2/M is the most radiosensitive phase of the cell cycle
- Primarily used in induction rather than concurrent settings due to mucosal toxicity

### Models of Chemotherapy-Radiation Interaction

| Model | Mechanism | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Spatial cooperation** | Chemo treats distant micrometastases; RT controls local disease | Reduces distant failure rate |
| **Radiosensitization** | Chemo enhances radiation-induced DNA damage | Improved local control |
| **Inhibition of repair** | Platinum adducts block sublethal damage repair | Converts SLD to lethal damage |
| **Cell cycle synchronization** | Agents arrest cells in radiosensitive phases | Increased cell kill per fraction |
| **Reoxygenation enhancement** | Chemo-induced tumour shrinkage improves oxygenation | Overcomes hypoxia-mediated radioresistance |
| **Inhibition of repopulation** | Cetuximab/cisplatin suppress accelerated repopulation | Counters tumour proliferation during RT |

## MACH-NC Meta-Analysis: The Definitive Evidence

The **Meta-Analysis of Chemotherapy in Head and Neck Cancer (MACH-NC)** is the largest and most important body of evidence supporting CRT in HNSCC. The 2009 update (Pignon et al.) analysed individual patient data from 93 randomised trials including 17,346 patients.

### Key Findings

| Parameter | Result |
|---|---|
| Overall HR for death (all chemo timing) | 0.88 (p < 0.0001) |
| Absolute OS benefit at 5 years (all chemo) | **4.5%** |
| **Concurrent CRT** — HR for death | **0.81** (p < 0.0001) |
| **Concurrent CRT** — absolute OS benefit at 5 years | **6.5%** |
| Induction chemotherapy — HR for death | 0.96 (p = 0.18) |
| Induction chemotherapy — absolute OS benefit | 2.4% (not significant) |
| Adjuvant chemotherapy — HR for death | 1.06 (not significant) |
| Adjuvant chemotherapy — absolute OS benefit | None |

### Critical Subgroup Analyses

- **Age interaction:** Benefit of concurrent CRT diminishes with age; **no significant benefit in patients >70 years** (HR approaches 1.0). This is a frequently tested exam point.
- **Timing:** The survival benefit is confined almost entirely to **concurrent** administration. Neither induction nor adjuvant chemotherapy alone provides a statistically significant overall survival benefit.
- **Platinum-based regimens:** Concurrent platinum-based CRT provides the greatest benefit (HR 0.78 for cisplatin-based regimens).
- **PF concurrent:** Concurrent cisplatin + 5-FU did not outperform single-agent cisplatin in concurrent settings.
- **Mono vs polychemotherapy:** No clear advantage for polychemotherapy over single-agent cisplatin in the concurrent setting.

## Concurrent CRT Regimens

### High-Dose Cisplatin (Standard of Care)

- **Regimen:** Cisplatin 100 mg/m2 IV on days 1, 22, and 43 of radiation
- **Radiation:** 70 Gy in 35 fractions over 7 weeks (conventional fractionation)
- **Evidence:** Intergroup 0126 (Adelstein 2003); RTOG 91-11 (Forastiere 2003); MACH-NC meta-analysis
- **Target:** Minimum 2 of 3 planned cycles (cumulative dose >=200 mg/m2) for optimal benefit
- **Compliance:** Only 50-70% of patients complete all 3 cycles due to toxicity
- **Remains the most evidence-supported concurrent regimen**

### Weekly Cisplatin

- **Regimen:** Cisplatin 40 mg/m2 IV weekly x 6-7 doses during radiation
- **Rationale:** Potentially better tolerated with less nephrotoxicity and emesis; easier outpatient administration
- **Evidence:** No large phase III trial directly comparing weekly vs 3-weekly cisplatin; multiple retrospective series and phase II data
- **Meta-analyses:** Szturz et al. (2017) systematic review/meta-analysis of 59 studies suggested non-inferior outcomes for weekly cisplatin; however, the data is heterogeneous and largely non-randomised
- **Cumulative dose target:** 200-240 mg/m2 over the course of RT
- **Status:** Widely used in practice despite lower level of evidence; increasingly accepted as an alternative, particularly in patients with borderline fitness for 3-weekly dosing

### Weekly vs 3-Weekly Cisplatin — The Ongoing Debate

| Parameter | 3-Weekly (100 mg/m2 x 3) | Weekly (40 mg/m2 x 6-7) |
|---|---|---|
| Level of evidence | Phase III RCTs (gold standard) | Phase II, retrospective, meta-analyses |
| Cumulative dose | 200-300 mg/m2 | 200-280 mg/m2 |
| Nephrotoxicity | Higher (bolus peak levels) | Lower |
| Ototoxicity | Higher | Lower |
| Mucositis | Similar (grade 3-4: 40-60%) | Similar |
| Compliance | 50-70% complete 3 cycles | 70-85% complete >=5 doses |
| Acute hospitalization | Higher | Lower |
| NCCN recommendation | Category 1 | Category 2A (acceptable alternative) |

### Carboplatin-Based Regimens

- **Carboplatin + 5-FU:** Used in patients unfit for cisplatin (renal impairment, hearing loss, neuropathy)
- **Regimen:** Carboplatin AUC 5 + 5-FU 600 mg/m2/day CI x 4 days, cycles q3 weeks x 3
- **Evidence:** GORTEC trial; inferior to cisplatin-based regimens in some comparisons
- **Carboplatin + paclitaxel:** Alternative; used in some centres for recurrent/metastatic disease but not standard concurrent regimen

### Cetuximab + Radiation (Bonner Regimen)

- **Regimen:** Cetuximab 400 mg/m2 loading dose, then 250 mg/m2 weekly during RT
- **Evidence:** Bonner et al. (NEJM 2006) — median OS 49.0 vs 29.3 months; 5-year OS 45.6% vs 36.4%
- **DOI:** [10.1056/NEJMoa053422](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa053422)
- **Indication:** Primarily for patients who cannot tolerate cisplatin (age >70, renal impairment, poor PS)
- **RTOG 1016 and De-ESCALaTE:** Cetuximab-RT was **inferior** to cisplatin-RT in HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer — cisplatin remains the standard even in HPV+ disease
- **Toxicity profile:** Acneiform rash (correlates with response), infusion reactions, hypomagnesaemia; significantly less mucositis and emesis than cisplatin-CRT

## Adjuvant (Postoperative) Chemoradiation

### Indications — RTOG 9501 / EORTC 22931

Two landmark parallel trials established the role of adjuvant CRT:

**RTOG 9501 (Cooper 2004):**
- 459 patients with high-risk resected HNSCC
- RT alone (60-66 Gy) vs RT + cisplatin 100 mg/m2 x 3 cycles
- CRT improved locoregional control (HR 0.61) and DFS (HR 0.78)
- No statistically significant OS benefit in the initial report

**EORTC 22931 (Bernier 2004):**
- 334 patients with high-risk resected HNSCC
- Same design: RT alone vs RT + cisplatin
- CRT improved PFS (HR 0.75), locoregional control (HR 0.69), and **OS (HR 0.70)**
- Broader inclusion criteria than RTOG 9501

### Combined Analysis (Bernier 2005) — The Critical Paper

The combined analysis identified **only two features** that consistently benefited from adding chemotherapy to postoperative RT:

| Feature | Benefit of Adding Chemo |
|---|---|
| **Positive surgical margins** | **Yes** — significant improvement |
| **[[Answers/General/Extranodal Extension\|Extranodal extension (ENE)]]** | **Yes** — significant improvement |
| >=2 positive nodes (without ENE) | Trend only — not significant |
| [[Answers/General/Perineural Invasion\|Perineural invasion]] alone | No significant benefit |
| pT3-T4 alone | No significant benefit |
| LVI alone | No significant benefit |

### Practical Points for Adjuvant CRT

- **Absolute OS benefit:** Approximately 10% at 5 years (EORTC 22931)
- **6-week window:** Adjuvant RT must commence within **6 weeks** of surgery for optimal outcomes; delays beyond 6 weeks are associated with inferior locoregional control (Peters 1993)
- **Package time:** Total treatment time (surgery to completion of RT) should ideally be <=11 weeks
- **Dose:** 60-66 Gy in 30-33 fractions to the tumour bed; 54-60 Gy to elective nodal regions
- **Chemotherapy:** Cisplatin 100 mg/m2 on days 1, 22, 43 of RT (same as definitive CRT)

## Induction Chemotherapy

### Rationale

Induction (neoadjuvant) chemotherapy is administered before definitive locoregional treatment with the goals of:

1. Rapid tumour volume reduction (debulking)
2. Early treatment of micrometastatic disease
3. Assessment of chemosensitivity as a surrogate for radiosensitivity (selection for organ preservation)
4. Potential improvement in subsequent RT delivery by reducing target volumes

### TPF vs PF — The Landmark Trials

Two pivotal trials established TPF (docetaxel + cisplatin + 5-FU) as the standard induction regimen when induction is used:

**TAX 323 (Vermorken 2007):**
- 358 patients with unresectable locally advanced HNSCC
- TPF vs PF followed by RT
- TPF improved median OS (18.8 vs 14.5 months, HR 0.73)
- TPF improved PFS (11.0 vs 8.2 months)

**TAX 324 (Posner 2007):**
- 501 patients with locally advanced HNSCC
- TPF vs PF followed by CRT with weekly carboplatin
- TPF improved median OS (71 vs 30 months, HR 0.70)
- TPF improved 3-year OS (62% vs 48%)

**Blanchard MACH-NC IPD Meta-Analysis (2013):**
- Pooled analysis of 5 TPF vs PF trials (1,772 patients)
- TPF significantly improved OS (HR 0.79, p = 0.001)
- Absolute OS benefit of 7.4% at 5 years for TPF over PF
- More febrile neutropenia with TPF (requires G-CSF prophylaxis)

### TPF Regimen (Standard)

| Agent | Dose | Schedule |
|---|---|---|
| Docetaxel | 75 mg/m2 IV day 1 | q3 weeks x 3-4 cycles |
| Cisplatin | 75-100 mg/m2 IV day 1 | q3 weeks x 3-4 cycles |
| 5-Fluorouracil | 750-1000 mg/m2/day CI days 1-4 (or 1-5) | q3 weeks x 3-4 cycles |
| G-CSF | Mandatory prophylaxis | Days 5-15 |

### Sequential vs Concurrent Chemotherapy

A key unresolved question is whether induction chemotherapy followed by CRT (sequential approach) is superior to CRT alone. The key trials addressing this:

- **DeCIDE trial (Cohen 2014):** Induction TPF followed by CRT vs CRT alone in N2/N3 HNSCC — no OS benefit for induction
- **PARADIGM trial (Haddad 2013):** Induction TPF followed by CRT vs CRT alone — no OS benefit; trial closed early due to poor accrual
- **Conclusion:** Adding induction TPF before CRT has **not been shown** to improve OS over CRT alone in unselected populations. CRT alone remains the standard. Induction chemotherapy retains a role primarily in **organ preservation** protocols.

### Role in Organ Preservation

Induction chemotherapy retains its strongest role in organ preservation for laryngeal and hypopharyngeal cancers:

- **Response to induction chemotherapy** serves as a biomarker for radiosensitivity
- Complete responders proceed to definitive RT or CRT (organ preservation)
- Non-responders are triaged to surgery (total laryngectomy/pharyngolaryngectomy)
- This "selection" approach avoids futile organ preservation in chemo-resistant tumours

## Hypopharyngeal Cancer: Concurrent CRT and Organ Preservation

### Overview

Hypopharyngeal SCC is among the most aggressive head and neck subsites, characterised by:

- Late presentation (80% present as stage III-IV)
- High rates of submucosal spread and skip lesions
- Bilateral and retropharyngeal nodal metastases
- High distant metastasis rate (15-30%)
- Poor overall prognosis (5-year OS: 25-40% for advanced disease)
- Traditional treatment: pharyngolaryngectomy + adjuvant RT — devastating functional impact

### EORTC 24891 — The Foundation Trial

The EORTC 24891 trial (Lefebvre et al.) was the first major RCT to establish the organ preservation paradigm in hypopharyngeal cancer:

- **Design:** 202 patients with resectable hypopharyngeal SCC randomised to:
  - **Arm A:** Immediate surgery (pharyngolaryngectomy) + postoperative RT
  - **Arm B:** Induction PF (cisplatin 100 mg/m2 + 5-FU 1000 mg/m2/day x 5 days) x 2-3 cycles; complete responders received RT; non-responders underwent surgery + RT
- **Results (10-year update, 2012):**
  - No significant difference in OS (13.8% vs 13.1% at 10 years)
  - Larynx preservation achieved in **35% of survivors** at 10 years
  - Retains functional larynx without compromising survival
  - Distant metastasis rate was lower in the induction arm (25% vs 36%)
- **DOI:** [10.1093/annonc/mds065](https://doi.org/10.1093/annonc/mds065)
- **Conclusion:** Induction chemotherapy-based organ preservation is a valid alternative to upfront surgery in hypopharyngeal cancer

### GORTEC 2000-01 — TPF Induction for Larynx/Hypopharynx Preservation

- Randomised 220 patients with laryngeal/hypopharyngeal cancer to induction TPF vs PF
- Responders received definitive RT; non-responders underwent surgery
- **5-year larynx preservation:** TPF 60% vs PF 39% (p < 0.01)
- **10-year larynx preservation:** TPF 70.3% vs PF 46.5%
- No significant difference in OS between arms
- **Established TPF as the preferred induction regimen for organ preservation**

### RTOG 91-11 — Concurrent CRT as the Optimal Organ Preservation Strategy

Although primarily a laryngeal cancer trial, RTOG 91-11 (Forastiere 2003) has profoundly influenced hypopharyngeal cancer management:

- **Design:** 547 patients with stage III-IV glottic/supraglottic laryngeal cancer randomised to:
  - Arm 1: Induction PF x 3 followed by RT (70 Gy)
  - Arm 2: Concurrent cisplatin (100 mg/m2 x 3) + RT (70 Gy)
  - Arm 3: RT alone (70 Gy)
- **Results:**
  - Larynx preservation rate: Concurrent CRT **88%** vs Induction PF+RT 75% vs RT alone 70% (p < 0.001 for concurrent vs RT)
  - 10-year update: Laryngectomy-free survival similar between concurrent CRT and induction PF+RT
  - OS: No significant difference between all three arms (unexplained increase in non-cancer deaths in concurrent CRT arm on long-term follow-up)
  - Locoregional control: Concurrent CRT superior

### Current Approach to Hypopharyngeal Cancer CRT

**For organ preservation candidates (T2-T3, selected T4a without cartilage destruction):**

1. **Option 1 — Induction chemotherapy approach (selection strategy):**
   - TPF x 3 cycles
   - Assess response: >=50% response (clinical + imaging) → definitive RT or CRT
   - Non-responders → salvage pharyngolaryngectomy + adjuvant RT
   - Advantage: avoids futile organ preservation in non-responders

2. **Option 2 — Definitive concurrent CRT (direct approach):**
   - Cisplatin 100 mg/m2 q3 weeks x 3 + RT 70 Gy
   - Requires close follow-up for salvage surgery in non-responders
   - Higher larynx preservation rates (RTOG 91-11 extrapolation)
   - Risk: late toxicity and potential for non-salvageable recurrence

3. **Option 3 — Sequential approach:**
   - Induction TPF x 3 → responders receive concurrent CRT (cisplatin or cetuximab + RT)
   - Combines tumour selection with radiosensitization
   - TREMPLIN (GORTEC) trial explored cisplatin vs cetuximab concurrent with RT after induction TPF

**For non-organ-preservation candidates (T4b, extensive cartilage destruction, poor function):**
- Primary pharyngolaryngectomy + bilateral neck dissection + adjuvant RT/CRT
- Reconstruction with jejunal free flap, ALT flap, or gastric pull-up

### Surgical Salvage after CRT in Hypopharynx

- Salvage pharyngolaryngectomy is feasible but carries high complication rates (pharyngocutaneous fistula 30-60%, wound dehiscence)
- Salvage surgery should be considered early when recurrence is detected
- [[Answers/General/PET-CT in Head and Neck Cancer|PET-CT]] at 12 weeks post-CRT is the standard for response assessment
- Negative PET-CT has high negative predictive value (>95%) — observation
- Positive or equivocal PET-CT — examination under anaesthesia + biopsy + consideration of salvage surgery

## Toxicity of Concurrent Chemoradiation

### Acute Toxicity

| Toxicity | Incidence (Grade 3-4) | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Mucositis** | **40-60%** | Direct mucosal damage; most dose-limiting toxicity |
| Dysphagia | 30-50% | Mucositis + oedema |
| Dermatitis | 20-30% | Radiation effect amplified by chemotherapy |
| Nausea/vomiting | 15-25% | Cisplatin-induced (CTZ stimulation) |
| **Nephrotoxicity** | 5-10% | Cisplatin tubular damage; requires aggressive hydration |
| Myelosuppression | 10-20% | Chemotherapy-induced; neutropenia most common |
| Feeding tube dependence | 40-70% | Prophylactic PEG vs reactive NG tube — varies by centre |

### Late Toxicity

| Toxicity | Incidence | Clinical Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Xerostomia** | 30-60% (with IMRT) | Reduced with parotid-sparing [[Answers/Radiotherapy and Chemotherapy/IMRT and IGRT in Head and Neck Cancer\|IMRT]] |
| **Late dysphagia** | 10-25% | Pharyngeal constrictor fibrosis; aspiration risk |
| **Ototoxicity** | 20-40% (high-frequency) | Cisplatin-induced; irreversible sensorineural hearing loss |
| Hypothyroidism | 20-50% | Thyroid gland in radiation field |
| Osteoradionecrosis | 5-15% (mandible) | Especially with concurrent chemo |
| Carotid stenosis | 15-30% at 5 years | Accelerated atherosclerosis; stroke risk |
| Fibrosis/trismus | 10-20% | Masticator muscle/TMJ fibrosis |
| Second primary | 3-5%/year | Field cancerization; not caused by CRT |

### Management of Key Toxicities

**Mucositis management:**
- Oral hygiene protocol (benzydamine, sodium bicarbonate rinses)
- Pain management (WHO ladder; topical anaesthetics; patient-controlled analgesia)
- Nutritional support (enteral feeding when oral intake <60% of requirements)
- Treatment breaks should be avoided — each week of prolongation reduces local control by ~1.4%

**Nephroprotection with cisplatin:**
- Pre-hydration: 1-2L normal saline with magnesium and potassium supplementation
- Post-hydration: 1L normal saline over 2-4 hours
- Monitoring: creatinine clearance before each cycle; hold cisplatin if GFR <50 mL/min
- Mannitol-induced diuresis in some protocols

## Emerging Agents and Future Directions

### Immunotherapy Combinations

**KEYNOTE-412 (Pembrolizumab + CRT):**
- Phase III trial: pembrolizumab + CRT vs placebo + CRT in locally advanced HNSCC
- Pembrolizumab given concurrently and as maintenance (up to 17 cycles)
- **Primary result: Did NOT meet primary endpoint** — no significant improvement in event-free survival
- DOI: [10.1016/S1470-2045(24)00100-1](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(24)00100-1)
- Interpretation: Adding checkpoint inhibitors to CRT in unselected LA-HNSCC does not improve outcomes

**Nivolumab adjuvant (CheckMate 577 analogy):**
- JAVELIN Head and Neck 100: Avelumab + CRT — negative trial
- Multiple ongoing trials exploring immunotherapy in the adjuvant setting after definitive CRT
- No current standard role for immunotherapy with definitive CRT

**KEYNOTE-048 (Recurrent/Metastatic Setting — Indirect Relevance):**
- Pembrolizumab ± chemotherapy is the standard first-line for recurrent/metastatic HNSCC
- CPS >=20: pembrolizumab monotherapy
- CPS >=1: pembrolizumab + chemotherapy
- These results have NOT translated to the curative CRT setting

### De-escalation Strategies in HPV-Positive Disease

- HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer has superior prognosis
- Trials exploring reduced radiation dose (60 Gy vs 70 Gy) and/or replacement of cisplatin with cetuximab
- **RTOG 1016 and De-ESCALaTE:** Cetuximab-RT inferior to cisplatin-RT — de-escalation by agent substitution failed
- **NRG HN002:** Reduced-dose RT (60 Gy) with concurrent cisplatin — promising results
- **ECOG 3311:** Transoral surgery + risk-adapted adjuvant therapy for HPV+ OPC

### Proton Therapy

- Potential to reduce late toxicity through superior dose conformality
- Ongoing trials comparing proton vs photon CRT (NCT01893307)
- Not yet standard of care for routine HNSCC

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
>
> 1. **MACH-NC bottom line:** Only **concurrent** CRT provides a significant OS benefit (6.5% absolute at 5 years); neither induction nor adjuvant chemotherapy alone improves OS significantly.
>
> 2. **Age cutoff:** The benefit of concurrent CRT diminishes significantly in patients **>70 years** (MACH-NC subgroup analysis) — always weigh toxicity against benefit in the elderly.
>
> 3. **Cumulative cisplatin dose matters:** A cumulative dose of >=200 mg/m2 during RT is the minimum threshold associated with improved outcomes; patients completing only 1 cycle of 3-weekly cisplatin (100 mg/m2) may not derive full benefit.
>
> 4. **Adjuvant CRT indications are narrow:** Only **positive margins and extranodal extension** are validated indications for adding cisplatin to postoperative RT (Bernier 2005 combined analysis). PNI, LVI, and multiple nodes without ENE are indications for adjuvant RT alone, not CRT.
>
> 5. **6-week rule:** Adjuvant RT must start within **6 weeks of surgery**; delays are associated with inferior locoregional control. The total "package time" (surgery to completion of RT) should be <=11 weeks.
>
> 6. **Induction TPF is not a substitute for concurrent CRT:** DeCIDE and PARADIGM trials showed no OS benefit for adding induction TPF before CRT. Induction chemotherapy is primarily indicated for **organ preservation selection** (hypopharynx, larynx) and potentially for rapid symptom relief in bulky disease.
>
> 7. **Cetuximab is NOT equivalent to cisplatin:** RTOG 1016 and De-ESCALaTE conclusively demonstrated that cetuximab-RT is inferior to cisplatin-RT, even in HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer. Cetuximab-RT should be reserved only for patients truly unfit for any platinum.
>
> 8. **Hypopharynx organ preservation is valid:** EORTC 24891 demonstrated equivalent survival with induction PF-based organ preservation vs upfront surgery. GORTEC 2000-01 established TPF as superior to PF for induction in this setting.
>
> 9. **Mucositis is the dose-limiting toxicity:** Grade 3-4 mucositis occurs in 40-60% of patients receiving concurrent CRT; treatment breaks must be minimized as each week of prolongation reduces local control by approximately 1.4%.
>
> 10. **Immunotherapy has NOT improved CRT outcomes:** KEYNOTE-412 (pembrolizumab + CRT) and JAVELIN HN 100 (avelumab + CRT) both failed to meet primary endpoints. Checkpoint inhibitors do not currently have a role in the definitive CRT setting for HNSCC.
