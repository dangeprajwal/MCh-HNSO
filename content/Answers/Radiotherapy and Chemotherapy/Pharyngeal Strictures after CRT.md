> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Caudell et al. (2010) published a dosimetric analysis from the University of Alabama demonstrating that mean radiation dose to the superior pharyngeal constrictor (SPC) was the strongest dosimetric predictor of severe dysphagia after IMRT for oropharyngeal cancer, with a mean SPC dose >55 Gy associated with significantly higher rates of aspiration and PEG dependency. This work defined the pharyngeal constrictors as critical dysphagia-risk structures and prompted the development of PARSPORT and subsequent dysphagia-optimised (DO-IMRT) protocols.
>
> Nutting et al. (2011) published the PARSPORT phase III randomised controlled trial in the Lancet Oncology, randomising 94 patients with pharyngeal carcinoma to standard parotid-sparing IMRT versus pharyngeal constrictor-sparing IMRT. Dysphagia-optimised IMRT significantly reduced the incidence of grade 2+ dysphagia at 12 months (39% vs 74%; p = 0.006) and reduced PEG dependency, without compromising locoregional control. This trial established pharyngeal constrictor sparing as a clinically meaningful objective in head and neck IMRT planning.
>
> van der Molen et al. (2011) reported the phase II DARS (Dysphagia- and Aspiration-Related Structures) trial, demonstrating that prospective swallowing exercises beginning before CRT and continued throughout treatment significantly reduced the incidence of pharyngeal stricture and maintained superior swallowing function at 6 months compared to historical controls. This established preventive rehabilitation as standard practice at centres with high CRT volumes.

## Pharyngeal Strictures after Chemoradiotherapy

### Definition and Clinical Context

Pharyngeal stricture (also termed **post-radiation stenosis** or **pharyngooesophageal stricture**) is a fibrotic narrowing of the pharyngeal lumen resulting from radiation-induced and chemotherapy-potentiated injury to the pharyngeal mucosa, submucosa, and musculature. It is one of the most functionally disabling late complications of chemoradiotherapy (CRT) for head and neck cancer, primarily affecting patients treated for oropharyngeal, hypopharyngeal, and nasopharyngeal carcinoma.

**Incidence:** Clinically significant stricture occurs in 10–25% of patients after definitive CRT; severe stricture requiring intervention in 5–15%. Concurrent chemotherapy (particularly cisplatin-based) substantially increases risk compared to radiotherapy alone.

---

### Pathophysiology

The development of pharyngeal stricture after CRT is driven by a cascade of radiation-induced tissue injury:

**1. Acute mucosal injury (weeks 2–6 of CRT):**
- Radiation directly damages rapidly proliferating mucosal epithelial cells → mucositis, ulceration
- Chemotherapy (cisplatin, 5-FU) potentiates mucosal injury through radiosensitization
- Loss of mucosal barrier → bacterial colonisation, secondary infection, ulceration deepening

**2. Subacute fibrovascular injury (months 1–6 post-CRT):**
- Vascular endothelial injury → capillary occlusion, ischaemia
- Fibroblast activation (driven by **TGF-β1** signalling) → excessive collagen deposition
- Replacement of normal muscle and mucosal tissue with dense fibrous scar
- Loss of pharyngeal constrictor muscle contractility

**3. Chronic progressive fibrosis (months to years):**
- Ongoing TGF-β1-mediated fibrosis progresses even after radiation is complete
- Radiation-induced endarteritis → progressive ischaemia → late fibrosis
- Reduction in pharyngeal cross-sectional area → dysphagia, aspiration

**4. Key molecular drivers:**
- **TGF-β1** (transforming growth factor beta-1): master regulator of radiation fibrosis; activates fibroblasts and promotes epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition
- **CTGF** (connective tissue growth factor): downstream of TGF-β1; amplifies extracellular matrix deposition
- **Reactive oxygen species (ROS):** generated by radiation; activate NF-κB and AP-1 → pro-fibrotic cytokine production
- **PDGF** and **FGF-2:** promote fibroblast proliferation

### Risk Factors for Pharyngeal Stricture

| Risk Factor | Comment |
|---|---|
| Concurrent cisplatin-based CRT | 2–3× greater risk vs RT alone; cisplatin potentiates mucosal injury |
| High dose to pharyngeal constrictors | Mean dose to SPC >55 Gy strongly predictive (Caudell 2010) |
| Primary site (hypopharynx, base of tongue) | Structures within high-dose field |
| Advanced T stage (T3-T4) | Larger treatment volumes; higher integral dose |
| N stage (N2-N3) | Larger fields, bilateral neck RT |
| Prior neck dissection | Impairs tissue vascularity; additive fibrosis |
| Trismus from medial pterygoid irradiation | Compounds dysphagia |
| Gastrostomy during treatment (PEG dependency) | Disuse pharyngeal atrophy — PEG use itself may worsen swallowing |
| Hypothyroidism post-CRT | Impairs mucosal healing |
| Total radiation dose >60 Gy | Dose-response relationship for fibrosis |
| Accelerated fractionation | Higher late complication rates |

---

### Clinical Presentation and Classification

**Symptoms:**
- Dysphagia (initially to solids, progressing to liquids)
- Odynophagia
- Aspiration → recurrent pneumonia (silent aspiration common)
- Weight loss, malnutrition
- Requirement for feeding tube (PEG or nasogastric tube)
- Dysphonia (cricopharyngeal involvement)

**Grade (CTCAE v5.0):**
- Grade 1: Symptomatic; able to eat regular diet
- Grade 2: Symptomatic; altered diet (e.g., soft diet)
- Grade 3: Severely altered eating or swallowing; tube feeding/TPN indicated
- Grade 4: Life-threatening consequences; urgent intervention required
- Grade 5: Death

**Investigations:**
- **Modified barium swallow (MBS) / videofluoroscopy** — functional assessment; identifies aspiration, timing of swallow, level of stricture
- **Flexible fibreoptic endoscopic evaluation of swallowing (FEES)** — real-time laryngopharyngeal visualisation; identifies vallecular/piriform pooling, aspiration
- **CT neck with contrast** — evaluates soft tissue planes, identifies stricture location and tumour recurrence
- **Rigid oesophagoscopy** — diagnostic and potentially therapeutic (direct dilatation)
- **Manometry** — pharyngeal and oesophageal pressures; cricopharyngeal dysmotility

---

### Prevention Strategies

**1. DARS-Sparing / DO-IMRT (Dysphagia-Optimised IMRT):**
- Identify the pharyngeal constrictor muscles (superior, middle, inferior), cricopharyngeus, and glottis as **dysphagia-risk organs at risk (OARs)**
- Dose constraints (SPC mean dose <50 Gy; cricopharyngeus mean dose <50 Gy)
- PARSPORT trial (Nutting 2011): pharyngeal constrictor sparing reduced grade 2+ dysphagia from 74% to 39% at 12 months (p = 0.006) without compromising tumour control
- Widely adopted at high-volume centres; DARS-II and DARS-III trials ongoing

**2. Prophylactic swallowing exercises (Preventive Rehabilitation):**
- Begin exercises **before** CRT commences; continue throughout treatment and for 6–12 months post-CRT
- Exercises: tongue base retraction, effortful swallow, Mendelsohn manoeuvre, Shaker head-lift, Masako (tongue-hold) exercise
- Evidence (van der Molen 2011): preventive rehabilitation significantly reduces stricture and PEG dependency
- **Principle:** stimulates neuromuscular activity of pharyngeal muscles during treatment, preventing disuse atrophy and maintaining mucosal contact forces

**3. Avoid prophylactic PEG:**
- Prophylactic PEG (placed before CRT) is associated with worse long-term swallowing outcomes due to pharyngeal disuse — reserve for patients who develop severe mucositis

**4. Intensity-modulated radiotherapy (IMRT):**
- Superior sparing of uninvolved pharyngeal structures compared to 3D-CRT
- Standard of care at high-volume centres; reduces dysphagia rates

---

### Treatment of Established Stricture

#### Conservative Management
- Swallowing rehabilitation with speech-language therapist (SLT): thermal-tactile stimulation, effortful swallow, Mendelsohn manoeuvre
- Dietary modification (texture-modified diet, thickened fluids)
- Nutritional supplementation; nasogastric or PEG feeding for severe cases

#### Endoscopic Dilatation
The first-line interventional treatment for pharyngeal stricture:

**Types:**
- **Bougie dilatation (Savary-Gilliard bougie):** Guidewire passed through stricture under fluoroscopy; bougie dilators of increasing diameter passed over guidewire; performed under GA or conscious sedation
- **Balloon dilatation (hydrostatic/pneumatic):** Through-the-scope (TTS) balloon or fluoroscopy-guided balloon dilator; gradual insufflation to desired diameter; preferred for tight/long strictures
- **Rigid oesophagoscopy:** Direct visualisation + dilatation; allows biopsy to exclude recurrence

**Outcomes:**
- Initial success in 70–90% for short/simple strictures
- Recurrence common (>50% at 12 months) — multiple sessions often required
- Complication rate: perforation 1–5% (higher in irradiated tissue due to fragility)

**Principles:**
- Never dilate blindly through a complete obstruction
- Biopsy prior to or at first dilatation to exclude local recurrence
- Serial dilatations at 4–8 week intervals to achieve target diameter (≥12–14 mm for adequate oral diet)

#### Injection of Corticosteroids / Mitomycin C
- Intralesional triamcinolone injection at the stricture site during endoscopy: reduces fibroblast activity; extends dilatation interval
- Topical **mitomycin C** (MMC) swab application post-dilatation: anti-fibrotic; antimetabolite (cross-links DNA in fibroblasts); modest evidence in small series

#### Surgical Management
Reserved for endoscopy-refractory, complete, or complex strictures:

| Procedure | Indication |
|---|---|
| **Pharyngoplasty with local/regional flap** | Segmental stricture; mucosal defect after resection of dense scar |
| **Pectoralis major myocutaneous flap** | Partial pharyngeal wall defect after scar resection |
| **Free flap reconstruction** (radial forearm, anterolateral thigh) | Circumferential pharyngeal stricture; complete pharyngeal obstruction |
| **Jejunal free flap** | Circumferential defect; pharyngooesophageal reconstruction |
| **Total pharyngolaryngectomy + gastric pull-up** | Severe pan-pharyngeal stricture; when functional pharyngeal salvage not possible |

**Important:** Prior CRT dramatically increases surgical complication rates (fistula, wound breakdown, flap failure) due to poor tissue vascularity. Thorough pre-operative planning with assessment of donor and recipient vessel quality is essential.

### Comparison: Pharyngeal Stricture vs Cricopharyngeal Dysfunction

| Feature | Pharyngeal Stricture | Cricopharyngeal Dysfunction |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Fibrotic narrowing; TGF-β1-driven | Failure of upper oesophageal sphincter to relax (UOS achalasia or hypertonia) |
| Site | Oropharynx, hypopharynx | Cricopharyngeus muscle |
| Endoscopy | Visible scar/narrowing | Posterior bar; cervical web |
| Manometry | Reduced pharyngeal pressures | Elevated UOS pressure; failed relaxation |
| Treatment | Dilatation, surgery | Cricopharyngeal myotomy or botulinum toxin injection |
| Post-CRT | Common | Less common but can co-exist |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Pharyngeal strictures after CRT are fibrotic narrowings of the pharyngeal lumen driven by TGF-β1-mediated radiation fibrosis, potentiated by concurrent cisplatin. Key pathophysiological steps are vascular endothelial injury → ischaemia → fibroblast activation → collagen deposition replacing functional mucosal and muscular tissue. Risk is greatest with high dose to pharyngeal constrictors (mean SPC dose >55 Gy), cisplatin-based CRT, and hypopharyngeal/base-of-tongue sites. Prevention relies on DARS-sparing IMRT (constraining mean constrictor dose <50 Gy) and prophylactic swallowing exercises begun before CRT; PARSPORT trial confirmed that pharyngeal constrictor sparing halved grade 2+ dysphagia. Treatment escalates from swallowing rehabilitation through serial endoscopic balloon dilatation (±intralesional steroid/mitomycin C) to surgical reconstruction with free flaps for complete or refractory strictures.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. TGF-β1 is the master driver of radiation-induced pharyngeal fibrosis — its persistent signalling explains why strictures can progress years after RT completion.
> 2. Prophylactic swallowing exercises before CRT prevent disuse atrophy of pharyngeal constrictors — critically, they must start *before* treatment, not after stricture develops.
> 3. PARSPORT trial (Nutting 2011): pharyngeal constrictor-sparing IMRT reduced grade 2+ dysphagia from 74% to 39% at 12 months — this is the landmark trial for DO-IMRT.
> 4. Prophylactic PEG (placed pre-CRT) is associated with *worse* long-term swallowing outcomes due to pharyngeal disuse — reserve reactive (nasogastric/PEG) feeding for patients who develop severe grade 3–4 mucositis during treatment.
> 5. Balloon dilatation is preferred over bougie for tight/long radiation-induced strictures due to radial (not shear) force distribution; perforation risk in irradiated tissue is 1–5%.
> 6. Always biopsy the stricture before or at first dilatation to exclude locoregional recurrence presenting as dysphagia — a critical oncological principle.
> 7. Mitomycin C topical application post-dilatation is an anti-fibrotic adjunct (alkylates fibroblast DNA) — used in refractory strictures to extend dilatation intervals.
> 8. Circumferential pharyngeal strictures refractory to dilatation require free flap reconstruction (radial forearm or ALT for partial; jejunum or gastric pull-up for total pharyngooesophageal obliteration).
> 9. Cricopharyngeal dysfunction (failed UOS relaxation) co-exists in some post-CRT patients — manometry distinguishes it from mechanical stricture; treat with cricopharyngeal myotomy or botulinum toxin.
> 10. The most severe consequence is total pharyngeal obliteration requiring laryngopharyngectomy and gastric pull-up or free jejunal reconstruction — a procedure carrying 20–30% major complication rates in post-CRT fields.
