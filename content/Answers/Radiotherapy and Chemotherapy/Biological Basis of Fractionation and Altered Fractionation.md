> Withers HR (1975) published the foundational chapter defining the radiobiological principles underlying fractionated radiotherapy — repair of sublethal damage, reassortment through the cell cycle, repopulation of surviving cells, and reoxygenation of hypoxic tumour cells — collectively known as the **Four Rs of Radiotherapy**, which remain the conceptual framework for all fractionation schedules. Horiot JC et al. (1992) reported the EORTC 22791 randomised trial comparing hyperfractionation (80.5 Gy in 70 fractions, 1.15 Gy BID, 7 weeks) with conventional fractionation (70 Gy in 35 fractions, 7 weeks) in T2-T3 oropharyngeal carcinoma, demonstrating a significant improvement in locoregional control (59% vs 40% at 5 years, p = 0.02) without increased late toxicity, providing the first high-level evidence for the therapeutic gain of hyperfractionation. Fu KK et al. (2000) published the landmark RTOG 90-03 four-arm randomised phase III trial comparing standard fractionation, hyperfractionation, accelerated fractionation with split, and accelerated fractionation with concomitant boost in 1,073 patients with locally advanced HNSCC, demonstrating that hyperfractionation (81.6 Gy in 68 fractions BID) and concomitant boost (72 Gy in 42 fractions over 6 weeks) significantly improved locoregional control compared to standard fractionation. Overgaard J et al. (2003) reported the DAHANCA 6 and 7 randomised controlled trial comparing six fractions per week (accelerated) with five fractions per week (conventional) in 1,476 patients with HNSCC, demonstrating that reducing overall treatment time by one week significantly improved locoregional control (70% vs 60% at 5 years, p = 0.0005) with all patients except glottic cancers also receiving the hypoxic radiosensitiser nimorazole. Bourhis J et al. (2006) published the MARCH (Meta-Analysis of Radiotherapy in Carcinomas of Head and Neck) collaborative study, the first individual patient data meta-analysis of altered fractionation in 6,515 patients from 15 randomised trials, demonstrating a significant overall survival benefit of 3.4% at 5 years with altered fractionation, with the benefit being greatest for hyperfractionation (8% absolute benefit at 5 years). Lacas B et al. (2017) published the updated MARCH meta-analysis including 34 trials and 11,969 patients with individual patient data, confirming the survival benefit of altered fractionation (HR 0.92, p = 0.003) with the greatest benefit from hyperfractionation (HR 0.83), and establishing that altered fractionation RT alone was inferior to concurrent chemoradiation as standard of care.

## Introduction

Fractionated radiotherapy is the backbone of curative radiation treatment for head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC). The division of a total radiation dose into multiple small fractions, delivered over several weeks, is not arbitrary — it is grounded in fundamental radiobiological principles that maximise tumour cell kill while sparing normal tissues. Understanding these principles is essential for the head and neck surgeon, as they directly inform decisions regarding fractionation schedules, treatment duration, gap corrections, and the integration of chemotherapy.

The biological basis of fractionation rests on a set of processes that occur between fractions — the **Four Rs of Radiotherapy** — first articulated by Withers in 1975 and later expanded to five Rs with the addition of intrinsic radiosensitivity. These concepts underpin the rationale for conventional, hyperfractionated, accelerated, and hypofractionated schedules.

## The Four Rs of Radiotherapy

### 1. Repair (of Sublethal Damage)

- Between radiation fractions, cells repair **sublethal damage (SLD)** — DNA damage that is insufficient to kill the cell on its own but can become lethal if additional damage occurs before repair is complete
- This phenomenon was first demonstrated by **Elkind and Sutton (1960)** in split-dose experiments: cells irradiated with two fractions separated by a time interval showed higher survival than those receiving the same total dose in a single fraction
- **Elkind recovery** is the increase in cell survival observed when a dose is split, reflecting SLD repair during the interfraction interval
- Repair half-time (T1/2) is typically **1-2 hours** for most mammalian cells; a minimum of **6 hours** between fractions is recommended when delivering twice-daily (BID) treatment to allow adequate repair
- **Clinical significance**: Late-responding normal tissues (e.g., spinal cord, brain, kidney) have a greater capacity for SLD repair than early-responding tissues and tumours. Fractionation therefore preferentially spares late-responding tissues — this is the **fundamental rationale for fractionated radiotherapy**

### 2. Reassortment (Redistribution through the Cell Cycle)

- Radiation sensitivity varies dramatically across the cell cycle: cells in **late G2 and M phases** are most sensitive, while cells in **late S phase** are most resistant
- After a radiation fraction, the surviving population is enriched for resistant (S-phase) cells. During the interfraction interval, these cells progress through the cycle and redistribute into more sensitive phases (**self-sensitisation**)
- This redistribution or reassortment increases the effectiveness of subsequent fractions
- **Clinical significance**: Multiple fractions exploit cell-cycle reassortment to progressively sensitise the tumour population. Tumours with rapid cycling benefit most from this effect

### 3. Repopulation

- Surviving tumour cells and normal tissue stem cells proliferate (repopulate) during the course of fractionated radiotherapy
- In HNSCC, **accelerated repopulation** is a critical phenomenon: after an initial lag period, surviving tumour clonogens begin to proliferate at an accelerated rate
- The onset of accelerated repopulation is estimated at approximately **day 21-28 (Tk or "kickoff time")** after the start of radiotherapy (Withers et al., 1988)
- After the kickoff time, approximately **0.6 Gy per day** is "wasted" compensating for tumour repopulation
- **Clinical significance**: Prolongation of overall treatment time beyond the standard 7 weeks (due to treatment breaks, holidays, or toxicity) significantly reduces locoregional control. Every day of treatment prolongation is estimated to reduce local control by approximately **1.4% per day** in HNSCC (Bese et al., 2007)
- Normal mucosal tissues also repopulate during treatment — this is beneficial, as it allows mucosal recovery during and after RT

### 4. Reoxygenation

- Solid tumours contain a significant fraction of **hypoxic cells** (pO2 < 10 mmHg), which are 2.5-3 times more resistant to radiation than well-oxygenated cells (the **Oxygen Enhancement Ratio, OER = 2.5-3.0**)
- A single fraction of radiation preferentially kills the better-oxygenated cells, increasing the proportion of hypoxic cells immediately after irradiation
- Between fractions, tumour shrinkage, reduced oxygen consumption by killed cells, and vascular remodelling allow previously hypoxic cells to **reoxygenate**, becoming sensitive to the next fraction
- **Clinical significance**: Fractionation allows repeated reoxygenation cycles, progressively eliminating the hypoxic tumour cell population that would otherwise survive. This is one reason why single large fractions are less effective per unit dose than multiple smaller fractions for bulky, hypoxic tumours

## The Fifth R: Radiosensitivity

- **Intrinsic radiosensitivity** varies between different tumour types and among individual tumours of the same type
- This heterogeneity is determined by genetic factors, including DNA repair capacity, p53 status, and cell-cycle checkpoint integrity
- The concept is quantified using the **alpha/beta (α/β) ratio** derived from the linear-quadratic (LQ) model, which characterises the dose-response relationship of different tissues
- **Clinical significance**: Tumours and tissues with a high α/β ratio (e.g., HNSCC, ~10 Gy) are relatively more sensitive to changes in total dose, while tissues with a low α/β ratio (e.g., late-responding normal tissues, ~3 Gy) are more sensitive to changes in fraction size

## The Linear-Quadratic Model

The LQ model is the standard mathematical framework for understanding fractionation effects in clinical radiotherapy.

### Cell Survival Equation

The surviving fraction (SF) after a dose *d* is given by:

**SF = e^-(αd + βd²)**

- The **α component** (αd) represents single-hit, irreparable (lethal) damage — dominant at low doses
- The **β component** (βd²) represents two-hit, repairable (sublethal) damage — dominant at higher doses
- The **α/β ratio** is the dose at which the linear and quadratic components of cell killing are equal

### Alpha/Beta Ratios

| Tissue Type | α/β Ratio | Clinical Implication |
|---|---|---|
| HNSCC / acute-responding tissues | ~10 Gy | More sensitive to total dose than fraction size |
| Late-responding normal tissues (spinal cord, brain, lung, kidney) | ~3 Gy | More sensitive to fraction size than total dose |
| Prostate cancer | ~1.5 Gy | Behaves like late-responding tissue |

### Biologically Effective Dose (BED)

The BED allows comparison of different fractionation schedules:

**BED = nd [1 + d/(α/β)]**

Where:
- n = number of fractions
- d = dose per fraction
- α/β = the alpha/beta ratio of the tissue of interest

### Equivalent Dose in 2-Gy Fractions (EQD2)

To convert any schedule to its equivalent in standard 2-Gy fractions:

**EQD2 = BED / [1 + 2/(α/β)]**

**Example**: Conventional RT — 70 Gy in 35 fractions of 2 Gy:
- BED (tumour, α/β = 10) = 70 × [1 + 2/10] = **84 Gy₁₀**
- BED (late tissue, α/β = 3) = 70 × [1 + 2/3] = **116.7 Gy₃**

### Therapeutic Window

The entire rationale for altered fractionation rests on exploiting the **differential α/β ratio** between tumour (high α/β) and late-responding normal tissues (low α/β):

- **Smaller fractions** (hyperfractionation): reduce damage to late-responding tissues (low α/β) disproportionately more than to tumour (high α/β), allowing dose escalation without increased late toxicity
- **Larger fractions** (hypofractionation): increase damage to late-responding tissues more than to tumour — potentially hazardous in HNC unless total dose is reduced

## Conventional Fractionation

The **standard fractionation** regimen for curative treatment of HNSCC:

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Dose per fraction | 2 Gy |
| Fractions per week | 5 (Monday-Friday) |
| Total dose | 66-70 Gy |
| Overall treatment time | 6.5-7 weeks |
| Interfraction interval | 24 hours |

This schedule represents an empirical balance between tumour control and acceptable toxicity, refined over decades of clinical experience. It allows adequate repair of sublethal damage in late-responding normal tissues between fractions while maintaining sufficient dose intensity for tumour control.

## Altered Fractionation Schedules

### Hyperfractionation

**Definition**: Reduced dose per fraction (1.1-1.2 Gy), delivered twice daily (BID), same or slightly longer overall treatment time, **increased total dose**.

**Radiobiological rationale**:
- Exploits the α/β ratio differential: smaller fractions spare late-responding tissues (α/β ~3 Gy) more than tumour (α/β ~10 Gy)
- Allows delivery of a **higher total dose** (10-15% more) without exceeding the late-toxicity tolerance of normal tissues
- The higher total dose overcomes the radioresistance of tumour clonogens
- Does NOT address the problem of accelerated repopulation (overall time unchanged)

**Practical requirements**:
- Minimum **6-hour interfraction interval** to allow complete SLD repair
- BID treatment schedule
- Increased acute mucosal toxicity (but NOT increased late effects)

**Key trial — EORTC 22791** (Horiot 1992):
- T2-T3 oropharyngeal carcinoma
- Hyperfractionation (80.5 Gy, 1.15 Gy BID, 7 weeks) vs conventional (70 Gy, 2 Gy/day, 7 weeks)
- 5-year locoregional control: **59% vs 40%** (p = 0.02)
- No increase in late toxicity

**Key trial — RTOG 90-03** (Fu 2000):
- Hyperfractionation arm: 81.6 Gy in 68 fractions, 1.2 Gy BID, 7 weeks
- Significantly improved locoregional control vs standard fractionation (HR 0.79, p = 0.05)
- 5-year locoregional control improved by approximately 8%
- Increased acute mucositis (Grade 3-4: 46% vs 25%) but no significant increase in late effects

### Accelerated Fractionation

**Definition**: Standard or near-standard dose per fraction, same total dose, **shortened overall treatment time** (by delivering more fractions per week or multiple fractions per day).

**Radiobiological rationale**:
- Directly counters **accelerated tumour repopulation** by completing treatment before significant repopulation occurs
- Reduces the dose "wasted" on compensating for repopulating tumour clonogens
- Every day saved potentially recovers ~0.6 Gy of effective dose

**Variants**:

#### a) Pure Accelerated Fractionation
- Same fraction size and total dose, delivered over a shorter time
- Example: 6 fractions/week instead of 5

**Key trial — DAHANCA 6 and 7** (Overgaard 2003):
- 1,476 patients with HNSCC
- 6 fractions/week vs 5 fractions/week (same total dose 66-68 Gy)
- Overall treatment time reduced by ~1 week
- 5-year locoregional control: **70% vs 60%** (p = 0.0005)
- All non-glottic patients also received nimorazole (hypoxic radiosensitiser)
- Confirmed that even a one-week reduction in overall treatment time significantly improves outcomes

#### b) Accelerated Fractionation with Concomitant Boost
- Standard daily fractionation for initial phase, then a second daily fraction ("boost") added during the final 2-2.5 weeks
- Reduces overall treatment time while limiting severe acute toxicity to the boost period

**Key trial — RTOG 90-03 Concomitant Boost arm** (Fu 2000):
- 72 Gy in 42 fractions over 6 weeks
- 1.8 Gy/fraction to large field (30 fractions) + 1.5 Gy boost to cone-down field (12 fractions in last 2.5 weeks, as second daily fraction)
- Significantly improved locoregional control vs standard (trend, HR 0.82, p = 0.11 in final analysis)
- Increased acute toxicity; manageable late effects

#### c) Continuous Hyperfractionated Accelerated Radiotherapy (CHART)
- Multiple small fractions per day, 7 days per week, very short overall treatment time
- **CHART regimen**: 54 Gy in 36 fractions, 1.5 Gy TID, over 12 consecutive days
- Dische et al. (1997): randomised trial in HNSCC; trend towards improved locoregional control but lower total dose was a concern
- CHART concept demonstrated the feasibility of extreme acceleration but limited by severe acute mucositis and logistical difficulty

#### d) Accelerated Fractionation with Split
- Treatment break inserted to allow mucosal recovery
- RTOG 90-03 split-course arm: 67.2 Gy in 42 fractions, 1.6 Gy BID, with a 2-week planned rest after 38.4 Gy
- **Did NOT improve locoregional control** vs standard fractionation — the planned rest allowed tumour repopulation, negating the benefit of acceleration
- This arm demonstrated that treatment gaps are detrimental

### Hypofractionation

**Definition**: Larger dose per fraction (>2.5 Gy), fewer total fractions, shorter overall treatment time, often **reduced total dose**.

**Radiobiological considerations**:
- Large fractions disproportionately increase damage to late-responding tissues (low α/β)
- Risk of severe late toxicity (fibrosis, necrosis, vascular damage) is substantially higher
- May be acceptable when late toxicity is not dose-limiting (e.g., palliative treatment) or when very precise dose delivery minimises normal tissue exposure (SBRT/SRS)

**Applications in head and neck**:
- **Stereotactic Body Radiotherapy (SBRT)** / **Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS)**: Used for reirradiation of recurrent/second primary HNC in previously irradiated fields; highly conformal dose delivery limits high-dose exposure to surrounding tissues
- **Palliative RT**: Hypofractionated schedules (e.g., 30 Gy in 10 fractions, 20 Gy in 5 fractions, "Quad Shot" — 14 Gy in 4 fractions BID over 2 days repeated monthly) provide symptomatic relief with minimal treatment burden
- **Cautions**: Hypofractionation is generally NOT used for curative-intent treatment of HNSCC (except SBRT for select cases) due to the unacceptable risk of late normal tissue complications in the head and neck region

## Summary of Altered Fractionation Schedules

| Schedule | Dose/Fraction | Total Dose | Overall Time | Fractions/Day | Key Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional | 2 Gy | 70 Gy | 7 weeks | 1 | Standard balance |
| Hyperfractionation | 1.1-1.2 Gy | 80-82 Gy | 7 weeks | 2 (BID) | Exploit α/β differential |
| Accelerated (pure) | 2 Gy | 66-70 Gy | 5-6 weeks | 1 (6/week) | Counter repopulation |
| Concomitant boost | 1.8 + 1.5 Gy | 72 Gy | 6 weeks | 1 then 2 | Combine both strategies |
| CHART | 1.5 Gy | 54 Gy | 12 days | 3 (TID) | Extreme acceleration |
| Hypofractionation | 3-20 Gy | Variable | 1-3 weeks | 1 | Palliative / SBRT |

## Meta-Analyses: The Definitive Evidence

### MARCH Meta-Analysis (Bourhis 2006)

- Individual patient data meta-analysis: 15 trials, 6,515 patients
- Altered fractionation vs conventional fractionation (RT alone — no chemotherapy)
- **Overall survival benefit**: HR 0.92, absolute benefit **3.4% at 5 years** (p = 0.003)
- **Locoregional control benefit**: HR 0.82, absolute benefit **6.4% at 5 years**
- **Hyperfractionation subgroup**: HR 0.78, absolute benefit **8% at 5 years** — the greatest benefit
- **Accelerated fractionation** (without total dose reduction): HR 0.88, benefit ~2%
- **Accelerated fractionation with dose reduction**: No significant benefit
- **Conclusion**: Hyperfractionation produced the greatest survival benefit among altered fractionation strategies

### Updated MARCH Meta-Analysis (Lacas 2017)

- Expanded analysis: 34 trials, 11,969 patients with individual patient data
- Confirmed overall survival benefit of altered fractionation: **HR 0.92** (p = 0.003)
- Hyperfractionation: **HR 0.83** — largest magnitude of benefit
- Benefit most pronounced in **younger patients (<50 years)** and in **advanced tumours (T3-T4)**
- **Critical finding**: Altered fractionation RT alone was significantly inferior to concurrent chemoradiation (HR 1.16 for altered fractionation vs CRT)
- **Current interpretation**: For fit patients with locally advanced HNSCC, concurrent chemoradiation (70 Gy conventional fractionation + cisplatin) remains the standard of care. Hyperfractionated RT alone may be considered when chemotherapy is contraindicated

## Steel-Peckham Principles of Combined Modality Therapy

Steel and Peckham (1979) articulated the four principles governing the rational combination of two or more anticancer modalities (e.g., radiotherapy + chemotherapy):

### 1. Spatial Cooperation

- Each modality targets a **different anatomical compartment** of disease
- Example: RT treats the primary tumour and regional nodes; systemic chemotherapy treats distant micrometastases
- The two modalities do not need to interact — they simply cover different sites of disease
- **HNC application**: Adjuvant chemotherapy added to locoregional RT to address potential distant metastatic disease

### 2. Independent Toxicity

- Each modality has a **different dose-limiting toxicity profile**
- Combining them does not increase the toxicity of either agent beyond its expected single-agent level
- Example: Cisplatin (nephrotoxicity) and RT (mucositis) — different primary toxicities
- **Limitation in practice**: Concurrent CRT in HNSCC does produce overlapping toxicities (mucositis from both RT and chemotherapy), so true independence is rarely achieved

### 3. Enhancement of Tumour Response

- One modality increases the effectiveness of the other **specifically against the tumour**
- Mechanisms: radiosensitisation (e.g., cisplatin inhibits DNA repair after radiation damage), cell-cycle synchronisation, reoxygenation
- **HNC application**: Concurrent cisplatin during RT — the primary mechanism of benefit in concurrent CRT for HNSCC is radiosensitisation, not independent cytotoxic effect

### 4. Protection of Normal Tissues

- One agent selectively protects normal tissues from the toxicity of the other
- Example: Amifostine as a radioprotector to reduce xerostomia during head and neck RT
- **Limitation**: Few agents achieve selective normal-tissue protection without also protecting the tumour

### Clinical Application in HNSCC

The combination of concurrent cisplatin with RT in HNSCC exploits primarily **spatial cooperation** (chemotherapy addresses micrometastatic disease) and **enhancement of tumour response** (cisplatin radiosensitisation). The use of IMRT and proton therapy exploits the concept of **normal tissue protection** through physical rather than pharmacological means.

## Methods of RT Delivery and Technological Advances

### External Beam Radiotherapy (EBRT) Techniques

| Technique | Description | Advantage |
|---|---|---|
| 2D Conventional | Parallel-opposed lateral fields using bony landmarks | Simple; historical standard |
| 3D Conformal (3D-CRT) | CT-based planning with shaped fields | Improved dose conformity to target |
| IMRT | Modulated beam intensity across each field | Parotid sparing; reduced xerostomia |
| VMAT (RapidArc) | Continuous arc delivery with modulated dose rate | Faster treatment delivery; equivalent to IMRT |
| Proton therapy | Charged particle beam with Bragg peak | Superior dose conformity; reduced exit dose |
| SBRT/SRS | Highly focused, high-dose-per-fraction delivery | Reirradiation; small-volume recurrences |

### Brachytherapy

- **Interstitial brachytherapy**: Radioactive sources implanted directly into the tumour (e.g., Ir-192 wires for oral tongue, floor of mouth)
- Delivers high local dose with rapid dose fall-off, sparing surrounding tissues
- Used for early-stage oral cavity and oropharyngeal cancers (boost or definitive)
- HDR (high dose rate) afterloading has largely replaced LDR (low dose rate) continuous implants

### Image-Guided Radiotherapy (IGRT)

- Daily imaging (cone-beam CT, kV imaging) before each fraction to verify patient positioning
- Corrects for interfraction setup variability and anatomical changes (tumour shrinkage, weight loss)
- Enables **adaptive radiotherapy**: replanning during treatment course to account for anatomical changes

## Practical Considerations

### Gap Correction and Treatment Prolongation

- **Any unplanned gap** in radiotherapy is detrimental due to tumour repopulation
- If a gap occurs, compensation strategies include:
  - Treating on weekends to make up missed fractions
  - BID fractions on selected days (with ≥6 hours between fractions)
  - Increasing total dose (approximately 0.6 Gy per day of gap after Tk)
- **DAHANCA data**: Each day of treatment prolongation reduces local control by ~1.4%
- National guidelines (e.g., RCR UK) mandate that unplanned gaps must not extend overall treatment time beyond 7 weeks

### Consequential Late Effects

- Severe **acute** mucosal reactions (confluent mucositis) from accelerated or hyperfractionated schedules can lead to **consequential late effects** — late complications that arise as a direct consequence of severe acute injury rather than from classical late radiation injury
- Examples: mucosal telangiectasia, soft tissue necrosis, chronic dysphagia
- These must be distinguished from true late effects (which depend on fraction size, not treatment intensity)
- Consequential late effects are more common with accelerated fractionation and concurrent CRT

### Mucositis Management During Altered Fractionation

- Grade 3-4 acute mucositis occurs in 40-60% of patients receiving altered fractionation
- Management: adequate oral hygiene, benzydamine rinse, nutritional support (PEG/NG tube), pain control (topical and systemic analgesics), growth factors (palifermin — limited evidence in HNC)
- Treatment breaks for mucositis must be minimised due to the adverse effect on tumour control

### Selection of Fractionation Schedule: Current Practice

- **Standard of care for locally advanced HNSCC**: Concurrent CRT with conventional fractionation (70 Gy in 35 fractions + cisplatin 100 mg/m² q3 weekly) — based on MACH-NC meta-analysis
- **When chemotherapy is contraindicated**: Hyperfractionated RT (81.6 Gy in 68 fractions BID) or accelerated RT with concomitant boost (72 Gy in 6 weeks) — based on RTOG 90-03 and MARCH
- **Altered fractionation + concurrent chemotherapy**: Being investigated; RTOG 0129 tested accelerated fractionation with concomitant boost + cisplatin vs standard CRT — no significant difference in outcomes, suggesting concurrent CRT already captures the maximal benefit
- **Elderly or unfit patients**: Accelerated RT alone (e.g., DAHANCA-type 6 fractions/week) or cetuximab-based bioradiation may be considered

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
>
> 1. **The 4 Rs favour fractionation**: Repair, Reassortment, Repopulation, and Reoxygenation collectively explain why fractionated RT is superior to single-dose treatment — the first three primarily benefit normal tissues, while reoxygenation primarily benefits tumour kill
> 2. **α/β ratio is the key concept**: High α/β tissues (tumour, ~10 Gy) are sensitive to total dose; low α/β tissues (late normal, ~3 Gy) are sensitive to fraction size — this differential is the entire basis for hyperfractionation
> 3. **Hyperfractionation = dose escalation without increased late effects**: Smaller fractions spare late tissues, permitting a 15% higher total dose (81.6 vs 70 Gy) — the MARCH meta-analysis shows this gives the greatest survival benefit (8% at 5 years)
> 4. **Accelerated fractionation = counter repopulation**: Shortening overall treatment time by even 1 week (DAHANCA 6&7) significantly improves locoregional control; every day of treatment prolongation wastes ~0.6 Gy
> 5. **Treatment gaps are the enemy**: Unplanned breaks allow tumour repopulation — the RTOG 90-03 split-course arm proved that a planned 2-week break negated the benefit of acceleration
> 6. **6-hour rule for BID treatment**: A minimum 6-hour interfraction interval is mandatory for twice-daily fractionation to allow complete sublethal damage repair in normal tissues; shorter intervals increase late toxicity
> 7. **Concurrent CRT trumps altered fractionation alone**: The updated MARCH meta-analysis (Lacas 2017) confirmed that altered fractionation RT alone is inferior to concurrent CRT — for fit patients, CRT with conventional fractionation remains the standard
> 8. **Kickoff time (Tk) ~28 days**: Accelerated tumour repopulation begins approximately 4 weeks into treatment — this is why overall treatment time matters most for HNSCC (rapid proliferation) and less for slow-growing tumours
> 9. **Steel-Peckham spatial cooperation is the dominant principle in adjuvant chemotherapy**: The addition of systemic chemotherapy to locoregional RT primarily addresses distant micrometastatic disease; radiosensitisation is the dominant mechanism in concurrent CRT
> 10. **BED and EQD2 are essential tools**: Always calculate BED for both tumour (α/β = 10) and late tissues (α/β = 3) when comparing or modifying fractionation schedules — this prevents inadvertent overdosing of late-responding normal tissues
