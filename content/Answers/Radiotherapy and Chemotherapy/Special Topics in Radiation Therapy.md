## A. Neutron Therapy for Cancer

### Physics of Neutron Beams

Neutrons are **uncharged particles** that interact with matter through nuclear collisions rather than electromagnetic interactions. This fundamental difference from photons and electrons results in distinct radiobiological properties.

#### Linear Energy Transfer (LET)

| Radiation Type | LET (keV/micrometer) | Category |
|---|---|---|
| Photons (X-rays, gamma) | 0.2-2 | Low LET |
| Protons (Bragg peak) | 5-20 | Low-intermediate LET |
| **Neutrons** | **20-100** | **High LET** |
| Carbon ions | 50-200 | High LET |
| Alpha particles | 100-200 | High LET |

#### Relative Biological Effectiveness (RBE)

- Neutron RBE ranges from **3 to 5** (compared to 1.0 for photons and 1.1 for protons)
- This means a given physical dose of neutrons produces 3-5 times the biological damage of the equivalent photon dose
- RBE is highest at low doses per fraction and decreases at higher doses

### Radiobiological Advantages of Neutrons

| Property | Photons | Neutrons | Clinical Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oxygen enhancement ratio (OER) | 2.5-3.0 | **1.4-1.6** | Less dependence on tumour oxygenation |
| Cell cycle variation | Marked (S-phase resistant) | **Minimal** | Effective across all cell cycle phases |
| Sublethal damage repair | Significant | **Minimal** | Less repair of radiation damage between fractions |
| Dose-rate effect | Significant | **Minimal** | Effective regardless of dose rate |

The reduced OER is clinically significant because many solid tumours contain **hypoxic regions** that are resistant to conventional photon radiotherapy. Neutrons overcome this resistance through direct DNA damage (double-strand breaks) that is largely independent of oxygen-mediated free radical formation.

### Clinical Indications

#### Primary Indication: Salivary Gland Tumours

Salivary gland tumours, particularly **adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC)**, are the most established indication for neutron therapy because they are:

- Slow-growing with low proliferative rates
- Relatively radioresistant to conventional photon therapy
- Often inoperable due to proximity to critical structures (skull base, facial nerve)
- Characterised by high hypoxic fractions

#### RTOG-MRC Randomized Trial (Laramore 1993)

This landmark trial randomized patients with unresectable malignant salivary gland tumours to fast neutron versus conventional photon/electron radiotherapy.

| Parameter | Neutrons | Photons/Electrons | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Complete response rate | 67% | 33% | — |
| **10-year local-regional control** | **56%** | **17%** | **0.009** |
| 10-year overall survival | 15% | 25% | NS |
| 10-year disease-free survival | 18% | 9% | — |

Key findings:
- Dramatically superior local-regional control with neutrons
- No survival advantage (due to distant metastases and late toxicity)
- Established neutrons as the radiation modality of choice for inoperable salivary gland tumours

#### Other Potential Indications

- Locally advanced or unresectable head and neck SCC
- Melanoma (radioresistant to photons)
- Soft tissue sarcomas
- Prostate cancer (historical use, now largely replaced)

### Limitations and Toxicity

| Limitation | Details |
|---|---|
| **Late toxicity** | Higher rates of fibrosis, nerve damage, soft tissue necrosis, and bone necrosis compared to photons |
| Dose distribution | Poorer physical dose distribution than photons (no Bragg peak, broad penumbra) |
| Facility requirements | Cyclotron-based, extremely expensive infrastructure |
| Availability | Very few operational facilities worldwide (fewer than 5 active centres) |
| Beam characteristics | Difficult to shape beams precisely; cannot spare normal tissues as effectively |

### Current Status and Future

- Neutron therapy has been **largely supplanted by carbon ion therapy**, which combines the radiobiological advantages of high LET with superior dose distribution (Bragg peak)
- Carbon ion therapy provides similar radiobiological benefits (high LET, low OER, minimal cell cycle dependence) with far better normal tissue sparing
- Active carbon ion centres include NIRS (Japan), HIT (Germany), CNAO (Italy), and Shanghai Proton and Heavy Ion Center (China)
- Very few neutron therapy centres remain operational worldwide — the University of Washington facility (Seattle) closed in 2014

## B. Dental Care During and After Radiation

Radiation therapy to the head and neck causes predictable and often severe effects on oral and dental structures. A structured, phased approach to dental management is essential to minimise morbidity.

### Pathophysiology of Radiation Effects on Oral Structures

| Structure | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Salivary glands | Xerostomia | Acinar cell destruction (serous > mucous), fibrosis |
| Teeth | Radiation caries | Altered saliva (reduced flow, acidic pH, loss of mineralising capacity) |
| Mucosa | Mucositis | Epithelial stem cell depletion, mucosal atrophy |
| Bone (mandible) | Osteoradionecrosis | Hypoxic-hypocellular-hypovascular tissue (Marx 3H theory) |
| Muscles of mastication | Trismus | Radiation-induced fibrosis of masticatory muscles and TMJ |
| Taste buds | Dysgeusia | Degeneration of taste receptor cells |

### Phase 1: Pre-Radiotherapy Dental Assessment

The pre-radiotherapy dental evaluation is **critical** and must be completed with adequate time for healing before radiation begins.

#### Mandatory Assessments

1. **Comprehensive dental examination** including periodontal probing
2. **Panoramic radiograph (OPG)** — baseline assessment of dentition and mandible
3. **Periapical radiographs** of suspicious teeth
4. **Assessment of radiation field** — dentist must know planned dose distribution

#### Treatment Decisions

| Dental Status | Action | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Non-restorable teeth | **Extract** | Eliminate future extraction risk in irradiated bone |
| Severe periodontal disease (>5 mm pockets) | **Extract** | High risk of post-RT infection and ORN |
| Partially impacted third molars | **Extract** | Pericoronitis risk unacceptable after RT |
| Teeth in direct radiation field (>50 Gy) with questionable prognosis | **Extract** | Threshold for extraction should be lower |
| Restorable teeth | **Restore** | Preserve dentition where possible |
| Edentulous patients | Assess denture fit | Ill-fitting dentures cause mucosal trauma |

#### Timing of Extractions

- **Minimum 14 days** (ideally 21 days) before start of radiotherapy
- Allow complete mucosal healing before radiation commences
- Primary closure of extraction sockets recommended
- Alveoloplasty to smooth sharp bony edges

#### Fluoride Tray Fabrication

- Custom-fitted fluoride carrier trays are fabricated **before RT begins**
- Made from soft vinyl or silicone material
- Cover all remaining teeth
- Patient instructed on daily use protocol

### Phase 2: During Radiotherapy

#### Daily Fluoride Application

- **1.1% neutral sodium fluoride (NaF) gel** in custom trays
- Apply for **5 minutes daily** after brushing
- Do not eat, drink, or rinse for 30 minutes after application
- Continue without interruption throughout RT course
- Stannous fluoride (0.4%) is an alternative but may cause mucosal irritation

#### Oral Hygiene Protocol

| Component | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Tooth brushing | Soft or ultra-soft brush, 2-3 times daily |
| Toothpaste | Fluoride-containing, mild flavour, non-abrasive |
| Mouth rinses | 0.9% saline or sodium bicarbonate solution, 4-6 times daily |
| Avoid | Alcohol-based mouthwashes, tobacco, spicy/acidic foods |
| Denture use | Discontinue during RT if mucositis develops |

#### Management of Mucositis

- **WHO grading** (Grade 1-4) guides intervention
- Bland oral rinses (salt and soda solution)
- Topical anaesthetics (2% viscous lidocaine) before meals
- Mucosal protectants (sucralfate suspension)
- Adequate analgesia (systemic opioids for Grade 3-4)
- Nutritional support (PEG tube if unable to maintain >60% oral intake)

#### Candidiasis Prophylaxis and Treatment

| Agent | Regimen | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Nystatin suspension | 5 mL, swish and swallow, QID | Prophylaxis/mild disease |
| Clotrimazole troches | 10 mg, dissolved in mouth, 5 times daily | Mild-moderate disease |
| Fluconazole | 100-200 mg PO daily for 7-14 days | Moderate-severe/refractory |

#### Dietary Counselling

- Avoid refined sugars (cariogenic in xerostomic environment)
- Soft, moist, bland foods
- Frequent small meals
- Adequate hydration
- Sugar-free gum or candies to stimulate residual saliva

### Phase 3: Post-Radiotherapy (Lifelong)

#### Lifelong Fluoride Protocol

- **Daily fluoride tray application must continue indefinitely**
- This is the single most important measure to prevent radiation caries
- Non-compliance with fluoride leads to rampant caries within months
- Compliance rates are unfortunately poor (reported 30-60%)

#### Hyposalivation Management

| Strategy | Details |
|---|---|
| Saliva substitutes | Carboxymethylcellulose-based sprays, mucin-based products |
| Sialogogues | Pilocarpine 5 mg TID (contraindicated in asthma, glaucoma); cevimeline 30 mg TID |
| Acupuncture | Some evidence for stimulating residual salivary function |
| Salivary gland transfer | Surgical transfer of submandibular gland before RT (Jha technique) |
| IMRT sparing | Parotid-sparing IMRT reduces incidence and severity of xerostomia |

#### Radiation Caries

- **Characteristic pattern**: cervical, incisal edge, and cusp tip involvement (unlike typical caries)
- Rapid progression from enamel to dentin
- Can lead to complete crown amputation
- Prevention: lifelong fluoride, dietary modification, regular dental reviews (every 3-4 months)
- Restoration: glass ionomer cements preferred (fluoride release)

#### Trismus Prevention and Management

- Onset typically 3-6 months post-RT, may progress for years
- **Prevention**: begin jaw exercises during RT (TheraBite, tongue depressor stacking)
- Target: maintain inter-incisal opening >35 mm
- Active stretching exercises 3 times daily
- Pentoxifylline 400 mg TID may reduce fibrosis progression

### Dental Extractions After Radiotherapy

This is the most feared scenario due to **osteoradionecrosis (ORN)** risk.

#### Risk Stratification

| Risk Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| Dose to mandible | **>60 Gy**: high risk; 50-60 Gy: moderate risk; <50 Gy: low risk |
| Location | Posterior mandible > anterior mandible > maxilla |
| Time since RT | Risk persists indefinitely but highest in first 2 years |
| Dental status | Periodontal disease increases ORN risk |
| Concurrent factors | Smoking, alcohol, poor nutrition, bisphosphonates |

#### Protocol for Post-RT Extractions

1. **Conservative approach** preferred — endodontic treatment over extraction when possible
2. Pre-extraction antibiotics (amoxicillin/clindamycin), starting 24 hours before
3. **Atraumatic technique**: minimal flap elevation, avoid excessive bone removal
4. Primary closure of socket
5. Antibiotic continuation for 7-10 days post-extraction
6. Close follow-up for healing assessment
7. Consider **hyperbaric oxygen (HBO)** for high-risk patients (mandible, >60 Gy)

#### HBO Protocol (Marx Protocol)

| Phase | Details |
|---|---|
| Pre-extraction | 20 sessions at 2.4 ATA, 90 minutes each |
| Extraction | Performed within 2 weeks of completing pre-extraction dives |
| Post-extraction | 10 additional sessions |
| Total | 30 sessions (20 pre + 10 post) |

**Note**: The role of HBO remains controversial — the HOPON trial (Annane 2004) was stopped early, and DAHANCA-21 showed no benefit. Current evidence supports HBO primarily for established ORN, not prophylaxis.

#### PENTOCLO Protocol for Established ORN

The PENTOCLO protocol (Delanian) represents a non-surgical approach to established ORN:

| Component | Dose | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Pent**oxifylline | 800 mg/day | Vasodilator, anti-fibrotic, anti-TNF-alpha |
| To**co**pherol (Vitamin E) | 1000 IU/day | Antioxidant, anti-fibrotic |
| C**lo**dronate | 1600 mg/day (5 days/week, alternating weeks) | Anti-resorptive, promotes bone healing |

- Treatment duration: minimum 6-12 months
- Response rates: 70-90% improvement reported
- May avoid need for surgical intervention (free flap reconstruction)

## C. Radiotherapy in Verrucous Carcinoma of the Head and Neck

### Definition and Characteristics

Verrucous carcinoma (VC), first described by **Ackerman in 1948**, is a distinct variant of well-differentiated squamous cell carcinoma characterised by:

- **Exophytic, papillomatous, warty growth** pattern
- Broad, pushing (rather than infiltrating) deep margins
- Heavy keratinisation
- Minimal nuclear atypia and rare mitoses
- **Locally destructive** behaviour with bone invasion possible
- Extremely rare lymph node metastasis (<5%)
- No distant metastasis

### Common Sites in Head and Neck

| Site | Frequency | Association |
|---|---|---|
| Oral cavity (buccal mucosa, gingiva) | Most common | Smokeless tobacco, betel quid |
| Larynx (glottis) | Second most common | Smoking |
| Oropharynx | Uncommon | — |
| Sinonasal | Rare | — |

### Diagnosis

- **Adequate biopsy is critical** — superficial biopsies are insufficient
- Must include the deep pushing margin to demonstrate the characteristic architecture
- Multiple/deep biopsies required to exclude a **hybrid tumour** (VC component + conventional SCC component)
- HPV is generally NOT associated with head and neck VC

### The Anaplastic Transformation Controversy

#### Historical Concern

- **Perez et al. (1966)** first reported "anaplastic transformation" of verrucous carcinoma following radiation therapy
- Reports suggested that RT induced dedifferentiation into aggressive, poorly differentiated SCC
- This led many centres to consider RT **contraindicated** in VC
- Reported transformation rates varied widely (7-30%)

#### Modern Reassessment

The concept of anaplastic transformation has been **largely discredited** based on several observations:

1. **Hybrid tumours**: Many "transformed" cases were likely **hybrid verrucous-squamous carcinomas** from the outset, with the conventional SCC component missed on initial superficial biopsy
2. **Sampling error**: Inadequate initial biopsies failed to capture the invasive SCC component
3. **Ferlito et al. (1998)** systematic review: true anaplastic transformation rate was only **6.7%** across published series
4. **Spontaneous transformation**: VC can dedifferentiate without any radiation exposure
5. **No biological mechanism**: There is no established mechanism by which radiation would induce a specific phenotypic switch to anaplastic morphology

### Radiotherapy Outcomes

| Parameter | Results |
|---|---|
| Local control (T1-T2) | **70-90%** |
| Local control (T3-T4) | 40-60% |
| Overall local control (all stages) | 43-80% (varies by series and stage) |
| Recurrence pattern | Local recurrence; regional failure extremely rare |
| True anaplastic transformation | <7% (likely overestimated) |

### Indications for Radiotherapy in Verrucous Carcinoma

While **surgery remains the preferred treatment** when feasible, RT is appropriate in specific circumstances:

| Indication | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Significant functional deficit from surgery | Large oral cavity or laryngeal lesions where surgery would cause major morbidity |
| Patient refuses surgery | Informed consent for RT as alternative |
| Medically inoperable patient | Comorbidities preclude general anaesthesia |
| Laryngeal preservation | Glottic VC where RT can preserve voice |
| Adjuvant (controversial) | Positive or close margins after surgery |

### Radiotherapy Technique

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Dose | **60-66 Gy** in conventional fractionation (2 Gy/fraction) |
| Fractionation | Conventional (no role for altered fractionation) |
| Target volume | Primary tumour with adequate margin |
| Elective nodal RT | **Not indicated** (metastatic rate <5%) |
| Technique | IMRT or 3D-CRT depending on site |
| Boost | Consider boost to GTV if needed to achieve 66 Gy |

### Surgery for Verrucous Carcinoma

| Aspect | Details |
|---|---|
| Preferred treatment | Wide local excision with adequate margins |
| Margins | 1 cm clinical margins recommended |
| Neck dissection | Not routinely indicated |
| Recurrence after surgery | Salvage re-excision or RT |
| Prognosis | Excellent: 5-year OS >85% for all stages |

### Algorithm: Management of Head and Neck Verrucous Carcinoma

```
Verrucous Carcinoma (adequate biopsy confirmed)
|
├── Surgery feasible with acceptable morbidity?
|   ├── YES → Wide local excision (preferred)
|   |         ├── Clear margins → Observation
|   |         └── Positive margins → Consider re-excision or adjuvant RT
|   |
|   └── NO → Definitive RT (60-66 Gy)
|            ├── No elective nodal RT
|            └── Monitor for local recurrence
|
└── Always: Exclude hybrid tumour (deep biopsy)
```

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
>
> 1. **Neutron RBE of 3-5** means that a 1 Gy neutron dose produces the biological damage equivalent of 3-5 Gy of photons — dose prescriptions must account for this to avoid catastrophic late toxicity
> 2. **Neutrons overcome hypoxia resistance** because their OER (1.4-1.6) is far lower than photons (2.5-3.0) — this is the primary rationale for their use in slow-growing, hypoxic salivary gland tumours
> 3. **RTOG-MRC trial bottom line**: neutrons achieved 56% vs 17% local control in inoperable salivary gland tumours, but no survival benefit due to distant metastases — local control alone does not guarantee cure
> 4. **Pre-RT dental extractions require minimum 14 days** (ideally 21 days) healing time before radiation begins — rushing this interval invites ORN
> 5. **Daily fluoride trays are lifelong** after head and neck RT — this is the single most important intervention to prevent radiation caries, yet compliance is dismally poor (30-60%)
> 6. **Mandible ORN risk is dose-dependent**: >60 Gy carries high risk, and the posterior mandible is most vulnerable due to its end-artery blood supply from the inferior alveolar artery
> 7. **PENTOCLO protocol** (pentoxifylline + tocopherol + clodronate) is a non-surgical option for established ORN with 70-90% response rates, potentially avoiding free flap reconstruction
> 8. **Verrucous carcinoma anaplastic transformation is a myth** in most cases — the 6.7% true transformation rate reported by Ferlito likely reflects hybrid tumours missed on initial superficial biopsy
> 9. **Deep biopsy is mandatory** for verrucous carcinoma diagnosis — superficial biopsies cannot exclude a hybrid lesion with conventional SCC at the deep margin, which is the commonest diagnostic pitfall
> 10. **No elective nodal RT for verrucous carcinoma** — the metastatic rate is <5%, making prophylactic nodal treatment unnecessary and adding morbidity without benefit
