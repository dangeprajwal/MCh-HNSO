> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> The TAX 323 trial (Vermorken et al., 2007) established TPF (docetaxel + cisplatin + 5-FU) as the superior induction chemotherapy regimen over PF (cisplatin + 5-FU) in patients with unresectable locally advanced HNSCC. In 358 patients, TPF induction followed by radiotherapy significantly improved progression-free survival (median 11.0 vs 8.2 months; HR 0.72; p = 0.007) and overall survival (median 18.8 vs 14.5 months; HR 0.73; p = 0.02) versus PF, with less febrile neutropenia and less grade 3–4 mucositis, thus replacing PF as the standard induction regimen.
>
> The TAX 324 trial (Posner et al., 2007) randomised 501 patients with stage III–IV HNSCC to TPF versus PF induction chemotherapy followed by concurrent chemoradiotherapy (carboplatin + radiation). TPF significantly improved 3-year overall survival (62% vs 48%; median OS 71 vs 30 months; HR 0.70; p = 0.006) and locoregional progression-free survival, with higher rates of laryngeal preservation in laryngeal and hypopharyngeal subsets. These two contemporaneous trials — TAX 323 and TAX 324 — together constitute level I evidence for TPF as the standard induction regimen.
>
> Sun et al. (2016) published a phase III randomised trial in The Lancet Oncology evaluating induction TPF plus concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CCRT) versus CCRT alone in 480 patients with locoregionally advanced nasopharyngeal carcinoma (stage III–IVB). Induction TPF significantly improved failure-free survival (HR 0.68; p = 0.034) with 3-year FFS of 63% versus 55%, and improved OS (HR 0.69; p = 0.029), establishing induction TPF followed by CCRT as a preferred strategy for high-risk locally advanced NPC.

## Neoadjuvant and Induction Chemotherapy in HNC

### Definitions

**Neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NACT):** Any chemotherapy administered before definitive locoregional treatment (surgery or radiotherapy) with the intent of reducing tumour burden, facilitating organ preservation, or treating micrometastatic disease.

**Induction chemotherapy (ICT):** A specific form of neoadjuvant chemotherapy in which 2–4 cycles are administered first, the patient is re-staged, and then definitive treatment (surgery, radical RT, or concurrent CRT) is delivered. The term "induction" implies that the chemotherapy "induces" a response that informs subsequent management (bioselection).

### Rationale and Mechanisms

Induction chemotherapy achieves therapeutic benefit through four complementary mechanisms:

1. **In vivo chemosensitivity testing:** Tumour response to ICT predicts subsequent responsiveness to radiation (chemo-radiosensitivity); responders proceed to organ-preserving RT while non-responders undergo surgery
2. **Tumour cytoreduction:** Reduction in primary tumour volume and nodal disease may downstage resectable tumours to allow less extensive surgery, or convert unresectable disease to resectable
3. **Early treatment of micrometastatic disease:** Systemic therapy precedes locoregional treatment, treating occult distant micrometastases at the earliest opportunity, particularly relevant in high-risk T4/N3 disease
4. **Non-overlapping toxicity:** Chemotherapy and RT toxicities (mucositis, myelosuppression) are largely non-overlapping when ICT precedes RT; sequential rather than concurrent toxicity
5. **No compromise of RT:** Completing ICT before RT does not reduce radiation dose or technique; by contrast, adjuvant chemotherapy after RT may be compromised by residual RT toxicity

### Standard Induction Chemotherapy Regimens

#### TPF Regimen (Standard — Level I Evidence)
- **Docetaxel** 75 mg/m² IV, Day 1
- **Cisplatin** 75 mg/m² IV, Day 1 (with aggressive hydration 2–3 L/day)
- **5-Fluorouracil** 750 mg/m²/day continuous infusion, Days 1–5
- **Cycle:** Every 21 days × 3 cycles
- Requires G-CSF (filgrastim) prophylaxis from Day 6 (grade 3–4 neutropenia ~76–80%)
- Docetaxel premedication: dexamethasone 8 mg BD × 3 days starting 1 day before docetaxel (hypersensitivity prophylaxis)

#### PF Regimen (Historical — Inferior to TPF)
- Cisplatin 100 mg/m² Day 1 + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/day Days 1–4/5
- VA Laryngeal Trial, RTOG 91-11: used PF for larynx preservation; superseded by TPF
- Still used when docetaxel is unavailable or in cisplatin-ineligible patients with carboplatin substitution

#### Modified TPF (Some Centres)
- Docetaxel 75 + cisplatin 75 + 5-FU 1000 mg/m²/day Days 1–3 (abbreviated infusion)
- Reduces 5-FU toxicity while maintaining efficacy; used for elderly/borderline-fit patients

#### Carboplatin + Paclitaxel (Cisplatin-Ineligible)
- AUC 5–6 + paclitaxel 175 mg/m², q21d
- Used when cisplatin is contraindicated (renal impairment, hearing loss, neuropathy, poor performance status)

#### EP (Cisplatin + Etoposide)
- Used for sinonasal undifferentiated carcinoma (SNUC) and neuroendocrine tumours of the head and neck
- **Not** TPF-based; highly effective in chemosensitive undifferentiated histologies

### Evidence by Site

#### Larynx and Hypopharynx — Organ Preservation

The concept of **bioselection** using induction chemotherapy is most firmly established in laryngeal and hypopharyngeal cancers:

- **VA Laryngeal Trial (1991):** Induction PF × 3 → definitive RT vs total laryngectomy; 64% larynx preservation in ICT arm with equivalent 2-year OS; established feasibility of organ preservation
- **RTOG 91-11 (Forastiere, 2003):** Three arms — induction PF → RT vs concurrent cisplatin + RT vs RT alone; concurrent CRT superior for larynx preservation (88% vs 75% vs 70%); induction PF inferior to concurrent CRT for larynx preservation but with better swallowing function
- **GORTEC 2000-01 (Pointreau, 2009):** Induction TPF → RT vs PF → RT for larynx/hypopharynx preservation; 3-year laryngeal preservation 70.3% (TPF) vs 57.5% (PF); TPF superior

**Current standard:** For T3–T4 laryngeal/hypopharyngeal cancer where organ preservation is desired and concurrent CRT is feasible, concurrent CRT (cisplatin + RT) is preferred for larynx preservation. Induction TPF → RT is preferred for **hypopharyngeal** cancer or when concurrent CRT toxicity is prohibitive. Induction is used as a "bioselection" strategy: complete/partial responders proceed to definitive RT; non-responders undergo surgery.

#### Oral Cavity Cancer — NOT Standard

- Oral cavity SCC is **primarily a surgical disease**; neoadjuvant chemotherapy is not standard for resectable oral SCC
- **GORTEC 2000-02:** ICT + surgery vs surgery alone in oral/oropharyngeal SCC — no OS or DFS benefit; ICT delayed surgery and added toxicity
- **Role of NACT in oral cavity:**
  - Borderline resectable or unresectable oral SCC (T4b): ICT may downstage for eventual resectability
  - High-risk T4 oral SCC with skin involvement or skull base proximity: may allow less morbid surgery
  - ICT response as a surrogate marker for adjuvant chemotherapy need (investigational)
- See also: [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy in Oral Cancer|Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy in Oral Cancer]] for full oral cavity evidence

#### Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma (NPC) — ICT + CCRT

- **MAC-NPC meta-analysis:** Adjuvant chemotherapy (platinum-based) after CCRT improves OS in LA-NPC; established CCRT + adjuvant as historical standard
- **Sun et al. (2016) — Lancet Oncology:** TPF induction × 3 + CCRT vs CCRT alone; induction TPF significantly improved FFS (HR 0.68) and OS (HR 0.69)
- **Current evidence:** For locoregionally advanced NPC (T3–T4 or N2–N3), **induction TPF + CCRT** is increasingly preferred over CCRT + adjuvant; induction addresses systemic micrometastases earlier and avoids the compliance challenge of adjuvant chemotherapy post-CRT
- Gemcitabine + cisplatin (GP) induction is emerging as an alternative (Zhang et al., 2019, NEJM: GP induction + CCRT vs CCRT alone in NPC — significant improvement in EFS)

#### Oropharyngeal and Unresectable HNSCC

- Induction TPF + CCRT is a reasonable strategy for technically unresectable LA-HNSCC to maximise locoregional control
- **DeCIDE (Cohen, 2014), PARADIGM (Haddad, 2013):** Phase III trials of induction TPF + CCRT vs CCRT alone in LA-HNSCC — no OS benefit with induction; however, distant metastasis rate was lower with induction in DeCIDE
- General conclusion: **Induction TPF + CCRT is NOT superior to CCRT alone for most LA-HNSCC** (except NPC and organ preservation scenarios)

### Induction vs Adjuvant Chemotherapy in NPC

| Feature | Induction Chemotherapy | Adjuvant Chemotherapy |
|---|---|---|
| Timing | Before CCRT | After CCRT |
| Goal | Reduce tumour bulk, treat micrometastases early | Consolidate systemic therapy |
| Evidence in NPC | Sun 2016 (TPF + CCRT superior to CCRT alone) | MAC-NPC meta-analysis (benefit confirmed) |
| Compliance | Better tolerated before RT toxicity | Compromised by post-RT mucositis, fatigue |
| Current preference | Increasing (especially T4/N3, EBV+ NPC) | Historical standard; still used at many centres |

### Complications of Induction Chemotherapy (TPF)

#### Haematological Toxicity
- **Febrile neutropenia (FN):** Most feared complication; grade 3–4 neutropenia in 76–80% with TPF without G-CSF; G-CSF prophylaxis from Day 6 is mandatory
- **Thrombocytopenia:** Grade 3–4 in ~10%; risk of bleeding; platelet transfusion threshold <10,000/mm³ (or <50,000 if bleeding/surgical)
- **Anaemia:** Cumulative cisplatin-related; ESA agents or transfusion for symptomatic anaemia; check ferritin/B12/folate

#### Renal Toxicity (Cisplatin Nephrotoxicity)
- Proximal tubular injury (segments S2/S3); leads to ↓GFR, Mg²⁺/K⁺ wasting, Fanconi-like syndrome
- **Prevention:** Vigorous IV hydration (1–2 L normal saline pre- and post-cisplatin), amifostine (limited evidence), avoid nephrotoxic agents (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, IV contrast) for 72 hours
- Cisplatin dose reduction/substitution with carboplatin if creatinine clearance <50–60 mL/min

#### GI Toxicity
- **Mucositis:** Grades 3–4 in 20–27% with TPF; less than with PF; worsened if RT follows within weeks; good oral hygiene, magic mouthwash
- **Nausea/vomiting:** Cisplatin is highly emetogenic; triple antiemetic protocol mandatory:
  - 5-HT3 antagonist (ondansetron/palonosetron)
  - NK1 receptor antagonist (aprepitant/fosaprepitant)
  - Dexamethasone (note: already used for docetaxel premedication)
- **Diarrhoea:** 5-FU-induced colitis; grade 3–4 in 4–14%; loperamide first-line; severe cases require IV fluids/antibiotics for C. diff co-infection

#### Neurological Toxicity
- **Cisplatin ototoxicity:** High-frequency sensorineural hearing loss (cochlea outer hair cells); cumulative and irreversible; audiometry at baseline and after each cycle; amifostine may partially protect; dose-reduce or substitute carboplatin
- **Peripheral neuropathy (taxane-related):** Sensorimotor; cumulative; primarily from docetaxel; assess with FACT/GOG-Ntx scale; may persist after treatment completion
- **Cisplatin neurotoxicity:** Less common with modern dosing; encephalopathy rare

#### Alopecia
- Taxane-related; total alopecia common; reversible ~3 months after last cycle; scalp cooling may reduce but less practical with IV regimens

#### Infusion Reactions
- Docetaxel hypersensitivity: despite premedication, grade 3–4 reactions occur in ~2%; managed with epinephrine, antihistamines, steroid escalation
- Cisplatin hypersensitivity: usually after multiple cycles; manifests as urticaria, bronchospasm; switch to carboplatin

#### Treatment Delays
- Every delay in initiating definitive RT after completing ICT is associated with worse outcomes; aim to start RT within 3–4 weeks of last ICT cycle
- Emergency dental review before ICT: extraction of unrestorable teeth, baseline OPG, fluoride trays — especially important if RT to oral cavity/jaw is planned after ICT

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Induction chemotherapy with TPF (docetaxel + cisplatin + 5-FU) is the standard regimen, superior to PF in both TAX 323 and TAX 324 trials, for organ preservation in laryngeal/hypopharyngeal cancers and in locally advanced NPC. The key principle of bioselection — that responders to ICT proceed to organ-preserving RT while non-responders undergo surgery — underpins the rationale in laryngeal/hypopharyngeal cancers. For NPC, induction TPF + CCRT is increasingly preferred over CCRT alone based on the Sun et al. (2016) Lancet Oncology trial. For oral cavity SCC, NACT is not standard for resectable disease; it is reserved for borderline/unresectable cases. Major complications include febrile neutropenia (G-CSF prophylaxis mandatory), cisplatin nephrotoxicity (aggressive hydration), and ototoxicity (audiometry monitoring).

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. TPF is the standard induction regimen (Level I: TAX 323 and TAX 324); PF is now used only when docetaxel is unavailable.
> 2. G-CSF prophylaxis (filgrastim from Day 6) is mandatory with TPF — without it, febrile neutropenia risk is ~76–80%.
> 3. Docetaxel premedication: dexamethasone 8 mg BD × 3 days (starting day before) prevents hypersensitivity and fluid retention.
> 4. Cisplatin is highly emetogenic — triple antiemetic protocol (5-HT3 antagonist + NK1 antagonist + dexamethasone) is mandatory.
> 5. For hypopharyngeal cancer, induction TPF → RT is still preferred over concurrent CRT (GORTEC 2000-01) because hypopharyngeal tumours have higher distant metastasis rates and concurrent CRT toxicity is problematic.
> 6. For NPC, the trend is induction TPF + CCRT (Sun 2016) rather than CCRT + adjuvant; compliance with adjuvant chemotherapy post-RT is poor (~60–70%).
> 7. Oral cavity SCC — induction chemotherapy does NOT improve OS for resectable tumours (GORTEC 2000-02 negative); surgery remains the primary treatment.
> 8. Renal function must be assessed before every cisplatin cycle (CrCl ≥60 mL/min); delay 1 week and re-check if CrCl 50–60; switch to carboplatin if <50.
> 9. Audiometry is mandatory at baseline and after each cisplatin cycle; ototoxicity is cumulative and irreversible; cisplatin ototoxicity targets outer hair cells of basal turn of cochlea.
> 10. Aim to start definitive RT/surgery within 3–4 weeks of the last ICT cycle; protracted interval reduces the benefit of tumour cytoreduction.
