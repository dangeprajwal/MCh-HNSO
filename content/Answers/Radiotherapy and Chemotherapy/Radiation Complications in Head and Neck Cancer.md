## Introduction

Radiotherapy is a cornerstone of head and neck cancer treatment, used as primary definitive therapy, as adjuvant treatment following surgery, or concurrently with chemotherapy. However, the proximity of critical normal structures to tumour targets means that radiation-induced complications are nearly universal and represent a major source of morbidity. These complications span a temporal continuum from acute effects during treatment through subacute changes to permanent late sequelae. Understanding the pathobiology, dose-response relationships, and management strategies for each complication is essential for the head and neck surgeon and oncologist.

Radiation injury occurs through two mechanisms: **direct DNA damage** (primarily from photon interactions) and **indirect damage** via free radical generation from water radiolysis. Rapidly dividing tissues (mucosa, salivary acini, skin) manifest acute injury, whereas slowly dividing tissues (bone, connective tissue, nervous tissue) develop late complications. The linear-quadratic model describes this differential: acute-responding tissues have a high alpha/beta ratio (~10 Gy), while late-responding tissues have a low alpha/beta ratio (~3 Gy), making them disproportionately sensitive to fraction size.

## Acute Complications (During Treatment / Within 6 Weeks)

### Oral Mucositis

Mucositis is the most common and debilitating acute complication of head and neck radiotherapy. Trotti et al. reported an overall incidence of approximately 80% with conventional fractionation and near-universal occurrence with concurrent chemoradiation.

#### Grading (WHO / RTOG / CTCAE)

| Grade | WHO Scale | RTOG Scale |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | No mucositis | No change |
| 1 | Soreness, erythema | Erythema, mild pain, not requiring analgesics |
| 2 | Erythema, ulcers; can eat solids | Patchy mucositis; moderate pain requiring analgesics |
| 3 | Ulcers; liquid diet only | Confluent fibrinous mucositis; severe pain requiring narcotics |
| 4 | Cannot eat or drink | Ulceration, haemorrhage, necrosis |

#### Pathobiology: Sonis Five-Phase Model

1. **Initiation** — DNA and non-DNA damage from radiation; generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS)
2. **Primary damage response (upregulation)** — Activation of NF-kappaB, p53, and MAP kinase pathways; release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6)
3. **Signal amplification** — Positive feedback loops amplify cytokine cascades; damage to submucosal connective tissue
4. **Ulceration** — Loss of mucosal integrity; bacterial colonisation of ulcerated surfaces leading to secondary infection and further cytokine release; this is the most symptomatic and clinically significant phase
5. **Healing** — Signals from extracellular matrix promote epithelial migration, proliferation, and restoration of mucosal integrity; typically begins after completion of radiotherapy

#### Mucositis Management

- **Benzydamine hydrochloride** — anti-inflammatory mouth rinse; MASCC/ISOO recommendation for prevention during moderate-dose RT (up to 50 Gy) without concurrent chemotherapy
- **Low-level laser therapy (LLLT) / Photobiomodulation (PBM)** — wavelength 632-660 nm or 780-830 nm; MASCC/ISOO recommendation for prevention; reduces severity and duration by promoting cellular bioenergetics and reducing inflammation
- **Basic oral care** — gentle tooth brushing, bland rinses (0.9% saline, sodium bicarbonate); foundation of all mucositis management
- **Cryotherapy** — limited evidence in RT setting (primarily for bolus 5-FU chemotherapy)
- **Pain management** — topical analgesics (viscous lidocaine, morphine mouthwash), systemic opioids for grade 3-4 mucositis
- **Nutritional support** — PEG tube or nasogastric feeding when oral intake falls below requirements
- **Palifermin (KGF-1)** — FDA-approved for mucositis in haematopoietic stem cell transplant; evidence in head and neck RT is limited

### Radiation Dermatitis

Skin reactions follow a predictable dose-dependent pattern:

| Dose Range | Reaction |
|---|---|
| 10-20 Gy | Erythema (vasodilation) |
| 20-30 Gy | Dry desquamation (peeling, pruritus) |
| 30-40 Gy | Moist desquamation (epidermal loss, serous exudate) |
| >40 Gy | Confluent moist desquamation; risk of secondary infection |

**Management**: gentle skin care, aqueous cream moisturisers, avoiding trauma to irradiated skin, topical corticosteroids for pruritus, silver sulfadiazine or hydrocolloid dressings for moist desquamation.

### Acute Xerostomia

Serous acinar cells of the parotid gland are exquisitely radiosensitive. Salivary flow reduction begins within the first week of radiotherapy and can decrease by 50-60% after the first 10 Gy. The mechanism involves apoptosis of serous acinar cells and membrane damage to aquaporin-5 water channels. Saliva becomes thick, viscous, and acidic (reduced bicarbonate buffering) as mucinous acini initially predominate.

### Dysgeusia (Taste Disturbance)

Taste buds on the tongue have a turnover time of approximately 10 days and are highly radiosensitive. Most patients experience significant taste disturbance after 20-30 Gy. Dysgeusia affects appetite, nutritional intake, and quality of life. Partial recovery occurs over 2-12 months; some patients have permanent altered taste.

### Oropharyngeal Candidiasis

Altered salivary composition and mucosal damage create a favourable environment for *Candida* overgrowth. Prophylactic antifungals (nystatin suspension, clotrimazole troches, or fluconazole) are commonly prescribed, particularly in patients with clinical evidence of candidiasis (pseudomembranous plaques).

### Acute Dysphagia

Mucosal oedema and pain from mucositis impair swallowing function. Radiation to the pharyngeal constrictors and supraglottic larynx causes oedema and reduced laryngeal elevation. This may necessitate dietary modification, swallowing therapy, or enteral feeding.

## Subacute / Early Late Complications (6 Weeks to 6 Months)

### Persistent Xerostomia

Salivary function continues to decline after completion of RT before reaching a nadir at approximately 2-3 months. Recovery depends on dose to the parotid glands and may take 12-24 months. With IMRT and parotid-sparing techniques, meaningful recovery is seen in a higher proportion of patients.

### Prolonged Taste Changes

Recovery of taste function is variable. Most patients experience partial recovery by 6-12 months, but approximately 20-30% have persistent dysgeusia beyond one year, particularly for bitter and salt taste modalities.

### Early Trismus

Fibrosis of the masticatory muscles (masseter, medial and lateral pterygoids, temporalis) and the temporomandibular joint begins in this period. Radiation to the pterygoid muscles is the strongest predictor of trismus development. Early initiation of jaw exercises is critical for prevention.

### Subcutaneous Fibrosis

Progressive induration and thickening of subcutaneous tissues in the irradiated field begins in the subacute period. This can limit neck mobility and contribute to lymphoedema, particularly after combined surgery and radiotherapy.

## Late Complications (>6 Months)

### Chronic Xerostomia

This is the most common permanent late effect of head and neck radiotherapy. The QUANTEC review by Deasy et al. established the critical dose thresholds:
- **Mean parotid dose <20 Gy** to at least one gland: severe xerostomia usually avoided
- **Mean parotid dose <25 Gy** to both glands: also associated with acceptable long-term function
- **Mean parotid dose >26 Gy**: progressive and likely irreversible loss of function

IMRT has dramatically reduced xerostomia rates compared to conventional 3D-conformal techniques by enabling selective parotid sparing. Submandibular gland transfer (surgical relocation to the submental space outside the radiation field) is another option for selected patients.

### Osteoradionecrosis (ORN)

Defined as exposed, non-healing bone in an irradiated field persisting for >3 months without tumour recurrence. The mandible is far more susceptible than the maxilla due to its end-arterial blood supply (inferior alveolar artery). Risk factors include radiation dose >60 Gy, dental extractions post-RT, poor oral hygiene, and concurrent chemotherapy.

**Pathophysiology**: Marx's "Three H" theory (hypovascular, hypocellular, hypoxic tissue) and Delanian's fibro-atrophic (RIF) theory.

**Management**: conservative debridement and antibiotics for early-stage disease; pentoxifylline + tocopherol (PENTOCLO protocol) based on the RIF theory; [[Answers/General/Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy|hyperbaric oxygen therapy]]; segmental mandibulectomy with microvascular free flap reconstruction for advanced ORN.

### Chondroradionecrosis

Necrosis of irradiated cartilage, most commonly affecting the laryngeal cartilages (arytenoids, thyroid cartilage, cricoid). Presents with pain, oedema, airway compromise, and sometimes fistula formation. Risk increases with doses >70 Gy to the larynx, concurrent chemotherapy, and continued smoking. Management ranges from conservative (antibiotics, humidification, voice rest) to surgical (debridement, laryngectomy in severe cases).

### Radiation Caries

A rapidly progressive and distinctive pattern of dental decay resulting from:
- Xerostomia (loss of salivary buffering and antimicrobial properties)
- Shift to aciduric and cariogenic oral microflora (*Streptococcus mutans*, *Lactobacillus*)
- Direct radiation damage to the dentinoenamel junction (DEJ), causing demineralisation and enamel delamination

Characteristically affects the cervical (gingival) margins, incisal edges, and cusp tips — areas not typically involved in conventional caries. Prevention requires lifelong daily application of custom fluoride trays (1.1% neutral sodium fluoride gel).

### Hypothyroidism

Occurs in 20-50% of patients receiving radiation to the neck, particularly when bilateral neck fields include the thyroid gland. The mechanism involves radiation-induced microvascular damage and fibrosis of the thyroid gland. Onset typically 6-12 months post-RT. Subclinical hypothyroidism (elevated TSH with normal T4) is more common than clinical hypothyroidism. All patients should undergo regular TSH monitoring (every 6-12 months) and receive thyroxine replacement when indicated.

### Carotid Artery Stenosis

Radiation accelerates atherosclerotic disease in the carotid arteries. Radiation-induced vasculopathy involves endothelial damage, intimal fibrosis and thickening, adventitial fibrosis, and accelerated plaque formation. The risk of significant stenosis (>50%) increases with time post-RT and may reach 30-40% at 10 years. Increases the risk of transient ischaemic attacks and stroke. Screening with carotid Doppler ultrasonography is recommended in long-term survivors.

### Second Primary Malignancies

Radiation-induced cancers can develop in or adjacent to the irradiated field, typically after a latency of >5-10 years. Sarcomas (osteosarcoma, fibrosarcoma, undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma) are the most characteristic radiation-induced tumours. The estimated risk is 0.5-1% per decade.

### Fibrosis

Progressive radiation-induced fibrosis affects skin, subcutaneous tissue, muscles, and connective tissue. Clinically manifests as induration, decreased neck mobility, lymphoedema, and trismus. The Delanian RIF theory describes a three-phase process: pre-fibrotic inflammatory phase, constitutive organised phase (active fibrosis), and late fibro-atrophic phase.

### Cranial Neuropathy

Radiation to the skull base can cause delayed neuropathy of cranial nerves, most commonly CN XII (hypoglossal), CN X (vagus), CN XI (accessory), and CN IX (glossopharyngeal). Mechanism involves perineural fibrosis and microvascular damage to the vasa nervorum. Presents months to years post-RT.

### Temporal Lobe Necrosis

Occurs when the temporal lobes receive radiation beyond tolerance during treatment of nasopharyngeal or skull base tumours. Presents with headache, cognitive decline, seizures, or focal neurological deficits. MRI shows characteristic contrast enhancement and perilesional oedema. Treatment includes corticosteroids and bevacizumab for symptomatic cases.

### Radiation Myelopathy

Devastating but rare complication when the spinal cord exceeds dose tolerance. Presents as progressive Brown-Sequard syndrome or transverse myelitis. Onset typically 6-18 months post-RT. Irreversible. Strict adherence to spinal cord dose constraints is essential for prevention.

## QUANTEC Dose Constraints for Key Organs at Risk

| Organ at Risk | Constraint | Endpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Parotid gland** | Mean dose <20 Gy (unilateral) or <25 Gy (bilateral) | Severe xerostomia |
| **Spinal cord** | Maximum dose <50 Gy (conventional); <46 Gy preferred | Myelopathy (<1% risk) |
| **Brainstem** | Maximum dose <54 Gy (entire); small volume up to 59 Gy | Cranial neuropathy, necrosis |
| **Mandible** | Maximum dose <70 Gy; volume receiving >60 Gy minimised | Osteoradionecrosis |
| **Pharyngeal constrictors** | Mean dose <50 Gy | Dysphagia, aspiration |
| **Cochlea** | Mean dose <45 Gy (without cisplatin); <35 Gy (with cisplatin) | Sensorineural hearing loss |
| **Optic nerves / chiasm** | Maximum dose <55 Gy | Optic neuropathy (<3% risk) |
| **Brachial plexus** | Maximum dose <66 Gy; ideally <60 Gy | Plexopathy |
| **Temporal lobes** | Maximum dose <60 Gy; D1cc <65 Gy | Temporal lobe necrosis |
| **Larynx (glottis)** | Mean dose <44 Gy | Aspiration, oedema |

## Prevention Strategies

### Intensity-Modulated Radiotherapy (IMRT)

IMRT is the standard of care for head and neck radiotherapy, enabling inverse-planned dose distributions that spare organs at risk while maintaining tumour coverage. Key advantages include:
- Significant reduction in xerostomia through parotid sparing
- Reduced pharyngeal constrictor doses (lower dysphagia and aspiration rates)
- Better conformality to complex target volumes (e.g., concave targets wrapping around the spinal cord)

Image-guided radiotherapy (IGRT) with daily cone-beam CT further improves accuracy and allows tighter margins.

### Amifostine

Amifostine (WR-2721) is the only FDA-approved radioprotective agent. Brizel et al. demonstrated significant reductions in acute and chronic xerostomia in their phase III trial. It is selectively concentrated in normal tissues (particularly salivary glands) due to higher alkaline phosphatase activity, which dephosphorylates the prodrug to the active free thiol WR-1065 that scavenges free radicals. Dose: 200 mg/m2 IV 15-30 minutes before each RT fraction. Side effects (nausea, hypotension) limit its routine use.

### Pilocarpine (Cholinergic Stimulation)

Pilocarpine (5 mg TDS) stimulates residual salivary gland function via muscarinic M3 receptors. Most effective when started during RT or within 3 months post-RT to stimulate surviving acinar cells. Contraindicated in narrow-angle glaucoma, uncontrolled asthma.

### Acupuncture

Randomised trials have shown that acupuncture during radiotherapy can reduce the severity of xerostomia and improve salivary flow rates. Mechanism is thought to involve parasympathetic stimulation of residual salivary tissue. Considered a reasonable complementary approach.

### Prophylactic Dental Care

Pre-radiotherapy dental assessment and management is mandatory:
- Extraction of teeth with poor prognosis (advanced caries, severe periodontal disease) at least **2-3 weeks before RT** to allow socket healing
- Restoration of salvageable teeth
- Fabrication of **custom fluoride trays** — lifelong daily use of 1.1% neutral sodium fluoride gel
- Education on oral hygiene, avoidance of tobacco and alcohol

### Submandibular Gland Transfer

Surgical transfer of the contralateral submandibular gland to the submental space (below the anterior belly of digastric) before radiotherapy, thereby relocating it outside the radiation field. Technique described by Jha et al.; preserves unstimulated salivary flow.

## Management of Established Complications

### Xerostomia Management

- **Pilocarpine** — 5 mg orally TDS; onset of effect 30 minutes; most effective with residual gland function
- **Cevimeline** — 30 mg orally TDS; longer half-life; more selective for M1 and M3 muscarinic receptors
- **Saliva substitutes** — carboxymethylcellulose-based or mucin-based artificial saliva; symptomatic relief
- **Oral hygiene** — frequent water sips; sugar-free gum/lozenges to stimulate residual secretion
- **Stem cell therapy / gene therapy** — investigational approaches to regenerate salivary gland tissue

### Trismus Management

- **Therabite jaw motion rehabilitation system** — stacked tongue depressors or commercially available devices providing passive stretch
- **Jaw exercises** — initiated early (during RT if possible); active range-of-motion exercises 3-5 times daily
- **Pentoxifylline (400 mg TDS) + Vitamin E (1000 IU/day)** — antifibrotic combination; evidence supports improved jaw opening over 6-12 months
- **Botulinum toxin** — adjunct for associated masticatory muscle spasm
- **Coronoidectomy** — reserved for severe refractory trismus unresponsive to conservative measures

### Fibrosis Management

- **Pentoxifylline + Vitamin E (PENTOCLO regimen)** — pentoxifylline (methylxanthine derivative) inhibits TGF-beta-mediated fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis; vitamin E (tocopherol) is an antioxidant that scavenges free radicals
- **Physical therapy** — stretching, massage, range-of-motion exercises
- **Hyperbaric oxygen** — limited evidence for soft tissue fibrosis; better evidence for ORN

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Mucositis occurs in ~80% of patients** receiving head and neck RT; near-universal with concurrent chemoradiation — it is the most common dose-limiting acute toxicity
> 2. **Sonis five-phase model** (initiation, upregulation, amplification, ulceration, healing) explains why mucositis severity peaks at weeks 3-5 of RT when cumulative dose reaches 30-40 Gy
> 3. **QUANTEC parotid threshold**: mean dose <20 Gy to at least one parotid gland (or <25 Gy bilateral) prevents severe long-term xerostomia — this is the single most important planning constraint for quality of life
> 4. **Dental extractions before RT** must be completed at least 2-3 weeks prior to allow socket healing; extractions after RT carry a significant ORN risk (especially at doses >60 Gy) and require HBO or antibiotic prophylaxis
> 5. **Amifostine** is the only FDA-approved radioprotector; it selectively protects normal tissue due to higher alkaline phosphatase activity but nausea and hypotension limit its routine use
> 6. **Radiation caries** has a pathognomonic pattern (cervical margins, incisal edges) distinct from conventional caries — lifelong custom fluoride trays are mandatory
> 7. **Hypothyroidism** occurs in 20-50% of patients irradiated to the neck; TSH monitoring every 6-12 months is essential — the most commonly overlooked late complication
> 8. **Trismus prevention** is far more effective than treatment — early jaw exercises during RT and limiting dose to pterygoid muscles are key; pentoxifylline + vitamin E can improve established trismus
> 9. **Pharyngeal constrictor mean dose <50 Gy** is the critical QUANTEC threshold for preventing long-term dysphagia and aspiration — swallowing exercises during RT are protective
> 10. **Carotid stenosis** is an under-recognised late effect; screening Doppler ultrasonography should be considered in long-term survivors, particularly those irradiated >5 years ago
