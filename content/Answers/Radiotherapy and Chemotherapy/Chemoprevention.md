> [!cite] Landmark Articles
>
> Hong et al. (1990) published the first landmark chemoprevention trial in head and neck cancer in the New England Journal of Medicine, randomising 103 patients who were disease-free after treatment for squamous cell carcinoma of the larynx, pharynx, or oral cavity to high-dose isotretinoin (13-cis-retinoic acid, 50–100 mg/m²/day) versus placebo for 12 months. Isotretinoin significantly reduced the rate of second primary tumours (4% vs 24%; p = 0.005) with no significant effect on disease recurrence, establishing the concept of secondary chemoprevention with retinoids for field cancerisation in HNSCC. This proof-of-concept trial generated enormous enthusiasm for retinoid chemoprevention and led to multiple large subsequent trials.
>
> Khuri et al. (2006) published the definitive large-scale randomised phase III trial of low-dose isotretinoin (30 mg/day × 3 years) versus placebo in 1,190 patients with stage I–II HNSCC in the Journal of the National Cancer Institute (Eurotam/Intergroup trial). Contrary to the Hong 1990 findings, low-dose isotretinoin did NOT significantly reduce the rate of second primary tumours (HR 1.06; p = 0.64) or improve overall survival (HR 1.03; p = 0.84), irrespective of smoking status. This large, adequately powered negative trial effectively concluded that isotretinoin cannot be recommended as a routine chemoprevention strategy for HNSCC survivors, highlighting the importance of validating early-phase findings in properly powered phase III studies.
>
> Papadimitrakopoulou et al. (2008) published a pilot randomised phase II study of celecoxib in oral premalignant lesions in Clinical Cancer Research, randomising patients to placebo, celecoxib 100 mg BD, or 200 mg BD for 12 weeks. No statistically significant difference in response rates was observed (33%, 41%, 20% respectively), and celecoxib was found ineffective for controlling oral premalignant lesions in this RCT, discouraging further investigation given concurrent cardiovascular toxicity concerns with COX-2 inhibitors identified in other large trials.

## Chemoprevention in Head and Neck Cancer

### Definition and Rationale

**Chemoprevention** is the use of natural or synthetic chemical agents to reverse, suppress, or prevent the carcinogenic process before the development of invasive malignancy.

**Three levels of chemoprevention:**
- **Primary prevention:** Prevention of cancer development in normal healthy individuals (e.g., tobacco cessation, HPV vaccination)
- **Secondary prevention:** Treatment of premalignant lesions to prevent progression to invasive cancer (e.g., retinoids for oral leukoplakia)
- **Tertiary prevention:** Prevention of second primary tumours (SPTs) in patients who have already been treated for a first cancer (e.g., isotretinoin for HNSCC survivors)

**Field cancerisation — the rationale for chemoprevention in HNSCC:**
- The entire upper aerodigestive tract mucosa is exposed to carcinogens (tobacco, alcohol, HPV) simultaneously
- This creates a "field" of prematurely aged, pre-initiated cells throughout the mucosa — Slaughter's field cancerisation (1953)
- Patients treated for one HNSCC have a 3–4% annual risk of a second primary tumour arising from this field
- Chemoprevention aims to target this entire mucosal field rather than individual lesions, addressing the underlying molecular changes in the carcinogenic field

### Agents Studied in HNSCC Chemoprevention

#### A. Retinoids (Vitamin A Analogues)

**Biology:** Retinoids (natural and synthetic derivatives of vitamin A) bind retinoic acid receptors (RARα, RARβ, RARγ) and retinoid X receptors (RXRα, RXRβ, RXRγ). RAR-β is frequently downregulated in HNSCC premalignant tissue; retinoids restore RAR-β expression, promoting differentiation and inhibiting proliferation.

**13-cis-Retinoic Acid (Isotretinoin):**
- **Hong et al. (1990):** High-dose isotretinoin (50–100 mg/m²/day × 12 months) — significantly reduced SPTs (4% vs 24%); did not prevent recurrence; proof-of-concept
- **Khuri et al. (2006) — Eurotam/Intergroup:** Low-dose isotretinoin (30 mg/day × 3 years) — NO reduction in SPTs or mortality in 1190 patients; **practice-changing negative trial**; high-dose/long-term isotretinoin was neither feasible nor effective at prevention
- Toxicity of isotretinoin: **teratogenicity** (Category X — mandatory contraception), hypertriglyceridaemia, mucocutaneous toxicity (cheilitis, xerostomia, dry eyes), hepatotoxicity, mood alteration; limits tolerability especially at high doses

**Retinol Palmitate (Natural Vitamin A):**
- Some evidence for regression of oral leukoplakia in small studies; not established as standard

**Beta-Carotene:**
- **CARET trial (lung cancer in smokers):** Beta-carotene + retinol supplementation significantly INCREASED lung cancer incidence and mortality in heavy smokers
- **Conclusion:** Beta-carotene is **contraindicated** as a chemopreventive agent in current or former smokers; no role in HNSCC chemoprevention

**Retinol + Beta-Carotene vs Isotretinoin (Lippman, 1993 NEJM):**
- Low-dose isotretinoin vs beta-carotene for oral leukoplakia prevention — isotretinoin superior for initial response; beta-carotene had fewer side effects but no long-term benefit

#### B. COX-2 Inhibitors (NSAIDs)

**Biology:** COX-2 (cyclooxygenase-2) is upregulated in oral premalignant lesions and invasive HNSCC. COX-2 → PGE2 → promotes angiogenesis, immune evasion, anti-apoptosis, and tumour cell proliferation. COX-2 inhibition has theoretical anti-carcinogenic effects.

**Celecoxib:**
- **Papadimitrakopoulou et al. (2008, Lippman SM group):** Randomised phase II — celecoxib 100 mg BD vs 200 mg BD vs placebo × 12 weeks; **no significant difference in response rates** in oral premalignant lesions
- Cardiovascular toxicity of COX-2 inhibitors (increased MI, stroke risk — demonstrated in APPROVe and CLASS trials) has discouraged further large-scale celecoxib chemoprevention trials
- **Conclusion:** Celecoxib is not currently recommended as a standard chemoprevention agent; may have an adjuvant role in metronomic regimens (palliative, not preventive)

**Aspirin/Other NSAIDs:**
- Epidemiological data suggest regular aspirin use is associated with reduced risk of HNSCC; no prospective RCT evidence in HNSCC chemoprevention

#### C. Green Tea Polyphenols — Epigallocatechin-3-gallate (EGCG)

- Mechanism: antioxidant; inhibits NF-κB, AP-1; anti-angiogenic; induces apoptosis via p53 pathway
- Phase II evidence for modest regression of oral leukoplakia
- Not established as standard clinical practice; safe with minimal toxicity
- Administered as standardised green tea extract capsules or topical oral rinse (limited bioavailability)

#### D. Curcumin

- Derived from turmeric (Curcuma longa); anti-inflammatory (inhibits NF-κB, COX-2, IL-6), anti-oxidant, anti-proliferative
- Pre-clinical activity against HNSCC cell lines
- Small clinical studies in oral submucous fibrosis (OSMF): symptom improvement (burning sensation, mouth opening) with curcumin supplementation
- **Not established as chemoprevention standard**; bioavailability limitations (poor oral absorption); ongoing interest in nano-formulations

#### E. Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor (EGFR) Inhibitors

- Erlotinib phase II studies for oral premalignant lesions — modest histological regression; not approved for chemoprevention
- **Erlotinib for 2nd primary prevention:** Phase II trials (Kies et al.) — some reduction in premalignant histological score; large phase III trial (EPOC, NCT00068445) ongoing

#### F. Primary Prevention — Tobacco and Alcohol Cessation

The most effective form of chemoprevention for HNSCC is **removal of the aetiological agent:**
- **Tobacco cessation:** Reduces risk of all tobacco-related HNSCC; NRT (nicotine replacement therapy — patch, gum, lozenge), varenicline (Champix), bupropion; reduces second primary tumour risk in survivors
- **Alcohol cessation:** Independent carcinogen; synergistic with tobacco; cessation counselling, disulfiram, naltrexone if dependent
- Behavioural: combined counselling + pharmacotherapy most effective for sustained cessation

#### G. HPV Vaccination (Primary Prevention)

- **Gardasil-9** (nonavalent HPV vaccine: HPV 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58): approved up to age 45; school-based programmes target 9–14 year olds (3 doses → 2 doses in <15 years)
- Prevents HPV 16/18-associated oropharyngeal cancers, cervical cancer, and other HPV-related malignancies
- No therapeutic vaccine approved for HNSCC; ISA101 (therapeutic HPV vaccine) + pembrolizumab — promising phase II data in HPV+ R/M OPC (response rate 33%)
- **Most impactful primary prevention strategy** for HPV-associated OPC in the long term

#### H. Other Agents

**Anethole Dithiolethione (5-ATP):**
- Synthetic dithiolethione; upregulates glutathione (anti-oxidant)
- Used in some Indian centres for oral submucous fibrosis; improves mouth opening and burning; not established as HNSCC chemoprevention agent

**Bowman-Birk Inhibitor Concentrate (BBI-C):**
- Soybean-derived serine protease inhibitor; inhibits chymotrypsin and trypsin activities
- Phase IIb trial in oral leukoplakia — modest reduction in severity score; well tolerated
- Not in routine clinical practice

**Spirulina:**
- Algal extract; small RCT from Kerala (Mathew et al., 1995) — regression of oral submucous fibrosis; limited evidence base

### Summary: Oral Leukoplakia Chemoprevention

| Agent | Evidence | Current Status |
|---|---|---|
| Isotretinoin (high dose) | Short-term regression; no long-term RCT benefit (Khuri 2006 negative) | Not recommended |
| Retinol palmitate | Some regression; not RCT-proven | Not standard |
| Beta-carotene | Harmful in smokers (CARET) | Contraindicated in smokers |
| Celecoxib | Phase II negative (Papadimitrakopoulou 2008) | Not recommended |
| EGCG (green tea) | Modest phase II data | Investigational |
| Curcumin | Small studies; not validated | Investigational |
| Erlotinib | Phase II data; phase III ongoing | Investigational |
| Surgical excision | Removes the lesion; does not prevent recurrence in field | Standard for high-risk lesions |
| Tobacco cessation | Most effective; reduces progression and recurrence | **Standard — first-line recommendation** |

### Second Primary Prevention in HNSCC Survivors

Patients with HNSCC have a 3–4% annual risk of a second primary tumour (same or different site in the aerodigestive tract). Strategies:
- **Tobacco and alcohol cessation:** single most important intervention; reduces SPT risk and overall mortality
- Isotretinoin: proved effective at high doses (Hong 1990) but NOT at low doses long-term (Khuri 2006) → **not recommended**
- Erlotinib for 2nd primary prevention: phase II and phase III trials; not yet standard
- Pembrolizumab as secondary prevention: investigational; rationale from high SPT risk in immunosuppressed field

**Conclusion:** No validated pharmacological chemoprevention agent currently has established standard-of-care status in HNSCC. Primary prevention (tobacco/alcohol cessation, HPV vaccination) remains the most effective strategy. Oral leukoplakia and high-risk lesions should be managed with biopsy, excision if high-grade dysplasia, and intensive tobacco cessation counselling. Surveillance for SPTs in treated HNSCC patients should include annual or biannual panendoscopy/clinical examination.

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Chemoprevention in HNSCC aims to reverse, suppress, or prevent carcinogenesis using natural or synthetic agents, targeting the field cancerisation phenomenon. Retinoids are the most extensively studied agents: Hong et al. (1990, NEJM) showed high-dose isotretinoin prevented second primary tumours, but Khuri et al. (2006, JNCI) demonstrated that low-dose isotretinoin does NOT prevent SPTs in a large phase III trial, effectively ruling out retinoids as a clinical standard. Celecoxib (COX-2 inhibitor) was ineffective for oral premalignant lesions (Papadimitrakopoulou 2008). No pharmacological agent currently meets the threshold of a validated chemoprevention standard in HNSCC. Primary prevention through tobacco/alcohol cessation and HPV vaccination (Gardasil-9) remain the mainstay; oral leukoplakia with high-grade dysplasia should be excised rather than treated with pharmacological chemoprevention alone.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Field cancerisation (Slaughter, 1953): the entire upper aerodigestive tract mucosa is pre-initiated by carcinogen exposure — this underpins the rationale for chemoprevention targeting the whole field.
> 2. Isotretinoin (high-dose): reduced SPTs in Hong 1990 but the larger phase III Khuri 2006 trial was NEGATIVE — isotretinoin is NOT recommended as chemoprevention.
> 3. Beta-carotene INCREASED lung cancer risk in smokers (CARET) — it is contraindicated as a chemopreventive agent in tobacco users.
> 4. Celecoxib randomised trial (Papadimitrakopoulou/Lippman 2008) was negative for oral premalignant lesions; cardiovascular toxicity discourages further COX-2 inhibitor chemoprevention trials.
> 5. HPV vaccination (Gardasil-9, nonavalent) is the most impactful primary prevention strategy for HPV-associated oropharyngeal cancers — targets HPV 16/18 responsible for most HPV+ OPC.
> 6. For oral submucous fibrosis: anethole dithiolethione and curcumin have small trial data for symptom relief; these are NOT validated chemoprevention agents for prevention of malignant transformation.
> 7. Tobacco cessation is the single most effective chemoprevention strategy across all HNSCC sites; varenicline (Champix) is the most effective pharmacotherapy for smoking cessation.
> 8. Three levels: primary (prevent cancer in healthy individuals — tobacco cessation, HPV vaccine), secondary (treat premalignant lesions — excision + cessation), tertiary (prevent SPTs in treated patients — surveillance + cessation).
> 9. Erlotinib for oral premalignant lesions — modest phase II data; large phase III (EPOC) ongoing; not yet standard but the most promising pharmacological agent in the pipeline.
> 10. The honest answer to "current status of chemoprevention in HNSCC": no validated pharmacological agent; primary prevention dominates; ongoing trials with EGFR inhibitors and investigational PD-1 approaches for secondary prevention.
