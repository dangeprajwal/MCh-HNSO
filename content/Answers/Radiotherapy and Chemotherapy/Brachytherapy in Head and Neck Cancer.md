## Introduction

Brachytherapy (Greek: brachy = short distance) is a form of radiation therapy in which a sealed radioactive source is placed directly into or adjacent to the tumour. It exploits the **inverse square law** — radiation dose falls off rapidly with distance from the source, delivering a high dose to the tumour while sparing surrounding normal tissues. In head and neck cancer, brachytherapy has a long history dating back to radium needles in the early 20th century, and remains a valuable tool for selected sites, particularly early-stage oral cavity cancers. It can be used as **sole definitive treatment**, as a **boost after external beam radiation therapy (EBRT)**, or as **adjuvant therapy after surgery**.

## Physics of Brachytherapy

### Inverse Square Law

The fundamental physical principle governing brachytherapy is the **inverse square law**: dose rate is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the source. At 2 cm from the source, the dose is only 25% of the dose at 1 cm. This steep dose gradient is both the greatest advantage (normal tissue sparing) and the greatest challenge (accurate source placement is critical) of brachytherapy.

### Dose Rate Classification

| Category | Dose Rate | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **LDR** (Low Dose Rate) | 0.4-2 Gy/hr | Continuous irradiation over days; classical technique; Paris system dosimetry |
| **HDR** (High Dose Rate) | >12 Gy/hr | Fractionated treatments (minutes each); outpatient; afterloading with stepping source |
| **PDR** (Pulsed Dose Rate) | 0.5-1 Gy/pulse every 1-4 hrs | Mimics LDR radiobiology with HDR stepping source; inpatient but safer than LDR |
| **MDR** (Medium Dose Rate) | 2-12 Gy/hr | Rarely used in modern practice |

### Radioactive Sources Used in HNC

| Isotope | Half-life | Energy | Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Iridium-192 (Ir-192)** | 73.8 days | 0.38 MeV (average gamma) | Most commonly used; HDR afterloading and LDR wires |
| **Caesium-137 (Cs-137)** | 30.2 years | 0.662 MeV | Intracavitary applications; largely replaced by Ir-192 |
| **Iodine-125 (I-125)** | 59.4 days | 27-35 keV | Permanent implants; recurrent HNC; low energy = minimal shielding |
| **Radium-226** | 1600 years | Multiple gamma | Historical only; replaced due to radiation safety concerns |

### Dosimetry Systems

- **Paris System (LDR):** Sources are arranged in parallel, equidistant lines with uniform linear activity. The reference dose is 85% of the basal dose (average minimum dose between sources). Requires regular geometry with inter-source spacing of 8-15 mm
- **Dwell Time Optimization (HDR):** A single high-activity Ir-192 source steps through catheter positions. Computer optimization adjusts dwell times at each position to shape the dose distribution conformally. Methods include geometric optimization, inverse planning simulated annealing (IPSA), and hybrid inverse planning optimization (HIPO)

## Types of Brachytherapy in HNC

### 1. Interstitial Brachytherapy (Most Common in HNC)

Radioactive sources or afterloading catheters are implanted **directly into the tumour tissue**. This is the workhorse technique for oral cavity cancers.

- **Technique:** Flexible afterloading catheters (plastic tubes) are inserted through the tumour with guide needles, maintaining parallel arrangement with 1-1.5 cm spacing
- **Afterloading:** Catheters are placed first (cold), then loaded with radioactive sources (hot) — reduces radiation exposure to staff
- **Methods:** Template-guided (more reproducible) vs. freehand (more flexible for irregular anatomy)

### 2. Intracavitary Brachytherapy

Applicators placed within a body cavity adjacent to the tumour. In HNC, used primarily for:
- **Nasopharyngeal carcinoma (NPC)** — intracavitary boost after EBRT via nasal or oral applicators
- Custom mould applicators for the hard palate

### 3. Intraluminal Brachytherapy

Catheter placed within a lumen (e.g., trachea, oesophagus). Limited role in HNC; occasionally used for subglottic or tracheal stump recurrences.

### 4. Surface Mould Brachytherapy

Custom moulds (acrylic, silicone) with embedded catheters placed on the tumour surface. Used for:
- Superficial lip cancers
- Hard palate lesions
- Skin cancers of the face/scalp
- Superficial buccal mucosa lesions

## Indications by Site in HNC

### Oral Tongue (Most Established Indication)

- **T1-T2 N0 squamous cell carcinoma** — brachytherapy alone as definitive treatment (most evidence)
- Tongue is ideal: accessible, mobile (allows implant placement), well-vascularized, no adjacent bone (unlike FOM)
- Local control: **85-95% for T1, 70-80% for T2** (Mazeron 1990)
- Preferred over surgery in select patients for functional preservation (speech, swallowing)

### Floor of Mouth (FOM)

- T1-T2 lesions — brachytherapy alone or boost after EBRT
- Higher complication rate than tongue due to **proximity to mandible** — risk of osteoradionecrosis (ORN)
- Pernot (1996): reported outcomes in 207 FOM cases; bone complications grade 2-3 more frequent than other oral sites
- **Mandatory mandible protection** with dental guard or lead shield

### Lip Cancer

- Surface mould or interstitial brachytherapy for T1-T2
- Excellent cosmetic outcomes compared to surgery
- Local control >90% for early-stage disease
- Well suited to HDR surface mould technique

### Buccal Mucosa

- Interstitial brachytherapy for T1-T2 lesions
- Single-plane or double-plane implant depending on tumour thickness
- Must maintain distance from mandible and skin

### Base of Tongue

- Interstitial brachytherapy as boost after EBRT (45-50 Gy EBRT + 20-30 Gy brachytherapy boost)
- More technically challenging than oral tongue — deeper location, risk of airway compromise
- Requires elective nodal irradiation with EBRT (high nodal metastasis rate)

### Nasopharynx (NPC Boost)

- Intracavitary brachytherapy boost after definitive chemoradiation
- Particularly useful for residual/recurrent T1-T2 NPC confined to the nasopharynx
- Rotterdam technique (Levendag): transnasal applicator with HDR Ir-192
- Boost dose: 10-15 Gy in 3-5 fractions (HDR)

## Technique of Interstitial Brachytherapy

### Pre-implant Assessment

1. **Clinical examination** — tumour mapping, palpation for depth and extent
2. **Imaging** — MRI (preferred) or CT for accurate tumour delineation
3. **Dental assessment** — mandatory before any oral cavity implant; carious teeth extraction; fabrication of dental guard
4. **Nutritional optimization** — NG tube or PEG if prolonged treatment anticipated
5. **Tracheostomy** — considered for base of tongue or large oral cavity implants (airway protection)

### Implant Procedure

1. **General anaesthesia** with nasotracheal intubation (oral access)
2. **Tumour marking** — tattooed margins under direct vision, 5-10 mm beyond palpable tumour
3. **Template vs. freehand:**
   - Template-guided: pre-drilled acrylic template ensures parallel catheter placement at uniform spacing
   - Freehand: guide needles inserted through tumour; more flexibility but operator-dependent geometry
4. **Catheter insertion:**
   - Guide needles (hollow, sharp-tipped) inserted through submental/submandibular skin → through tumour → exit oral cavity (or vice versa)
   - Flexible afterloading catheters threaded through and secured with buttons/crimps
   - **Spacing: 1-1.5 cm** between catheters (Paris system requirement)
   - Single-plane implant for tumours <1 cm thick; double-plane for thicker tumours
5. **Verification imaging** — CT scan with catheters in situ for treatment planning
6. **3D treatment planning** — target volume delineation, dose optimization, dose-volume histograms (DVH)

### CT-Based 3D Conformal Brachytherapy Planning

Modern brachytherapy mandates CT-based (or MRI-based) planning:
- **CTV (Clinical Target Volume)** delineated on axial images
- **Dose optimization** to CTV while respecting OAR constraints (mandible, skin, carotid)
- **DVH analysis:** D90 (dose covering 90% of CTV) should equal the prescription dose; V150 (volume receiving 150% of prescribed dose) should be minimized (<35-40% of CTV)
- **Conformity index and dose homogeneity index** assessed

## Dose Prescriptions

### LDR Brachytherapy

| Setting | Total Dose | Dose Rate |
|---|---|---|
| **Sole therapy** (T1-T2) | 60-70 Gy | 0.4-0.6 Gy/hr |
| **Boost after EBRT** (45-50 Gy EBRT) | 25-30 Gy | 0.4-0.6 Gy/hr |
| **Post-operative boost** | 15-25 Gy | 0.4-0.6 Gy/hr |

- Paris system: prescribed to the **85% reference isodose**
- Treatment duration: typically 4-7 days for sole therapy
- Dose rate is critical: <0.4 Gy/hr increases treatment time and recurrence; >0.6 Gy/hr increases complications

### HDR Brachytherapy

| Setting | Fractionation | Total Dose |
|---|---|---|
| **Sole therapy** | 3-4 Gy x 10-12 fractions (BID) | 36-48 Gy |
| **Boost after EBRT** | 3-4 Gy x 5-7 fractions (BID) | 15-24 Gy |
| **Post-operative boost** | 3-3.5 Gy x 5-6 fractions | 15-21 Gy |

- Minimum 6-hour interfraction interval (BID schedule)
- BED calculations essential for HDR to ensure radiobiological equivalence to LDR
- Biological effective dose (BED) for tumour (alpha/beta = 10): typically target BED ~72-80 Gy10 for sole therapy

### PDR Brachytherapy

- Pulses of 0.5-1 Gy delivered every 1-4 hours (typically hourly)
- Total dose equivalent to LDR (60-70 Gy sole therapy)
- Radiobiologically similar to LDR if pulse dose <0.5-1 Gy and interval 1-3 hours
- Advantage: uses HDR afterloader (radiation safety) with LDR-like radiobiology

## Combined EBRT and Brachytherapy

### Rationale

- EBRT covers the primary tumour plus draining lymph nodes (elective nodal irradiation)
- Brachytherapy provides a **conformal boost** to the primary tumour with rapid dose fall-off
- Combining modalities allows dose escalation at the primary site beyond what EBRT alone can safely deliver

### Sequencing and Dose Contributions

| Component | Dose | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **EBRT** | 45-50 Gy in 25 fractions | Primary + bilateral neck nodes |
| **Brachytherapy boost** | 20-30 Gy (LDR) or 15-24 Gy (HDR equivalent) | Primary tumour + margin |
| **Total effective dose** | 70-80 Gy equivalent | Primary site |

### Gap Management

- **Ideally no gap** between EBRT and brachytherapy — tumour repopulation accelerates after 4 weeks
- If gap unavoidable, keep <2 weeks
- Brachytherapy can precede or follow EBRT; most commonly EBRT first, then brachytherapy boost
- **Total treatment time** (EBRT + brachytherapy) should not exceed 7-8 weeks

## Outcomes by Site

### Oral Tongue

| Stage | Local Control | 5-Year OS | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1 N0 | 85-95% | 75-85% | Brachytherapy alone; comparable to surgery |
| T2 N0 | 70-80% | 55-70% | Brachytherapy alone or EBRT + boost |
| T3-T4 | 40-60% | 30-45% | Combined EBRT + brachytherapy boost; rarely sole therapy |

| T2 N0 | 65-75% | ORN 10-20% | Higher ORN rate than tongue |

|---|---|---|---|
| T1-T2 | >90% | Excellent | Surface mould or interstitial |

## Complications

### Soft Tissue Necrosis (5-15%)

- Most common significant complication
- Usually manifests 2-6 months post-treatment
- Related to total dose, dose rate, and implant volume
- Management: conservative (debridement, antibiotics); most heal spontaneously over weeks to months
- Risk factors: large implant volume, high dose rate (>0.5 Gy/hr for LDR), smoking, continued alcohol use

### Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) of the Mandible

- **Dose-dependent** — risk increases sharply above 60 Gy to the mandible
- Most critical complication for FOM and lower alveolar ridge implants
- Incidence: 2-5% with proper mandible protection; up to 20% without protection
- **Prevention is paramount** (see Mandible Protection below)
- Classification: Marx staging (Stage I-III) guides management

### Haemorrhage

- Rare but potentially life-threatening
- Risk of carotid blow-out if catheters placed near great vessels
- Preventive measure: maintain >5 mm distance from carotid artery
- Minor bleeding from catheter sites is common and self-limiting

### Other Complications

- **Infection** — catheter site infection (5-10%); managed with antibiotics
- **Trismus** — if pterygoid muscles or TMJ region irradiated
- **Xerostomia** — much less than EBRT (advantage of brachytherapy)
- **Dysgeusia** — temporary taste alteration
- **Fistula** — rare; more common with combined surgery + brachytherapy

## Mandible Protection

Protection of the mandible from excessive radiation dose is **critical** for all oral cavity brachytherapy, particularly FOM implants.

### Strategies

1. **Dental guard (lead-lined):**
   - Custom acrylic dental stent with embedded lead (1-2 mm thick)
   - Displaces the tongue/FOM away from the mandible
   - Provides partial shielding of the mandibular periosteum
   - **Most important and widely used protective measure**

2. **Catheter spacing from mandible:**
   - Maintain **>10 mm distance** from the closest catheter to the mandibular periosteum
   - Paris system geometry should be designed to keep the reference isodose away from bone
   - Dose to mandible ideally <60 Gy (LDR equivalent)

3. **Lead shields:**
   - Placed intraorally between the implant and the mandible
   - More effective shielding than dental guard alone
   - Combined lead shield + dental guard for high-risk cases

4. **Pre-treatment dental care:**
   - Extract carious/periodontally compromised teeth at least 2-3 weeks before implant
   - Fluoride trays for remaining dentition
   - Avoid post-implant dental extractions in irradiated field (risk of ORN)

5. **Dose optimization (HDR/PDR):**
   - Reduce dwell times in positions closest to mandible
   - DVH constraint: mandible D0.1cc <75% of prescription dose

## Newer Approaches

### 3D Image-Guided Brachytherapy (IGBT)

- CT or MRI-based treatment planning is now standard (GEC-ESTRO 2009, 2017 updates)
- Real-time imaging during implant placement (ultrasound, fluoroscopy)
- Allows volumetric dose optimization rather than point-based prescriptions
- Improved target coverage and OAR sparing compared to 2D planning

### MRI-Guided Brachytherapy

- Superior soft tissue contrast for tumour delineation in oral cavity
- MRI-compatible catheters and applicators now available
- Enables adaptive planning — re-image and re-optimize if tumour shrinks during treatment
- Most developed for cervical cancer; emerging application in HNC

### 3D-Printed Templates and Applicators

- Patient-specific templates designed from pre-operative CT/MRI
- Ensure optimal catheter trajectory and spacing
- Particularly useful for complex anatomy (base of tongue, buccal sulcus)
- Reduce implant time and improve reproducibility
- Custom surface moulds for lip and palate lesions with precise source positioning

### Intensity Modulated Brachytherapy (IMBT)

- Kovacs (2014): concept of dose painting with stepping source HDR
- Asymmetric dose distributions achievable by varying dwell times
- Analogous to IMRT concept but applied to brachytherapy
- Allows dose escalation to GTV while sparing adjacent OARs

### Intraoperative Brachytherapy

- Catheter placement during surgical resection (perioperative brachytherapy)
- For close/positive margins after resection of recurrent disease
- Loading 5-7 days post-operatively to allow initial wound healing
- Useful in the re-irradiation setting where further EBRT is limited

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Most testable site:** Oral tongue T1-T2 is the most established indication for brachytherapy in HNC — local control rates of 85-95% (T1) and 70-80% (T2) are frequently asked figures
> 2. **Inverse square law** is the fundamental physics principle — always mention this when explaining why brachytherapy spares normal tissue better than EBRT
> 3. **Ir-192** is the most commonly used isotope in modern HNC brachytherapy — used for both LDR wire implants and HDR afterloading
> 4. **Paris system** governs LDR dosimetry (85% reference isodose, parallel equidistant sources); **dwell time optimization** governs HDR dosimetry — know both systems
> 5. **Mandible protection is non-negotiable** for floor of mouth implants: dental guard + catheter spacing >10 mm from periosteum + dose constraint <60 Gy to bone
> 6. **Dose rate matters for LDR:** the sweet spot is 0.4-0.6 Gy/hr; below 0.4 increases recurrence, above 0.6 increases complications
> 7. **Gap between EBRT and brachytherapy** should be minimized (<2 weeks) — accelerated tumour repopulation begins after week 4 of any radiation course
> 8. **GEC-ESTRO recommendations** (Mazeron 2009, updated Kovacs/Strnad 2017) are the reference standard for technique, dose prescription, and quality assurance in HNC brachytherapy
