> Spencer et al. (2008) reported the final results of RTOG 9610, the first prospective multi-institutional trial of reirradiation with concurrent chemotherapy (hydroxyurea and 5-fluorouracil) for unresectable recurrent squamous cell carcinoma of the head and neck, delivering 60 Gy at 1.5 Gy twice daily in a split-course regimen; 79 of 86 enrolled patients were analysable with grade 5 acute toxicity in 7.6%, establishing the feasibility of this approach despite significant morbidity. (2007) published the results of RTOG 9911, a phase II study of low-dose paclitaxel and cisplatin combined with split-course concomitant twice-daily reirradiation in recurrent SCCHN; 105 patients were enrolled and median overall survival was 12.1 months compared to 8.5 months in the RTOG 9610 historical cohort, suggesting improved efficacy with the cisplatin-paclitaxel backbone albeit with grade 3+ late toxicity in 44.2%. Janot et al. (2008) conducted a landmark randomised trial (GETTEC-GORTEC) comparing postoperative reirradiation combined with chemotherapy versus salvage surgery alone in 130 patients; disease-free survival was significantly improved in the reirradiation arm (HR 1.68) though overall survival did not reach statistical significance, with grade 3-4 late toxicity at 2 years of 39% versus 10%. De Crevoisier et al. (1998) reported the Gustave-Roussy experience of full-dose reirradiation for unresectable head and neck carcinoma in 169 patients; overall survival was 21% at 2 years and 9% at 5 years with complete response in 37% at 6 months, establishing the foundation for curative-intent reirradiation while documenting significant late toxicities including mucosal necrosis (21%), osteoradionecrosis (8%), and trismus (30%). Sulman et al. (2009) retrospectively analysed 74 consecutive patients reirradiated with IMRT at MD Anderson Cancer Centre; 2-year overall survival and locoregional control rates were 58% and 64% respectively with severe reirradiation-related toxicity in 20%, demonstrating that IMRT-based reirradiation could achieve encouraging disease control with potentially lower morbidity than conventional techniques. Tortochaux et al. (2011) reported the randomised phase III GORTEC 98-03 trial comparing reirradiation plus concurrent chemotherapy (5-FU/hydroxyurea) versus methotrexate alone in patients with recurrent or second primary HNSCC treated with palliative intent; the trial was closed after 57 patients due to slow accrual and showed no overall survival benefit for the reirradiation arm in the palliative setting, underscoring the importance of patient selection for curative-intent reirradiation.

## Rationale for Re-irradiation

### The Problem of Locoregional Recurrence

Locoregional recurrence remains the **dominant pattern of failure** in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC):

- **30-50%** of patients with locally advanced HNSCC develop locoregional recurrence despite primary chemoradiation
- **Second primary tumours** within previously irradiated fields occur in approximately 3-5% of survivors per year
- The majority of recurrences (60-70%) occur within the first 2 years after initial treatment
- Without treatment, median survival for recurrent disease is only **3-6 months**

### Limitations of Other Salvage Modalities

| Modality | Limitation |
|---|---|
| **Salvage surgery** | Feasible in only 20-30% of recurrences; complicated by prior RT-induced fibrosis, compromised vascularity, and wound healing |
| **Chemotherapy alone** | Response rates 10-35%; median OS 6-8 months; rarely curative |
| **Immunotherapy** | Pembrolizumab/nivolumab offer modest benefit (OS 7-9 months); durable responses in a minority |
| **Best supportive care** | Median survival 3-4 months |

### Biological Basis for Re-irradiation

The rationale for re-irradiation rests on several radiobiological principles:

1. **Repopulation of tumour clonogens** -- recurrent tumour represents a new population of clonogenic cells that can be targeted with curative-intent radiation
2. **Partial recovery of normal tissue tolerance** -- normal tissues recover a fraction of their radiation tolerance over time, with the degree of recovery dependent on the interval from initial RT
3. **Different tumour biology** -- recurrent tumours may have different radiosensitivity compared to the initial tumour
4. **Technological advances** -- modern techniques (IMRT, SBRT, protons) allow delivery of tumoricidal doses while limiting re-exposure of critical structures
5. **Sublethal damage repair (SLD repair)** in normal tissues is largely complete by 6-12 months post-initial RT, permitting a therapeutic window for retreatment

## Patient Selection

Appropriate patient selection is the **single most important determinant** of outcomes in re-irradiation. A multidisciplinary tumour board assessment is mandatory.

### Key Selection Criteria

| Factor | Favourable | Unfavourable |
|---|---|---|
| **Performance status** | ECOG 0-1 | ECOG >=2 |
| **Disease-free interval (DFI)** | >12 months (ideally >24 months) | <6 months |
| **Tumour volume** | Small, well-defined recurrence | Bulky, diffuse disease |
| **Prior RT dose** | <66 Gy | >70 Gy |
| **Site of recurrence** | Oral cavity, oropharynx (accessible for surgery + re-RT) | Nasopharynx, hypopharynx (deep-seated) |
| **Resectability** | Resectable (surgery + adjuvant re-RT) | Unresectable, palliative intent |
| **Overlap volume** | Minimal overlap with prior high-dose region | Extensive overlap |
| **Nutritional status** | Adequate oral intake, BMI >18.5 | Severe cachexia, PEG-dependent |
| **Organ function** | Preserved renal, hepatic function | Organ dysfunction limiting concurrent systemic therapy |

### Disease-Free Interval

The disease-free interval is one of the most consistent prognostic factors across re-irradiation studies:

- **DFI >6 months**: minimum threshold for consideration (RTOG trials)
- **DFI >12 months**: moderate normal tissue recovery expected
- **DFI >24 months**: substantial normal tissue recovery; best outcomes reported
- **DFI <6 months**: generally considered a contraindication due to both biological aggressiveness and insufficient normal tissue recovery

## Cumulative Dose Considerations and Normal Tissue Tolerance

### QUANTEC-Based Guidelines for Re-irradiation

The major challenge of re-irradiation is the cumulative dose to critical structures. Normal tissues vary in their capacity for long-term repair.

| Organ at Risk | Initial RT Tolerance (Dmax/Dmean) | Recovery Potential | Re-irradiation Constraint | Key Toxicity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Spinal cord** | 50 Gy (Dmax) | ~25% recovery at 6 months; up to 30-35% at >12 months | Cumulative Dmax <55-60 Gy with interval >6 months | Myelopathy |
| **Brainstem** | 54 Gy (Dmax) | Limited recovery data | Cumulative Dmax <60 Gy; minimise overlap | Cranial neuropathy, death |
| **Optic nerves/chiasm** | 55 Gy (Dmax) | Minimal recovery | Cumulative Dmax <55-60 Gy | Blindness |
| **Brachial plexus** | 66 Gy (Dmax) | Moderate recovery | Cumulative Dmax <70 Gy | Plexopathy |
| **Mandible** | 70 Gy (Dmax) | Minimal recovery (end artery supply) | Cumulative Dmax <80 Gy; minimise volume >60 Gy | Osteoradionecrosis |
| **Temporal lobe** | 60 Gy (Dmax) | Limited | Cumulative Dmax <65-70 Gy | Temporal lobe necrosis |
| **Carotid artery** | No established threshold | Unknown | Minimise cumulative dose; avoid circumferential irradiation | Carotid blowout |
| **Pharyngeal constrictors** | Mean <50 Gy | Partial recovery | Minimise mean dose | Severe dysphagia |
| **Larynx** | Mean <44 Gy | Limited | Cumulative mean <50 Gy | Laryngeal dysfunction, aspiration |

### Spinal Cord Tolerance in Re-irradiation

The spinal cord deserves special attention as the dose-limiting organ:

- **Ang et al. primate data**: demonstrated that approximately 25% of initial spinal cord damage is repaired by 6 months, and 30-35% by 1-2 years
- Practical guideline: if initial cord Dmax was 45 Gy, a re-irradiation cord Dmax of 25-30 Gy may be acceptable at intervals >12 months (cumulative ~70-75 Gy, with partial recovery factored in)
- **BED calculation** is essential: use the linear-quadratic model with alpha/beta = 2 Gy for late-responding tissues
- Cumulative BED to spinal cord should ideally remain <135 Gy2

## Re-irradiation Techniques

### 1. Intensity-Modulated Radiation Therapy (IMRT)

IMRT is the **most widely used technique** for re-irradiation:

- **Advantages**: steep dose gradients allowing sparing of previously irradiated OARs; ability to deliver conformal dose distributions to irregularly shaped targets
- **Sulman et al. (2009)**: 74 patients treated with IMRT re-irradiation at MD Anderson; 2-year OS 58%, locoregional control 64%, severe toxicity 20%
- **Typical prescription**: 60 Gy in 30 fractions or 66 Gy in 33 fractions to gross disease; 54 Gy to high-risk subclinical regions
- **Image guidance (IGRT)**: daily CBCT or kV imaging is mandatory to ensure accuracy given tight margins

### 2. Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy (SBRT) / Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS)

SBRT delivers ablative doses in 1-5 fractions:

- **Typical doses**: 35-44 Gy in 5 fractions; 30-36 Gy in 3 fractions; single-fraction SRS 14-18 Gy
- **Advantages**: short treatment duration (1-2 weeks), high local BED, reduced repopulation during treatment
- **Limitations**: suitable only for small-volume disease (<4-5 cm); proximity to critical structures (carotid, spinal cord) may preclude ablative dosing
- **Outcomes**: 1-year local control 50-70% for appropriately selected patients
- **Carotid blowout risk**: increased with SBRT when the carotid artery is within or abutting the target volume

### 3. Proton Beam Therapy

Protons offer a dosimetric advantage due to the **Bragg peak**:

- **Reduced integral dose** to surrounding normal tissues compared to photon-based IMRT
- Particularly advantageous when the recurrence is in close proximity to previously heavily irradiated structures (brainstem, spinal cord, optic apparatus)
- **Pencil beam scanning (PBS)** with robust optimisation is the preferred technique
- Limited availability and cost remain barriers to widespread adoption
- Emerging data suggest comparable or improved toxicity profiles, though randomised data are lacking

### 4. Brachytherapy

Interstitial or intracavitary brachytherapy offers an alternative approach:

- **Advantages**: highly conformal dose delivery with rapid dose fall-off; short treatment times; can be combined with external beam re-RT
- **HDR brachytherapy**: commonly used for accessible mucosal recurrences (oral cavity, oropharynx)
- **Typical doses**: 30-40 Gy by HDR (3-4 Gy per fraction twice daily) or 25-30 Gy LDR as boost
- **Interstitial implants**: suitable for well-defined, accessible recurrences <4 cm
- Best outcomes when combined with surgical resection (perioperative brachytherapy)

### 5. Intraoperative Radiation Therapy (IORT)

IORT delivers a single large dose at the time of surgical resection:

- **Technique**: electron beam IORT (10-20 MeV electrons) or HDR-IORT with surface applicators delivered to the tumour bed during salvage surgery
- **Dose**: single fraction of 10-15 Gy to the surgical bed
- **Indications**: close or positive margins after salvage surgery; high-risk tumour bed in previously irradiated field
- **Advantages**: direct visualisation of target; displacement of normal tissues; single-fraction convenience
- **Outcomes**: improved local control (50-60% at 2 years) when combined with external beam re-RT
- **Limitations**: requires specialised equipment (mobile linear accelerator or dedicated IORT suite); limited availability

## Dose and Fractionation Schemes

### Conventional Fractionation

| Regimen | Total Dose | Fractionation | Indication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard re-RT | 60 Gy | 2 Gy/fraction, 30 fractions over 6 weeks | Definitive re-RT for gross disease |
| Postoperative re-RT | 54-60 Gy | 1.8-2 Gy/fraction | Adjuvant after salvage surgery |
| Reduced dose re-RT | 45-50 Gy | 1.8-2 Gy/fraction | Limited by cumulative OAR doses |

### Hyperfractionation

Hyperfractionation (1.2-1.5 Gy BID) is biologically advantageous in re-irradiation:

- **Rationale**: smaller fraction sizes spare late-responding normal tissues (low alpha/beta ratio) while maintaining tumour cell kill (high alpha/beta ratio)
- **RTOG 9610**: 60 Gy at 1.5 Gy BID in a split-course (1 week on, 1 week off) with concurrent 5-FU/hydroxyurea
- **RTOG 9911**: 60 Gy at 1.5 Gy BID split-course with concurrent cisplatin/paclitaxel
- **Advantage over conventional fractionation**: lower BED to late-responding tissues for equivalent tumour BED
- **Disadvantage**: prolonged overall treatment time (with split-course) may allow tumour repopulation

### SBRT Dosing

| Fractions | Total Dose | BED10 (Tumour) | Common Indication |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 | 35 Gy | 59.5 Gy | Small recurrence, close to OARs |
| 5 | 40 Gy | 72 Gy | Well-separated from critical OARs |
| 5 | 44 Gy | 82.7 Gy | Optimal geometry, away from carotid |
| 3 | 30 Gy | 60 Gy | Very small target |
| 1 | 14-18 Gy | 33.6-50.4 Gy | SRS for small skull base/nasopharyngeal recurrence |

## Concurrent Systemic Therapy

### Platinum-Based Chemotherapy

- **Cisplatin**: most commonly used concurrent agent; standard dose 40 mg/m2 weekly or 100 mg/m2 q3 weekly
- **Carboplatin**: alternative for cisplatin-ineligible patients (renal impairment, hearing loss, neuropathy)
- In the re-irradiation setting, lower doses are often used due to cumulative toxicity (e.g., cisplatin 30-40 mg/m2 weekly)
- **RTOG 9911 regimen**: cisplatin 15 mg/m2 daily x5 + paclitaxel 20 mg/m2 daily x5, q2 weeks for 4 cycles, concurrent with BID RT

### Cetuximab

- **Biological rationale**: EGFR is overexpressed in >90% of HNSCC; cetuximab radiosensitises tumour cells without additive mucosal toxicity
- **Dosing**: loading dose 400 mg/m2 week 1, then 250 mg/m2 weekly during RT
- Preferred agent when platinum is contraindicated or in patients who previously received concurrent cisplatin
- **GORTEC 2008-01 trial**: compared cetuximab-based versus 5-FU/hydroxyurea-based concurrent regimens with re-irradiation

### 5-Fluorouracil and Hydroxyurea (FHX)

- **RTOG 9610 regimen**: 5-FU 300 mg/m2 IV bolus + hydroxyurea 1.5 g PO, concurrent with hyperfractionated re-RT
- Historical backbone of the split-course BID re-irradiation approach
- Hydroxyurea acts as a radiosensitiser by depleting intracellular deoxyribonucleotide pools and synchronising cells in the S-phase

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Multi-institutional phase II |
| **Patients** | 105 enrolled, 99 assessable |
| **Treatment** | 60 Gy at 1.5 Gy BID split-course + concurrent cisplatin 15 mg/m2/day x5 + paclitaxel 20 mg/m2/day x5 q2 weeks x4 cycles |

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Randomised phase III |
| **Patients** | 130 randomised |
| **Arms** | Salvage surgery + postoperative re-RT/chemo vs. salvage surgery alone |
| **Re-RT regimen** | 60 Gy in 33 fractions (1.8 Gy/fraction BID for 11 days, split) + concurrent 5-FU/hydroxyurea |

## Toxicities of Re-irradiation

### Acute Toxicities

- **Mucositis**: grade 3-4 in 25-40% of patients; exacerbated by concurrent chemotherapy
- **Dermatitis**: moist desquamation in previously irradiated skin; impaired healing
- **Dysphagia**: often severe; PEG tube required in 30-50% of patients undergoing re-RT
- **Pain**: significant pain requiring opioid analgesia in the majority

### Late Toxicities

| Toxicity | Incidence | Risk Factors | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Carotid blowout syndrome (CBS)** | 3-7% | Tumour encasing carotid, prior neck dissection, fistula, skin necrosis, cumulative dose >100 Gy | Emergent interventional radiology (embolisation/stenting); prophylactic carotid coverage in surgery |
| **Osteoradionecrosis (ORN)** | 5-15% | Cumulative mandibular dose >80 Gy, dental extraction, poor oral hygiene | Hyperbaric oxygen, pentoxifylline-tocopherol, surgical debridement, free flap reconstruction |
| **Pharyngocutaneous fistula** | 5-10% | Prior surgery + re-RT, poor nutritional status, concurrent chemotherapy | Surgical closure with vascularised flap; conservative management in small fistulae |
| **Severe dysphagia** | 15-30% | Cumulative pharyngeal constrictor dose, extensive mucosal re-irradiation | PEG dependency; swallowing rehabilitation |
| **Trismus** | 10-30% | Cumulative pterygoid/masticator space dose | Therabite exercises; botulinum toxin |
| **Myelopathy** | <2% with modern constraints | Cumulative spinal cord BED >135 Gy2 | Preventable with strict dose constraints; no effective treatment once established |
| **Temporal lobe necrosis** | 2-5% (nasopharynx re-RT) | Cumulative temporal lobe dose >70 Gy | Corticosteroids, bevacizumab, surgical resection |
| **Cranial neuropathy** | 3-8% | Skull base re-irradiation, cumulative brainstem dose | Supportive care |

### Carotid Blowout Syndrome -- Special Consideration

CBS is the most feared complication of re-irradiation:

- **Mortality rate**: 40-60% if untreated; 15-20% with emergent intervention
- **Classification**: threatened (sentinel bleed), impending (expanding pseudoaneurysm), acute (active haemorrhage)
- **Risk mitigation**: avoid circumferential high-dose irradiation of carotid; vascularised tissue interposition at surgery; close surveillance in high-risk patients

## Decision Framework

### Surgery-First Approach

When feasible, **salvage surgery followed by adjuvant re-irradiation** yields the best outcomes:

1. **Surgical resection** removes gross disease and reduces the volume requiring re-irradiation
2. **Pathological assessment** of margins and extranodal extension guides re-RT dose
3. **Vascularised flap reconstruction** (pectoralis major, anterolateral thigh, or free flap) protects the carotid and provides vascularised tissue cover
4. **Adjuvant re-RT** dose can be lower (50-60 Gy) compared to definitive re-RT (60-70 Gy)

### Multidisciplinary Tumour Board Algorithm

```
Locoregional recurrence in previously irradiated field
            |
    Assess DFI, PS, volume, prior dose
            |
    DFI < 6 months or PS >= 3 --> Palliative care / systemic therapy / immunotherapy
            |
    DFI >= 6 months, PS 0-2
            |
    ---- Resectable? ----
    |                    |
   YES                  NO
    |                    |
  Salvage surgery     Re-irradiation +/- concurrent systemic therapy
  + adjuvant re-RT    (IMRT preferred; SBRT for small volume)
  +/- chemo           Consider clinical trial
    |
  Postop re-RT 54-60 Gy
  +/- concurrent cisplatin/cetuximab
```

### Contraindications to Re-irradiation

- DFI <6 months (relative) or active progression on recent treatment
- ECOG performance status >=3
- Cumulative spinal cord BED >135 Gy2 that cannot be avoided
- Presence of distant metastases (unless oligometastatic, treated with curative intent)
- Severe late effects from prior RT that would be compounded (e.g., existing fistula, severe fibrosis)
- Patient preference for palliative approach after informed discussion

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Disease-free interval >12 months** is the strongest favourable prognostic factor for re-irradiation outcomes; patients with DFI <6 months derive minimal benefit
> 2. **Surgery-first strategy** is preferred when feasible -- salvage resection followed by adjuvant re-RT yields better outcomes than definitive re-RT alone
> 3. **Spinal cord recovery**: approximately 25-35% of initial radiation damage recovers at 1-2 years; cumulative BED to cord should remain <135 Gy2 (alpha/beta = 2 Gy)
> 4. **IMRT is the technique of choice** for re-irradiation: Sulman et al. demonstrated 2-year OS of 58% and locoregional control of 64% with IMRT-based re-RT
> 5. **Carotid blowout** (3-7% incidence) is the most lethal complication; risk is increased by tumour encasement of the carotid, prior neck dissection, and cumulative dose >100 Gy to the vessel wall
> 6. **Hyperfractionation** (1.2-1.5 Gy BID) provides a therapeutic advantage by exploiting the differential alpha/beta ratio between tumour (~10 Gy) and late-responding normal tissues (~2-3 Gy)
> 7. **RTOG 9911** established cisplatin/paclitaxel as the preferred concurrent regimen (median OS 12.1 months vs. 8.5 months with 5-FU/hydroxyurea in RTOG 9610)
> 8. **Janot trial**: only RCT showing DFS benefit for postoperative re-RT after salvage surgery (HR 1.68); however, grade 3-4 late toxicity was 39% at 2 years
> 9. **SBRT** (35-44 Gy in 5 fractions) is an alternative for small-volume recurrences but carries a higher risk of carotid blowout when the target abuts the vessel
> 10. **Cumulative mandibular dose >80 Gy** significantly increases osteoradionecrosis risk; pentoxifylline-tocopherol (PENTOCLO) protocol may mitigate this complication
