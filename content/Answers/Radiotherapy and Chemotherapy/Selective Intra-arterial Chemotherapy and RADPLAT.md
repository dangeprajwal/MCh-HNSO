> Robbins et al. (1994) published the foundational proof-of-concept of RADPLAT in the American Journal of Surgery, demonstrating a 96% complete response rate in 29 patients with untreated stage IV HNSCC treated with intra-arterial cisplatin 150 mg/m² weekly (4 cycles) with simultaneous IV sodium thiosulfate neutralisation and concurrent conventionally fractionated RT. This established the pharmacological basis of the technique — supradose IA cisplatin with systemic thiosulfate neutralisation to achieve extremely high tumour drug concentrations while sharply limiting systemic toxicity. Robbins et al. (1996) subsequently reported on 46 patients with advanced laryngeal and pharyngeal cancer treated with this protocol, confirming an 86% primary-site complete response rate and 2-year overall survival of 64%, firmly establishing the organ-preservation rationale for RADPLAT. Robbins et al. (2000) reported the largest institutional series in 213 consecutive patients with stage III–IV HNSCC treated with RADPLAT at the University of Tennessee, with an 88% complete response rate and estimated 5-year overall survival of approximately 50% in this heavily advanced cohort; this landmark analysis confirmed the reproducibility of the protocol in a large series and delineated the complication spectrum.

## Principle of Intra-arterial Chemotherapy

The fundamental concept underlying selective intra-arterial (IA) chemotherapy is the exploitation of **first-pass extraction** to deliver suprapharmacological concentrations of a cytotoxic agent directly into the tumour while simultaneously neutralising the same agent in the systemic circulation to prevent systemic toxicity.

For a drug with high first-pass extraction (such as cisplatin), intra-arterial delivery into the tumour-feeding artery achieves drug concentrations at the tumour that are **5–10 times higher** than those achievable with equivalent-dose intravenous administration. The tumour extracts a large fraction of drug during its first pass through the capillary bed, and the residual drug that drains into the systemic venous circulation is neutralised before it can exert systemic toxicity.

### Pharmacological Basis

The pharmacokinetic advantage of IA delivery is expressed as the **regional advantage (R)**:

> R = 1 + (Cl_total / Q_regional)

Where Cl_total is total body drug clearance and Q_regional is regional blood flow. For cisplatin, with high total body clearance (approximately 250–300 mL/min/m²) and relatively low regional arterial flow (5–20 mL/min), R can reach 5–15, meaning tumour cisplatin exposure is 5–15 times greater than with IV administration at the same dose.

**Sodium thiosulfate (STS)** acts as a nucleophilic thiol that reacts irreversibly with activated aquaplatin species (the cytotoxic form of cisplatin formed after chloride displacement in low-chloride intracellular environments). When administered intravenously simultaneously with IA cisplatin, STS neutralises any cisplatin that has entered the systemic venous circulation before it can reach the kidneys, cochlea, or peripheral nerves — sharply reducing nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, and neuropathy while preserving antitumour activity in the IA-perfused tumour.

## The RADPLAT Protocol

**RADPLAT** (Radiation therapy and concomitant intra-Arterial cisPlATin) is the standardised protocol developed by Robbins and colleagues at the University of Tennessee, Memphis, and is the best-characterised IA chemoradiation regimen for advanced HNSCC.

### Patient Selection

- Advanced (T3–T4) squamous cell carcinoma of the oral cavity, oropharynx, hypopharynx, or larynx
- Primary tumour must be anatomically accessible via superselective catheterisation of a named feeding artery (lingual artery, facial artery, superior thyroid artery, or named external carotid branches)
- Adequate renal function (creatinine clearance ≥60 mL/min — less critical than with IV cisplatin because systemic neutralisation dramatically reduces nephrotoxicity)
- Absence of contraindication to catheterisation (severe atherosclerosis, unfavourable anatomy, coagulopathy)
- Access to experienced neurointerventional radiology team and DSA (digital subtraction angiography) suite

### Technical Requirements

Superselective catheterisation is performed under fluoroscopic guidance using DSA. A microcatheter is advanced coaxially through a guiding catheter placed in the external carotid artery into the specific feeding artery supplying the tumour, confirmed by contrast injection. Accurate selection of the feeding vessel is critical: misplaced infusion reduces tumour drug delivery and risks normal-tissue ischaemia or neurological injury.

### Chemotherapy Component

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Agent | Cisplatin (cis-diamminedichloroplatinum II) |
| Dose | 150 mg/m² per cycle (supradose — approximately double the standard IV dose) |
| Route | Intra-arterial, via superselective catheterisation of tumour-feeding artery |
| Schedule | 4 cycles, each delivered weekly (Days 1, 8, 15, 22 of RT) |
| Infusion rate | Rapid infusion over 20–30 minutes (to maximise first-pass extraction) |
| Thiosulfate neutralisation | IV sodium thiosulfate: 9 g/m² IV bolus beginning 5 minutes after start of cisplatin infusion, then 12 g/m² IV over 6 hours — neutralises systemic cisplatin; must be administered simultaneously |

### Radiation Component

- Concurrent external-beam RT: **70 Gy in 35 fractions** (2 Gy/fraction, 5 days/week, 7 weeks) — standard fractionation
- Alternatively, hyperfractionated or accelerated fractionation schedules have been used at some centres
- Target volumes defined by conventional CT-based planning or IMRT (more recent series)
- Chemotherapy infusion scheduled to coincide with a radiation fraction on each treatment day

## Advantages of RADPLAT over IV Cisplatin CRT

| Feature | RADPLAT (IA) | Standard IV Cisplatin CRT |
|---|---|---|
| Tumour cisplatin concentration | 5–10× systemic level | Standard systemic level |
| Nephrotoxicity | Substantially reduced (STS neutralisation) | Dose-limiting; requires aggressive hydration |
| Ototoxicity | Substantially reduced | Significant — high-frequency loss |
| Neuropathy | Reduced | Cumulative peripheral neuropathy |
| Myelosuppression | Mild–moderate | Moderate |
| Organ preservation potential | High (direct tumour drug delivery) | High (comparably — systemic trials) |
| Technical complexity | High (IR suite, superselective catheterisation) | Low (standard IV infusion) |
| Availability | Limited (specialist IR centres only) | Universally available |

## Efficacy Data

### Robbins' Series (University of Tennessee)

- 1994 pilot (n=29): complete response rate 96%; established feasibility
- 1996 organ preservation series (n=46, larynx/pharynx): 86% primary CR; 2-year OS 64%
- 2000 large series (n=213): 88% complete response rate (combined primary + nodal); estimated 5-year OS approximately 50% in stage III–IV disease; organ preservation maintained in approximately 70% of survivors

### Comparison with IV CRT

No Phase III randomised controlled trial has directly compared RADPLAT with standard IV cisplatin CRT. The apparent very high response rates in RADPLAT series (88% CR) compared to IV CRT trials (approximately 60–70% CR at primary) likely reflect **patient selection bias** — RADPLAT series typically include patients with anatomically accessible tumours at experienced centres, and selection excludes patients unsuitable for catheterisation. Without an RCT, RADPLAT cannot be considered superior to IV cisplatin CRT, and most guidelines do not recommend it as standard care outside specialised centres.

## Complications

### Catheterisation-Related
- **Stroke / cerebral infarction**: 1–2%; most feared complication; from catheter-induced thromboembolism, arterial dissection, or inadvertent injection into cerebral vessels; risk mandates experienced neurointerventional operator
- Cranial nerve palsy: from inadvertent drug delivery to cranial nerve-supplying branches
- Arterial spasm, dissection, or thrombosis at catheter site
- Carotid blowout (rare, typically in recurrent setting after prior surgery/RT)

### Cisplatin-Related (Attenuated by STS)
- Nephrotoxicity: substantially reduced vs IV cisplatin due to STS neutralisation
- Ototoxicity: residual high-frequency hearing loss possible, less severe than IV cisplatin
- Nausea and vomiting: persist (central emetogenic mechanism not blocked by STS)
- Myelosuppression: mild–moderate

### Radiation-Related
- Standard mucositis, xerostomia, dysphagia — as per any head and neck RT (see Radiation Complications file)

## Current Role and Limitations

RADPLAT occupies a **narrow specialist niche** in current HNSCC management:

1. **Not a standard of care** at most institutions; absent from major guidelines (NCCN, ESMO) as a recommended regimen
2. Applicable only at centres with dedicated interventional radiology expertise and appropriate DSA infrastructure
3. Largely superseded in routine practice by IV cisplatin-RT (which achieves comparable locoregional control with proven Phase III evidence) and, increasingly, by combined IV CRT plus immune checkpoint inhibitor combinations
4. A possible role in select patients with advanced oral cavity or oropharyngeal cancer where organ preservation is the primary goal and anatomy permits superselective catheterisation
5. The RADPLAT approach has been explored for tumours of the maxillary sinus (Japanese series, JCOG1212) where IA delivery is technically straightforward via the internal maxillary artery — this subsite remains an active area of investigation

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> RADPLAT is a regimen of superselective intra-arterial cisplatin 150 mg/m² per week for 4 cycles with simultaneous IV sodium thiosulfate neutralisation, delivered concurrent with conventional fractionation RT to 70 Gy, achieving suprapharmacological tumour drug concentrations of 5–10 times systemic levels. The technique exploits first-pass extraction pharmacokinetics: high-dose cisplatin is rapidly infused into the tumour-feeding artery while intravenous thiosulfate irreversibly neutralises cisplatin in the systemic circulation, sharply reducing nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, and neuropathy. Robbins' series of 213 patients reported 88% complete response rates, with organ preservation in the majority of survivors. However, no Phase III RCT has established superiority over standard IV cisplatin CRT, and RADPLAT is not a standard guideline recommendation outside specialised centres with experienced neurointerventional radiology. The principal catheterisation risk is stroke (1–2%), and technical demands limit applicability to specialist institutions.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The key pharmacological principle is **first-pass extraction**: IA cisplatin achieves 5–10× higher tumour concentrations than equivalent-dose IV cisplatin.
> 2. **Sodium thiosulfate** is not a chemotherapy drug — it is a neutralising agent administered IV to eliminate systemic cisplatin toxicity; it does not reduce tumour drug effect because the tumour has already extracted the drug.
> 3. The cisplatin dose in RADPLAT (150 mg/m²/week) is approximately **double** the standard IV dose (80–100 mg/m² q3w or 40 mg/m² weekly); the higher dose is tolerable only because of STS neutralisation.
> 4. **Superselective** catheterisation is essential: catheterisation must reach the named feeding artery (not just the external carotid trunk) to confine drug delivery to the tumour and avoid cranial nerve ischaemia.
> 5. The most feared serious complication is **stroke** from catheter-related thromboembolism — requires experienced neurointerventional radiology team.
> 6. RADPLAT data show impressive complete response rates (~88%) but **no Phase III RCT vs IV cisplatin CRT** has been conducted; apparent superiority reflects patient selection.
> 7. Standard IV cisplatin CRT remains the globally recommended standard; RADPLAT is appropriate only at specialist centres with appropriate infrastructure.
> 8. IA chemotherapy has a validated role in **maxillary sinus cancer** (JCOG1212 Japanese data) where the internal maxillary artery is accessible and organ preservation of the orbit is a priority.
> 9. The technique is technically demanding and should **not** be attempted without dedicated interventional radiology support and digital subtraction angiography capability.
> 10. Thiosulfate **neutralises systemic cisplatin** but cannot protect against cisplatin already within the tumour microenvironment — this differential is the therapeutic window that the protocol exploits.
