## Introduction: Why Targeted Therapy Mucositis Differs

The term "targeted therapy" in oncology refers to agents that exploit specific molecular vulnerabilities in cancer cells — primarily EGFR inhibitors (cetuximab, panitumumab, erlotinib, gefitinib), mTOR inhibitors (everolimus, temsirolimus), and immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab). Their mucosal side effects differ mechanistically, clinically, and in management from radiation mucositis and cytotoxic chemotherapy mucositis.

Understanding these differences is essential because:
- Applying standard radiation mucositis protocols to mTOR stomatitis can be ineffective or counterproductive
- The mechanism determines the correct management — topical steroids for mTOR stomatitis, not antifungals or growth factors
- Rash severity paradoxically predicts response to cetuximab — a concept unique to EGFR inhibitor-associated skin/mucosal toxicity

## Grading of Oral Mucositis (WHO / CTCAE)

| Grade | WHO / CTCAE Description |
|---|---|
| Grade 1 | Erythema and soreness; asymptomatic |
| Grade 2 | Patchy pseudomembranes/ulcers; can eat; moderate pain |
| Grade 3 | Confluent pseudomembranes/ulcers; cannot eat solid food; severe pain |
| Grade 4 | Life-threatening; cannot swallow; requires parenteral nutrition; haemorrhage possible |
| Grade 5 | Death |

## 1. Radiation-Induced Mucositis (Standard CRT)

### Pathophysiology (Sonis Model, 5 Phases)

1. **Initiation**: radiation-induced ROS cause DNA double-strand breaks in basal epithelial cells; p53 activation
2. **Primary damage response**: NF-κB activation; transcription of TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6
3. **Signal amplification**: pro-inflammatory cytokines stimulate further NF-κB activation, COX-2 upregulation, metalloproteinases; tissue injury amplified beyond direct DNA damage
4. **Ulceration phase**: loss of epithelial barrier; bacterial colonisation of ulcer surface; further pro-inflammatory stimulus from LPS (Toll-like receptor activation); this is the symptomatic phase — painful, pseudomembranous ulceration; peaks at 2–3 weeks RT (cumulative dose ~40–50 Gy)
5. **Healing**: signalling from extracellular matrix stimulates epithelial migration and proliferation; resolution typically 2–4 weeks after completion of RT

### Clinical Features in Head and Neck RT

- Onset: erythema by Week 2–3 (approximately 20 Gy)
- Peak: confluent ulceration at Week 4–5 (approximately 40–50 Gy)
- Affected: soft palate, buccal mucosa, floor of mouth, lateral tongue, oropharynx — within the radiation field
- Concurrent cisplatin worsens grade and duration

### Management of RT Mucositis (Standard Protocol)

**Prevention:**
- **Oral hygiene**: soft-bristle toothbrush, fluoride toothpaste, atraumatic flossing twice daily; continued throughout RT
- **Saline/bicarbonate rinse**: 1 tsp NaCl + 1 tsp NaHCO₃ in 1 litre water; rinse 4–6 times daily; maintains mucosal pH and removes debris
- **Benzydamine hydrochloride mouthwash** (0.15% solution): non-steroidal anti-inflammatory (NSAID); anti-inflammatory, anaesthetic, and antimicrobial properties; MASCC/ISOO **recommends** benzydamine for prevention of radiation-induced oral mucositis in patients receiving moderate-dose RT (up to 50 Gy, not in concurrent CRT); rinse for 2 minutes and expectorate; 15 mL QID
- **Low-level laser therapy (LLLT/photobiomodulation)**: wavelength 650 nm, fluence 1 J/cm²; MASCC/ISOO recommends for prevention and treatment of RT mucositis; reduces severity and duration; requires dedicated equipment

**Treatment:**
- **Pain**: topical anaesthetic (lidocaine viscous 2% — "magic mouthwash" base); morphine patient-controlled analgesia (PCA/PCAS) for severe Grade 3–4 mucositis; systemic opioids as required
- **"Magic mouthwash"**: variable formulations; typically lidocaine 2% viscous + diphenhydramine + antacid (e.g., Maalox) ± nystatin ± dexamethasone; QID; provides temporary symptomatic relief
- **Palifermin (KGF-1)**: NOT recommended for RT-alone mucositis (concern about stimulating KGF receptors on squamous cell carcinoma — potential tumour growth promotion); approved for TBI-based HSCT mucositis only
- **Nutritional support**: NG tube or PEG if Grade 3–4 mucositis prevents adequate oral nutrition

## 2. EGFR Inhibitor-Induced Mucositis and Skin Toxicity

### Mechanism

EGFR (epidermal growth factor receptor) is highly expressed in normal basal epithelial keratinocytes and is essential for:
- Epithelial proliferation and wound healing
- Maintenance of skin/mucosal barrier integrity (by upregulating epithelial tight junction proteins)
- Hair follicle homeostasis

EGFR inhibitors (cetuximab, erlotinib, panitumumab) block EGFR signalling → impaired epithelial proliferation and wound healing → barrier dysfunction → mucosal sensitivity and susceptibility to minor trauma.

### Clinical Features

**Oral mucositis with EGFR inhibitors:**
- Grade ≥2 oral mucositis in 20–30% of patients receiving cetuximab (lower than RT mucositis Grade 3–4 rates of 50–80%)
- Typically less severe than RT mucositis; diffuse rather than confluent ulceration
- Does not follow the radiation field distribution — can affect mucosa outside the RT field if cetuximab is used as radiosensitiser

**Acneiform skin rash (most characteristic):**
- Follicular papulopustular rash, predominantly face, scalp, chest, and back (sebaceous-gland-rich areas); onset within 1–2 weeks of starting therapy
- **Grading**: Grade 1: ≤10% BSA, not bothersome; Grade 2: 10–30% BSA ± local superinfection; Grade 3: >30% BSA, severely impacted QoL ± secondary cellulitis
- **Key clinical concept**: severity of EGFR inhibitor rash correlates with **anti-tumour response** — patients with Grade 2–3 rash have better progression-free and overall survival; rash is a pharmacodynamic biomarker of EGFR inhibition. This paradoxical relationship should be explained to patients — rash means the drug is working.

**Other EGFR inhibitor toxicities:**
- Dry skin (xerosis)
- Paronychia (periungual inflammation)
- Mucositis of conjunctiva, nasal, and genital mucosae (rare)

### Management of EGFR Inhibitor Skin/Mucosal Toxicity

**Skin rash:**
- **Prophylaxis**: minocycline 100 mg BD (tetracycline antibiotic with anti-inflammatory properties) started with cetuximab, reduces rash severity; or doxycycline 100 mg BD
- **Established rash**: topical clindamycin 1% lotion (affected areas); emollient moisturisers (urea-containing creams); sun protection (UVA/UVB SPF 30+)
- **Grade 3 rash**: dose modification; oral tetracycline; dermatology referral; short course topical methylprednisolone

**Oral mucositis:**
- Standard saline/bicarbonate rinses; oral hygiene protocol as above
- Barrier agents (e.g., Episil, Gelclair oral gel) — form protective film over mucosa
- No evidence that palifermin improves EGFR inhibitor mucositis

## 3. mTOR Inhibitor-Associated Stomatitis

### Mechanism and Distinction from "True" Mucositis

mTOR inhibitor stomatitis is **not** classical RT/chemotherapy mucositis. It is a distinct inflammatory oral condition:

- mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin) regulates cellular metabolism, proliferation, and immune function
- mTOR inhibition → impaired mucosal immune tolerance → T-cell-mediated mucosal inflammation → **aphthous-like ulcers**
- The lesions are discrete, well-defined, shallow ulcers with an erythematous halo — identical in appearance to recurrent aphthous stomatitis (RAS/canker sores)
- Contrast with RT mucositis: RT mucositis produces **diffuse, confluent pseudomembranous** ulceration involving large mucosal areas; mTOR stomatitis produces **discrete, small (2–10 mm), well-defined** aphthous ulcers at specific intraoral sites

### Incidence

- All-grade stomatitis: 59–71% of patients receiving everolimus (BOLERO-2, BALLET trials)
- Grade ≥3 stomatitis: 8–10%
- Median time to onset: 10 days (range 4–25 days)
- Temsirolimus (IV mTOR inhibitor): similar stomatitis profile

### Management: SWISH Protocol (First-Line Standard)

The **SWISH trial** (Rugo et al., Lancet Oncology 2017) established dexamethasone oral solution as the standard:

**Protocol:**
- Dexamethasone 0.5 mg/5 mL oral solution (alcohol-free)
- 10 mL: rinse/swish for 2 minutes, then **spit** (do not swallow) — 4 times daily
- Duration: start from Day 1 of mTOR inhibitor therapy; continue for first 8 weeks (highest risk period)

**Results of SWISH**: Grade ≥2 stomatitis rate 2% with prophylactic dexamethasone rinse vs 33% historical rate from BOLERO-2 — an 84% relative risk reduction.

**What NOT to use:**
- Antifungal mouthwashes (nystatin, amphotericin) — mTOR stomatitis is not fungal; these are ineffective and may irritate
- Chlorhexidine — alcohol-containing formulations irritate already-inflamed mucosa
- Palifermin (KGF) — mechanism is proliferative (keratinocyte growth); does not address the immune-mediated mTOR stomatitis; NOT recommended
- Sucralfate — no evidence for mTOR stomatitis

**For established Grade 2–3 ulcers:**
- Intralesional or topical triamcinolone acetonide (in orabase) applied directly to ulcers
- Continue dexamethasone rinse
- Colchicine 0.5 mg BD (as for aphthous stomatitis — anti-inflammatory)
- mTOR inhibitor dose reduction or temporary interruption for Grade 3 stomatitis

## 4. Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor (ICI) Oral Toxicity

### Mechanism

ICI (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, ipilimumab) oral immune-related adverse events (irAE) are T-cell-mediated autoimmune reactions, not drug toxicity to mucosa.

### Clinical Features

- Overall oral irAE: approximately 5% of patients on ICI
- **Lichenoid reactions** (most common): white striae ± erosions on buccal mucosa; painful; resembles oral lichen planus; biopsy shows band-like T-cell infiltrate — characteristic histology
- **Aphthous-like ulcers**: less common
- **Bullous lesions**: rare; associated with ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4)
- Sicca syndrome (dry mouth) — ICI-mediated salivary gland inflammation

### Management

- **Grade 1**: topical steroids (triamcinolone orabase, clobetasol 0.05% gel); avoid irritants; continue ICI
- **Grade 2**: topical high-potency steroids; consider ICI dose delay; oral hygiene; dermatology/oral medicine referral
- **Grade 3**: systemic prednisolone 0.5–1 mg/kg/day; **hold ICI**; taper over 4–6 weeks; resume ICI only when toxicity resolves to Grade ≤1 and steroid dose ≤10 mg/day prednisone equivalent
- **Grade 4**: **permanently discontinue ICI**; IV methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day until improvement; then oral taper

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Targeted therapy mucositis requires a mechanism-based approach: EGFR inhibitor (cetuximab) oral toxicity results from impaired barrier repair due to EGFR blockade in keratinocytes, manifesting as mild-to-moderate mucositis managed with standard oral hygiene and barrier agents, while the associated acneiform rash paradoxically predicts treatment response. mTOR inhibitor stomatitis (everolimus, temsirolimus) is an immune-mediated aphthous-like condition — not true mucositis — managed by prophylactic dexamethasone oral rinse (SWISH protocol: 0.5 mg/5 mL, swish-and-spit QID), which reduced Grade ≥2 stomatitis from 33% to 2% in the pivotal trial; antifungals and palifermin are specifically contraindicated. Immune checkpoint inhibitor oral irAEs are autoimmune lichenoid reactions managed with topical steroids (Grade 1–2), systemic steroids and ICI hold (Grade 3), and permanent ICI discontinuation (Grade 4). These are distinct mechanisms requiring distinct management — standard radiation mucositis protocols should not be applied indiscriminately.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. mTOR stomatitis produces **discrete aphthous ulcers** (not diffuse pseudomembranous mucositis) — the distinction is critical for correct management.
> 2. **Dexamethasone 0.5 mg/5 mL swish-and-spit QID** (SWISH protocol) is the evidence-based standard for mTOR inhibitor stomatitis prophylaxis; start from Day 1 of everolimus.
> 3. Do NOT use antifungals, chlorhexidine, or palifermin for mTOR stomatitis — none are effective and some worsen symptoms.
> 4. Cetuximab rash severity paradoxically **correlates with anti-tumour response** — explain this to patients before starting therapy to prevent premature drug discontinuation.
> 5. Prophylactic minocycline 100 mg BD (or doxycycline) started with cetuximab reduces EGFR inhibitor rash severity.
> 6. Benzydamine mouthwash is the MASCC/ISOO-recommended preventive agent for **RT-induced mucositis** (moderate-dose RT, up to 50 Gy); not for mTOR stomatitis.
> 7. Palifermin (KGF-1) is approved for mucositis in TBI-based autologous HSCT only; **NOT recommended** for head and neck RT mucositis (risk of KGF receptor stimulation on squamous carcinoma cells).
> 8. ICI oral irAE (lichenoid reactions) — if Grade ≥3, **hold the ICI** and start systemic corticosteroids.
> 9. Low-level laser therapy (650 nm, 1 J/cm²) is MASCC/ISOO-recommended for both prevention and treatment of RT-induced oral mucositis.
> 10. Nutritional support (NG tube or PEG) is indicated when Grade 3–4 mucositis prevents adequate oral intake; prolonged NPO without swallowing activity accelerates pharyngeal atrophy — some oral intake should be maintained where possible.
