## Part A: Surgical Anatomy of the Temporal Bone

### Divisions of the Temporal Bone

The temporal bone consists of five parts, all relevant to surgical oncology:

| Division | Key Structures | Cancer Relevance |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Squamous** | Zygomatic process, squamotympanic suture | Lateral boundary; osteotomy site in LTBR |
| **Tympanic** | External auditory canal (bony), tympanic ring | Primary site for EAC carcinoma |
| **Mastoid** | Mastoid air cells, sigmoid sinus, facial nerve (mastoid segment) | Posterior extent of resection |
| **Petrous** | Cochlea, IAC, petrous ICA, jugular fossa, semicircular canals, facial nerve (labyrinthine + tympanic segments) | Medial extent in STBR; ICA involvement = T4 |
| **Styloid** | Styloid process, stylomastoid foramen (CN VII exit) | Inferior margin; FN identification |

### Key Anatomical Relationships

- **Facial nerve (CN VII):** Exits the stylomastoid foramen → enters parotid → divides into temporofacial and cervicofacial trunks. Within temporal bone: labyrinthine (geniculate ganglion), tympanic (horizontal), mastoid (descending/vertical) segments
- **Petrous ICA:** Vertical (ascending) then horizontal (petrous) segments — lateral limit for safe resection in STBR
- **Jugular bulb:** Posteroinferior petrous — proximity to sigmoid sinus; involved in T4 disease
- **Eustachian tube:** Anterior; connects middle ear to nasopharynx — spread route to parapharyngeal space
- **Dura (middle cranial fossa floor = tegmen):** Superior; erosion = T3/T4

## Part B: Malignant Tumours of the Temporal Bone

### Classification

| Tumour Type | Origin | Frequency | Notes |
|-------------|--------|-----------|-------|
| **SCC of EAC** | Tympanic bone / EAC skin | Most common (~60%) | Arising from EAC squamous epithelium |
| **Adenoid cystic carcinoma** | Ceruminous glands | 10–15% | Perineural spread; skip lesions; long natural history |
| **Ceruminous adenocarcinoma** | Ceruminous glands | 5–8% | Aggressive; poor prognosis |
| **Basal cell carcinoma** | EAC skin | 5–10% | Often extends from pinna/periauricular skin |
| **Rhabdomyosarcoma** | Mesenchymal (middle ear) | Paediatric | Botryoid variant; IVA/IVB stage common |
| **Melanoma** | EAC skin | Rare | Extension from pinna; staging per melanoma criteria |
| **Paraganglioma (glomus jugulare)** | Jugular fossa paraganglia | Benign primarily | Locally invasive; malignant in ~5% |
| **Metastases** | Breast, kidney, lung, prostate | Rare | Haematogenous; petrous apex |

## Part C: SCC of the External Auditory Canal

### Pittsburgh Staging System (Arriaga 1990)

The universally accepted staging system, based on CT findings and clinical examination:

| Stage | Criteria |
|-------|---------|
| **T1** | Tumour limited to the EAC without bony erosion or evidence of soft tissue involvement |
| **T2** | Tumour with limited EAC bone erosion (not full thickness) with limited (<0.5 cm) soft tissue involvement |
| **T3** | Tumour eroding the osseous EAC (full thickness) with limited (<0.5 cm) soft tissue involvement; OR tumour involving the middle ear, mastoid, or both |
| **T4** | Tumour eroding the cochlea, petrous apex, medial wall of middle ear, carotid canal, or jugular foramen or dura; OR with extensive soft tissue involvement (>0.5 cm) such as involvement of the TMJ or stylomastoid foramen; OR with evidence of facial paresis |

**Stage grouping:**
- Stage I = T1 N0 M0; Stage II = T2 N0 M0; Stage III = T3 N0 M0 or T1 N1 M0
- Stage IV = T4 N0 M0, T2–4 N1 M0, any T N2–3 M0, any T any N M1
- N1 disease in a T2 or higher lesion = Stage IV — nodal metastasis dramatically worsens prognosis (Stell & Maran)

### Routes of Spread of SCC of EAC

Tumour spreads in four directions from the EAC (Stell & Maran, Chapter 40):

**Anteriorly:**
- Into the **parotid gland** (most common anterior route — via fissures of Santorini and Huschke's foramen in the tympanic plate)
- **Infratemporal fossa** via glenoid fossa erosion
- **Facial skin**

**Posteriorly:**
- Into **mastoid air cells** → posterior fossa
- Through mastoid cortex into retroauricular soft tissues

**Inferiorly:**
- Into **neck soft tissues** via floor of EAC
- **Jugular foramen** → sigmoid-jugular complex
- **Foramen magnum** or cervical spine in advanced disease

**Superiorly:**
- Into **epitympanum** → tegmen tympani → **middle cranial fossa floor** → dura
- Extradural spread along middle fossa

*Note: Medial spread (cochlea → IAC → carotid canal) is incorporated into the superior and inferior spread paths and is reflected in T4 staging criteria (involvement of cochlea, petrous apex, carotid canal, jugular foramen, dura).*

### Nodal Drainage (Echelon Stations)

**First echelon nodes (per Stell & Maran):**
- **Parotid/preauricular nodes** — anterior EAC drainage
- **Upper deep cervical / internal jugular nodes** — inferior drainage
- **Post-auricular (retroauricular/mastoid) nodes** — posterior EAC skin drainage
- **Middle ear and mastoid** lymphatics → Eustachian tube area → deep upper jugular and retropharyngeal nodes

**Second echelon nodes:**
- **Levels II–III (upper/mid jugular chain)** — from parotid/preauricular
- **Level V (posterior triangle)** — from retroauricular nodes

**Clinical implication:** Parotidectomy is an integral part of LTBR and NTTBR — it exposes the facial nerve trunk at the stylomastoid foramen and clears parotid nodes as first echelon. Routine elective neck dissection (N0) is not performed for temporal bone SCC because: (1) occult cervical involvement is low (10–15%), and (2) postoperative RT routinely covers the neck (Stell & Maran). Neck dissection is added when there is clinical or radiological evidence of neck lymphadenopathy, performed en bloc with the temporal bone specimen where possible.

## Part D: Classification of Temporal Bone Resection

Three levels of resection, escalating medially:

### 1. Lateral Temporal Bone Resection (LTBR)

**What is removed:**
- Entire EAC (cartilaginous + bony)
- Tympanic membrane
- Malleus and incus (ossicular chain lateral to stapes)
- Mastoid cortex (outer cortex only)
- **Pinna:** preserved unless involved

**What is preserved:**
- Petrous bone, cochlea, labyrinth
- Facial nerve (after identification and skeletonisation)
- Middle ear medial wall, stapes

**Osteotomy sites:**
1. **Anteriorly:** Zygomatic root — cuts anterior wall of EAC at its root
2. **Posteriorly:** Through outer mastoid cortex
3. **Superiorly:** Squamous temporal bone — superior EAC wall
4. **Inferiorly:** Through floor of EAC below Huschke's foramen

**Indication:** T1 disease; T2 without medial extension beyond tympanic membrane

---

### 2. Near-Total / Subtotal Temporal Bone Resection (NTTBR / STBR)

*Stell & Maran prefer "NTTBR" (near-total temporal bone resection); the term "STBR" (subtotal) is also widely used in the literature — both refer to the same level of resection.*

**What is removed:**
- Everything in LTBR PLUS:
- Middle ear cleft — en-bloc resection of the medial surfaces of the mesotympanum
- Cochlea and labyrinth
- Lateral petrous bone up to (but leaving) the petrous apex air cells
- Complete mastoidectomy
- Facial nerve (sacrificed from stylomastoid foramen to geniculate ganglion; reconstructed with cable graft)

**What is preserved:**
- Petrous apex (air cells medial to cochlea)
- ICA itself (if not invaded — canal wall and roof removed but artery preserved)

**Standard Osteotomy Sites for STBR (TMH favourite question):**
1. **Anterior osteotomy:** Through zygomatic root, anterior to TMJ (glenoid fossa); soft tissue dissection protects the TMJ
2. **Posterior osteotomy:** Through posterior petrous bone, posterior to sigmoid sinus (the sigmoid is ligated and divided)
3. **Superior osteotomy:** Through middle cranial fossa floor (tegmen); cuts across the petrous ridge
4. **Inferior osteotomy:** Through the floor of EAC/hypotympanum at the level of the jugular fossa (jugular vein ligated if involved)
5. **Medial cut:** Through petrous bone anterior to cochlea, lateral to ICA (petrous vertical segment)

**Sequence of STBR:**
1. Parotidectomy (superficial or total, depending on parotid involvement) — exposes facial nerve trunk at stylomastoid foramen
2. Complete mastoidectomy — removes mastoid air cells, identifies sigmoid sinus, facial nerve
3. Facial nerve dissection from stylomastoid foramen to second genu (horizontal/tympanic segment)
4. Ligation and division of sigmoid sinus (if intradural extension, posterior fossa dura opened)
5. Middle cranial fossa craniotomy if tegmen involved; retract temporal lobe
6. Anterior osteotomy through zygomatic root
7. Inferior cuts through hypotympanum and jugular fossa
8. Medial petrous cut anterior to cochlea
9. En-bloc removal of specimen
10. Reconstruction

**Indication:** T2 with medial extension; T3 disease involving middle ear/mastoid; T3 without ICA involvement

---

### 3. Total Temporal Bone Resection (TTBR)

**What is removed:**
- All of STBR PLUS petrous apex, horizontal petrous ICA (resected with vascular reconstruction or ICA sacrifice with balloon occlusion test)

**Indication:** T4 with ICA involvement; historically attempted for very advanced disease
**Reality:** Very rarely performed now — extreme morbidity with no proven survival benefit; most T4 disease is managed with CRT ± limited surgery

**Contraindications to surgery — Unresectable criteria (Pensak, cited in Stell & Maran):**
Pensak et al. reported that invasion of any of the following structures renders a patient unresectable — all such patients in their series of 46 died within 3 years despite surgery and postoperative RT:
- **Cavernous sinus**
- **Carotid artery** (involvement beyond the carotid canal)
- **Infratemporal fossa** (deep invasion)
- **Paraspinous musculature**

Additional absolute contraindications: extensive bilateral disease, distant metastases.

## Part E: Facial Nerve Management in Temporal Bone Malignancy

### Assessment

| Finding | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Normal facial function, tumour not adjacent to nerve | LTBR with nerve preservation |
| Normal facial function, T3 disease | NTTBR/STBR with nerve skeletonisation; preserve if margins clear |
| Preoperative facial nerve palsy (partial or complete) | Nerve involved — resect and reconstruct |
| Intraoperative positive frozen section at facial nerve margin | Sacrifice nerve; immediate cable graft |
| T4 with nerve in field of resection | Sacrifice and reconstruct |

### Reconstruction of Facial Nerve

| Technique | Indication | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Primary neurorrhaphy** | Tension-free anastomosis possible | Rare in temporal bone; ends deep in temporal bone |
| **Cable graft (interpositional)** | Gap <8 cm; best results | Great auricular nerve or sural nerve |
| **Hypoglossal-facial anastomosis** | Proximal stump unavailable | Split XII–VII neurorraphy; Stell & Maran report favourable experience at Cleveland Clinic |
| **Cross-facial nerve graft** | Delayed reconstruction; can babysitter with XII–VII | Long recovery; less useful in elderly |
| **Static procedures** | Concurrent with cable grafting; mandatory in all cases per Stell & Maran | Gold weight (upper lid), fascial slings (lower face); always performed alongside cable graft as static reanimation |

## Part F: Evaluation and Management of SCC of Temporal Bone with Facial Nerve Paralysis

### Evaluation

**History:**
- Duration of facial nerve palsy (acute = tumour involvement; very acute onset may be idiopathic — but any EAC mass + FNP = malignancy until proven otherwise)
- Otalgia (deep pain = perineural invasion), otorrhoea, hearing loss, tinnitus, pulsatile mass (glomus)
- History of chronic ear disease, skin cancer of pinna/periauricular skin

**Examination:**
- EAC mass, bleeding/ulcerating lesion, polypoid mass
- Facial nerve function grading (House-Brackmann scale I–VI)
- Neck: parotid, periparotid, cervical lymphadenopathy
- Cranial nerves IX–XII (jugular foramen involvement)

**Investigations:**
1. **Audiometry** — establish baseline hearing (sensorineural vs conductive loss)
2. **CT temporal bone** (high-resolution, thin-slices) — extent of bone destruction, EAC involvement, mastoid, middle ear, petrous apex, ICA canal
3. **MRI temporal bone** (T1+Gd) — perineural spread along facial nerve, dural involvement, brain invasion, soft tissue extent in parotid/ITF
4. **FDG-PET/CT** — regional nodes, distant metastases
5. **Biopsy** — under GA; EAC biopsy (punch/cup forceps under otomicroscopy); avoid contaminating uninvolved spaces

### Management — SCC with Facial Palsy

**This finding = T4 disease (Pittsburgh)**

**Preoperative workup:**
- Confirm resectability (no cavernous sinus, no bilateral ICA)
- Carotid balloon occlusion test (BOT) if ICA near resection margin
- Multi-disciplinary review (HNS, neurosurgery, plastics, radiology)

**Surgical management:**
- **STBR** (or TTBR if petrous apex involved) + **total parotidectomy** + **selective neck dissection (levels I–III, parotid)**
- Facial nerve resected from geniculate ganglion to pes anserinus (if needed)
- Immediate facial nerve cable graft (great auricular or sural nerve) — placed at time of primary resection
- Reconstruction: ALT free flap for temporal fossa/lateral skull base defect; temporalis muscle rotation for smaller defects; obliteration of temporal bone cavity with abdominal fat or muscle

**Adjuvant treatment:**
- **Postoperative radiotherapy** (60–66 Gy) mandatory for T3–T4 disease regardless of margin status
- Concurrent cisplatin if margins positive or dural/ICA involvement
- RT fields: temporal bone + ipsilateral parotid + ipsilateral neck levels I–III/V

**Prognosis:**
| Stage | 5-Year DSS |
|-------|-----------|
| T1 | 85–100% |
| T2 | 60–80% |
| T3 | 40–55% |
| T4 | 15–30% |
| N+ | Significantly worse regardless of T stage |

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. SCC of EAC is the most common malignant tumour of the temporal bone (~60%); others: adenoid cystic, ceruminous adenocarcinoma, BCC, rhabdomyosarcoma (children), melanoma, metastases
> 2. Pittsburgh staging (Arriaga 1990): T1 = EAC only; T2 = incomplete bony erosion or limited soft tissue; T3 = full-thickness erosion / middle ear / mastoid; T4 = cochlea/ICA/dura/petrous apex/CN VII palsy/>0.5 cm soft tissue
> 3. Routes of spread: anterior (parotid via fissures of Santorini/Huschke's foramen), medial (cochlea → IAC → ICA), superior (tegmen → MCF dura), posterior (mastoid → sigmoid sinus), inferior (stylomastoid foramen → CN VII)
> 4. First echelon nodes: parotid/periparotid, preauricular, retroauricular. Second echelon: levels II, III, V
> 5. LTBR: removes EAC + TM + malleus/incus + outer mastoid; for T1 disease
> 6. STBR: removes all of LTBR + middle ear + cochlea/labyrinth + lateral petrous to ICA; for T2–T3; 5 standard osteotomies (anterior through zygomatic root, posterior through sigmoid, superior through tegmen/MCF, inferior through hypotympanum, medial through petrous lateral to ICA)
> 7. TTBR: rarely performed; includes petrous apex and ICA resection; high morbidity, no proven survival benefit over STBR + adjuvant RT
> 8. Facial nerve palsy = T4 disease; indicates nerve involvement → sacrifice + immediate cable graft reconstruction
> 9. Parotidectomy is an integral part of temporal bone resection (exposes CN VII, clears parotid nodes as first echelon)
> 10. Postoperative RT (60–66 Gy) mandatory for all T3–T4 disease; 5-year DSS 15–30% for T4

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - "Huschke's foramen and fissures of Santorini" — name these when asked about anterior spread routes; they explain why parotid is the first site of anterior extension
> - STBR osteotomy sites for MCQ: **5 cuts** — anterior (zygomatic root), posterior (sigmoid sinus), superior (tegmen), inferior (hypotympanum/jugular fossa), medial (petrous lateral to ICA)
> - Preoperative facial palsy = CN VII already involved = T4; always resect nerve and perform immediate cable graft (great auricular nerve, same operative field)
> - Carotid BOT (balloon occlusion test) is mandatory before resections that may encroach on the ICA; permanent occlusion without adequate collaterals causes stroke
> - Elective parotidectomy + selective ND (levels II–III) for all T2–T4 disease — parotid nodes are first echelon and must be cleared even if clinically N0
> - Post-STBR reconstruction: abdominal fat obliterates temporal bone cavity; ALT or rectus flap for large lateral skull base defects
> - T4 disease with preoperative paralysis — prognosis is poor (15–30% 5-year DSS); patient counselling essential; combined STBR + adjuvant CRT is the standard approach
