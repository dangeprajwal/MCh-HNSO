## Part A: Robotic Neck Dissection

### Rationale

Conventional neck dissection leaves a visible anterior cervical scar. In selected patients (especially young women, early-stage disease), a scar-free or scar-hidden approach is desirable. Robotic neck dissection achieves this via remote access incisions while maintaining oncological adequacy.

### Approaches to Robotic Neck Dissection

#### 1. Retroauricular (Hairline / Modified Facelift) Approach

**Incision:** Post-auricular sulcus extending into the hair-bearing scalp (hidden)

**Steps:**
1. Post-auricular curvilinear incision in hair-bearing scalp
2. Subcutaneous skin flap elevated over mastoid, sternomastoid, and trapezius (similar to facelift flap)
3. SMAS layer identified and preserved
4. Self-retaining retractors maintain working space
5. Da Vinci robotic arms introduced via retroauricular corridor
6. Level II–III–IV dissection performed under robotic visualisation
7. Facial nerve (marginal mandibular and cervical branches) carefully preserved

**Achievable levels:** IIA, IIB, III, IV, V (posterior triangle)

**Limitations:**
- Level I (submental/submandibular) requires separate incision or extended dissection
- Level VI (central compartment) not accessible

#### 2. Transaxillary Approach
- Arm elevated, axillary incision, subcutaneous tunnel created over clavicle
- Primarily used for thyroidectomy; can be extended for central neck dissection
- Long working distance — magnification advantage important

#### 3. Bilateral Axillo-Breast Approach (BABA)
- Two axillary and two periareolar incisions
- Gasless, subcutaneous tunnel
- Access to bilateral thyroid lobes and central compartment
- Described for bilateral thyroid cancer with central neck dissection

### Oncological Adequacy of Robotic Neck Dissection

| Parameter | Robotic | Conventional |
|-----------|---------|--------------|
| **Node yield** | Comparable (10–25 nodes) | Standard (10–25 nodes) |
| **Complication rates** | Similar | Similar |
| **Operative time** | Longer (100–150 min) | Shorter (60–90 min) |
| **Hospital stay** | Similar | Similar |
| **Cosmesis** | Superior (hidden scar) | Visible anterior scar |
| **Learning curve** | Steep (30–50 cases) | Moderate |

**Oncological equivalence** has been demonstrated in multiple series (Koh 2011, Chung 2012) for elective neck dissection in N0 disease and selected N1 disease. **Not yet standard** for bulky N2–N3 disease or extranodal extension.

### Current Status of Robotic Neck Dissection

- **Accepted:** N0/N1 elective neck dissection for thyroid cancer (central and lateral) and selected HNSCC
- **Not standard:** N2/N3 disease, salvage neck dissection, extranodal extension
- **Advantages:** Cosmesis, reduced shoulder/neck morbidity, magnified view
- **Disadvantages:** Cost, operative time, limited levels accessible, learning curve, no haptic feedback

## Part B: Robotic Thyroidectomy

### Approaches
1. **Transaxillary (gasless)** — Chung's approach; most widely used globally
2. **Bilateral axillo-breast approach (BABA)**
3. **Facelift/retroauricular approach** — Terris approach
4. **Transoral robotic thyroidectomy** — most recent; no external scar

### Transaxillary Robotic Thyroidectomy — Technique (Chung's Method)

**Patient selection:**
- Benign thyroid nodule (Bethesda III–IV, size ≤6 cm)
- Well-differentiated thyroid carcinoma (T1–T2, no extrathyroidal extension)
- Patient preference for scar-free neck
- BMI <30 recommended (technical difficulty increases with obesity)
- Not suitable: large goitre >6 cm, Graves' disease, prior neck surgery/fibrosis, T3/T4 thyroid cancer

**Steps:**
1. Arm elevated and abducted; axillary incision (5–6 cm)
2. Subcutaneous tunnel created with retractor over pectoralis major and clavicle to the neck
3. Three robotic arms introduced through axillary incision
4. Strap muscles separated by incision → thyroid gland exposed
5. Superior pole vessels ligated; superior thyroid artery divided close to thyroid
6. **Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) identification:** Medial approach from inferior; nerve traced from thoracic inlet superiorly to cricothyroid joint
7. **Parathyroid identification and preservation:** Superior and inferior parathyroids identified; blood supply preserved on pedicle
8. Berry's ligament divided; specimen removed via axillary incision
9. Drain placed

**Advantages:**
- No neck scar (axillary scar hidden in skin crease)
- Magnified 3D view for RLN and parathyroid identification
- Ergonomic operating position

**Disadvantages:**
- Longer operative time vs conventional (30–60 minutes additional)
- Limited to hemithyroidectomy or total thyroidectomy (bilateral axillary ports for total)
- Cannot perform lateral neck dissection through same port (requires additional incision)
- Nerve monitoring (IONM) is more challenging (non-standard probes needed)
- Arm pain, brachial plexus injury if over-abducted (arm >90°)

### Transoral Robotic Thyroidectomy
- Incisions in gingivobuccal sulcus (mouth) and bilateral mental regions
- Carbon dioxide insufflation to create working space
- Robot introduced transorally
- Completely scar-free on neck and body
- Most recent development; limited experience globally

### Comparison of Approaches

| Approach | Scar Location | Feasibility | Best For |
|----------|--------------|-------------|----------|
| **Conventional open** | Neck (Kocher) | All cases | Universal standard |
| **Transaxillary robotic** | Axillary skin crease | Unilateral/bilateral thyroidectomy, central ND | Young women, Asian preference |
| **BABA** | Bilateral axilla + periareolar | Total thyroidectomy + central ND | Bilateral disease, cosmesis-conscious |
| **Facelift robotic** | Post-auricular hairline | Hemithyroidectomy | Very selected |
| **Transoral robotic** | Inside mouth | Hemithyroidectomy, very selected | No external scar desired |

## Part C: Overall Status of Robotic Surgery in Head and Neck

### Current Applications

| Application | Status |
|-------------|--------|
| **TORS (oropharynx T1–T2)** | Established; FDA-approved 2009 |
| **TORS (supraglottic larynx)** | Established; FDA-approved |
| **Robotic thyroidectomy (transaxillary)** | Widely practiced in Asia/Korea; growing adoption globally |
| **Robotic neck dissection** | Selected centres; N0/N1 disease |
| **Robotic parapharyngeal space** | Emerging; case reports |
| **TORS hypopharynx** | Limited; selected centres |
| **Robotic skull base** | Investigational |

### Advantages of Robotic Surgery (General)
1. **Magnified 3D visualisation** — superior to 2D endoscopes; identifies critical structures (nerves, vessels)
2. **Tremor filtration** — precise dissection around facial nerve, RLN
3. **Wristed instruments (7 DOF)** — superior dexterity vs laparoscopic
4. **Ergonomics** — seated console; reduced surgeon fatigue
5. **Motion scaling** — allows micro-precise movements
6. **Scar minimisation** — remote access incisions
7. **Reduced blood loss and shorter hospitalisation** in selected procedures

### Disadvantages of Robotic Surgery (General)
1. **No haptic feedback** — cannot feel tissue tension; requires visual cues
2. **Very high cost** — da Vinci system: USD $1–2 million capital; consumables per case USD $1,000–3,000
3. **Steep learning curve** — 30–50 cases for TORS; 50+ for neck dissection
4. **Longer operative time** — particularly during learning curve
5. **Limited portability** — large, fixed system
6. **Single-vendor monopoly** — limited competition reduces innovation
7. **Setup time** — docking and undocking adds 20–30 min
8. **Not available in most Indian/developing country centres** — restricts access
9. **No standardised training pathway** globally

### Future Developments
- **Flexible robotic systems** (Flex® Robotic System, Medrobotics) for more distal sites
- **Single-port da Vinci SP** — single incision, articulating instruments
- **Haptic feedback** systems — under development
- **Artificial intelligence integration** — auto-identification of critical structures
- **Cost reduction** — new competitors (Versius, Senhance, CMR Surgical) entering market

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Robotic neck dissection achieves oncological equivalence to conventional neck dissection with superior cosmesis via hidden retroauricular or transaxillary incisions
> 2. Robotic neck dissection most suited for N0/N1 elective dissection; NOT standard for N2–N3 or ENE
> 3. Transaxillary robotic thyroidectomy (Chung's approach): axillary scar hidden; suitable for T1–T2 thyroid cancer with no extrathyroidal extension and BMI <30
> 4. Critical steps of robotic thyroidectomy: RLN identification from thoracic inlet superiorly, parathyroid preservation on pedicle, Berry's ligament division
> 5. Key advantages of robotic surgery: 3D magnification, tremor filtration, 7-DOF wristed instruments, remote access (cosmesis)
> 6. Key limitations: no haptic feedback (most critical), very high cost, steep learning curve, long setup time
> 7. Transoral robotic thyroidectomy is the most recent innovation — completely scar-free, access through gingivobuccal sulcus
> 8. TORS received FDA approval in 2009 for oropharyngeal cancer and supraglottic larynx

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - When asked about robotic surgery advantages, always lead with "no haptic feedback" as the key limitation — examiners test this
> - Robotic thyroidectomy is most popular in Korea/Asia where neck scar is culturally unacceptable — in India/UK, conventional thyroidectomy remains standard
> - The transaxillary approach cannot access bilateral central neck in one port (BABA needed for bilateral) — a common exam catch
> - IONM (intraoperative nerve monitoring) is technically more challenging in robotic thyroidectomy due to non-standard electrode placement — an important limitation
> - Arm abduction >90° for >2 hours risks brachial plexus traction injury — a specific complication of transaxillary robotic thyroidectomy
