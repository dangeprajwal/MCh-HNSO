## Part A: The da Vinci Robotic System

### Components
| Component | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| **Surgeon console** | Ergonomic seated workstation with 3D high-definition view; surgeon controls instrument arms with master manipulators and foot pedals |
| **Patient-side cart** | 3–4 robotic arms positioned at patient; holds endoscope and instruments |
| **Vision cart** | Processing tower for image display and recording |
| **Instruments** | Wristed EndoWrist® instruments: Maryland dissector, monopolar scissors, bipolar forceps, needle driver; articulate 7 degrees of freedom |

### Advantages Over Conventional Endoscopic Surgery
- **Tremor filtration:** Eliminates physiological hand tremor (filters movements <6 Hz)
- **Motion scaling:** 5:1 or 3:1 motion scaling — allows precise microsurgical movements
- **3D magnified visualisation:** 10× magnification, stereoscopic vision vs 2D flat screen in conventional endoscopy
- **Wristed instruments:** 7 degrees of freedom vs 4 in laparoscopic instruments; superior dexterity in confined spaces
- **Ergonomics:** Surgeon seated; reduces fatigue during long procedures

### Limitations
- **No haptic (tactile) feedback:** Surgeon cannot feel tissue tension — significant learning curve
- **Bulk of patient-side cart:** Requires careful positioning; risk of collision between arms
- **Cost:** Very high capital and running costs (consumables, maintenance)
- **Learning curve:** 30–50 cases to achieve proficiency
- **Cannot use simultaneously with other instruments** (e.g., nasopharyngoscope)
- **Single-vendor monopoly** (Intuitive Surgical) — limits innovation

## Part B: TORS — Indications

### Established Indications (FDA Approved)
1. **T1–T2 oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma** (tonsil, base of tongue, posterior pharyngeal wall) — primary indication
2. **T1–T2 supraglottic laryngeal carcinoma** — supraglottic laryngectomy
3. **T3 supraglottic carcinoma** — selected cases
4. **Benign tumours of oropharynx** — vallecular cysts, lingual tonsil hypertrophy, parapharyngeal space tumours (select)
5. **Unknown primary search** — systematic TORS exploration of BOT and tonsil

### Extended Indications (Selected Cases)
- T1–T2 hypopharyngeal cancer (pyriform sinus)
- Recurrent oropharyngeal cancer (salvage TORS)
- T3 BOT carcinoma (selected, limited depth of invasion)
- Benign skull base lesions accessible transorally

### Patient Selection Criteria — TORS Feasibility

**Anatomical (must assess at examination under anaesthesia):**
| Criterion | Favourable | Unfavourable |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| **Mouth opening** | >35 mm inter-incisal distance | <25 mm — cannot dock robot |
| **Neck mobility** | Normal extension | Fused cervical spine, kyphosis |
| **Tongue base bulk** | Normal | Macroglossia, BMI >35 |
| **Mandibular arch** | Wide | Narrow — restricts access |
| **Dentition** | Normal/edentulous | Prominent upper incisors |
| **Laryngeal view** | Grade I–II Cormack-Lehane | Grade III–IV |

**Tumour (oncological):**
| Criterion | TORS Suitable | TORS Unsuitable |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| **T-stage** | T1, T2 | T4a, T4b |
| **Depth of invasion** | ≤1 cm | >1.5 cm into BOT musculature |
| **Mandibular involvement** | None | Any mandibular involvement |
| **ICA involvement** | None | ICA within 1 cm of tumour |
| **Pre-epiglottic space** | Free | Involved |
| **Bilateral BOT** | Relative CI | High aspiration risk |

**Physiological:**
- ECOG performance status 0–1
- Adequate pulmonary reserve (aspiration risk post-op)
- No active anticoagulation
- Ability to tolerate general anaesthesia

### Absolute Contraindications to TORS
1. Trismus (<25 mm mouth opening) — cannot dock robot
2. T4 disease — not curative; requires composite resection
3. ICA encasement / involvement
4. Unresectable nodal disease (fixed, matted nodes)
5. Distant metastases (relative — palliative TORS possible)
6. Posterior pharyngeal wall tumours (bilateral) — risk of stenosis
7. Poor performance status (ECOG ≥3)

## Part C: TORS Technique — Base of Tongue Resection

### Setup and Positioning
1. General anaesthesia — oral intubation (small diameter ETT or nasal RAE tube)
2. Patient: supine, head extended, shoulder roll
3. Mouth opened with Feyh-Kastenbauer (FK) retractor or Crowe-Davis/Dingman mouth gag
4. 0° or 30° 8.5 mm HD endoscope positioned centrally
5. Two instrument arms introduced (Maryland dissector + monopolar scissors)
6. Surgeon at console 2–3 metres from patient

### Resection Steps — Base of Tongue SCC
1. **Tumour visualisation:** Identify margins under 3D magnification; mark with electrocautery
2. **Lateral margins:** Incise mucosa lateral to tumour with 2 cm margin
3. **Depth of resection:** Transect through intrinsic/extrinsic tongue musculature to achieve adequate deep margin (typically to level of hyoglossus/genioglossus junction)
4. **Lingual artery:** Identified and clips applied if encountered
5. **Haemostasis:** Combination of bipolar cautery, clips, sutures
6. **Specimen orientation:** Marked with sutures before removal
7. **Wound care:** Primary healing (no closure) or local advancement flap

### Simultaneous Neck Dissection
- Almost always performed in same sitting (upfront or staged)
- Reverse Trendelenburg position after oropharyngeal resection
- Selective neck dissection (levels I–IV or II–IV depending on subsite)
- Sentinel node biopsy being investigated

## Part D: TORS Outcomes

### Oncological Outcomes
| Study | n | Local Control | OS (2-yr) | Notes |
|-------|---|---------------|-----------|-------|
| De Almeida 2015 (multicentre) | 410 | 91% | 91% | T1–T2 OPC |
| Moore 2012 | 66 | 95% (T1/T2) | — | HPV+ had better outcomes |
| Weinstein 2010 | 47 | 95% | 96% | Stage I–II BOT |
| ORATOR 2019 | 68 (TORS arm) | Similar to RT | 85% | QOL primary endpoint |

### Functional Outcomes — The Key TORS Advantage
- **Swallowing:** Significantly superior to primary CRT (Genden 2012); median time to unrestricted oral diet 10–14 days
- **Xerostomia:** Less than CRT (parotid-sparing) — major QOL advantage
- **Trismus:** Less common than post-RT fibrosis
- **Voice:** Usually preserved (no laryngectomy, no post-RT fibrosis)
- **PEG dependency:** Lower long-term PEG rate vs CRT
- **Hospital stay:** 2–3 days

### ORATOR Trial (Nichols/Mehanna 2019) — KEY TRIAL
**Design:** Phase II RCT, 68 patients, T1–T2 N0–N2b oropharyngeal SCC

| Feature | TORS Arm | RT Arm |
|---------|----------|--------|
| Treatment | TORS ± adjuvant RT | Primary RT ± chemotherapy |
| Primary endpoint | MD Anderson Dysphagia Inventory (MDADI) at 1 year | — |
| MDADI score | 86.9 | 80.1 (p=0.042 — RT worse) |
| 2-year OS | 91% | 96% (NS) |
| Grade ≥3 toxicity | 23.5% | 61.8% |

**Conclusion:** TORS arm had significantly better swallowing function at 1 year with comparable oncological outcomes. TORS established as a valid alternative to RT for T1–T2 oropharyngeal cancer.

**ORATOR2 (ongoing):** Evaluating TORS in T1–T2 N0–N2b HPV+ OPC specifically.

## Part E: Adjuvant Therapy Decision After TORS

### Indications for Adjuvant Radiotherapy After TORS
Based on ECOG 3311 and PATHOS frameworks:

| Risk Category | Criteria | Adjuvant Treatment |
|---------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Low risk** | pT1–T2, pN0–N1, negative margins, no LVI/PNI, no ENE | Observation (no adjuvant) |
| **Intermediate risk** | pT2, pN2a–N2b without ENE, close margins | Reduced dose RT (50 Gy — de-escalation) |
| **High risk** | Positive margins, ENE, pN2–N3 | Standard RT (60 Gy) ± cisplatin |

### ECOG 3311 Trial
- Non-inferiority RCT for HPV+ OPSCC post-TORS
- Randomises intermediate-risk patients to 50 vs 60 Gy adjuvant RT
- Low-risk patients: observation arm
- High-risk patients: 60 Gy + cisplatin (standard)
- **Purpose:** De-escalate adjuvant RT dose without compromising outcomes in HPV+ disease

### PATHOS Trial (UK)
- Randomises TORS patients by risk:
  - Low risk: observation vs 50 Gy RT
  - Intermediate risk: 50 Gy vs 60 Gy RT
- Primary endpoint: swallowing function (MDADI)

## Part F: Reconstruction After TORS

Most TORS defects heal by **secondary intention** (primary healing). However, larger defects may require reconstruction:

| Defect Size | Reconstruction Option |
|-------------|----------------------|
| **T1–T2 tonsil/small BOT** | Secondary intention — 2–3 weeks healing |
| **T2–T3 BOT with significant bulk removed** | Tongue base rotational flap or radial forearm free flap (RFFF) |
| **Circumferential pharyngeal defects** | Anterolateral thigh (ALT) free flap or jejunum free flap |
| **Palate/soft palate defects** | Buccal mucosal advancement, palatal island flap, or obturator |
| **Hypopharynx post-TORS** | ALT or tubed RFFF for circumferential defects |

**Key principle:** Reconstruction should not compromise swallowing function — the key advantage of TORS. Bulky flaps can impair swallowing.

## Part G: Swallowing Rehabilitation After TORS

Described in detail as this is a combined question with TORS and swallowing assessment (PGI 2021):

### Phases of Swallowing
1. **Oral preparatory:** Mastication, bolus formation (voluntary)
2. **Oral propulsive:** Tongue propels bolus posteriorly (voluntary)
3. **Pharyngeal:** Triggered by bolus contact with anterior faucial pillars; soft palate elevates, larynx rises, epiglottis deflects, vocal folds close, pharyngeal constrictors propel bolus (involuntary)
4. **Oesophageal:** UOS relaxes, peristalsis propels bolus to stomach (involuntary)

### Assessment of Swallowing Post-TORS
| Tool | Details |
|------|---------|
| **FEES** (Fibre-optic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing) | Direct visualisation of pharyngeal and laryngeal phases; identifies aspiration before and after swallow |
| **Videofluoroscopy/MBS** (Modified Barium Swallow) | Gold standard; evaluates all phases; detects silent aspiration |
| **MDADI** | MD Anderson Dysphagia Inventory — validated QOL questionnaire |
| **PSS-HN** | Performance Status Scale for Head and Neck Cancer — eating/speaking/normalcy of diet |
| **EAT-10** | Eating Assessment Tool — 10-item validated questionnaire |

### Management of Aspiration at 2 Weeks Post-TORS BOT Resection
1. **NPO and NG tube** — nil by mouth, nasogastric feeding
2. **FEES/MBS** — characterise aspiration (pre/post-swallow, silent)
3. **SLP input** — swallowing exercises; supraglottic swallow technique; chin tuck manoeuvre
4. **Thickened fluids** — if thin liquids aspirated
5. **PEG consideration** — if aspiration severe and prolonged (>4 weeks)
6. **Avoid** CRT in setting of active aspiration (will worsen)
7. **Tracheostomy** — rarely required; consider if aspiration pneumonia
8. **Review resection extent** — if bilateral BOT or >50% resected, aspiration risk is high

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. TORS uses the da Vinci robotic system: key advantages are 3D magnification, 7-DOF wristed instruments, tremor filtration — key limitation is no haptic feedback
> 2. Indications: T1–T2 oropharyngeal SCC (tonsil, BOT), supraglottic larynx T1–T2, unknown primary workup
> 3. Case selection is critical: mouth opening >35 mm required, no mandibular/ICA involvement, T stage ≤T2
> 4. ORATOR trial (2019): TORS vs RT — TORS arm had significantly better swallowing at 1 year (MDADI 86.9 vs 80.1); comparable 2-year OS
> 5. Adjuvant therapy after TORS stratified by risk: low risk = observation, intermediate = de-escalated RT (50 Gy), high risk = 60 Gy ± cisplatin
> 6. ECOG 3311 and PATHOS are key de-escalation trials evaluating adjuvant RT dose reduction after TORS
> 7. Most TORS wounds heal by secondary intention; larger defects need RFFF or ALT free flap
> 8. Swallowing assessment: FEES (direct laryngoscopic view), MBS/videofluoroscopy (gold standard), MDADI questionnaire
> 9. Aspiration at 2 weeks post-TORS: NPO + NG feeding, FEES/MBS, SLP therapy, supraglottic swallow technique
> 10. HPV+ status is the dominant prognostic factor in TORS outcomes — most T1–T2 HPV+ patients do very well regardless of treatment modality

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - The greatest advantage of TORS over CRT is swallowing function and xerostomia avoidance — quote the ORATOR trial MDADI scores
> - "No haptic feedback" is the single most important limitation of robotic surgery — always state this in any TORS answer
> - Trismus is the most common reason TORS is technically impossible — always examine mouth opening at pre-operative assessment
> - ICA within 1 cm of tumour = absolute contraindication (catastrophic haemorrhage if entered)
> - TORS for unknown primary: systematic bilateral tonsillectomy + BOT mucosectomy — identifies ~50–80% of previously undetected primaries
> - Post-TORS aspiration: chin tuck manoeuvre closes the posterior laryngeal inlet, compensating for reduced epiglottic deflection after BOT resection
> - The bilateral BOT resection is high risk for aspiration — discuss with patient pre-operatively; may need temporary tracheostomy
