# Basal Cell Carcinoma — Advances in Management

> [!question] Questions Covered
> - Discuss briefly the advances in the management of basal cell carcinomas (AMRITA 2017)

**Source:** Harrison — Head and Neck Cancer: A Multidisciplinary Approach, Chapter 23a (Basal and Squamous Cell Skin Cancers: General Principles and Management); Stell and Maran's Head and Neck Surgery and Oncology, Chapter 19 (Thyroid and Parathyroid Gland Pathology — BCC section); Jatin Shah — Head and Neck Surgery and Oncology

> [!cite] Landmark Articles

Zitelli et al. demonstrated that 4 mm margins achieve 95% confidence tumour clearance for well-circumscribed NMSCs. Mohs micrographic surgery cure rates were established in large studies cited by Harrison.

## Part A: Epidemiology and Significance

- **Most common cancer** in humans — more common than all other cancers combined in most Western countries
- USA: **800,000–900,000 BCCs per year** (American Cancer Society estimate; Harrison)
- Whites have a **30% lifetime risk** of developing a BCC; those with prior BCC have 35–50% chance of second tumour within 5 years
- Incidence increased ~100% in the last decade; still <1% of cancer deaths (low mortality, high morbidity)
- **Head and neck = >80%** of all BCC presentations — most common site
- Males > females; incidence increases markedly with age

## Part B: Cell of Origin and Molecular Pathology

### Origin

BCCs arise from **pluripotent cells in the basal layer of the epidermis** (also appendageal structures — hair follicle outer root sheath) — NOT from keratinocytes differentiated toward keratinisation.

### Sonic Hedgehog (SHH) Pathway — The Key Pathway in BCC

The defining molecular event in BCC is dysregulation of the **Sonic Hedgehog (SHH) signalling pathway** (Harrison):

```
Normal state:
PTCH1 (receptor) — INHIBITS → SMO (smoothened)
→ No downstream transcription → No cell proliferation

BCC (mutated state):
PTCH1 mutated/lost → SMO uninhibited → continuous activation
→ Gli1/Gli2 transcription factors activated → cell proliferation → BCC
```

- **PTCH1 gene** (chromosome 9q) — tumour suppressor; encodes patched receptor; mutations found in >90% of BCCs
- **SMO** — transmembrane protein; the target for hedgehog inhibitors
- **p53 mutations** — common in skin cancers; more prevalent in SCC than BCC (Harrison)

## Part C: Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Mechanism |
|-------------|----------|
| **UV radiation (UVB > UVA)** | DNA pyrimidine dimers → PTCH1, p53 mutations; cumulative + intense exposure; Fitzpatrick skin types I–II at highest risk |
| **Ionising radiation** | Prior RT; BCC risk increased 3.3× in previously irradiated skin (Lichter); especially at low-dose edges |
| **Gorlin syndrome** (basal cell nevus syndrome) | Autosomal dominant; PTCH1 germline mutation; prevalence 1/100,000; presents in early adulthood with **multiple BCCs, palmar/plantar pitting, craniofacial abnormalities, bifid ribs**; 3–5% risk of medulloblastoma |
| **Xeroderma pigmentosum** | Defective nucleotide excision repair; photosensitivity; multiple skin cancers from childhood |
| **Chemical carcinogens** | Arsenic, coal tar |
| **Immunosuppression** | Organ transplant recipients; AIDS |
| **Smoking, HPV** | Contributing factors (Harrison) |

## Part D: Clinical Variants

| Variant | Clinical Features | Behaviour |
|---------|-----------------|----------|
| **Nodular** (most common) | Pearly, translucent papule; raised rolled edges; telangiectases; central ulceration ("rodent ulcer") | Locally aggressive; low metastatic risk |
| **Superficial** | Erythematous macule/patch; scaly surface; trunk/limbs | Less aggressive; field change |
| **Morpheaform / Sclerotic** | Scar-like plaque; pale; ill-defined borders | **Most aggressive**; subclinical extension; highest recurrence risk |
| **Infiltrative** | Ill-defined borders; deeper invasion | Aggressive; high recurrence |
| **Basosquamous (metatypical)** | Mixed BCC and SCC features | **Higher metastatic potential** than pure BCC; treat as SCC |

## Part E: Treatment Modalities — Including Advances

### 1. Curettage and Electrodesiccation

- Curette removes tumour (less resistance than normal tissue); 3 cycles of curettage + electrodesiccation
- 5-year cure rates up to **96%** for small primary BCC in low-risk areas
- **No histological margin assessment** — disadvantage
- Not suitable for: hair-bearing skin, morpheaform/infiltrative BCC, subcutaneous extension, high-risk locations

### 2. Standard Surgical Excision

- **4 mm margins** for well-circumscribed BCC <2 cm → 95% confidence clearance (Zitelli)
- **4–6 mm margins** for high-risk lesions; 1 cm for lesions >2 cm (NCCN)
- 5-year cure rates: **99%** for trunk/limb BCC; **97%** for head BCC <6 mm; **92%** for head BCC >6 mm (Harrison)
- Closure with skin graft or flap (tissue rearrangement only after intraoperative margin confirmation)

### 3. Mohs Micrographic Surgery (MMS) — THE KEY ADVANCE

**Dr. Frederick Mohs (1930s)** — described the technique; a general surgeon at the University of Wisconsin.

**Principle:**
- Removal of tumour with narrow margin under local anaesthesia
- Specimen **mapped** with coloured inks (hatches mark tissue orientation relative to patient)
- Processed as **fresh frozen sections** such that **100% of peripheral and deep margins** are visualised in a single section
- Traditional "bread-loafing" pathology visualises <1% of the margin

**Procedure:**
1. Excise tumour with narrow margin + map with coloured dye
2. Horizontal sectioning → examine all peripheral + deep margins
3. If positive margin found → map its location → excise additional tissue only from positive area
4. Repeat until all margins clear

**Advantages (Harrison):**
- **Highest cure rates** of any NMSC treatment:
  - Primary BCC: **99%** (vs 90–93% standard excision)
  - Recurrent BCC: **94.4%** (vs 80.1% standard)
- **Tissue sparing** — only positive areas re-excised → smallest possible defect
- Simultaneous assessment during surgery (no waiting for histology)

**Indications for Mohs Surgery (high-risk BCC):**
- Recurrent BCC
- Morpheaform/infiltrative/sclerosing BCC (ill-defined borders)
- Perineural invasion
- High-risk locations: central face (nose, periorbital, ears, lips, temples)
- Large tumours (>2 cm on face; >4 cm on trunk)
- Immunosuppressed patient
- Gorlin syndrome

### 4. Radiotherapy

- 5-year local control rates **>90%** for well-selected BCC (Harrison)
- Indicated for: elderly patients unsuitable for surgery; patients declining surgery; large BCCs in difficult locations; adjuvant after positive margin in re-excision surgery
- **Limitations:** Stimulates further BCC formation in field; cannot be used in Gorlin syndrome (RT-induced BCCs); poor cosmetic outcome over time
- **Adjuvant RT for perineural invasion:** pPNI → 90% local control; cPNI → 57% local control (Harrison: Jackson series, 118 patients)

### 5. Topical Therapies

**Imiquimod 5% cream:**
- Mechanism: binds Toll-like receptor 7 → innate + adaptive immune activation → IFN-α, IL-6, TNF-α → tumour cell death
- For **superficial BCC**: once-daily × 6 weeks → 77–93% histologic clearance at 12 weeks; 79% free of disease at 24 months
- For nodular BCC: significantly lower clearance (42–76%) — not recommended
- **Not recommended** for: invasive/recurrent/morpheaform BCC or high-risk locations
- Side effects: local erythema, erosion (can be marked)

**Photodynamic Therapy (PDT):**
- Photosensitizer (ALA or methyl-ALA) → protoporphyrin IX in tumour → visible light → reactive oxygen species → tumour necrosis
- Topical MAL-PDT: superficial BCC 82% clearance; nodular BCC 33% clearance; ineffective for morpheaform
- Reserved for superficial BCC when surgery/RT not feasible; not standard care

### 6. Vismodegib (GDC-0449) — MAJOR ADVANCE — Hedgehog Pathway Inhibitor

**Mechanism (Harrison):**
> *"GDC-0449 or vismodegib is a small molecule inhibitor of the SMO protein."*

- Directly binds and inhibits **SMO** → blocks downstream GLI1/GLI2 transcription → arrests BCC cell proliferation
- **FDA approved (2012)** for locally advanced or metastatic BCC

**ERIVANCE BCC trial (Sekulic 2012, NEJM):**
- **Locally advanced BCC:** 43% objective response rate
- **Metastatic BCC:** 30% objective response rate
- 2 complete responders; 16 partial responders (≥50% reduction in visible/palpable tumour)

**Side effects:** Muscle spasms, alopecia, dysgeusia (altered taste), weight loss, fatigue, nausea, anorexia, diarrhoea

**Indications:**
- Locally advanced BCC not amenable to surgery or RT
- Metastatic BCC
- Gorlin syndrome with multiple BCCs (limited by side effects)

**Sonidegib (LDE225):**
- Second-generation SMO inhibitor (FDA approved 2015)
- Similar mechanism; oral; once daily; for locally advanced BCC after surgery/RT failure

### 7. Summary of Treatment Advances

| Innovation | What Changed |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Mohs micrographic surgery** | 100% margin examination → highest cure rates; tissue-sparing for complex HN defects |
| **Vismodegib (hedgehog inhibitor)** | First systemic targeted therapy for BCC; addresses unresectable/metastatic disease previously without effective treatment |
| **Imiquimod 5%** | Non-surgical option for superficial BCC; immune-mediated clearance |
| **PDT (photodynamic therapy)** | Minimally invasive; for superficial BCC in patients unsuitable for surgery |
| **Sentinel node biopsy** | Now validated for basosquamous carcinoma and high-risk BCC with perineural invasion (evolving evidence) |
| **Cetuximab** | Emerging role for SCC with EGFR overexpression; less established for BCC |

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. BCC arises from basal/appendageal pluripotent cells; most common human cancer; >80% on head and neck; Whites 30% lifetime risk
> 2. Molecular basis: PTCH1 mutation (chr 9q) → uninhibited SMO → continuous SHH pathway activation → Gli transcription factors → BCC; target for vismodegib
> 3. Gorlin syndrome: PTCH1 germline mutation; AD; multiple BCCs + palmar pitting + craniofacial abnormalities + 3-5% medulloblastoma risk; avoid RT (causes more BCCs)
> 4. Morpheaform/sclerotic BCC = most aggressive variant; ill-defined borders; subclinical extension; mandates Mohs or wide margin excision
> 5. Standard excision: 4 mm margins → 95% confidence clearance for BCC <2 cm; 99% 5-yr cure on trunk/limbs
> 6. Mohs micrographic surgery: 100% peripheral + deep margin examined via horizontal frozen sections; 99% 5-yr cure for primary BCC; 94.4% for recurrent; highest cure rate of any technique
> 7. Vismodegib (SMO inhibitor): 43% response in locally advanced; 30% in metastatic; side effects: muscle spasms, alopecia, dysgeusia; FDA-approved 2012 (Harrison)
> 8. Imiquimod 5%: TLR-7 agonist; 77-93% clearance for superficial BCC at 12 weeks; not for nodular/morpheaform/high-risk
> 9. Adjuvant RT for perineural invasion: pathological PNI (pPNI) → 90% local control; clinical PNI (cPNI) → 57% local control
> 10. Basosquamous (metatypical) BCC: both BCC and SCC features; higher metastatic potential; treat as SCC

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - "The advances in management of BCC" = **Mohs surgery** (tissue-sparing + highest cure) + **vismodegib** (SMO inhibitor for advanced/metastatic) + **imiquimod** (superficial BCC) — these 3 cover the question completely
> - Vismodegib = hedgehog pathway, SMO inhibitor — memorise: SHH → PTCH1 inhibits SMO → BCC when PTCH1 mutated → vismodegib blocks SMO
> - Gorlin syndrome + RT = contraindication — RT causes florid BCC formation in these patients; Mohs surgery is the preferred approach
> - Mohs "bread-loafing" vs "horizontal sections" — standard pathology bread-loafs (vertical) and examines <1% of margin; Mohs horizontal sections examine 100%
> - Morpheaform BCC: the dangerous one — scar-like, ill-defined, subclinical extension, highest recurrence; always treat with Mohs or wide margin
