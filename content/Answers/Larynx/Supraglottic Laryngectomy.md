## Anatomy of the Supraglottis

The supraglottis extends from the tip of the epiglottis superiorly to the floor of the laryngeal ventricle inferiorly. It contains the following structures:

### Subsites (AJCC)

| Subsite | Anatomical Boundaries |
|---|---|
| Suprahyoid epiglottis | Lingual (anterior) and laryngeal (posterior) surfaces above the hyoid bone |
| Infrahyoid epiglottis | Laryngeal surface below the hyoid; includes petiole |
| Aryepiglottic folds | Lateral mucosal folds from epiglottis to arytenoid cartilages (laryngeal aspect) |
| Arytenoids | Arytenoid cartilages and surrounding mucosa |
| False vocal cords (ventricular bands) | Paired folds above the true vocal cords, separated by the ventricle |

### Key Anatomical Features

- **Pre-epiglottic space (PES):** Fibrofatty space bounded by the epiglottis posteriorly, hyoid bone and thyrohyoid membrane anteriorly, and hyoepiglottic ligament superiorly. Routes of spread include through the epiglottic foramina and via the thyroepiglottic ligament.
- **Paraglottic space:** Lateral extension pathway; communicates with the pre-epiglottic space anteriorly
- **Rich lymphatic drainage:** Level II and III nodes bilaterally; accounts for high nodal metastasis rates (25--50% at presentation)
- **Embryology:** Supraglottis derives from the buccopharyngeal anlage (4th and 6th branchial arches); separate from the glottis (tracheobronchial anlage) -- this embryologic division creates a barrier at the ventricle

## Open Supraglottic Laryngectomy (Horizontal Partial Laryngectomy)

### Historical Background

- Alonso (1947) described the first horizontal supraglottic laryngectomy
- Ogura (1958) popularised the technique in the United States
- Based on the embryologic principle that the supraglottis and glottis develop separately, with the ventricle serving as a natural barrier

### Indications

| Criterion | Details |
|---|---|
| T-stage | T1-T2 supraglottic; select T3 with pre-epiglottic space invasion without cartilage destruction |
| Subsites | Suprahyoid and infrahyoid epiglottis, aryepiglottic folds, false cords |
| Pulmonary function | Adequate reserve (FEV1 >50% predicted; ability to climb two flights of stairs) |
| Age | Physiological rather than chronological; generally <70 years |
| Neurological | Intact swallowing reflexes; no neurological conditions causing dysphagia |
| Nutritional status | Adequate nutrition; BMI >18.5 |

### Absolute Contraindications

1. **Fixed vocal cord** (arytenoid fixation) -- indicates deep extension to the cricoarytenoid joint
2. **Thyroid cartilage invasion** -- extralaryngeal spread
3. **Cartilage invasion of cricoid or arytenoid** -- deep extension
4. **Subglottic extension >1 cm** anteriorly or >5 mm posteriorly
5. **Impaired pulmonary function** -- FEV1 <50% predicted; severe COPD
6. **Apex of pyriform sinus involvement** -- close to the cricoarytenoid joint
7. **Bilateral arytenoid involvement**
8. **Posterior commissure involvement**

### Relative Contraindications

1. **Age >70 years** (higher aspiration risk)
2. **Pre-epiglottic space invasion** extending to the anterior commissure
3. **Base of tongue extension** requiring >1 cm resection of tongue base
4. **Prior radiation** to the larynx (impaired healing, increased aspiration)
5. **Poor general condition** or significant comorbidities

### Surgical Technique (Open Approach)

#### Preparation and Incision

1. **Tracheostomy** performed first (temporary; usually decannulated within 2 weeks)
2. **Apron flap** or horizontal skin incision with subplatysmal flaps
3. **Bilateral selective neck dissection** (levels II-IV) performed first or simultaneously

#### Steps of Resection

1. **Expose the laryngeal skeleton:** Identify the thyroid cartilage, hyoid bone, and thyrohyoid membrane
2. **Release suprahyoid muscles** from the superior border of the hyoid
3. **Enter the vallecula:** Incise the mucosa of the vallecula from above the hyoid, opening the pre-epiglottic space
4. **Section the hyoid bone:** Divide the hyoid laterally at the junction of the body and greater cornu bilaterally
5. **Pharyngotomy:** Enter the pharynx at the vallecula; visualise the tumour endoscopically to confirm margins
6. **Mucosal incision:** Cut along the aryepiglottic folds laterally, preserving the arytenoid cartilages
7. **Inferior cut:** Made at the level of the ventricle -- the critical cut that separates supraglottis from glottis; must preserve the true vocal cords and arytenoids
8. **Remove the specimen:** En bloc removal of the epiglottis, pre-epiglottic space, false cords, ventricles, and aryepiglottic folds
9. **Frozen section margins:** Verify at all margins (superior, lateral, inferior)

#### Reconstruction and Closure

1. **Pexy (laryngeal suspension):** Suture the residual larynx (thyroid cartilage) to the base of the tongue and the mandible using non-absorbable sutures -- critical for swallowing rehabilitation
2. **Mucosal closure:** Approximate the tongue base mucosa to the remaining laryngeal mucosa
3. **Drain placement:** Closed suction drains bilaterally
4. **NG tube** for postoperative feeding (10--14 days minimum)

## Endoscopic Supraglottic Laryngectomy (ESGL)

### Technique

- Performed transorally using CO2 laser with the operating microscope
- Adequate laryngoscopic exposure is essential (Steiner direct laryngoscopy with distending laryngoscope)
- Tumour resected piecemeal or en bloc depending on size and exposure
- Uses the same oncologic principles as open surgery but with transoral access

### ELS Classification for Endoscopic Supraglottic Laryngectomy

The European Laryngological Society (ELS) classification system standardises the extent of endoscopic supraglottic resection:

| ELS Type | Description | Structures Resected |
|---|---|---|
| Type I | Suprahyoid epiglottectomy | Suprahyoid epiglottis only |
| Type IIa | Supraglottic laryngectomy (median) | Epiglottis + pre-epiglottic space |
| Type IIb | Supraglottic laryngectomy (lateral) | Type IIa + one aryepiglottic fold |
| Type III | Supraglottic laryngectomy (extended) | Epiglottis + PES + both AE folds + false cords; one arytenoid may be resected |
| Type IV | Extended to base of tongue or pyriform sinus | Type III + base of tongue or pyriform mucosa |

### Advantages of ESGL Over Open SGL

| Parameter | Endoscopic (ESGL) | Open SGL |
|---|---|---|
| Tracheostomy | Often avoided (30--50% rate) | Always required |
| Hospital stay | 5--7 days | 12--21 days |
| NG tube duration | 5--7 days | 10--14 days |
| Aspiration severity | Less severe, shorter duration | More severe, longer recovery |
| Swallowing rehabilitation | Faster (1--2 weeks) | Slower (2--4 weeks) |
| Voice quality | Preserved | Preserved |
| Cosmetic outcome | No external scar | Neck scar |
| Oncologic outcomes | Equivalent | Reference standard |
| Limitation | Requires adequate exposure | Not exposure-dependent |

### Selection for Endoscopic vs Open

- **ESGL preferred:** T1-T2 supraglottic tumours with adequate laryngoscopic exposure
- **Open SGL preferred:** T3 with pre-epiglottic space invasion; poor endoscopic exposure; base of tongue extension; need for bilateral neck dissection (can be staged with ESGL)

## Neck Management

### Rationale

- Supraglottic SCC has high nodal metastasis rates: 25--50% at presentation
- Even in the clinically N0 neck, occult metastases are present in 20--30%
- Bilateral nodal drainage is common due to midline location of the supraglottis

### Neck Dissection Protocol

| Neck Status | Recommended Management |
|---|---|
| cN0 (bilateral) | Bilateral selective neck dissection (levels II-IV) |
| cN+ (unilateral) | Ipsilateral modified radical ND + contralateral selective ND (II-IV) |
| cN+ (bilateral) | Bilateral modified radical ND (levels I-V) |

### Level II Considerations

- Level IIb (submuscular recess) is the most common site of nodal metastasis from supraglottic primaries
- Spinal accessory nerve should be preserved unless directly involved by tumour

## Complications

### Aspiration -- The Most Common and Most Important Complication

- **Incidence:** Nearly universal in the immediate postoperative period (40--70%); most patients learn compensatory swallowing within 2--4 weeks
- **Mechanism:** Loss of the epiglottis and false cords removes the supraglottic sphincteric mechanism; the true cords and arytenoids provide the only remaining airway protection
- **Risk factors for prolonged aspiration:**
  - Age >65 years
  - Poor pulmonary function
  - Base of tongue resection
  - Arytenoid resection (extended procedure)
  - Postoperative radiation therapy
  - Prior stroke or neurological disease

### Other Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Prolonged aspiration requiring TL | 2--5% | Total laryngectomy as salvage |
| Wound infection | 5--10% | Antibiotics, drainage |
| Pharyngocutaneous fistula | 3--5% | Conservative or surgical repair |
| Laryngeal stenosis | 2--5% | Dilatation or laryngeal stenting |
| Chondritis | 2--3% | Antibiotics, debridement |
| Haemorrhage | 1--3% | Return to theatre |

### Decannulation

- **Temporary tracheostomy** required in open SGL; usually decannulated within 7--14 days
- **Criteria for decannulation:** Adequate airway on laryngoscopy; toleration of capped tracheostomy for 24--48 hours; ability to manage secretions
- **Decannulation rate:** 90--96% (Herranz-Gonzalez 1996)

## Functional Rehabilitation

### Swallowing Rehabilitation Timeline

| Phase | Time Frame | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| NPO phase | Days 0--7 (ESGL) or 0--14 (open) | NG/PEG feeding; oral hygiene |
| Trial swallowing | Days 7--14 | Modified barium swallow to assess aspiration; begin with thick liquids |
| Graded diet | Weeks 2--4 | Progress from thick to thin liquids; soft solids |
| Compensatory strategies | Weeks 1--4 | Supraglottic swallow; chin tuck; head rotation |
| Full oral diet | Weeks 4--12 | Most patients achieve unrestricted diet |

### Supraglottic Swallow Technique

The patient is trained to:
1. Take a deep breath and hold it (closes the true vocal cords)
2. Place food/liquid in the mouth
3. Swallow while holding breath
4. Immediately cough after swallowing (clears residual material from the laryngeal vestibule)
5. Swallow again

## Extended Supraglottic Laryngectomy

### Types of Extension

| Extension | Additional Resection | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral (to pyriform) | Medial wall of pyriform sinus | Tumour extending to AE fold / medial pyriform |
| Anterior (to tongue base) | Portion of tongue base (up to 1 cm) | Vallecula / tongue base extension |
| Inferior (to one arytenoid) | One arytenoid cartilage | Tumour involving one arytenoid |
| Three-quarter laryngectomy | One arytenoid + ipsilateral cord | More extensive lateral extension |

### Functional Impact of Extension

- Arytenoid resection: significantly increases aspiration risk and duration
- Base of tongue resection: worsens bolus propulsion and swallowing
- Pyriform extension: minimal additional functional impact if medial wall only

## Comparison with Chemoradiotherapy (CRT)

| Parameter | Supraglottic Laryngectomy | CRT (70 Gy + cisplatin) |
|---|---|---|
| Local control (T1-T2) | 85--95% | 85--90% |
| Laryngeal preservation | Partial larynx retained | Whole larynx retained (if responding) |
| Voice | Preserved (near-normal) | Preserved (may be affected by RT) |
| Swallowing | Temporary aspiration; long-term good | Late dysphagia 20--30%; [[Answers/General/Xerostomia Management|xerostomia]] |
| Tracheostomy | Temporary (1--2 weeks) | Not usually required |
| Feeding tube | Temporary (1--2 weeks) | Often prolonged (weeks to months) |
| Salvage options | CRT if recurrence | Salvage laryngectomy (higher morbidity) |
| Neck control | ND provides pathological staging | Clinical/radiological assessment |

## Key Points for Exam

1. **Open supraglottic laryngectomy** is based on the embryologic principle of separate supraglottic and glottic development with the ventricle as the barrier
2. **Adequate pulmonary function** (FEV1 >50%) is essential -- aspiration is universal postoperatively
3. **Fixed vocal cord** is an absolute contraindication (indicates cricoarytenoid joint involvement)
4. **Bilateral selective neck dissection** (levels II-IV) is mandatory even in cN0 due to high occult metastasis rates (20--30%)
5. **Aspiration** is the most common complication; most patients compensate within 2--4 weeks using the supraglottic swallow technique
6. **ESGL** achieves equivalent oncologic outcomes with less morbidity, shorter hospital stay, and faster swallowing recovery
7. **ELS classification** standardises endoscopic supraglottic resection from Type I (suprahyoid epiglottectomy) to Type IV (extended to BOT/pyriform)
8. **Extended supraglottic laryngectomy** with arytenoid resection significantly increases aspiration risk
9. **Laryngeal suspension (pexy)** to the tongue base and mandible is critical for postoperative swallowing rehabilitation
10. **Decannulation rate** is >95% in properly selected patients
