## Introduction

Swallowing dysfunction is one of the most significant functional consequences of treatment for laryngeal and laryngopharyngeal cancer, whether the treatment modality is surgery, radiation, chemoradiation, or a combination. The larynx plays a critical role in airway protection during swallowing through three mechanisms: epiglottic inversion, false vocal fold approximation, and true vocal fold closure. Any treatment that disrupts these mechanisms — or the sensory feedback pathways that trigger them — will impair swallowing safety and efficiency. Assessment and rehabilitation of swallowing function is therefore an integral component of the treatment plan for every patient with laryngeal cancer.

---

## Impact of Treatment on Swallowing

### Surgery versus Chemoradiation: Different Mechanisms of Dysphagia

| Feature | Surgical Dysphagia | CRT-Related Dysphagia |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Onset** | Immediate (anatomical alteration) | Delayed (weeks to months; may worsen over years) |
| **Mechanism** | Loss of structures; altered anatomy; scar contracture | Fibrosis; edema; neuropathy; xerostomia; muscular atrophy |
| **Aspiration type** | Related to reduced airway closure | Often silent aspiration (sensory neuropathy) |
| **Structures affected** | Depends on resection extent | Pharyngeal constrictors, supraglottic larynx, glottic larynx (DARS) |
| **Progression** | Tends to improve with adaptation | May progressively worsen (late-onset fibrosis) |
| **Reversibility** | Compensatory strategies effective | Often irreversible; exercise-based mitigation |
| **Xerostomia** | Not typically a factor | Major contributing factor |

## Aspiration after Chemoradiation

### Pathophysiology

Concurrent chemoradiation for advanced laryngeal cancer (T3-T4) causes dysphagia through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Radiation fibrosis:** Progressive fibrosis of pharyngeal constrictors, suprahyoid muscles, and intrinsic laryngeal muscles reduces pharyngeal contraction and laryngeal elevation
2. **Sensory neuropathy:** Damage to the superior laryngeal nerve (internal branch) and pharyngeal plexus impairs the afferent limb of the swallowing reflex, leading to silent aspiration
3. **Laryngeal edema:** Persistent supraglottic and arytenoid edema impairs epiglottic inversion and vocal fold closure
4. **Xerostomia:** Reduced saliva production impairs bolus formation and lubrication
5. **Trismus:** Radiation-induced fibrosis of masticator muscles limits oral preparatory phase
6. **Esophageal dysmotility:** Radiation to the proximal esophagus impairs peristalsis and lower esophageal sphincter function

### Silent Aspiration

Silent aspiration (aspiration without cough) is a particularly dangerous consequence of CRT:

- Occurs in 20-40% of patients after CRT for advanced laryngeal cancer
- Results from sensory denervation — the patient cannot feel aspirated material
- Cannot be detected by clinical bedside swallowing evaluation alone
- Requires instrumental assessment (FEES or VFSS) for diagnosis
- Leads to aspiration pneumonia — a significant cause of morbidity and mortality in this population

### Assessment of Micro-aspiration after CRT

#### Clinical Assessment
- **History:** Coughing with liquids, wet/gurgly voice quality, recurrent pneumonia, weight loss, avoidance of specific food textures
- **Bedside swallowing evaluation (BSE):** Water swallow test, cough reflex testing — sensitivity is low (40-60%) for detecting aspiration; does NOT reliably detect silent aspiration
- **Voice quality assessment:** Wet dysphonia suggests laryngeal penetration

#### Instrumental Assessment

**FEES (Fiberoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing):**
- Gold standard for assessing laryngopharyngeal anatomy and physiology post-CRT
- Directly visualizes pooling, penetration, aspiration, and residue
- Assesses secretion management, laryngeal sensation (with air pulse — FEESST), and vocal fold mobility
- Can be performed at bedside; no radiation exposure
- Limitations: Cannot visualize the oral phase or the moment of swallow (white-out)

**VFSS/MBS (Videofluoroscopic Swallowing Study / Modified Barium Swallow):**
- Allows real-time radiographic visualization of all phases of swallowing
- Uses barium-coated boluses of varying consistency (thin liquid, nectar-thick, puree, solid)
- Quantifies aspiration using the Penetration-Aspiration Scale (PAS; Rosenbek et al., 1996):

| PAS Score | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | Material does not enter the airway |
| 2 | Material enters above vocal folds, is ejected |
| 3 | Material enters above vocal folds, is not ejected |
| 4 | Material contacts vocal folds, is ejected |
| 5 | Material contacts vocal folds, is not ejected |
| 6 | Material passes below vocal folds, is ejected |
| 7 | Material passes below vocal folds, is not ejected (aspiration with effort to eject) |
| 8 | Material passes below vocal folds, no effort to eject (silent aspiration) |

### Management of Post-CRT Aspiration

| Strategy | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Diet modification** | Thickened liquids (nectar/honey consistency); soft solids; avoidance of thin liquids and mixed textures |
| **Compensatory postures** | Chin tuck (reduces risk of premature spillage); head rotation to weaker side; side-lying position |
| **Swallowing maneuvers** | Supraglottic swallow (voluntary breath hold before and during swallow); super-supraglottic swallow (breath hold with Valsalva); Mendelsohn maneuver (prolonged laryngeal elevation) |
| **Exercises** | Shaker exercise (isometric/isotonic head lift); effortful swallow; tongue-hold (Masako) maneuver; CTAR (chin tuck against resistance) |
| **Medical** | PPI for gastric reflux contributing to aspiration; oral care to reduce bacterial load; pneumococcal vaccination |
| **Surgical** | Laryngeal suspension; medialization thyroplasty for glottic incompetence; total laryngectomy for intractable aspiration (salvage) |
| **Nutritional support** | PEG tube for aspiration-unsafe patients; see [[Answers/General/Nutritional Assessment and PEG Feeding|Nutritional Assessment and PEG Feeding]] |

## Post-Laryngectomy Dysphagia

### Causes

Although total laryngectomy eliminates aspiration risk (complete separation of airway and digestive tract), patients commonly experience dysphagia from structural and functional alterations:

| Cause | Mechanism | Incidence |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Neopharyngeal stenosis** | Stricture at the pharyngeal closure site; radiation fibrosis | 15-30% |
| **Pseudodiverticulum** | Mucosal pouch at the tongue base-neopharynx junction; food trapping | 10-20% |
| **Pseudovallecula** | Blind pouch at the tongue base; residue retention | 10-15% |
| **Pseudoepiglottis** | Prolapsing mucosal fold at the tongue base; bolus obstruction | 5-10% |
| **Cricopharyngeal bar/spasm** | Hypertonic UES remnant; functional obstruction | 10-15% |
| **Fistula/recurrence** | Persistent pharyngocutaneous fistula or local recurrence | Variable |
| **Esophageal dysmotility** | Radiation-induced impaired peristalsis | 20-30% |

### Evaluation

- **VFSS:** Essential first-line investigation — identifies the level and nature of obstruction, stricture, diverticulum, or dysmotility
- **Rigid or flexible esophagoscopy:** Allows direct visualization and potential therapeutic intervention (dilation, biopsy)
- **CT scan with oral contrast:** For suspected fistula, recurrence, or extrinsic compression

### Management

| Problem | Treatment |
|---------|-----------|
| **Neopharyngeal stenosis** | Serial endoscopic dilation (Savary bougie or balloon); intralesional triamcinolone injection; salvage revision surgery with flap |
| **Pseudodiverticulum** | Observation if asymptomatic; surgical excision if causing significant food trapping |
| **Pseudoepiglottis** | Surgical excision (laser or cold steel); may require reattachment of suprahyoid muscles |
| **Cricopharyngeal bar** | Botulinum toxin injection; endoscopic laser myotomy; open cricopharyngeal myotomy |
| **Esophageal stricture** | Endoscopic balloon dilation; self-expanding stent for refractory cases |

## Cricopharyngeal Myotomy

### Anatomy

The cricopharyngeus muscle forms the upper esophageal sphincter (UES). It is a C-shaped muscle arising from the lateral margins of the cricoid cartilage, wrapping posteriorly around the proximal esophagus. It maintains tonic contraction at rest (preventing air swallowing and reflux) and relaxes during swallowing to allow bolus passage.

### Indications

| Indication | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Cricopharyngeal bar** | Posterior indentation on barium swallow; incomplete UES relaxation |
| **Zenker's diverticulum** | Pulsion diverticulum arising above a non-relaxing cricopharyngeus |
| **Post-laryngectomy dysphagia** | Hypertonic UES contributing to neopharyngeal obstruction |
| **Neurogenic dysphagia** | Selected cases of oculopharyngeal muscular dystrophy, post-stroke dysphagia with intact pharyngeal contraction |
| **PE segment hypertonicity** | Failed tracheoesophageal voice due to cricopharyngeal spasm (see [[Answers/Larynx/TEP and Prosthetic Voice Rehabilitation|TEP and Prosthetic Voice Rehabilitation]]) |

### Contraindications

- **Gastroesophageal reflux:** Myotomy removes the upper sphincter barrier; may worsen reflux and aspiration
- **Poor pharyngeal contraction:** If the pharyngeal constrictors are weak (e.g., after radiation), myotomy will not improve swallowing because the driving force is insufficient
- **Esophageal dysmotility:** Poor esophageal peristalsis below the UES
- **Active malignancy:** At the cricopharyngeal level

### Technique

#### Open Cricopharyngeal Myotomy
1. **Approach:** Left lateral neck incision along the anterior border of the sternocleidomastoid
2. **Exposure:** Retract thyroid lobe and carotid sheath; identify the posterior cricoid and proximal esophagus
3. **Myotomy:** Longitudinal incision through the cricopharyngeus muscle fibers in the midline posteriorly, extending 3-5 cm (from inferior constrictor above to proximal esophageal circular muscle below)
4. **Depth:** Through all muscle layers to expose the intact submucosa/mucosa (which should bulge through the myotomy)
5. **Closure:** Drain placement; layered closure

#### Endoscopic Laser Cricopharyngeal Myotomy
1. **Approach:** Rigid laryngoscopy under general anesthesia; CO2 laser
2. **Technique:** Posterior midline incision through the cricopharyngeal bar under magnification; divides muscle fibers from the mucosal side
3. **Advantages:** Minimally invasive; shorter hospital stay; can be combined with Zenker's diverticulotomy
4. **Limitations:** Risk of mediastinitis if perforation occurs; limited depth control

#### Botulinum Toxin Injection
- **Technique:** Injection of botulinum toxin type A (25-100 units) into the cricopharyngeus under EMG guidance or endoscopic visualization
- **Advantages:** Minimally invasive; reversible; can be repeated; useful as a diagnostic trial before surgical myotomy
- **Onset:** 2-5 days; duration 3-6 months
- **Indication:** PE segment hypertonicity causing TEP speech failure (first-line treatment)

### Outcomes

- Open myotomy: Improvement in swallowing in 60-80% of appropriately selected patients
- Endoscopic myotomy: Comparable success with lower morbidity
- Botulinum toxin: Temporary improvement in 70-80%; useful for patient selection before definitive myotomy

## Swallowing after Supracricoid Partial Laryngectomy

### Surgical Context

Supracricoid partial laryngectomy (SCPL) removes the entire thyroid cartilage, true and false vocal folds, paraglottic spaces, and pre-epiglottic space while preserving at least one functional arytenoid cartilage, the cricoid cartilage, and the hyoid bone. Reconstruction is by cricohyoidopexy (CHP) or cricohyoidoepiglottopexy (CHEP). See [[Answers/Larynx/Supracricoid Laryngectomy and Conservation Surgery|Supracricoid Laryngectomy and Conservation Surgery]] for detailed surgical technique.

### Mechanism of Post-SCPL Aspiration

Aspiration is universal in the early postoperative period after SCPL because:

1. **Loss of epiglottis (CHP):** Epiglottic inversion during swallowing is absent; the arytenoid must contact the tongue base for airway protection
2. **Loss of true vocal folds:** The primary glottic sphincter is removed; neoglottic closure depends on arytenoid-to-tongue base contact
3. **Reduced laryngeal elevation:** Pexy (CHP/CHEP) limits the excursion of the residual laryngeal framework
4. **Sensory impairment:** Disruption of the internal branch of the superior laryngeal nerve reduces protective reflexes

### Aspiration Rates and Recovery Timeline

| Time Point | Aspiration Rate | Clinical Implication |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Immediate postop** | 80-100% | Expected; nasogastric tube maintained |
| **2 weeks** | 60-80% | Begin oral trials under SLP guidance |
| **1 month** | 30-50% | Most patients tolerate soft diet |
| **3 months** | 15-25% | Significant improvement; most patients on full oral diet |
| **6 months** | 5-15% | Stable; residual aspiration usually compensated |
| **12 months** | 3-10% | Final functional plateau |

### Factors Affecting Swallowing Recovery

- **Number of arytenoids preserved:** Two arytenoids yield better swallowing than one
- **Preservation of epiglottis (CHEP vs. CHP):** CHEP preserves some epiglottic protection
- **Patient age:** Older patients (>65 years) have slower recovery and higher persistent aspiration
- **Previous radiation:** Irradiated tissues are less compliant and heal slower
- **Pulmonary reserve:** Compromised pulmonary function reduces tolerance for aspiration

### Rehabilitation Strategies

#### Compensatory Swallowing Techniques

| Technique | Mechanism | Indication |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Chin tuck** | Narrows airway entrance; pushes tongue base posteriorly | Premature spillage; delayed pharyngeal swallow |
| **Supraglottic swallow** | Voluntary breath hold closes neoglottis before and during swallow | Reduced neoglottic closure |
| **Super-supraglottic swallow** | Breath hold with effortful bearing down (Valsalva) before swallow | Severe neoglottic incompetence |
| **Head rotation** | Closes piriform sinus on rotated side; directs bolus to stronger side | Unilateral pharyngeal weakness |
| **Multiple swallows** | Clears residue from pharynx | Pharyngeal residue |
| **Alternating solids and liquids** | Liquid wash clears solid residue | Mixed texture difficulty |

#### Strengthening Exercises

| Exercise | Target | Description |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Shaker exercise** | Suprahyoid muscles; UES opening | Supine head-lift: 3 repetitions of 1-minute sustained lift + 30 repetitions of head-lift |
| **Mendelsohn maneuver** | Prolonged laryngeal elevation and UES opening | Patient holds the larynx at its highest point during swallow for 2-3 seconds |
| **Effortful swallow** | Tongue base retraction; pharyngeal contraction | Patient swallows with maximum effort, squeezing all muscles |
| **Tongue-hold (Masako)** | Posterior pharyngeal wall contraction | Patient holds tongue protruded between teeth while swallowing (dry or with bolus) |
| **CTAR (Chin Tuck Against Resistance)** | Suprahyoid muscle strengthening | Patient pushes chin against a rubber ball held at the chest |
| **Jaw resistance exercises** | Masticator muscles (for trismus) | Progressive stretching with Therabite or stacked tongue depressors |

## Methods of Assessment of Laryngeal Function

### Comprehensive Assessment Framework

Laryngeal function assessment encompasses voice, airway, and swallowing evaluation using clinical, instrumental, and patient-reported measures.

### Voice Assessment

#### Perceptual Scales

**GRBAS Scale (Hirano, 1981):**

| Parameter | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| **G** — Grade | Overall degree of hoarseness (0-3) |
| **R** — Roughness | Irregularity of vibration (0-3) |
| **B** — Breathiness | Air escape during phonation (0-3) |
| **A** — Asthenia | Weakness of voice (0-3) |
| **S** — Strain | Hyperfunction/tension (0-3) |

**CAPE-V (Consensus Auditory-Perceptual Evaluation of Voice):**
- Uses 100-mm visual analog scales for: overall severity, roughness, breathiness, strain, pitch, loudness
- More sensitive than GRBAS for detecting subtle voice changes
- Standardized sustained vowels and sentences for evaluation

**VHI (Voice Handicap Index):**
- 30-item patient-reported questionnaire (3 domains: functional, physical, emotional)
- Each item scored 0-4; total score 0-120
- Higher scores indicate greater perceived voice handicap
- VHI-10 is a validated abbreviated version

#### Acoustic Analysis

| Parameter | Normal Range | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Fundamental frequency (F0)** | Male: 100-150 Hz; Female: 180-250 Hz | Altered by vocal fold mass, tension, and length changes |
| **Jitter (%)** | <1.04% | Cycle-to-cycle frequency perturbation; increased in vocal fold pathology |
| **Shimmer (%)** | <3.81% | Cycle-to-cycle amplitude perturbation; reflects irregular vocal fold closure |
| **Harmonics-to-Noise Ratio (HNR)** | >20 dB | Signal-to-noise ratio of voice; decreased in breathy/rough voices |
| **Maximum Phonation Time (MPT)** | >15 seconds | Reflects glottic efficiency; reduced in vocal fold paralysis, glottic gap |
| **S/Z ratio** | <1.4 | S/Z >1.4 suggests vocal fold pathology (incomplete closure) |

#### Aerodynamic Assessment

| Parameter | Significance |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Subglottic pressure (Ps)** | Estimated from intraoral pressure during /pi/ production; elevated in hyperfunctional voice |
| **Phonation threshold pressure** | Minimum pressure required to initiate phonation; elevated with stiff or edematous vocal folds |
| **Airflow rate** | Mean airflow during sustained phonation; elevated in breathy voice (glottic gap) |
| **Laryngeal resistance** | Ps/airflow; reflects glottic valving efficiency |

### Videolaryngostroboscopy (VLS)

- **Principle:** Stroboscopic light flashes at a frequency slightly different from the vocal fold vibration frequency, creating a slow-motion illusion (Talbot's law)
- **Assessment parameters:** Vocal fold symmetry, mucosal wave, amplitude of vibration, glottic closure pattern, phase closure, non-vibrating segments
- **Clinical utility:** Essential for evaluation of vocal fold lesions, subtle motion abnormalities, mucosal wave disruption (scarring, sulcus), and pre/post-treatment monitoring
- **Limitation:** Requires semi-periodic vibration; cannot assess severely dysphonic or aphonic patients

### Swallowing Assessment Instruments

**FEES (Fiberoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing):** Described above

**VFSS/MBS (Videofluoroscopic Swallowing Study):** Described above

### Swallowing Outcome Scales

| Scale | Type | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **MDADI** (MD Anderson Dysphagia Inventory) | Patient-reported | 20-item questionnaire; global, emotional, functional, physical subscales; validated for HNC |
| **FOIS** (Functional Oral Intake Scale) | Clinician-rated | 7-point ordinal scale from nothing by mouth (1) to total oral diet with no restrictions (7) |
| **DIGEST** (Dynamic Imaging Grade of Swallowing Toxicity) | Clinician-rated | Grades safety (PAS) and efficiency (residue) from VFSS; specifically designed for HNC treatment toxicity |
| **PSS-HN** (Performance Status Scale for Head and Neck) | Clinician-rated | Normalcy of diet, eating in public, intelligibility of speech subscales |
| **EAT-10** | Patient-reported | 10-item screening tool; score >3 indicates swallowing difficulty |

## Nutritional Assessment before Total Laryngopharyngectomy

### Rationale

Patients undergoing total laryngopharyngectomy (TLP) are at extremely high risk for malnutrition due to:

- Pre-existing dysphagia from tumor obstruction
- Catabolic effects of cancer and prior treatments (CRT)
- Anticipated prolonged oral intake restriction postoperatively (7-14 days minimum)
- Increased metabolic demand from major surgery and reconstruction

### Assessment Tools

| Tool | Parameters | Clinical Use |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **PG-SGA** (Patient-Generated Subjective Global Assessment) | Weight history, food intake, symptoms, functional capacity, physical examination | Gold standard for nutritional screening in oncology; scores >9 indicate critical need for intervention |
| **BMI** | Weight/height^2 | <18.5 = underweight; <20 in HNC suggests malnutrition risk |
| **Serum albumin** | Half-life 20 days | <3.5 g/dL indicates malnutrition; <3.0 g/dL increases surgical complication risk (wound dehiscence, fistula) |
| **Prealbumin (transthyretin)** | Half-life 2-3 days | <20 mg/dL indicates acute nutritional depletion; more responsive to short-term changes than albumin |
| **Transferrin** | Half-life 8-10 days | <200 mg/dL suggests protein-calorie malnutrition |
| **Weight loss %** | Over 1-6 months | >5% in 1 month or >10% in 6 months = significant malnutrition |
| **NRS-2002** (Nutritional Risk Screening) | Nutritional status + disease severity | Score >3 indicates nutritional risk requiring intervention |

### Nutritional Optimization Protocol

1. **Preoperative optimization (2-4 weeks before surgery):**
   - Oral nutritional supplements (ONS): High-calorie, high-protein formulas (1.5-2 kcal/ml)
   - Immunonutrition: Arginine, omega-3 fatty acids, nucleotides (e.g., Impact) for 5-7 days preoperatively — evidence suggests reduced postoperative infections
   - PEG tube placement if oral intake is severely compromised (<50% of requirements)
   - Correct specific deficiencies: iron, vitamin D, B12, folate

2. **Perioperative:**
   - Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) principles: minimize fasting, early enteral nutrition
   - Feeding jejunostomy or nasogastric tube for postoperative enteral nutrition
   - Target: 25-30 kcal/kg/day; 1.2-1.5 g protein/kg/day

3. **Postoperative:**
   - Enteral nutrition via feeding tube from POD 1
   - Transition to oral intake guided by VFSS or clinical assessment (typically POD 7-14 for primary closure; longer for flap reconstruction)
   - Ongoing dietetic monitoring and supplementation

For detailed management, see [[Answers/General/Nutritional Assessment and PEG Feeding|Nutritional Assessment and PEG Feeding]].

## Swallowing Assessment and Prevention Strategies for Advanced Laryngeal Cancer

### Baseline Assessment

Every patient with advanced laryngeal cancer (T3-T4) should undergo comprehensive swallowing evaluation before treatment:

1. **Clinical swallowing evaluation** by SLP
2. **Instrumental assessment** (FEES or VFSS) to document baseline function and aspiration status
3. **Nutritional screening** (PG-SGA, BMI, albumin)
4. **Dental evaluation** for pre-radiation dental extraction and fluoride tray fabrication
5. **QoL baseline** (MDADI, EORTC QLQ-H&N35, FACT-H&N)

### Preventive Exercises during CRT

The concept of "prehabilitation" and prophylactic exercises during CRT aims to maintain swallowing muscle strength and range of motion before radiation-induced fibrosis sets in:

| Exercise | Target Structure | Evidence |
|----------|-----------------|----------|
| **Shaker exercise** | Suprahyoid muscles; UES opening | RCT evidence for improved UES opening and reduced aspiration |
| **Mendelsohn maneuver** | Sustained laryngeal elevation | Improves hyolaryngeal excursion and pharyngeal clearance |
| **Effortful swallow** | Base of tongue; pharyngeal constrictors | Maintains tongue base retraction and pharyngeal squeeze |
| **Tongue base retraction (Masako)** | Posterior pharyngeal wall | May improve pharyngeal clearance |
| **Jaw stretching** | Masticator muscles | Prevents trismus; Therabite 3 times daily |
| **Oral range of motion** | Tongue, lips, jaw | Maintains articulatory precision and bolus control |

### DARS-Sparing Radiation

Eisbruch et al. (2004) identified the dysphagia/aspiration-related structures (DARS) whose radiation dose predicts swallowing toxicity:

- **Pharyngeal constrictors** (superior, middle, inferior)
- **Supraglottic larynx** (aryepiglottic folds, false vocal folds)
- **Glottic larynx** (true vocal folds)
- **Cricopharyngeal muscle**

IMRT planning that constrains dose to these structures (mean dose <50 Gy to constrictors; mean dose <40 Gy to glottic/supraglottic larynx) can significantly reduce late dysphagia. See [[Answers/Oropharynx/DARS-Sparing Radiation in OPC|DARS-Sparing Radiation]] for dose constraints.

## Evaluation of Swallowing Outcomes after CRT for Laryngeal Cancer

### Timing of Assessment

| Time Point | Purpose | Method |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Baseline (pre-CRT)** | Document baseline function; identify pre-existing dysphagia | FEES or VFSS + MDADI + FOIS |
| **During CRT (weeks 3-5)** | Monitor acute toxicity; adjust diet; maintain exercises | Clinical evaluation; FEES if concerns |
| **3 months post-CRT** | Peak dysfunction; guide rehabilitation | VFSS + FEES + MDADI + PAS scoring |
| **6 months post-CRT** | Assess recovery trajectory | VFSS + MDADI + FOIS |
| **12 months post-CRT** | Establish long-term functional plateau | Comprehensive reassessment |
| **Annual surveillance** | Detect late-onset progressive dysphagia | MDADI screening; instrumental if symptomatic |

### Patterns of Swallowing Recovery

Based on Logemann et al. (2008) and Hutcheson et al. (2012):

- **Acute phase (during CRT):** Progressive worsening due to mucositis, edema, pain
- **Nadir (1-3 months post-CRT):** Peak swallowing dysfunction; maximum aspiration risk
- **Recovery phase (3-12 months):** Gradual improvement in most patients; however, patients with severe dysfunction at 3 months are unlikely to recover significantly
- **Late-onset dysphagia (>12 months):** Progressive fibrosis may cause NEW dysphagia or worsening of previously stable function; occurs in 10-20% of long-term survivors
- **"Frozen larynx":** End-stage radiation fibrosis with immobile arytenoids, fixed epiglottis, and absent swallowing function — may necessitate salvage total laryngectomy for intractable aspiration

### Outcome Reporting

Standardized reporting should include:

1. **Safety:** PAS score (VFSS) or penetration/aspiration on FEES
2. **Efficiency:** Pharyngeal residue grading (vallecular and piriform sinus residue on FEES/VFSS)
3. **Diet level:** FOIS score (1-7)
4. **Patient-reported:** MDADI composite score
5. **Toxicity grade:** DIGEST grade (0-4 for safety and efficiency)
6. **Tube dependence:** Rate of feeding tube dependence at each time point

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Surgical and CRT-related dysphagia have fundamentally different mechanisms: anatomical alteration versus fibrosis/neuropathy
> 2. Silent aspiration occurs in 20-40% of patients after CRT for advanced laryngeal cancer and cannot be detected by bedside evaluation alone
> 3. FEES and VFSS are the two essential instrumental assessments; FEES is the gold standard for post-CRT laryngopharyngeal assessment
> 4. Post-laryngectomy dysphagia results from neopharyngeal stenosis, pseudodiverticulum, pseudoepiglottis, or cricopharyngeal spasm
> 5. Cricopharyngeal myotomy is indicated for CP bar, Zenker's diverticulum, post-laryngectomy dysphagia, and PE segment hypertonicity
> 6. After SCPL, aspiration is universal initially (80-100%) but improves to 5-15% by 6 months with rehabilitation
> 7. Laryngeal function assessment encompasses perceptual (GRBAS, CAPE-V, VHI), acoustic (jitter, shimmer, HNR), aerodynamic (MPT, airflow), and stroboscopic evaluation
> 8. PG-SGA is the gold standard for nutritional screening in oncology; albumin <3.0 g/dL increases surgical complications
> 9. DARS-sparing IMRT can reduce late dysphagia by constraining dose to pharyngeal constrictors and laryngeal structures
> 10. Late-onset progressive dysphagia (>12 months post-CRT) occurs in 10-20% of survivors and may necessitate salvage laryngectomy
