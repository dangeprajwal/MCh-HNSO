## Philosophy of Conservation Laryngeal Surgery

### Fundamental Principles

Conservation laryngeal surgery aims to achieve **oncologic cure while preserving laryngeal function** — specifically voice, swallowing, and airway protection without a permanent tracheostoma. The philosophy rests on three pillars:

1. **Oncologic adequacy**: Complete tumour resection with adequate margins
2. **Functional preservation**: Maintenance of at least one mobile cricoarytenoid unit for neoglottic phonation and sphincteric closure
3. **Avoidance of permanent stoma**: All conservation procedures aim for eventual decannulation

### Historical Evolution

| Era | Procedure | Surgeon |
|---|---|---|
| 1947 | Horizontal supraglottic laryngectomy | Alonso (Uruguay) |
| 1959 | Vertical hemilaryngectomy | Leroux-Robert (France) |
| 1959 | Supracricoid laryngectomy with CHP | Majer & Rieder (Austria) |
| 1974 | SCPL-CHP revival and refinement | Piquet & Laccourreye (France) |
| 1990 | SCPL-CHEP for glottic carcinoma | Laccourreye H (France) |
| 2014 | ELS OPHL classification (Succo et al.) | European Laryngological Society |

### Oncologic Rationale

The key anatomic concept is that of **laryngeal compartments** and the **cricoarytenoid unit (CAU)**:

- The cricoarytenoid unit comprises the arytenoid cartilage, the cricoid articular facet, the recurrent laryngeal nerve, and the posterior cricoarytenoid muscle
- Preservation of at least one functional CAU is the sine qua non of conservation surgery
- The thyroid cartilage can be entirely resected as it is not essential for neoglottic phonation
- The cricoid cartilage must be preserved as the structural foundation for reconstruction

## ELS Classification of Open Partial Horizontal Laryngectomies (OPHL)

The 2014 Succo classification replaces previous heterogeneous nomenclature:

| OPHL Type | Traditional Name | Structures Resected | Reconstruction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Type I** | Supraglottic laryngectomy | Supraglottis (epiglottis, AE folds, false cords, pre-epiglottic space) | Direct closure |
| **Type IIa** | SCPL-CHEP | Thyroid cartilage, true cords, paraglottic space; suprahyoid epiglottis preserved | CHEP |
| **Type IIb** | SCPL-CHP | Thyroid cartilage, true cords, paraglottic space, entire epiglottis, pre-epiglottic space | CHP |
| **Type III** | Supratracheal laryngectomy | All laryngeal structures above first tracheal ring except ≥1 CAU | Tracheohyoidopexy |

### Suffixes in the ELS Classification

- **+AE**: Resection of one arytenoid (extended)
- **+BOT**: Extension to base of tongue
- **+PS**: Extension to piriform sinus
- **+CAU**: Resection of one cricoarytenoid unit

## Supracricoid Partial Laryngectomy (SCPL)

### SCPL-CHEP (Cricohyoidoepiglottopexy)

**Resection**: Entire thyroid cartilage, both true vocal cords, both false vocal cords, both paraglottic spaces, ventricles; the **suprahyoid epiglottis is preserved**.

**Preserved structures**: Cricoid cartilage, hyoid bone, at least one arytenoid cartilage (ideally both), suprahyoid epiglottis.

**Reconstruction**: The cricoid is sutured directly to the hyoid through the preserved epiglottic stump — cricohyoidoepiglottopexy.

### SCPL-CHP (Cricohyoidopexy)

**Resection**: Same as CHEP **plus the entire epiglottis and pre-epiglottic space**.

**Preserved structures**: Cricoid cartilage, hyoid bone, at least one arytenoid cartilage.

**Reconstruction**: The cricoid is sutured directly to the hyoid — cricohyoidopexy. No epiglottic remnant interposes between cricoid and hyoid.

### CHP vs CHEP — Selection Criteria

| Feature | CHEP | CHP |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary indication** | Glottic carcinoma | Supraglottic carcinoma with glottic extension; transglottic carcinoma |
| **Epiglottis** | Preserved (suprahyoid portion) | Resected entirely |
| **Pre-epiglottic space** | Partially resected | Completely resected |
| **Aspiration risk** | Lower (epiglottic stump aids airway protection) | Higher (no epiglottic barrier) |
| **Decannulation time** | Shorter (mean 8-14 days) | Longer (mean 14-22 days) |
| **Typical T-stages** | T1b, T2, selected T3 glottic | T2-T3 supraglottic, transglottic |
| **Anterior commissure** | Can be involved | Can be involved |

### Indications for SCPL

| Criterion | CHEP | CHP |
|---|---|---|
| **T-stage** | T1b-T3 glottic | T2-T3 supraglottic/transglottic |
| **Subglottic extension** | ≤10 mm anteriorly, ≤5 mm posteriorly | ≤10 mm anteriorly, ≤5 mm posteriorly |
| **Anterior commissure** | Can be involved | Can be involved |
| **Arytenoid** | At least one mobile arytenoid required | At least one mobile arytenoid required |
| **Pre-epiglottic space** | Not invaded | May be invaded |
| **Thyroid cartilage** | Inner perichondrium not breached | Inner perichondrium not breached |
| **Cricoid cartilage** | Not invaded | Not invaded |

### Absolute Contraindications

1. **Fixed arytenoid(s)** — both arytenoids fixed or only functional arytenoid fixed
2. **Cricoid cartilage invasion**
3. **Extralaryngeal spread** (through thyroid cartilage to strap muscles/soft tissue)
4. **Subglottic extension** >10 mm anteriorly or >5 mm posteriorly
5. **Posterior commissure involvement** (both arytenoids)
6. **Poor pulmonary reserve** (FEV1 <60% predicted)
7. **Hypopharyngeal extension** to postcricoid region

### Relative Contraindications

1. Prior radiation therapy (higher complication rates, but feasible — salvage SCPL)
2. Extension to one piriform sinus apex (requires careful evaluation)
3. Age >75 years (higher aspiration risk)
4. Significant comorbidities affecting swallowing rehabilitation

## Surgical Technique: Step-by-Step SCPL

### Pre-operative Assessment

- **CT scan with contrast**: Evaluate thyroid/cricoid cartilage integrity, paraglottic space, pre-epiglottic space
- **MRI**: Superior for pre-epiglottic space and cartilage invasion assessment
- **Direct laryngoscopy and EUA**: Map tumour extent, assess arytenoid mobility
- **Pulmonary function tests**: FEV1 >60% predicted
- **Swallowing assessment**: Baseline functional evaluation

### Surgical Steps (SCPL-CHEP)

1. **Horizontal skin incision** at the level of the thyroid cartilage
2. **Elevation of subplatysmal flaps** superiorly to hyoid, inferiorly to cricoid
3. **Division of strap muscles** in the midline; preservation of at least one superior laryngeal nerve (external branch) when possible
4. **Skeletonisation of the thyroid cartilage** — detach all external muscles
5. **Release infrahyoid attachments** — cut thyrohyoid membrane, preserving the superior laryngeal neurovascular pedicle on at least one side
6. **Tracheotomy** below the cricoid; endotracheal tube repositioned through tracheotomy
7. **Transection of thyroid cartilage** — horizontal cut at the superior border of cricoid cartilage using an oscillating saw
8. **Paraglottic space dissection** — separate thyroid cartilage from the inner perichondrium laterally; include the entire paraglottic space
9. **Subglottic mucosal cut** — at least 5 mm below the tumour; preserve anterior cricoid mucosa when possible
10. **Supraglottic cut** — through the base of epiglottis (for CHEP, preserve suprahyoid epiglottis; for CHP, remove entire epiglottis)
11. **Arytenoid assessment** — preserve both arytenoids if not invaded; one arytenoid may be resected if needed
12. **Specimen removal** en bloc; frozen section margins
13. **Reconstruction (CHEP)**: Three pexy sutures (2-0 or 0 Vicryl) through the cricoid anteriorly, passed through the epiglottic remnant, then through the hyoid body — impaction of cricoid against hyoid with epiglottic stump interposed
14. **Wound closure** over a suction drain; nasogastric tube placed

### Critical Technical Points

- **Pexy sutures**: Must achieve solid impaction of cricoid against hyoid; loose sutures lead to aspiration and decannulation failure
- **Arytenoid preservation**: The posterior cricoarytenoid muscle and recurrent laryngeal nerve must be meticulously preserved
- **Mucosal preservation**: Preserve as much arytenoid mucosa and interarytenoid mucosa as possible for neoglottic function
- **Pre-epiglottic space**: In CHP, entire pre-epiglottic space is resected; in CHEP, only the infrahyoid portion

## Reconstruction Principles

### Neoglottic Mechanism

After SCPL, a **neoglottis** forms between the arytenoid(s) and the base of the tongue (or epiglottic remnant in CHEP):

| Feature | CHEP | CHP |
|---|---|---|
| Neoglottic vibrating source | Arytenoid(s) against epiglottic stump | Arytenoid(s) against tongue base |
| Sphincteric closure | Epiglottic remnant + arytenoid | Tongue base + arytenoid |
| Voice quality | More consistent | More variable |
| Swallowing protection | Better (epiglottic barrier) | Requires more compensation |

### Pexy Technique

The reconstruction relies on **three core sutures**:

1. **Central pexy suture**: Through anterior cricoid arch → through base of epiglottis (CHEP) or directly → through hyoid body
2. **Lateral pexy sutures** (x2): Through lateral cricoid → through remnant of thyrohyoid membrane/epiglottis → through hyoid

The sutures are tied with the neck in flexion to reduce tension. A chin-to-chest suture is placed to maintain neck flexion for 7-10 days.

## Functional Outcomes

### Voice (Neoglottic Phonation)

- **Mechanism**: Air-driven vibration of the neoglottic mucosa (arytenoid mucosa and/or epiglottic remnant)
- **Voice quality**: Consistently rough and breathy (GRBAS Grade 2-3); intelligible conversational speech in >95% of patients
- **Maximum phonation time**: Reduced (mean 6-10 seconds vs 15-20 seconds normal)
- **Fundamental frequency**: Lowered compared to normal larynx

### Swallowing

| Outcome | CHEP | CHP |
|---|---|---|
| Aspiration-free swallowing | 90-95% by 3 months | 85-90% by 6 months |
| Time to oral feeding | 14-21 days | 21-30 days |
| Aspiration pneumonia rate | 5-8% | 10-15% |
| Persistent dysphagia | 2-5% | 5-10% |

### Decannulation

| Outcome | CHEP | CHP |
|---|---|---|
| Decannulation rate | 95-98% | 90-95% |
| Mean time to decannulation | 8-14 days | 14-22 days |
| Failure to decannulate | 2-5% | 5-10% |

### Factors Affecting Functional Outcomes

1. **Number of arytenoids preserved**: Two arytenoids > one arytenoid
2. **Type of reconstruction**: CHEP > CHP (epiglottic stump aids protection)
3. **Age**: Patients >70 years have longer rehabilitation
4. **Prior radiation**: Salvage SCPL has higher complication rates
5. **Pulmonary function**: Poor reserve predisposes to aspiration complications

## SCPL vs Total Laryngectomy

| Parameter | SCPL | Total Laryngectomy |
|---|---|---|
| Permanent stoma | No | Yes |
| Voice | Neoglottic phonation (natural) | Requires TEP/electrolarynx/oesophageal speech |
| Swallowing | Normal (after rehabilitation) | Normal (after healing) |
| Local control (T3) | 85-92% | 90-95% |
| Quality of life | Superior (voice, social function) | Inferior (stoma-related issues) |
| Aspiration risk | Present during rehabilitation | Eliminated |
| Hospital stay | Longer (rehabilitation) | Shorter |
| Cost | Higher (rehabilitation) | Lower |

## SCPL vs Transoral Laser Microsurgery (TLM)

| Parameter | SCPL | TLM |
|---|---|---|
| Approach | Open (external) | Transoral (endoscopic) |
| T-stage applicability | T1b-T3 | T1-T2 (selected T3) |
| Anterior commissure involvement | Well suited | Technically challenging |
| Bilateral disease | Well suited | Limited |
| Impaired cord mobility | Suitable | Limited for T3 with fixation |
| Voice quality | Rough/breathy but functional | Near-normal for small resections |
| Recovery time | 3-6 months | 2-4 weeks |
| Tracheotomy | Always required (temporary) | Usually not required |
| Hospital stay | 10-21 days | 2-5 days |

## Role of SCPL in T3 Laryngeal Cancer

### Rationale

T3 laryngeal cancer presents a management dilemma between organ preservation (CRT) and total laryngectomy. SCPL offers a third option — **surgical organ preservation with functional larynx preservation**.

### Selection Criteria for SCPL in T3 Disease

| Favourable for SCPL | Unfavourable for SCPL |
|---|---|
| T3 with impaired cord mobility (not fixation) | T3 with true fixation (cricoarytenoid joint invasion) |
| Limited paraglottic space invasion | Extensive paraglottic space invasion |
| No cartilage invasion on imaging | Thyroid cartilage destruction |
| No subglottic extension >10 mm | Extensive subglottic extension |
| Good pulmonary function | Poor pulmonary reserve |
| No prior radiation | Prior full-dose radiation |

### Outcomes of SCPL for T3 Laryngeal Cancer

- **Local control**: 80-88%
- **5-year overall survival**: 65-75%
- **Larynx preservation**: 85-95%
- **Decannulation**: 90-95%

These results compare favourably with organ preservation chemoradiation (which achieves larynx preservation in 60-70% for T3 disease but with significant late functional toxicity).

## Patient Selection Criteria — Summary Algorithm

```
T1a glottic → TLM or RT (SCPL not indicated)
T1b glottic → TLM or SCPL-CHEP or RT
T2 glottic → TLM or SCPL-CHEP or RT
T3 glottic (mobile arytenoid) → SCPL-CHEP or CRT or TL
T3 glottic (fixed cord, mobile arytenoid) → SCPL-CHEP or CRT or TL
T2 supraglottic → OPHL Type I or SCPL-CHP or TLM or RT
T3 supraglottic → SCPL-CHP or CRT or TL
Transglottic → SCPL-CHP or TL
```

## Key Points for Exam

1. SCPL was developed by Majer and Rieder in 1959; revived by Piquet and Laccourreye in France during the 1970s-1990s
2. The **cricoarytenoid unit (CAU)** is the essential functional unit — at least one must be preserved
3. CHEP preserves the suprahyoid epiglottis; CHP removes the entire epiglottis and pre-epiglottic space
4. CHEP is primarily for glottic carcinoma; CHP for supraglottic/transglottic carcinoma
5. The **ELS classification** (Succo 2014) defines OPHL Types I (supraglottic), II (supracricoid), III (supratracheal)
6. Absolute contraindications: both arytenoids fixed, cricoid invasion, extralaryngeal spread, subglottic extension >10 mm anterior/>5 mm posterior
7. Decannulation rates exceed 90% for both CHEP and CHP
8. SCPL is a viable option for selected T3 laryngeal cancer with local control rates of 80-88%
9. Functional rehabilitation takes 3-6 months; age >70 and prior RT increase rehabilitation time
10. Three pexy sutures achieve impaction of cricoid against hyoid; neck flexion is maintained for 7-10 days post-operatively
