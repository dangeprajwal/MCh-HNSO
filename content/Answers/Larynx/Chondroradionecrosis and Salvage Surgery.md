Timmermans et al. ## Chondroradionecrosis: Pathophysiology

### Marx 3H Theory
Chondroradionecrosis (CRN) of the larynx results from the cumulative effects of radiation on tissue described by Marx's **3H theory**:

1. **Hypovascular** -- Radiation causes endarteritis obliterans, reducing blood supply to the irradiated tissues and especially the avascular laryngeal cartilages
2. **Hypocellular** -- Radiation depletes the fibroblast population, reducing the capacity for tissue repair and collagen synthesis
3. **Hypoxic** -- Reduced vascularity leads to chronic tissue hypoxia, creating a microenvironment unable to support normal healing

### Pathological Sequence
1. **Radiation-induced perichondritis** -- Inflammation of the perichondrium (the sole blood supply to cartilage)
2. **Chondrocyte death** -- Loss of blood supply causes cartilage cell necrosis
3. **Secondary infection** -- Devitalised cartilage becomes colonised by oral flora
4. **Progressive necrosis** -- Ongoing cartilage destruction with sequestration
5. **Exposure of necrotic cartilage** -- Through overlying mucosa into the airway lumen or externally through skin

### Key Concept
Laryngeal cartilages are **avascular structures** that depend entirely on the **perichondrium** for their blood supply. Radiation damages the perichondrial vessels, creating a vicious cycle of ischaemia, necrosis, and infection that the tissue cannot resolve due to the hypocellular, hypoxic environment.

## Risk Factors

| Category | Risk Factors |
|---|---|
| **Radiation-related** | Total RT dose >70 Gy; fraction size >2 Gy; concurrent chemotherapy; re-irradiation; brachytherapy boost |
| **Tumour-related** | Pre-existing cartilage invasion by tumour; T4 stage; large tumour volume |
| **Patient-related** | Continued smoking and alcohol use after RT; diabetes mellitus; poor nutrition; immunosuppression; peripheral vascular disease |
| **Iatrogenic** | Post-RT biopsy (disrupts already compromised mucosa); post-RT surgery; tracheostomy through irradiated field |

### Critical Risk Factors (Exam Focus)
- **Total dose >70 Gy** is the single most important radiation-related risk factor
- **Fraction size >2 Gy** (hypofractionation) increases late toxicity
- **Pre-existing cartilage invasion** -- tumour-induced cartilage destruction compounds radiation injury
- **Post-RT biopsy** -- disruption of the irradiated mucosal barrier is a well-recognised precipitant

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms
- **Pain** -- often the earliest symptom; may be severe and disproportionate to examination findings
- **Odynophagia** -- pain on swallowing, often referred to the ear
- **Dysphagia** -- difficulty swallowing; may lead to aspiration
- **Stridor** -- indicates significant airway compromise
- **Foul-smelling discharge** -- from infected necrotic cartilage
- **Voice change** -- worsening hoarseness or aphonia
- **Neck swelling** -- oedema or abscess formation

### Signs
- Laryngeal oedema and erythema
- Exposed necrotic cartilage visible on endoscopy
- Fistula formation (laryngocutaneous)
- Laryngeal framework tenderness and crepitus
- Neck abscess or cellulitis

### Clinical Dilemma
The **critical challenge** is distinguishing CRN from **tumour recurrence** -- both present with similar symptoms (pain, oedema, stridor, mass effect) in the irradiated larynx. This distinction has major therapeutic implications: CRN may be managed conservatively, while recurrence requires surgical salvage.

## Chandler Classification

| Grade | Description | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | Radiation oedema | Oedema of arytenoids and aryepiglottic folds; no exposed cartilage | Conservative: observation, voice rest, humidification |
| **II** | Radiation chondritis | Increased oedema; mild pain; no exposed cartilage visible | Conservative: antibiotics, steroids, PPI, voice rest |
| **III** | Exposed cartilage necrosis | Exposed necrotic cartilage visible endoscopically; significant pain; odynophagia | Surgical: debridement, possible partial laryngectomy; trial of HBO |
| **IV** | Laryngeal abscess or fistula | Abscess formation, laryngocutaneous fistula, laryngeal framework destruction | Surgical: total laryngectomy usually required |

### Key Principle
- **Grades I-II** are considered expected sequelae of radiation therapy and respond to conservative management
- **Grades III-IV** represent true chondroradionecrosis requiring surgical intervention
- Progression from Grade II to III may occur months to years after completion of RT

## Imaging in Chondroradionecrosis

### CT Assessment

CT is the **primary imaging modality** for evaluating the irradiated larynx.

#### CT Signs of Chondroradionecrosis
- **Cartilage sclerosis** -- increased density of previously non-ossified cartilage
- **Cartilage fragmentation** -- disruption of the normal cartilage architecture
- **Gas within the cartilage framework** -- pathognomonic for necrosis with infection
- **Extra-laryngeal soft tissue swelling** -- oedema extending beyond the laryngeal framework
- **Collapse of the cartilage framework** -- loss of structural integrity
- **Abscess formation** -- rim-enhancing fluid collection

#### CT Signs Favouring Tumour Recurrence Over CRN
- **Enhancing soft tissue mass** -- focal nodular enhancement suspicious for tumour
- **Destruction/erosion of cartilage with associated mass** -- cartilage destruction with an identifiable mass lesion
- **New lymphadenopathy** -- suggestive of recurrent disease

### Radiology of Laryngeal Cartilage Erosion

#### Normal Cartilage Appearances on CT
- **Thyroid cartilage:** Thin, shield-shaped; shows variable ossification (increases with age); non-ossified cartilage has low density
- **Cricoid cartilage:** Complete ring; usually well-ossified in adults
- **Arytenoid cartilage:** Small, pyramid-shaped; variable ossification

#### Staging Implications of Cartilage Erosion

| Finding | T-Stage | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Inner cortex erosion only | T3 | Cartilage invasion but not through-and-through |
| Through-and-through erosion of thyroid/cricoid cartilage | T4a | Extralaryngeal extension |
| Arytenoid sclerosis (reactive) | Not necessarily invasion | May be inflammatory; requires correlation |
| Gross cartilage destruction with extralaryngeal mass | T4a | Clear indication for surgery |

#### Criteria for Cartilage Invasion (CT)
- **Erosion or lysis** of the inner cortex (T3)
- **Sclerosis** alone is a non-specific finding (may be reactive or neoplastic)
- **Through-and-through invasion** with extralaryngeal soft tissue (T4a)
- **Sensitivity of CT** for cartilage invasion: 76-89%
- **Specificity of CT:** 74-82% (limited by post-inflammatory sclerosis causing overstaging)

### MRI Assessment

- **T2-weighted images:** High signal in cartilage may indicate tumour invasion or oedema/inflammation -- **non-specific**
- **Contrast-enhanced T1:** Enhancement within cartilage suggests invasion; perichondrial enhancement alone may be reactive
- **Becker/Zbaren criteria (2008):**
  - High signal on T2 **with** corresponding enhancement on post-contrast T1 = likely tumour invasion
  - High signal on T2 **without** enhancement = likely inflammatory/oedematous change
  - Signal intensity criteria improve specificity over morphological criteria alone
- MRI has **higher sensitivity** but **lower specificity** than CT for cartilage invasion

### PET-CT

- **High false-positive rate** in post-RT larynx -- inflammation, granulation tissue, and CRN all cause FDG uptake
- **SUVmax values** overlap significantly between CRN and recurrence
- **Visual patterns:** Focal intense uptake more suggestive of recurrence; diffuse mild uptake more suggestive of inflammation
- **Role:** More useful for detecting distant metastases and nodal recurrence than for distinguishing local CRN from recurrence
- **Timing:** PET-CT should be performed at least 12 weeks after completion of RT to reduce false-positive rates

### Definitive Assessment: EUA + Biopsy
- **Examination under anaesthesia with biopsy** is **essential** in any patient with clinical or radiological concern for recurrence
- Deep biopsies from the most suspicious areas
- **Caveat:** Post-RT biopsies carry a risk of precipitating or worsening CRN
- Negative biopsies do **not** exclude submucosal recurrence -- repeated biopsies may be necessary

## Management of Minimal Cartilage Erosion (Q30)

### Clinical Scenario
A laryngeal cancer is found to have **minimal cartilage erosion** (inner cortex involvement only) on imaging. This has significant staging and treatment implications.

### Staging Implications
- **Inner cortex erosion only** = **T3** (invasion into paraglottic space and/or minor thyroid cartilage erosion)
- **Through-and-through erosion** = **T4a** (invasion through outer cortex of thyroid cartilage and/or into adjacent structures)

### Treatment Decision

| Scenario | Preferred Treatment |
|---|---|
| Inner cortex erosion only, otherwise organ-preservable | CRT (organ preservation) -- minor cartilage involvement does not preclude CRT |
| Through-and-through thyroid cartilage erosion | Total laryngectomy (T4a) preferred; CRT associated with higher failure rates |
| Minimal arytenoid erosion | Correlate with clinical assessment; may still be amenable to organ preservation |
| Bilateral cartilage involvement | Total laryngectomy |

### Key Evidence
- The **NCCN guidelines** and major trials support organ preservation with CRT for T3 laryngeal cancers, including those with minor cartilage involvement
- **T4a with cartilage invasion** -- the VA Laryngeal Cancer Study and RTOG 91-11 excluded or had limited T4a patients; surgery remains preferred for bulky T4a disease
- Minor cartilage involvement on imaging may be **overstaged** on CT (sclerosis misinterpreted as invasion)

## Management of Chondroradionecrosis

### Conservative Management (Chandler Grade I-II)

| Intervention | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Antibiotics** | Broad-spectrum coverage for oral flora (amoxicillin-clavulanate or fluoroquinolone + metronidazole); prolonged courses (4-6 weeks) |
| **Steroids** | Systemic dexamethasone or prednisolone for oedema reduction; short courses |
| **Voice rest** | Reduces mechanical stress on the irradiated larynx |
| **Humidification** | Moisturises dry, irradiated mucosa; reduces crusting |
| **PPI therapy** | Controls laryngopharyngeal reflux, which exacerbates mucosal injury |
| **Nutritional support** | Enteral feeding (NG tube or PEG) if oral intake inadequate |
| **Smoking and alcohol cessation** | Mandatory; both impair healing and exacerbate tissue hypoxia |

### Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBO)

- **Mechanism:** Intermittent exposure to 100% oxygen at 2-2.5 atmospheres promotes:
  - Angiogenesis in the hypovascular tissue
  - Increased fibroblast proliferation
  - Enhanced collagen synthesis
  - Improved oxygen delivery to hypoxic tissues
- **Protocol:** Typically 20-30 sessions, each lasting 90-120 minutes
- **Evidence:** Moderate evidence for benefit in Grade II-III CRN; may prevent progression to higher grades
- **Limitations:** Availability; cost; not universally accessible; contraindicated in claustrophobia, untreated pneumothorax

### Surgical Management (Chandler Grade III-IV)

#### Endoscopic Debridement
- Removal of necrotic cartilage fragments and granulation tissue
- Laser (CO2) vaporisation of devitalised tissue
- May be combined with HBO therapy
- Suitable for limited necrosis without framework collapse

#### Partial Laryngectomy
- For limited CRN involving one side of the laryngeal framework
- Removes the necrotic cartilage while preserving laryngeal function
- Requires adequate residual framework for structural support

#### Total Laryngectomy
- **Definitive treatment** for Grade IV CRN (laryngeal framework destruction, abscess, fistula)
- Also indicated when **recurrence cannot be excluded** despite negative biopsies
- Pharyngeal closure may require vascularised tissue (PMMF or free flap) due to irradiated tissue
- Primary TEP can be performed at the time of salvage laryngectomy

## Salvage Laryngectomy

### Definition
Total laryngectomy performed for **biopsy-proven recurrence** of laryngeal cancer after definitive radiation therapy (RT) or chemoradiation therapy (CRT).

### Indications
- Biopsy-confirmed recurrent or residual laryngeal cancer after RT/CRT
- Functional laryngeal failure after RT (aspiration, non-functional larynx) even without proven recurrence
- Grade IV chondroradionecrosis with non-functional larynx

### Preoperative Assessment
1. **Biopsy confirmation** of recurrence (EUA + deep biopsies)
2. **Imaging:** CT/MRI to assess extent of recurrence; PET-CT for distant metastases
3. **Nutritional optimisation** -- many patients are malnourished after CRT
4. **Thyroid function** -- hypothyroidism is common post-RT (check TSH)
5. **Assessment of tissue quality** -- irradiated tissues have impaired healing

### Surgical Considerations
- **Wider margins** than primary TL -- irradiated tissue may harbour microscopic disease
- **Pharyngeal closure:** Irradiated pharyngeal mucosa is at high risk of breakdown
- **Vascularised tissue coverage** is strongly recommended to reduce fistula risk:
  - **PMMF (Pectoralis Major Myocutaneous Flap):** Most commonly used; reliable; provides bulk and vascularity
  - **Free flap** (radial forearm, ALT): For larger defects or when additional tissue is needed
- **Primary TEP** can be performed at the time of salvage TL (increasingly standard practice)
- **Neck dissection:** Often performed concurrently; irradiated neck tissue may be fibrotic

### Complications of Salvage Laryngectomy

| Complication | Incidence | Risk Factors |
|---|---|---|
| **Pharyngocutaneous fistula (PCF)** | 30-65% (vs 10-15% primary TL) | Prior CRT (vs RT alone); poor nutrition; diabetes; concurrent neck dissection; hypothyroidism |
| **Wound infection/breakdown** | 20-40% | Irradiated tissue; diabetes; smoking; malnutrition |
| **Hypothyroidism** | 50-80% | Prior RT to the neck; thyroidectomy during surgery |
| **Carotid blow-out** | 3-5% | PCF with exposed vessels; prior neck dissection; RT |
| **Stomal recurrence** | 5-10% | Subglottic extension; paratracheal nodes; emergency tracheostomy before laryngectomy |
| **Dysphagia/stricture** | 10-20% | Extensive pharyngeal resection; radiation fibrosis |

### Pharyngocutaneous Fistula: Prevention and Management

#### Prevention
- **Vascularised tissue interposition** (PMMF or free flap) between the neopharynx and skin -- single most effective prevention strategy
- Adequate nutritional status preoperatively (albumin >3.5 g/dL)
- Meticulous pharyngeal closure (two-layer or three-layer)
- Hypothyroidism correction preoperatively
- Salivary drainage (if parotid function preserved)

#### Management
- **Conservative:** Wound care, packing, nutritional support (enteral feeding), antibiotics -- majority (60-70%) heal spontaneously within 4-8 weeks
- **Surgical:** For persistent fistula (>6-8 weeks) -- PMMF or free flap closure

### Outcomes of Salvage Laryngectomy

| Parameter | Salvage TL | Primary TL |
|---|---|---|
| 5-year overall survival | 40-60% | 55-70% |
| Local-regional control | 70-85% | 80-90% |
| PCF rate | 30-65% | 10-15% |
| Perioperative mortality | 1-3% | <1% |
| Hospital stay | Longer | Shorter |

## Salvage Transoral Laser Microsurgery (TLM)

### Concept
For **early radio-recurrent laryngeal cancers** (rT1-rT2), salvage TLM offers the possibility of **organ preservation** while avoiding the morbidity of salvage TL.

### Steiner Criteria for Salvage TLM
- **rT1 or rT2** recurrence (early-stage recurrent disease)
- **Adequate endoscopic exposure** of the tumour
- **Clear margins achievable** endoscopically
- No cartilage invasion on imaging
- No fixation of the vocal fold (for glottic recurrence)
- Patient must accept the risk of requiring salvage TL if margins are positive

### Technique
- Direct microlaryngoscopy with CO2 laser resection
- Type I-V cordectomy as per European Laryngological Society classification
- Frozen section margins are essential
- Multiple passes with laser to ensure adequate depth

### Outcomes of Salvage TLM

| Parameter | Result |
|---|---|
| Local control (rT1) | 70-85% |
| Local control (rT2) | 50-70% |
| Laryngeal preservation rate | 60-80% |
| Functional voice preservation | 70-90% |
| Need for salvage TL after TLM failure | 20-40% |

### Advantages of Salvage TLM
- **Organ preservation** -- avoids permanent tracheostomy
- Lower morbidity than salvage TL
- Can be repeated if recurrence is again early-stage
- Salvage TL remains an option if TLM fails

### Limitations
- Only suitable for **early recurrences** (rT1-rT2)
- **Exposure-dependent** -- not all laryngeal lesions are accessible endoscopically
- **Frozen section** accuracy is reduced in irradiated tissue
- Higher local recurrence rate compared to salvage TL
- Patient must be counselled regarding the need for close surveillance

## Key Points for Exam

1. **Marx 3H theory:** Hypovascular, Hypocellular, Hypoxic -- explains pathophysiology of CRN
2. **Chandler classification:** Grade I-II conservative; Grade III-IV surgical
3. **Risk factors:** Total dose >70 Gy, fraction size >2 Gy, pre-existing cartilage invasion, post-RT biopsy, concurrent chemoRT, smoking, diabetes
4. **CT signs of CRN:** Cartilage sclerosis, fragmentation, gas within framework, extra-laryngeal soft tissue
5. **Distinguishing CRN from recurrence:** EUA + deep biopsy is essential; PET-CT has high false-positive rate; negative biopsy does not exclude submucosal recurrence
6. **Minimal cartilage erosion:** Inner cortex only = T3 (organ preservation possible); through-and-through = T4a (surgery preferred)
7. **Salvage TL complications:** PCF 30-65% (vs 10-15% primary); **PMMF** reduces fistula rate
8. **Weber (2003):** Salvage TL after organ preservation has acceptable morbidity; PCF up to one-third; survival not influenced by initial treatment modality
9. **Salvage TLM:** For rT1-rT2 recurrences with adequate exposure; local control 70-85% for rT1; laryngeal preservation 60-80%
10. **HBO therapy:** Promotes angiogenesis in irradiated tissue; 20-30 sessions; evidence for Grade II-III CRN
