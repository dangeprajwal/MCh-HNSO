## Introduction

Total laryngectomy results in the permanent loss of the vocal cords and normal phonation, separation of the airway from the digestive tract via a permanent tracheostoma, and loss of nasal airflow for humidification, warming, and olfaction. The consequence for the patient is the abrupt inability to communicate verbally — a profoundly disabling outcome that affects psychological health, social participation, and employment. As described in Scott-Brown's, the speech pathologist is a critical member of the multidisciplinary team because of the loss of laryngeal voice and the inability to produce audible and intelligible speech.

### Normal Speech Production

Speech requires the coordinated interaction of three systems:

1. **Initiator (power source):** Moving column of air from the lungs (tidal volume approximately 500 ml)
2. **Vibrator (sound source):** Vocal cords — the approximated vocal folds vibrate as air passes through them, producing a fundamental tone (male F0 approximately 125 Hz, female approximately 210 Hz)
3. **Resonators and articulators:** The pharynx, oral cavity, and nasal cavity amplify and shape the sound; the tongue, teeth, lips, and soft palate articulate the sound into recognizable speech

After total laryngectomy, the vibrator is removed and the initiator is disconnected from the vocal tract. All methods of alaryngeal speech must therefore provide an alternative vibrator and, in some methods, an alternative power source.

## Esophageal Speech

### Mechanism

Esophageal speech occurs when the patient introduces air into the upper esophagus (approximately 40-70 ml) and then expels it, causing the pharyngoesophageal (PE) segment to vibrate and produce sound. The resonating cavities (pharynx, mouth, nose) shape the sound, and articulation occurs through the lips, teeth, tongue, and palate — identical to normal laryngeal speakers.

### Techniques of Air Insufflation

As described in Scott-Brown's and Montgomery, two primary methods exist:

1. **Injection method (consonant injection):** The patient uses the articulatory movements of plosive consonants (/p/, /t/, /k/) to compress oral air backward through the PE segment into the esophagus. This is the most commonly taught and most functional method for connected speech.

2. **Inhalation method (aspiration):** The patient generates negative esophageal pressure during inspiration, drawing air through the PE segment into the esophagus. This method is less commonly used in clinical practice.

3. **Glossopharyngeal press (tongue pump):** The tongue pushes air posteriorly and inferiorly past the PE segment — a variant of the injection technique.

### PE Segment as Neoglottis

The PE segment serves as the neoglottis for both esophageal and TEP speakers. It comprises the cricopharyngeus muscle, the inferior constrictor, and the proximal esophageal circular muscle. For optimal voice, this segment must be tonic enough to vibrate but not so hypertonic as to prevent airflow.

### Learning Curve and Outcomes

- **Success rate:** Only 20-30% of patients achieve proficient, intelligible esophageal speech
- **Learning duration:** Typically 6-12 months of dedicated speech therapy
- **Factors affecting success:** Motivation, hearing ability, cognitive status, availability of SLP, PE segment tonicity, reconstruction method
- **Voice characteristics:** Low pitch (50-80 Hz), reduced intensity (55-65 dB), short phrase length (4-8 syllables per air charge), frequent pauses for reloading

### Limitations

- Requires prolonged speech therapy with highly specialized SLPs
- Low success rate limits clinical applicability
- Limited air reservoir (40-70 ml versus 500 ml with TEP) restricts loudness and phrase length
- Stoma noise during air injection can distract listeners
- Requires adequate PE segment tonicity and intact oropharyngeal musculature
- Patients with extensive pharyngeal resection or reconstruction may have inadequate tissue for PE segment vibration

## Electrolarynx (Artificial Larynx)

### Mechanism

The electrolarynx generates a buzzing sound from an electromagnetically driven vibrating diaphragm powered by rechargeable batteries. This sound is transmitted into the vocal tract either through the neck skin or directly into the oral cavity. The patient articulates the transmitted sound into intelligible speech using the lips, tongue, teeth, and palate.

### Types of Electrolarynx Devices

#### Transcervical (Neck-type) Devices
- **Mechanism:** Vibrating head placed firmly against the submandibular or lateral neck skin; sound conducted through tissues into the oral cavity
- **Examples:** Servox Inton (Siemens), TruTone, Romet, Nu-Vois
- **Advantages:** Most commonly used; produces louder voice; both hands may be used intermittently
- **Limitations:** Requires adequate tissue pliability for sound transmission; fibrotic or irradiated neck may attenuate sound; leaves visible external device during use

#### Intraoral Devices
- **Mechanism:** Small tube delivers vibrations directly into the oral cavity; bypasses neck tissues entirely
- **Examples:** Cooper-Rand Electronic Speech Aid, pneumatic devices
- **Indications:** Neck fibrosis after radiation, immediate postoperative period when neck tissues are edematous, poor neck tissue compliance
- **Limitations:** Tube occupies oral space, interfering with articulation; less natural sound quality; requires one hand

### Newer Technologies and Advances

- **Pitch-adjustable devices:** Newer electrolarynges (e.g., Servox Inton) allow adjustment of fundamental frequency via buttons, enabling pitch variation during speech and improved prosody
- **EMG-controlled devices:** Experimental myoelectric electrolarynges use surface EMG signals from residual neck muscles to control pitch and on/off switching, enabling hands-free operation
- **Noise reduction:** Digital signal processing algorithms reduce the radiated electromagnetic noise characteristic of conventional devices
- **App-based solutions:** Smartphone applications with customizable voice settings are under development for cost-effective alternatives

### Advantages of Electrolarynx

- **Immediate use:** Can be introduced within days of surgery, relieving communication frustration during the critical early postoperative period
- **High acquisition rate:** Approximately 90% of patients can produce intelligible speech
- **No surgery required:** Noninvasive option suitable for patients who cannot undergo TEP
- **Backup device:** All laryngectomees should possess an electrolarynx as a fallback communication option
- **Independence:** Requires minimal ongoing professional support after initial training

### Limitations

- **Robotic voice quality:** Monotonous buzzing tone is the most significant patient complaint
- **Social stigma:** Visible device draws attention and may cause embarrassment
- **One-hand operation:** Occupies one hand during use (except experimental EMG devices)
- **Battery dependence:** Requires regular charging; device failure leaves patient without voice
- **Reduced intelligibility:** Background noise significantly impairs communication

### Indian Context

In India, where access to specialized SLP services and prosthetic voice rehabilitation may be limited in resource-constrained settings, the electrolarynx assumes particular importance. Devices such as the Servox cost approximately INR 15,000-30,000 and provide immediate, reliable speech without the ongoing costs of prosthesis replacement. However, patient acceptance may be limited by cultural factors and the stigma of a visible assistive device. Indigenous low-cost alternatives are being developed to improve accessibility.

## Tracheoesophageal Puncture and Prosthetic Voice (Cross-Reference)

For detailed coverage of TEP technique, prosthesis types (Blom-Singer, Provox, Provox Vega), primary versus secondary TEP, complications (central and peripheral leakage), PE segment hypertonicity management, and hands-free speaking valves, see the companion file: [[Answers/Larynx/TEP and Prosthetic Voice Rehabilitation|TEP and Prosthetic Voice Rehabilitation]].

### Key TEP Points for Comparison

- TEP diverts lung air (up to 500 ml) through a one-way silicone valve prosthesis into the esophagus
- PE segment vibrates with significantly greater airflow and pressure than esophageal speech
- Voice quality is the most natural of all three alaryngeal methods
- Primary TEP at the time of laryngectomy is preferred (success rate 70-90%)
- Cricopharyngeal myotomy at surgery is essential to prevent PE segment hypertonicity
- Prosthesis lifetime averages 3-6 months (indwelling) or 1-3 months (patient-changed)

## Voice Preservation Strategies

### Rationale

Voice preservation aims to avoid total laryngectomy when oncologically safe, thereby preserving the native laryngeal voice. Strategies include conservation laryngeal surgery and organ preservation chemoradiation protocols.

### Surgical Voice Preservation

| Procedure | Indication | Voice Outcome |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Transoral laser microsurgery (TLM) | T1-T2 glottic | Good-excellent (85-90%) |
| Vertical partial laryngectomy | T1-selected T2 glottic | Moderate-good (breathy voice) |
| Supraglottic laryngectomy | T1-T2 supraglottic | Near-normal (preserved true cords) |
| Supracricoid laryngectomy (CHEP/CHP) | T2-T3 glottic/supraglottic | Functional but rough |
| TORS (selected cases) | T1-T2 supraglottic | Good |

### Chemoradiation for Voice Preservation

The Veterans Affairs Laryngeal Cancer Study Group (1991) and RTOG 91-11 trial established concurrent chemoradiation as an organ preservation approach for advanced laryngeal cancer (see [[Answers/Larynx/Landmark Trials and Organ Preservation Strategies|Organ Preservation Strategies]]). However, a "preserved but non-functional" larynx may result from radiation-induced fibrosis, edema, and aspiration — a condition where voice quality and swallowing may be worse than after laryngectomy with TEP voice rehabilitation.

### Voice Restoration in Laryngopharyngeal Cancers

For laryngopharyngeal cancers requiring total laryngopharyngectomy, voice restoration is achieved through:

1. **Primary TEP with pharyngeal reconstruction:** The choice of reconstruction tissue (fasciocutaneous flap vs. jejunum vs. gastric pull-up) critically influences TEP voice quality
2. **Electrolarynx:** When TEP is not feasible or fails
3. **Esophageal speech:** Rarely successful after extensive pharyngeal reconstruction

## Speech Rehabilitation after Pharyngeal Reconstruction with Free Flaps

### Challenges of Neopharyngeal Reconstruction

When total laryngopharyngectomy is required, the native PE segment is resected along with the pharyngeal constrictors. Reconstruction with free tissue transfer creates a neopharynx with fundamentally different biomechanical properties:

- **Adynamic tissue:** Fasciocutaneous flaps (ALT, radial forearm free flap) are passive conduits without muscular tone — the neopharynx does not actively vibrate
- **Peristaltic mismatch:** Jejunal flaps retain autonomous peristalsis that is uncoordinated with native pharyngeal function
- **Compliance differences:** The stiffness and caliber of the neopharynx affects both airflow resistance and vibration characteristics

### Reconstruction-Specific Voice Outcomes

| Reconstruction | TEP Voice Quality | Mechanism | Success Rate |
|---------------|-------------------|-----------|-------------|
| **RFFF (radial forearm)** | Moderate-good | Thin, pliable tissue vibrates with airflow | 60-70% |
| **ALT flap** | Moderate | Bulkier tissue; may require debulking | 50-65% |
| **Jejunal free flap** | Poor-moderate | Peristalsis interferes; wet voice | 30-50% |
| **Gastric pull-up** | Poor | Long conduit; high resistance | 20-40% |
| **Tubed RFFF** | Moderate-good | Circumferential thin skin tube | 55-65% |

### Key Surgical Principles for Voice Optimization

- **Mucosal width:** A minimum of 6 cm transverse mucosal width at the narrowest point is needed for effortless TE speech (Scott-Brown's)
- **PE segment creation:** When the native PE segment is resected, surgeons should attempt to fashion a tonically vibrating segment using constrictor remnants or flap design
- **Flap selection:** Fasciocutaneous flaps (RFFF, ALT) produce superior voice quality compared to visceral flaps (jejunum, stomach)
- **TEP placement:** Should be positioned in native tracheal tissue when possible, not in the flap

## Patient Selection and Multidisciplinary Approach

### Preoperative Assessment

The SLP should evaluate every patient preoperatively for:

- **Cognitive function:** Ability to learn new communication techniques
- **Hearing status:** Adequate hearing for speech therapy feedback
- **Manual dexterity:** Required for stoma occlusion (TEP) or electrolarynx operation
- **Vision:** For reading educational materials and device management
- **Motivation and psychosocial readiness:** Patient and family counseling about expected outcomes
- **Literacy level:** Affects ability to benefit from written instructions and exercises

### Rehabilitation Timeline

| Phase | Timing | Activities |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Preoperative** | 1-2 weeks before surgery | Counseling, introduction to all three methods, meeting a laryngectomee |
| **Immediate postoperative** | Days 1-7 | Written communication, electrolarynx introduction (intraoral type) |
| **Early rehabilitation** | Weeks 2-6 | Transcervical electrolarynx, stoma care, HME fitting |
| **TEP voice initiation** | Weeks 2-4 (primary TEP) | Prosthesis insertion, initial voicing, digital occlusion training |
| **Intensive therapy** | Months 1-3 | TEP voice refinement, esophageal speech training (if desired), pitch/loudness exercises |
| **Long-term follow-up** | Ongoing | Prosthesis changes, troubleshooting, hands-free valve training |

### Indian Context Considerations

- **Access to SLP services:** Limited availability of trained SLPs in Tier 2/3 cities necessitates telemedicine-based rehabilitation models
- **Cost of prosthesis:** Provox prostheses cost approximately INR 5,000-8,000 per change (every 3-6 months); this recurring cost is a significant barrier for economically disadvantaged patients
- **Literacy and education:** Lower literacy rates in some populations mandate greater reliance on visual demonstrations and family training rather than written instructions
- **Cultural factors:** Social stigma of visible devices and stoma may impair rehabilitation compliance
- **Indigenous alternatives:** Low-cost voice prostheses (e.g., Amatya prosthesis) have been developed in India to improve access
- **Family involvement:** Extended family networks in Indian society can be leveraged for rehabilitation support and emotional adjustment

## Role of the Speech-Language Pathologist

The SLP is an indispensable member of the head and neck multidisciplinary team:

1. **Preoperative counseling:** Setting realistic expectations, introducing rehabilitation options, connecting with peer support (laryngectomee visitor programs)
2. **Postoperative communication:** Establishing alternative communication methods immediately after surgery
3. **Voice prosthesis management:** Fitting, maintenance, troubleshooting, and regular prosthesis changes
4. **Alaryngeal speech training:** Systematic instruction in electrolarynx use, esophageal speech, and TEP voicing
5. **Swallowing assessment:** [[Answers/General/Swallowing Assessment in Head and Neck Cancer|FEES and VFSS evaluation]] of swallowing function
6. **Olfactory rehabilitation:** Polite yawning technique and nasal airflow-inducing maneuver (NAIM) for olfaction recovery
7. **Psychological support:** Addressing communication-related anxiety, depression, and social isolation
8. **Long-term follow-up:** Monitoring voice quality, prosthesis function, and quality of life outcomes

---

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. Three methods of alaryngeal speech exist: esophageal speech (20-30% success), electrolarynx (~90%), and TEP/SVR (70-90%)
> 2. TEP voice is the gold standard — most natural quality, loudest, and highest intelligibility among alaryngeal methods
> 3. Esophageal speech uses only 40-70 ml of air versus 500 ml with TEP, limiting loudness and phrase length
> 4. The electrolarynx should be introduced immediately postoperatively as a temporary communication aid — it does not interfere with subsequent TEP acquisition
> 5. Fasciocutaneous flaps (RFFF, ALT) yield superior TEP voice quality compared to visceral flaps (jejunum, gastric pull-up)
> 6. A minimum 6 cm transverse mucosal width is essential for effortless TE speech after reconstruction
> 7. Voice preservation strategies include conservation laryngeal surgery and organ preservation chemoradiation
> 8. SLP involvement from the preoperative period through long-term follow-up is critical for optimal rehabilitation
> 9. In the Indian context, electrolarynx may be the most accessible option where TEP prosthesis costs are prohibitive
> 10. All laryngectomees should possess an electrolarynx as a backup communication device regardless of primary rehabilitation method
