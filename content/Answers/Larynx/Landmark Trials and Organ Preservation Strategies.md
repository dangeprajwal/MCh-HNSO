> The Department of Veterans Affairs Laryngeal Cancer Study Group (1991) published the landmark randomised trial comparing induction chemotherapy (cisplatin and 5-fluorouracil) followed by radiation therapy versus surgery plus postoperative radiation in advanced laryngeal cancer, demonstrating equivalent overall survival with larynx preservation in 64% of the chemotherapy arm, thereby establishing the concept that the larynx could be preserved without compromising survival. Lefebvre et al. (1996) conducted the EORTC 24891 phase III trial extending the organ-preservation concept to hypopharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma, showing equivalent survival between induction chemotherapy followed by RT and immediate surgery with postoperative RT, with successful larynx preservation in approximately 42% of patients. Forastiere et al. (2003) published the RTOG 91-11 trial, the definitive three-arm randomised study comparing induction cisplatin/5-FU followed by RT, concurrent cisplatin with RT, and RT alone for advanced laryngeal cancer, establishing concurrent chemoradiation as the standard non-surgical organ-preservation approach with an 84% laryngeal preservation rate. The 10-year update (Forastiere 2013) confirmed the superiority of concurrent CRT for laryngeal preservation but raised concerns about non-cancer-related deaths in the concurrent arm. Posner et al. (2007) reported the TAX 324 trial demonstrating the superiority of the TPF (docetaxel, cisplatin, 5-FU) regimen over PF as induction chemotherapy in locally advanced head and neck cancer. Pointreau et al. (2009) published the GORTEC 2000-01 trial specifically for larynx preservation, confirming superior larynx preservation rates with TPF versus PF induction chemotherapy (70.3% vs 57.5% at 3 years).

## Historical Context

Before 1991, the standard treatment for locally advanced (stage III/IV) laryngeal cancer was **total laryngectomy with postoperative radiation therapy**. This approach resulted in loss of the natural voice and permanent tracheostomy, with profound impact on quality of life. The question of whether the larynx could be preserved without compromising survival led to a series of landmark trials that transformed the management of laryngeal cancer.

## Timeline of Landmark Trials

| Year | Trial | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| 1991 | VA Laryngeal Cancer Study | Induction chemo + RT equivalent to surgery + RT; 64% larynx preservation |
| 1996 | EORTC 24891 | Extended organ preservation to hypopharyngeal cancer |
| 2003 | RTOG 91-11 | Concurrent CRT superior to induction CRT and RT alone for larynx preservation |
| 2007 | TAX 324 | TPF superior to PF as induction chemotherapy |
| 2009 | GORTEC 2000-01 | TPF superior to PF for larynx preservation |
| 2013 | RTOG 91-11 (10-yr update) | Concurrent CRT best for preservation; increased non-cancer deaths |

## VA Laryngeal Cancer Study (1991)

### Study Design
- **Randomised phase III trial**
- **Population**: 332 patients with stage III or IV squamous cell carcinoma of the larynx (both glottic and supraglottic)
- **Arms**:
  - Arm A: Induction chemotherapy (3 cycles of cisplatin 100 mg/m2 + 5-FU 1000 mg/m2/d x 5 days) followed by definitive RT (66-76 Gy) in responders; non-responders underwent salvage laryngectomy + postoperative RT
  - Arm B: Total laryngectomy + postoperative RT (50-70 Gy)
- **Primary endpoint**: Overall survival

### Key Results
- **No significant difference in overall survival** between the two arms (2-year OS 68% both arms)
- **Larynx preservation** achieved in **64%** of patients in the chemotherapy arm
- Response to the first 2 cycles of induction chemotherapy predicted likelihood of ultimate larynx preservation
- **Patterns of failure**: higher rate of local recurrence in the chemotherapy arm (compensated by surgical salvage)
- **Distant metastasis**: trend toward fewer distant metastases in the chemotherapy arm

### Impact
- Established the **proof of concept** that organ preservation was feasible without compromising survival
- Introduced the concept of **chemoselection** (response to induction chemotherapy as a biomarker for radiosensitivity)
- Opened the door for further investigations into non-surgical organ preservation

## EORTC 24891 Trial (Lefebvre 1996)

### Study Design
- **Randomised phase III trial** for **hypopharyngeal** (pyriform sinus) SCC
- **Population**: 202 patients with resectable stage II-IV hypopharyngeal cancer
- **Arms**:
  - Arm A: Induction cisplatin/5-FU (2-3 cycles) --> complete responders received definitive RT; non-responders underwent surgery + RT
  - Arm B: Immediate surgery (total laryngectomy + partial pharyngectomy) + postoperative RT
- **Primary endpoint**: Overall survival

### Key Results
- **No significant difference in OS** (median survival ~25 months in both arms)
- Larynx preservation in **42%** at 3 years, **35%** at 5 years
- 10-year update (2012): confirmed equivalent survival; retained larynx functional in most survivors

### Significance
- Extended the organ-preservation concept from laryngeal to **hypopharyngeal** cancer
- Demonstrated feasibility of larynx preservation even in the more aggressive hypopharyngeal cancer site

## RTOG 91-11 Trial (Forastiere 2003, 2013)

### Study Design
- **The definitive three-arm randomised trial** for larynx preservation
- **Population**: 547 patients with stage III or IV (T2-T4, N0-N2c, excluding T1 and massive T4) squamous cell carcinoma of the glottic or supraglottic larynx
- **Three arms**:
  - Arm A (Induction): Cisplatin/5-FU x 3 cycles --> RT (70 Gy) in responders
  - Arm B (Concurrent): Cisplatin 100 mg/m2 on days 1, 22, 43 + RT (70 Gy)
  - Arm C (RT alone): RT (70 Gy)

### Key Results (2003 Publication)

| Endpoint | Induction + RT | Concurrent CRT | RT Alone | P value |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Laryngeal preservation (2-yr) | 72% | **84%** | 67% | CRT vs IC: 0.005; CRT vs RT: <0.001 |
| Locoregional control | 61% | **78%** | 56% | CRT superior |
| Overall survival (2-yr) | 76% | 74% | 75% | NS between all arms |
| Disease-free survival | Similar | Best | Worst | -- |
| Distant metastasis | Lowest | Intermediate | Highest | -- |

### 10-Year Update (2013 Publication)

| Endpoint | Induction + RT | Concurrent CRT | RT Alone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Larynx preservation (10-yr) | 63.5% | **81.7%** | 63.8% |
| Overall survival (10-yr) | 27.5% | 28.9% | 31.5% |
| Laryngectomy-free survival | 23.5% | 28.8% | 22.8% |
| **Non-cancer deaths** | -- | **Highest** | Lowest |

### Critical Findings
1. **Concurrent CRT is superior** to both induction and RT alone for **laryngeal preservation**
2. **Overall survival was NOT different** between the three arms at 10 years
3. **Concerning signal**: more non-cancer-related deaths in the concurrent arm (possibly related to treatment toxicity and dysphagia-related aspiration/malnutrition)
4. **The paradox**: best larynx preservation but no survival advantage, and possibly worse quality of life in some patients

### Criticisms and Limitations
- Excluded T1 tumours and bulky T4 tumours (T4a with >1 cm invasion through cartilage or BOT invasion)
- No quality of life or functional outcome data systematically collected
- The non-cancer death signal in the concurrent arm raised questions about the "preserved but non-functional larynx"
- Salvage surgery rates were similar across arms (~15-20%)

## Induction Chemotherapy: TPF vs PF

### TAX 324 (Posner 2007)

- **Population**: 501 patients with locally advanced HNSCC (all sites, not just larynx)
- **Comparison**: TPF (docetaxel 75 + cisplatin 100 + 5-FU 1000 x 4d) x 3 cycles --> CRT vs. PF (cisplatin 100 + 5-FU 1000 x 5d) x 3 cycles --> CRT
- **Results**: TPF superior in OS (median 71 vs 30 months), PFS, and overall response rate
- **Toxicity**: more neutropenia with TPF but less mucositis; febrile neutropenia managed with G-CSF

### GORTEC 2000-01 (Pointreau 2009)

- **Population**: 213 patients with stage III/IV **laryngeal or hypopharyngeal** SCC requiring total laryngectomy
- **Comparison**: TPF x 3 cycles --> RT in responders vs. PF x 3 cycles --> RT in responders
- **Primary endpoint**: Larynx preservation at 3 years
- **Results**:
  - Larynx preservation: **70.3% (TPF) vs 57.5% (PF)** (P = 0.03)
  - Overall response rate to induction: 80% TPF vs 59% PF (P = 0.002)
  - OS: no significant difference
- **Conclusion**: TPF is the preferred induction regimen when induction chemotherapy is chosen for organ preservation

### TAX 323 (Vermorken 2007)

- Similar design to TAX 324 but European trial, using RT alone after induction (no concurrent chemotherapy)
- Confirmed TPF superiority over PF for all endpoints

## The Bioselection Concept

The concept of bioselection (or chemoselection) was introduced by the VA trial and has been refined over time:

### Principle
- Response to induction chemotherapy serves as an **in vivo biomarker** of tumour chemo- and radiosensitivity
- Patients whose tumours respond well to chemotherapy are likely to respond to subsequent RT and can undergo organ preservation
- Non-responders are identified early and directed to surgery without delay

### Implementation
1. Administer **2 cycles** of induction chemotherapy (TPF)
2. Assess response endoscopically and radiologically:
   - **Complete response (CR) or good partial response (PR >=50%)**: proceed to definitive RT or CRT --> organ preservation
   - **Less than PR or progressive disease**: proceed to **total laryngectomy** + postoperative RT
3. This approach spares non-responders from futile CRT and its toxicity

### Advantages
- Identifies chemo-resistant tumours early
- Reduces distant metastasis (early systemic treatment)
- Preserves surgical salvage options

### Limitations
- Delays definitive treatment by 6-9 weeks
- Added chemotherapy toxicity
- Response assessment can be difficult (residual oedema vs. tumour)
- Not validated in a head-to-head trial against concurrent CRT (RTOG 91-11 used PF, not TPF, for induction)

## Surgical Organ Preservation

Surgery-based organ preservation refers to conservation laryngeal surgery that preserves laryngeal function while achieving oncologic clearance.

### Options

| Procedure | Indication | Structures Preserved | Voice Quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Transoral laser microsurgery (TLM)** | Tis-T2 (selected T3) glottic/supraglottic | Variable (Type I-V cordectomy) | Good-excellent for early stage |
| **TORS** | Selected supraglottic T1-T2 | Laryngeal framework | Good |
| **Supraglottic laryngectomy (OPHL Type I)** | T1-T3 supraglottic | True vocal folds, both arytenoids, cricoid | Good |
| **Supracricoid partial laryngectomy (OPHL Type II)** | Selected T2-T3 (some T4a) glottic/transglottic | Cricoid, at least 1 arytenoid, hyoid | Moderate (breathy) |
| **Supratracheal laryngectomy (OPHL Type III)** | Selected T3-T4a subglottic/transglottic | At least 1 cricoarytenoid unit | Variable |

### Advantages Over CRT-Based Preservation
- Provides pathological staging information
- Definitive treatment in a single modality (avoids CRT toxicity)
- Allows adjuvant RT/CRT if high-risk features found
- Potentially better functional outcomes (avoids chronic RT toxicity -- xerostomia, fibrosis, dysphagia)
- Higher rates of "functional" organ preservation (vs. preserved but non-functional larynx after CRT)

## Non-Surgical Organ Preservation

### Current Standard: Concurrent Chemoradiation

Based on RTOG 91-11, the standard non-surgical approach is:
- **RT 70 Gy** in 35 fractions (7 weeks)
- **Cisplatin 100 mg/m2** on days 1, 22, 43 (3 cycles)
- Alternative: weekly cisplatin 40 mg/m2 (non-inferior in some series, better tolerated)
- Alternative agents: carboplatin (if cisplatin-ineligible), [[Answers/General/Monoclonal Antibodies in Head and Neck Cancer|cetuximab]] (based on Bonner 2006, but inferior to cisplatin-RT per RTOG 0522)

### Patient Selection for CRT
- T3 N0-N2c glottic or supraglottic laryngeal SCC (the primary indication)
- Patient preference for non-surgical approach
- Adequate organ function (renal, hepatic, performance status)
- Low-volume T4a with limited cartilage invasion (controversial)
- **Contraindications**: massive T4a or T4b, inadequate performance status (ECOG >=3), severe comorbidities

### Factors Favouring Surgery
- Anterior commissure involvement
- Paraglottic space invasion (limited)
- Patient preference to avoid CRT
- Young patient (avoid long-term RT effects)
- Pre-existing swallowing dysfunction
- Single vocal fold fixation with mobile arytenoid

### Factors Favouring CRT
- Bilateral supraglottic disease
- Extensive pre-epiglottic space invasion
- Medical comorbidities precluding surgery
- Patient preference
- N2 neck disease (better managed with CRT + planned neck dissection)

## The Larynx Preservation vs Overall Survival Controversy

### The Problem
- RTOG 91-11 showed that **larynx preservation does NOT equal survival benefit**
- The 10-year update revealed a concerning trend toward more non-cancer deaths in the concurrent CRT arm
- Population-level data (SEER analyses) have shown a **decline in overall survival** for laryngeal cancer since the widespread adoption of organ preservation approaches

### Chen et al. (2007) SEER Analysis
- Analysed >7,000 laryngeal cancer patients from 1985-2001
- Found a **decline in relative survival** for laryngeal cancer coinciding with decreased use of surgery
- Raised concern that the shift toward CRT may have worsened outcomes at a population level
- **However**: multiple confounders (stage migration, case mix changes, increased T4 presentations)

### The "Functional Larynx" Concept
- Not all preserved larynxes are functional
- Some patients after CRT have a non-functional larynx (aspiration, dysphagia, tracheostomy-dependent)
- **Laryngectomy-free survival** and **functional larynx preservation** are more meaningful endpoints than simple "larynx preservation rate"
- The RTOG 91-11 10-year update showed laryngectomy-free survival was NOT significantly different between arms

### Current Perspective
- Organ preservation is appropriate for **well-selected patients**
- **Not all patients are candidates** for organ preservation -- careful multidisciplinary evaluation is essential
- Surgery (partial or total laryngectomy) remains a valid and sometimes superior option
- The focus should be on **functional outcome** and **overall survival**, not just anatomical larynx preservation

## Current NCCN Guidelines (Summary)

### T1-T2 Glottic Cancer
- **Primary options**: RT alone OR endoscopic surgery (TLM)
- Conservation surgery (open partial) for selected cases
- Equivalent oncologic outcomes; choice based on expertise and patient factors

### T3 Glottic/Supraglottic Cancer
- **Option 1**: Concurrent CRT (cisplatin preferred)
- **Option 2**: Surgery (partial or total laryngectomy) +/- adjuvant RT/CRT
- **Option 3**: Induction chemotherapy (TPF) --> response-based selection (CRT for responders, surgery for non-responders) -- this approach is acceptable but not the standard
- Multidisciplinary tumour board discussion mandatory

### T4a Laryngeal Cancer
- **Standard**: Total laryngectomy + adjuvant RT/CRT
- Selected T4a (low-volume cartilage invasion): concurrent CRT may be considered (controversial)
- NCCN states that "total laryngectomy achieves better survival outcomes in T4a disease"

### T4b Laryngeal Cancer
- Generally considered unresectable
- Concurrent CRT or induction CRT --> CRT
- Clinical trial enrolment encouraged

## Key Points for Exam

1. The **VA Laryngeal Cancer Study (1991)** was the first to demonstrate equivalent survival with organ preservation using induction chemotherapy + RT vs. surgery + RT
2. **RTOG 91-11** established **concurrent CRT** (not induction) as the standard non-surgical organ-preservation approach, with 84% laryngeal preservation at 2 years
3. The 10-year RTOG 91-11 update raised concern about **increased non-cancer deaths** in the concurrent CRT arm
4. **TPF is superior to PF** for induction chemotherapy (TAX 324, GORTEC 2000-01)
5. The **bioselection concept** uses response to induction chemotherapy as an in vivo marker of tumour sensitivity
6. **Surgical organ preservation** (OPHL, TLM, TORS) offers pathological staging and avoids CRT toxicity
7. T4a laryngeal cancer is **generally treated with total laryngectomy** -- organ preservation with CRT is controversial for this stage
8. **Larynx preservation does not equal survival benefit** -- the focus should be on functional outcome and OS
9. Not all preserved larynxes are functional -- "laryngectomy-free survival" is a better endpoint
10. Current NCCN guidelines recommend **multidisciplinary evaluation** and shared decision-making for T3 laryngeal cancer
