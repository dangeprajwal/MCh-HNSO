## Overview

A thorough understanding of the surgical anatomy of the larynx is the cornerstone of rational treatment planning for laryngeal cancer. The larynx functions as an elaborate sphincter with three principal functions: airway protection during swallowing, respiration, and phonation. The internal architecture of the larynx -- its cartilaginous framework, fibro-elastic membranes, fascial spaces, and mucosal compartments -- directly determines the patterns of cancer spread and dictates the feasibility of conservation surgery.

## Laryngeal Framework

### Cartilaginous Skeleton

| Cartilage | Type | Key Features | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Thyroid** | Hyaline | Two laminae fused anteriorly at laryngeal prominence; superior and inferior cornua | Ossification begins at ~20 years; ossified cartilage is more resistant to tumour invasion than non-ossified cartilage; anterior commissure tendon (Broyles ligament) attaches directly to inner perichondrium |
| **Cricoid** | Hyaline | Complete ring; signet-ring shape -- narrow anterior arch, broad posterior lamina | Only complete cartilaginous ring in the airway; must be preserved in all partial laryngeal surgery; subglottic tumours penetrate via cricothyroid membrane |
| **Arytenoid** (paired) | Hyaline (body) + elastic (vocal process) | Pyramidal; vocal process (anterior), muscular process (lateral) | Mobility assessment critical for staging; fixation may indicate cricoarytenoid joint invasion or recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement |
| **Epiglottis** | Elastic | Leaf-shaped; perforated by vessels and nerves | Multiple fenestrations (foramina) permit early pre-epiglottic space invasion; elastic cartilage does not ossify and offers minimal barrier to tumour |
| **Corniculate** | Elastic | Sits atop arytenoid apex | Minor clinical significance |
| **Cuneiform** | Elastic | Within aryepiglottic fold | Visible on laryngoscopy as a mucosal elevation |

### Key Anatomical Points

- **Thyroid cartilage ossification**: begins posteriorly and inferiorly and progresses anteriorly; at the anterior commissure, a gap often exists where the Broyles ligament attaches -- this is the weakest point of the thyroid cartilage framework
- **Cricothyroid membrane**: connects inferior border of thyroid to superior border of cricoid; represents the pathway for subglottic cancer to escape the larynx anteriorly
- **Thyrohyoid membrane**: connects superior border of thyroid to hyoid bone; penetrated by the superior laryngeal vessels and internal branch of the superior laryngeal nerve -- a route of extralaryngeal spread for supraglottic tumours

## Internal Anatomy: The Three Compartments

The interior of the larynx is divided into three anatomical compartments defined by the true and false vocal folds:

### Supraglottis

**Boundaries:**
- Superior: tip of epiglottis, aryepiglottic folds, arytenoid cartilages
- Inferior: horizontal plane through the lateral floor of the ventricle (junction of ventricle with superior surface of true vocal fold)

**Subsites (AJCC):**
1. Suprahyoid epiglottis (including tip, lingual and laryngeal surfaces)
2. Infrahyoid epiglottis
3. Aryepiglottic folds (laryngeal aspect)
4. Arytenoids
5. Ventricular bands (false vocal folds)

**Mucosal characteristics:**
- Loosely adherent respiratory epithelium with abundant submucosal lymphatics
- Rich lymphatic drainage -- bilateral cervical nodal metastasis common (25-50% at presentation)
- Mucosa is loosely attached to underlying structures, allowing submucosal spread

### Glottis

**Boundaries:**
- Superior: horizontal plane through lateral floor of ventricle
- Inferior: horizontal plane 1 cm below the free edge of the vocal fold (or 5 mm below the free edge in some definitions)
- Includes: true vocal folds, anterior commissure, posterior commissure

**Subsites:**
1. True vocal folds
2. Anterior commissure
3. Posterior commissure (interarytenoid region)

**Mucosal characteristics:**
- Stratified squamous epithelium covers the free edge of the true vocal fold
- Mucosa tightly adherent to the vocal ligament -- minimal submucosal space
- **Sparse lymphatic drainage** -- nodal metastasis in early glottic cancer is <5% (T1-T2)
- Reinke space: potential space beneath the epithelium of the membranous vocal fold, superficial to the vocal ligament

### Subglottis

**Boundaries:**
- Superior: 1 cm (or 5 mm) below free edge of vocal fold
- Inferior: lower border of cricoid cartilage

**Mucosal characteristics:**
- Respiratory epithelium with moderate lymphatic drainage
- Lymphatics drain to pretracheal (Delphian), paratracheal, and mediastinal nodes
- Primary subglottic cancer is rare (~5% of all laryngeal cancers) but carries a poor prognosis due to early extralaryngeal spread via the cricothyroid membrane

## Fibro-Elastic Membranes

### Quadrangular Membrane

- **Location**: extends from lateral margin of epiglottis to arytenoid cartilage on each side
- **Superior free edge**: forms the aryepiglottic fold
- **Inferior free edge**: forms the ventricular ligament (false vocal fold)
- **Clinical significance**: the quadrangular membrane acts as a partial barrier between the supraglottic mucosa and the paraglottic space; however, it is thin and incomplete and is readily breached by tumour

### Conus Elasticus (Cricovocal Membrane)

- **Location**: extends from superior border of cricoid cartilage inferiorly to the vocal ligament superiorly
- **Superior free edge**: forms the **vocal ligament** (which with the vocalis muscle forms the body of the true vocal fold)
- **Anterior attachment**: thyroid cartilage at the anterior commissure (via Broyles ligament)
- **Clinical significance**: the conus elasticus acts as a more robust barrier to inferior tumour spread from the glottis into the subglottis; tumour must penetrate this membrane to reach the subglottic compartment

### Relationship Between Membranes

The quadrangular membrane and conus elasticus together form the **fibro-elastic skeleton of the larynx**. They are separated by the gap of the laryngeal ventricle (sinus of Morgagni). The ventricle itself was historically considered a barrier, but Weinstein (1995) demonstrated that the paraglottic space provides a continuous pathway for tumour to bypass this barrier.

## Laryngeal Spaces

### Pre-Epiglottic Space (Space of Boyer)

**Boundaries:**
- Anterior: thyrohyoid membrane and upper portion of thyroid cartilage inner surface
- Posterior: epiglottis and thyroepiglottic ligament
- Superior: hyoepiglottic ligament and vallecula mucosa
- Lateral: continuous with the paraglottic space on each side

**Contents:**
- Fat, areolar tissue, minor salivary glands
- Branches of superior laryngeal artery

**Clinical significance:**
- The epiglottis is perforated by multiple **fenestrations** (foramina) through which blood vessels and nerves pass -- these same channels permit direct tumour invasion from the laryngeal surface of the epiglottis into the pre-epiglottic space
- Supraglottic tumours (especially suprahyoid epiglottis) invade this space early
- Once in the pre-epiglottic space, tumour can spread to:
  - **Vallecula** and **base of tongue** (superiorly through the hyoepiglottic ligament)
  - **Paraglottic space** bilaterally (lateral continuous extension)
  - **Anterior neck soft tissues** (through the thyrohyoid membrane)
- Pre-epiglottic space invasion upstages the tumour (T3 or higher) and may preclude supraglottic partial laryngectomy
- Best assessed by **sagittal MRI** (high signal on T1-weighted images replaced by tumour signal)

### Paraglottic Space

**Boundaries:**
- Lateral: thyroid cartilage inner perichondrium and cricothyroid membrane
- Medial: conus elasticus (inferiorly), quadrangular membrane (superiorly), ventricle (at the level of the glottis)
- Superior: continuous with pre-epiglottic space
- Inferior: upper border of cricoid cartilage

**Contents:**
- Fat, areolar tissue, vessels, nerves
- Body of the arytenoid cartilage (posteriorly)
- Saccule of the ventricle (appendix of Morgagni) projects into the paraglottic space

**Clinical significance:**
- The paraglottic space is the **critical pathway for transglottic tumour spread** -- it permits tumour to bypass the ventricle and extend from supraglottis to glottis (or vice versa) without crossing mucosal barriers
- Once tumour enters the paraglottic space, it can track:
  - Superiorly into the pre-epiglottic space
  - Inferiorly through the cricothyroid membrane (extralaryngeal spread)
  - Laterally through the thyroid cartilage (especially at ossified regions)
  - Posteriorly to fix the arytenoid cartilage
- Paraglottic space invasion is a key determinant in selecting conservation surgery vs. total laryngectomy
- Best assessed by **axial CT or MRI** at the level of the false and true vocal folds

## Broyles Ligament and the Anterior Commissure

### Broyles Ligament (Anterior Commissure Tendon)

The anterior commissure tendon (Broyles ligament) is a fibrous structure that anchors the anterior ends of both vocal ligaments to the inner surface of the thyroid cartilage at the midline.

**Anatomical details:**
- Composed of dense fibrous tissue continuous with the vocal ligaments
- Attaches directly to the **inner perichondrium** of the thyroid cartilage -- there is **no intervening cartilage** between the tendon and the external perichondrium at this point
- The thyroid cartilage is thinnest at this attachment point
- Small blood vessels and lymphatics pass through the Broyles ligament

**Clinical significance:**
- The anterior commissure is the **weakest point** in the laryngeal framework
- Tumours involving the anterior commissure can invade directly through the thyroid cartilage into the pre-epiglottic space or the anterior neck soft tissues via the Broyles ligament
- Anterior commissure involvement by cancer has significant implications:
  - Higher risk of cartilage invasion
  - Higher risk of extralaryngeal spread
  - May be a relative contraindication for endoscopic laser surgery (type V cordectomy required)
  - Requires careful assessment on CT for cartilage erosion
- The Broyles ligament is the only site in the larynx where there is a **direct connection between the mucosal surface and the external perichondrium** without intervening cartilage

### Posterior Commissure

- The interarytenoid region connecting the two arytenoid cartilages
- Contains the transverse and oblique arytenoid muscles
- Mucosa is loosely adherent
- Tumour involvement can extend to both arytenoids and compromise airway

## Barriers to Cancer Spread

The larynx contains several anatomical structures that function as natural barriers to the spread of cancer. Understanding these barriers is fundamental to oncologically sound conservation surgery.

### Effective Barriers

| Barrier | Location | Effectiveness | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conus elasticus** | Glottis to subglottis | Moderate-to-strong | Dense fibro-elastic membrane; tumour must erode through |
| **Thyroid cartilage** (non-ossified) | Lateral wall | Strong | Non-ossified hyaline cartilage is resistant to invasion |
| **Cricoid cartilage** | Subglottis | Strong | Dense, thick complete ring; rarely invaded early |
| **Vocal ligament** | Free edge of vocal fold | Moderate | Dense collagen resists superficial spread to depth |

### Weak or Incomplete Barriers

| Structure | Location | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| **Epiglottic cartilage** | Supraglottis | Multiple fenestrations allow direct invasion of pre-epiglottic space |
| **Quadrangular membrane** | Supraglottis | Thin, incomplete; readily breached |
| **Broyles ligament** | Anterior commissure | Direct pathway from mucosa to external perichondrium |
| **Laryngeal ventricle** | Between glottis and supraglottis | NOT a true barrier -- paraglottic space provides continuous pathway |
| **Ossified thyroid cartilage** | Variable locations | Ossification introduces Haversian canals with marrow spaces that permit tumour ingress (Yeager 1982) |
| **Cricothyroid membrane** | Anterior subglottis | Thin membrane; primary route for subglottic extralaryngeal spread |

### Kirchner's Observations (1977)

From the serial section study of 200 laryngeal cancers:
1. The **ventricle is NOT a reliable barrier** -- tumour spreads through the paraglottic space
2. **Cartilage invasion** is more common where cartilage is ossified (because ossification creates Haversian-like vascular channels)
3. **Extralaryngeal spread** most commonly occurs through:
   - Cricothyroid membrane (subglottic tumours)
   - Thyrohyoid membrane (supraglottic tumours)
   - Anterior commissure/Broyles ligament (glottic tumours)
4. **Tumour size and fixation** are the best clinical predictors of deep invasion

## Routes of Extralaryngeal Spread

### Supraglottic Cancer Spread

1. **Pre-epiglottic space** (most common route):
   - Tumour penetrates epiglottic fenestrations
   - Extends anteriorly and superiorly
   - Can reach the vallecula and base of tongue
   - Can cross the midline readily (bilateral pre-epiglottic space involvement)

2. **Paraglottic space** (transglottic spread):
   - Tumour enters from the supraglottic mucosa through the quadrangular membrane
   - Tracks inferiorly alongside the ventricle into the glottic and subglottic levels
   - Produces vocal fold fixation (T3 staging)
   - May exit through the cricothyroid membrane

3. **Thyrohyoid membrane**:
   - Direct anterior spread through the membrane
   - Along the path of the superior laryngeal neurovascular bundle

4. **Aryepiglottic fold** to pyriform sinus:
   - Lateral spread to the medial wall of pyriform sinus
   - Converts a laryngeal cancer to a laryngopharyngeal cancer

### Glottic Cancer Spread

1. **Anterior commissure** (via Broyles ligament):
   - Direct invasion through the thyroid cartilage at the midline
   - Into the pre-epiglottic space or anterior neck soft tissues
   - Can involve contralateral vocal fold (bilateral disease)

2. **Paraglottic space** (lateral and vertical spread):
   - Tumour invades lateral to the conus elasticus
   - Produces vocal fold fixation
   - Extends superiorly (transglottic) or inferiorly (subglottic)

3. **Posterior spread**:
   - To arytenoid cartilage and cricoarytenoid joint
   - Produces vocal fold fixation
   - To interarytenoid region (posterior commissure)

### Subglottic Cancer Spread

1. **Cricothyroid membrane** (most common route):
   - Thin membrane offers minimal resistance
   - Tumour exits into the anterior neck, involving the thyroid gland

2. **Inferior along the trachea**:
   - Submucosal extension into the cervical trachea
   - May extend several centimetres below the gross tumour margin
   - Requires adequate inferior margin at surgery (minimum 1-2 cm)

3. **Lateral through the cricoid cartilage**:
   - Less common due to the dense structure of the cricoid
   - When present, indicates advanced disease

### Transglottic Spread Pattern

Transglottic cancer is defined as tumour involving both the supraglottis and glottis (with or without subglottic extension). The mechanism is invasion of the **paraglottic space**, which provides a continuous pathway:

- Supraglottic mucosa --> quadrangular membrane --> paraglottic space --> alongside the ventricle --> glottic level --> conus elasticus --> subglottis
- This is a T3 or T4 tumour by definition (vocal fold fixation or framework invasion)
- Transglottic tumours typically require **total laryngectomy** or, in selected cases, **supracricoid partial laryngectomy** (OPHL Type II)

## Relevance to Conservation Surgery Planning

### Selection Criteria Based on Anatomy

| Anatomical Assessment | Impact on Surgical Decision |
|---|---|
| Pre-epiglottic space invasion | Precludes supraglottic laryngectomy if extensive; may be included in supracricoid resection |
| Paraglottic space invasion | If limited, supracricoid partial laryngectomy feasible; if extensive, total laryngectomy required |
| Anterior commissure involvement | May require open partial surgery (OPHL) rather than transoral laser; cartilage invasion must be excluded |
| Arytenoid fixation | If one arytenoid mobile, can be preserved in supracricoid surgery; bilateral fixation = total laryngectomy |
| Subglottic extension >10 mm anteriorly or >5 mm posteriorly | Precludes supracricoid partial laryngectomy |
| Thyroid cartilage invasion (inner cortex vs. through-and-through) | Inner cortex invasion: OPHL Type II may still be feasible; through-and-through invasion: total laryngectomy (T4a) |
| Cricoid cartilage invasion | Absolute contraindication for any conservation surgery |
| Extralaryngeal soft tissue spread | Total laryngectomy required |

### The European Laryngological Society Classification (Succo 2014)

| Type | Name | Inferior Limit of Resection | Structures Preserved |
|---|---|---|---|
| **OPHL Type I** | Supraglottic | Ventricle | Vocal folds, arytenoids, cricoid |
| **OPHL Type II** | Supracricoid | Cricoid | Cricoid, at least one arytenoid, hyoid |
| **OPHL Type III** | Supratracheal | Trachea | At least one cricoarytenoid unit |

Each type has subtypes based on extensions:
- Type Ia/Ib: with/without resection of pre-epiglottic space
- Type IIa/IIb: cricohyoidopexy (CHP) vs. cricohyoidoepiglottopexy (CHEP)
- Extensions to one arytenoid, base of tongue, or pyriform sinus

## Imaging Assessment

### CT vs. MRI for Anatomical Assessment

| Feature | CT | MRI |
|---|---|---|
| Cartilage invasion | Good (sclerosis, erosion, lysis) | Better for differentiating tumour from inflammation |
| Pre-epiglottic space | Good | Excellent (sagittal plane superior) |
| Paraglottic space | Good (axial imaging) | Excellent (multiplanar) |
| Subglottic extension | Good | Good |
| Extralaryngeal spread | Good | Good |
| Anterior commissure | Thin-slice axial CT excellent | Good |
| Vocal fold mobility | Not assessed (static) | Dynamic MRI sequences emerging |

### Key Imaging Findings to Report

1. **Pre-epiglottic space**: high-density tissue replacing normal fat on CT; loss of T1 high signal on MRI
2. **Paraglottic space**: asymmetric soft tissue density/signal at the glottic level
3. **Cartilage invasion**: sclerosis, erosion, or lysis of thyroid/cricoid cartilage on CT; abnormal signal replacement on MRI
4. **Anterior commissure**: soft tissue abutting or eroding the inner cortex of thyroid cartilage at the midline
5. **Subglottic extension**: measure distance below the free edge of the vocal fold
6. **Extralaryngeal spread**: soft tissue beyond the outer cortex of the thyroid cartilage or through the cricothyroid membrane

## Key Points for Exam

1. The larynx has three compartments: supraglottis (rich lymphatics), glottis (sparse lymphatics), and subglottis (moderate lymphatics draining to paratracheal nodes)
2. The **pre-epiglottic space** is continuous laterally with the **paraglottic space** -- together they form a horseshoe-shaped space around the larynx
3. **Broyles ligament** (anterior commissure tendon) is the only point where mucosa connects directly to external perichondrium without intervening cartilage -- the weakest point of the laryngeal framework
4. The **ventricle is NOT a reliable barrier** to cancer spread -- the paraglottic space permits transglottic spread (Weinstein 1995)
5. **Ossified cartilage is more susceptible** to tumour invasion than non-ossified cartilage because ossification creates vascular channels (Yeager 1982)
6. Kirchner's serial section studies (1977) established that tumour location, fixation, and size predict deep invasion patterns
7. The **conus elasticus** is a more effective barrier than the **quadrangular membrane**
8. Subglottic cancer spreads primarily through the **cricothyroid membrane** anteriorly
9. The European Laryngological Society OPHL classification (Succo 2014) provides a standardised anatomical framework for conservation surgery
10. Pre-epiglottic space, paraglottic space, anterior commissure, and subglottic extension must all be assessed on imaging before planning conservation surgery
