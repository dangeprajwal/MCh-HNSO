## Rationale for Imaging in Laryngeal Cancer

Clinical examination with indirect or fibreoptic laryngoscopy can assess mucosal extent of laryngeal tumours with reasonable accuracy but cannot evaluate:

1. **Submucosal spread** -- pre-epiglottic space, paraglottic space involvement
2. **Cartilage invasion** -- thyroid, cricoid, arytenoid cartilage erosion or destruction
3. **Extralaryngeal spread** -- extension through thyroid cartilage, invasion of strap muscles, thyroid gland, oesophagus
4. **Deep tumour extent** -- base of tongue, tracheal extension, subglottic extension
5. **Nodal staging** -- retropharyngeal, paratracheal, mediastinal nodes not palpable clinically

Imaging is therefore **essential** for accurate T and N staging, treatment planning (organ preservation vs surgery), radiation field design, and surgical planning.

### Indications for Imaging

- All T2--T4 laryngeal cancers
- Any lesion with suspected deep invasion (fixed cord, arytenoid fixation)
- Supraglottic cancers (high incidence of pre-epiglottic/paraglottic involvement)
- Assessment of nodal disease (N staging)
- Post-treatment surveillance
- T1 glottic cancers may not require cross-sectional imaging if clinically well-characterised

## Computed Tomography (CT) in Laryngeal Cancer

### CT Technique

**Optimal Protocol:**

| Parameter | Specification |
|---|---|
| Contrast | IV iodinated contrast (bolus injection) |
| Slice thickness | 1--2 mm axial sections through larynx |
| Window settings | Soft tissue window + bone window |
| Coverage | Skull base to thoracic inlet (for nodal staging) |
| Breathing manoeuvres | Quiet breathing (standard), Valsalva manoeuvre (distends pyriform sinuses), modified phonation ("e" sound -- abducts cords) |

**Breathing Manoeuvres and Their Utility:**

| Manoeuvre | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Quiet breathing | Standard assessment, cords in intermediate position |
| Valsalva | Distends pyriform sinuses, reveals hypopharyngeal extension |
| Phonation ("e" sound) | Adducts cords, assesses anterior commissure, glottic closure |
| Modified Valsalva (puffed cheeks) | Distends pharyngeal walls, delineates tumour extent |

### CT Advantages

1. **Cartilage invasion assessment** -- CT is the primary modality for evaluating thyroid and cricoid cartilage invasion
   - Sclerosis of non-ossified cartilage: sensitivity 71%, specificity 73%
   - Erosion/lysis of ossified cartilage: sensitivity 67--87%, specificity 84--94%
   - Combined criteria: sensitivity 80--90%, specificity 80--86%

2. **Extralaryngeal spread** -- extension through thyroid cartilage into strap muscles, thyroid gland

3. **Nodal staging** -- superior to clinical examination
   - Nodes >1 cm short axis (>1.5 cm jugulodigastric) considered positive
   - Central necrosis -- highly specific for metastasis (specificity >95%)
   - Extranodal extension (ENE) -- irregular nodal margin, infiltration of surrounding fat

4. **Subglottic extension** -- critical for planning laryngectomy (inferior tumour margin)

5. **Rapid acquisition** -- less motion artifact than MRI

6. **Wide availability** and standardised protocols

### CT Criteria for Cartilage Invasion

| CT Finding | Interpretation | Sensitivity | Specificity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sclerosis alone | Indeterminate (may be reactive) | 46--71% | 63--73% |
| Erosion/lysis | Strongly suggestive of invasion | 67--87% | 84--94% |
| Extralaryngeal tumour | Through-and-through invasion | 58--76% | 93--100% |
| Combined criteria | T4a staging | 80--90% | 80--86% |

**Pitfalls:**
- Non-ossified cartilage appears soft tissue density and cannot be distinguished from tumour
- Asymmetric ossification mimics invasion
- Perichondritis/inflammation mimics sclerosis
- Radiation-induced changes confound post-treatment assessment

### CT Limitations

- Poor soft tissue contrast compared to MRI
- Cannot reliably distinguish tumour from oedema or inflammation
- Beam hardening artifacts from dental amalgam
- Overstaging of cartilage invasion (inflammation mimics invasion)
- Radiation dose

## Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) in Laryngeal Cancer

### MRI Technique

| Sequence | Purpose |
|---|---|
| T1-weighted axial/coronal | Anatomy, fat-containing spaces (pre-epiglottic, paraglottic) |
| T2-weighted axial/coronal | Tumour detection (high signal), oedema assessment |
| T1 + gadolinium (fat-suppressed) | Tumour enhancement, cartilage invasion, nodal assessment |
| STIR | Tumour detection, suppresses fat signal |
| Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) | Differentiates tumour (restricted diffusion) from oedema |

### MRI Advantages

1. **Superior soft tissue contrast** -- best modality for evaluating:
   - **Pre-epiglottic space** invasion -- loss of normal high T1 fat signal
   - **Paraglottic space** involvement -- critical for T staging
   - **Tongue base extension** -- important in supraglottic cancers

2. **Cartilage invasion assessment** (Becker 2008 criteria):
   - Signal abnormality on T2-weighted images within cartilage
   - Enhancement of cartilage on post-gadolinium images
   - Disruption of low-signal cartilage cortex
   - Extralaryngeal tumour extension through cartilage
   - New MRI criteria achieve AUC 0.94 for cartilage invasion diagnosis

3. **Differentiation of tumour from oedema**:
   - Tumour: intermediate T2 signal, restricted diffusion (low ADC), enhancement
   - Oedema: high T2 signal, no restricted diffusion, minimal enhancement

4. **No ionising radiation** -- advantage for follow-up imaging

5. **Multiplanar capability** -- coronal images excellent for subglottic/supraglottic extent

### MRI Limitations

- Longer acquisition time -- more susceptible to motion artifact (swallowing, breathing)
- Claustrophobia
- Contraindications (pacemakers, metallic implants)
- Inferior spatial resolution compared to CT
- Less available and more expensive than CT
- Less reliable for cortical bone detail

## CT vs MRI: Comprehensive Comparison

| Parameter | CT | MRI |
|---|---|---|
| **Spatial resolution** | Superior | Inferior |
| **Soft tissue contrast** | Moderate | Superior |
| **Cartilage invasion (ossified)** | Excellent (erosion, lysis) | Good (signal change, enhancement) |
| **Cartilage invasion (non-ossified)** | Limited | Superior |
| **Pre-epiglottic space** | Moderate | Excellent |
| **Paraglottic space** | Good | Excellent |
| **Anterior commissure** | Good (with phonation) | Moderate |
| **Subglottic extension** | Good | Good |
| **Extralaryngeal spread** | Good | Good |
| **Nodal staging** | Excellent | Excellent |
| **Tumour vs oedema** | Poor | Good (DWI, T2) |
| **Acquisition time** | Fast (seconds) | Slow (20--30 min) |
| **Motion artifact** | Minimal | Significant |
| **Availability** | Universal | Limited |
| **Cost** | Lower | Higher |
| **Radiation** | Yes | No |
| **Sensitivity (overall T staging)** | 83--90% | 85--95% |
| **Specificity (overall T staging)** | 80--88% | 80--90% |

### When to Choose CT vs MRI

**CT preferred:**
- Initial staging in most institutions
- Suspected ossified cartilage invasion
- Nodal staging
- Rapid assessment needed
- Claustrophobic patients
- Post-treatment surveillance (baseline)

**MRI preferred:**
- Suspected pre-epiglottic/paraglottic space invasion
- Non-ossified cartilage assessment
- Differentiating tumour from oedema (post-treatment)
- Tongue base extension
- When CT is equivocal for cartilage invasion
- Follow-up studies (no radiation)

**Combined CT + MRI:**
- Complex T3/T4 tumours where staging affects treatment choice (organ preservation vs laryngectomy)
- Equivocal cartilage invasion on either modality alone

## Imaging-Based T Staging of Laryngeal Cancer

### Supraglottic Cancer T Staging by Imaging

| T Stage | Imaging Findings |
|---|---|
| T1 | Tumour confined to one supraglottic subsite, normal vocal cord mobility |
| T2 | Tumour involves mucosa of >1 adjacent supraglottic subsite or glottis or adjacent region outside supraglottis; no fixation |
| T3 | Tumour limited to larynx with vocal cord fixation OR invasion of postcricoid area, pre-epiglottic space, paraglottic space, minor thyroid cartilage erosion |
| T4a | Tumour invades through thyroid cartilage OR invades tissues beyond larynx (trachea, deep extrinsic tongue muscles, strap muscles, thyroid, oesophagus) |
| T4b | Tumour invades prevertebral space, encases carotid artery, or invades mediastinal structures |

### Glottic Cancer T Staging by Imaging

| T Stage | Imaging Findings |
|---|---|
| T1a | Tumour limited to one vocal cord (may involve anterior/posterior commissure), normal mobility |
| T1b | Tumour involves both vocal cords |
| T2 | Tumour extends to supraglottis/subglottis OR impaired vocal cord mobility |
| T3 | Tumour limited to larynx with vocal cord fixation OR invasion of paraglottic space OR minor thyroid cartilage erosion |
| T4a | Through thyroid cartilage invasion OR extralaryngeal spread |
| T4b | Prevertebral space, carotid encasement, mediastinal invasion |

### Key Imaging Findings That Change T Stage

1. **Paraglottic space involvement** (T2 → T3) -- loss of fat density in paraglottic space on CT, loss of T1 high signal on MRI
2. **Pre-epiglottic space invasion** (T2 → T3) -- loss of fat in pre-epiglottic space
3. **Vocal cord fixation** (T2 → T3) -- fixed cord in paramedian position, arytenoid fixation, cricoarytenoid joint invasion
4. **Thyroid cartilage invasion** (T3 minor erosion → T4a through-and-through) -- critical distinction for organ preservation eligibility
5. **Extralaryngeal spread** (→ T4a) -- tumour extending beyond laryngeal framework

## PET-CT in Laryngeal Cancer

### Principles and Indications

PET-CT using 18F-fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) has a defined role in laryngeal cancer management:

| Indication | Role | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Initial staging** | Not routinely indicated for early laryngeal cancer; may be useful for advanced (T3/T4) to detect distant metastases | Moderate |
| **Post-treatment surveillance** | Primary role -- assessment of treatment response after chemoradiation | High (PET-NECK trial) |
| **Residual vs recurrent disease** | Distinguishing residual/recurrent tumour from post-treatment changes | High |
| **Unknown primary** | Detecting occult primary with cervical metastasis | High |
| **Distant metastasis screening** | M staging in advanced disease | Moderate |

### Post-Treatment PET-CT: Optimal Timing

**Critical concept -- optimal timing is 12 weeks post-radiation therapy:**

| Timing | Rationale |
|---|---|
| < 8 weeks post-RT | High false-positive rate due to radiation-induced inflammation |
| **10--12 weeks post-RT** | **Optimal window -- inflammation subsides, residual tumour detectable** |
| > 16 weeks post-RT | Risk of disease progression if residual disease present |

### PET-NECK Trial (Mehanna 2016) -- Key Findings

- 564 patients with N2/N3 nodal disease after chemoradiotherapy
- PET-CT surveillance vs planned neck dissection
- **2-year OS**: 84.9% (surveillance) vs 81.5% (planned ND) -- non-inferior
- PET-CT surveillance resulted in **fewer neck dissections** (54 vs 221)
- Cost savings of ~$2,200 per patient
- **Conclusion**: PET-CT-guided surveillance is the standard of care for assessment of nodal response after chemoradiation in advanced head and neck cancer

### False Positives in PET-CT

| Cause of False Positive | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Chondroradionecrosis** | FDG uptake in irradiated cartilage mimics recurrence |
| Post-RT inflammation | Diffuse mucosal uptake early after treatment |
| Infection/abscess | Focal intense uptake |
| Granulation tissue | Moderate uptake |
| Laryngeal muscle activity | Symmetrical physiological uptake |

**Chondroradionecrosis:** A specific pitfall in laryngeal cancer PET-CT -- necrotic irradiated cartilage shows intense FDG uptake that mimics recurrent tumour. Correlation with CT component (cartilage fragmentation, gas) and clinical assessment is essential.

### PET-CT Performance

| Parameter | Primary Staging | Post-Treatment Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Sensitivity (primary) | 89--95% | 85--95% |
| Specificity (primary) | 80--88% | 75--90% |
| NPV (nodal assessment) | 90--97% | 92--98% |
| PPV | 75--85% | 60--80% |

## Narrow Band Imaging (NBI)

### Principles

NBI is an optical enhancement technology that uses specific wavelengths of light to enhance visualisation of mucosal surface patterns and superficial vasculature:

- **Blue light (415 nm)** -- absorbed by haemoglobin in superficial capillaries, highlights intraepithelial papillary capillary loops (IPCL)
- **Green light (540 nm)** -- absorbed by haemoglobin in submucosal vessels, highlights deeper vasculature
- Combined with high-definition television (HDTV) provides magnified view of mucosal microvasculature

### Enhanced Mucosal Vascular Patterns in NBI

| Pattern | Description | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Type I** | Small dark dots (normal IPCL) | Normal mucosa |
| **Type II** | Dilated, elongated IPCL in regular arrangement | Inflammatory/hyperplastic |
| **Type III** | Tortuous, irregular, dilated IPCL | Low-grade dysplasia |
| **Type IV** | Irregular, destroyed IPCL with irregular surface | High-grade dysplasia/CIS |
| **Type V** | Disorganised, thick, tortuous vessels; neoangiogenesis | Invasive carcinoma |

### Clinical Applications in Laryngeal Cancer

1. **Detection of mucosal lesions** -- NBI detects flat or superficial lesions missed by white-light endoscopy
2. **Delineation of tumour margins** -- improved identification of superficial spread for laser excision planning
3. **Identification of synchronous lesions** -- detection of second primary tumours in upper aerodigestive tract
4. **Post-treatment surveillance** -- early detection of mucosal recurrence
5. **Biopsy guidance** -- targeted biopsies of suspicious vascular patterns

### Evidence (Piazza 2010)

- Prospective study of 279 patients (96 with laryngeal SCC, 183 under follow-up)
- NBI + HDTV detected additional lesions missed by white-light endoscopy in 18% of cases
- Improved delineation of tumour margins in 32% of cases
- Sensitivity for detecting superficial neoplastic lesions: 97%
- Specificity: 92%
- NBI is particularly useful in the **follow-up setting** for detecting early recurrence and synchronous second primaries

### NBI Limitations

- Requires dedicated equipment (NBI-compatible endoscope)
- Learning curve for pattern interpretation
- Limited penetration depth (superficial mucosal assessment only)
- Cannot assess submucosal or deep tumour extent
- Blood/mucus obscures visualisation
- Not a replacement for cross-sectional imaging (CT/MRI)

## Integrated Imaging Algorithm for Laryngeal Cancer

### Initial Staging

1. **Clinical examination** -- indirect/fibreoptic laryngoscopy with documentation
2. **NBI endoscopy** (if available) -- assess mucosal extent, identify synchronous lesions
3. **Contrast-enhanced CT** -- standard cross-sectional imaging for T and N staging
4. **MRI** -- when CT equivocal for cartilage invasion, pre-epiglottic/paraglottic space, or to differentiate tumour from oedema
5. **PET-CT** -- advanced disease (T3/T4, N2/N3) for distant metastasis screening

### Post-Treatment Surveillance

1. **Clinical examination** at regular intervals
2. **NBI endoscopy** -- for mucosal surveillance, detect early recurrence
3. **PET-CT at 12 weeks post-CRT** -- standard for assessing treatment response (PET-NECK protocol)
4. **CT/MRI** -- for suspected recurrence on clinical examination or PET-CT

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **CT is the workhorse** for laryngeal cancer staging -- assess cartilage invasion, extralaryngeal spread, nodal disease
2. **MRI is superior** for pre-epiglottic/paraglottic space and differentiating tumour from oedema
3. **Breathing manoeuvres** during CT improve assessment -- Valsalva (pyriform sinuses), phonation (anterior commissure)
4. **Cartilage erosion/lysis on CT** is more specific than sclerosis for neoplastic invasion
5. **PET-CT at 12 weeks post-RT** is the optimal timing for post-treatment assessment -- earlier scans have high false-positive rates
6. **PET-NECK trial** established PET-CT surveillance as non-inferior to planned neck dissection
7. **NBI improves detection** of superficial mucosal lesions and synchronous tumours but cannot replace cross-sectional imaging for deep extent
8. **Chondroradionecrosis** is a major cause of false-positive PET-CT in post-treatment laryngeal cancer
9. **Paraglottic space invasion** changes T2 to T3 -- critical for organ preservation decisions
10. **Through-and-through cartilage invasion** (T4a) generally contraindicates organ preservation
