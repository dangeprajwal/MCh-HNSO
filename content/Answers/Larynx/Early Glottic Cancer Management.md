> Mendenhall et al. (2001) published a seminal analysis of 519 patients with T1-T2N0 squamous cell carcinoma of the glottic larynx treated with definitive radiotherapy at the University of Florida, establishing benchmark local control rates and identifying prognostic factors including T-stage, anterior commissure involvement, and overall treatment time. Steiner (1993) published the landmark results of curative laser microsurgery in 240 patients with laryngeal carcinoma (pTis-pT4), demonstrating that transoral laser microsurgery could achieve oncologic outcomes comparable to open surgery and radiotherapy while preserving laryngeal function. Feng et al. (2011) conducted a meta-analysis comparing laser surgery versus radiotherapy for T1-T2N0 glottic cancer, finding no significant difference in local control between the two modalities, providing level I evidence for treatment equivalence. Huang et al. (2017) published a systematic review of 52 studies comprising 2360 patients with T1b glottic squamous cell carcinoma, reporting an overall disease-free survival of 85% and comparing outcomes among RT, TLM, and open partial laryngectomy. Canis et al. (2015) reported a 27-year single-institution experience of TLM for T1a glottic carcinoma in 404 patients, demonstrating local control of 86%, larynx preservation of 97%, and disease-specific survival of 98%.

## Definition and Staging of Early Glottic Cancer

**Early glottic cancer** is defined as **T1-T2 N0 M0** squamous cell carcinoma of the glottic larynx.

### AJCC 8th Edition T-staging (Glottis)

| T-stage | Definition |
|---|---|
| **T1a** | Tumour limited to one vocal cord (may involve anterior or posterior commissure) with normal mobility |
| **T1b** | Tumour involves both vocal cords with normal mobility |
| **T2** | Tumour extends to supraglottis and/or subglottis, and/or with impaired vocal cord mobility |
| **Tis** | Carcinoma in situ |

### Key Anatomic Considerations

- The **true vocal cord** has sparse lymphatic drainage, resulting in very low nodal metastasis rates for T1 glottic cancer (<2%)
- The **anterior commissure** is a critical landmark where Broyles ligament allows direct tumour access to the thyroid cartilage
- **Subglottic extension** >10 mm anteriorly or >5 mm posteriorly significantly impacts treatment planning
- The **paraglottic space** is a potential pathway for deep invasion in T2 tumours

## Treatment Options

### Overview

Three primary treatment modalities exist for early glottic cancer, each offering equivalent oncologic outcomes:

1. **Radiotherapy (RT)** — external beam radiation
2. **Transoral laser microsurgery (TLM)** — CO2 laser excision
3. **Open partial laryngectomy (OPL)** — vertical partial laryngectomy or SCPL-CHEP

### Treatment Selection Factors

| Factor | Favours RT | Favours TLM | Favours OPL |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1a, small volume | First choice | First choice | Rarely needed |
| T1a, anterior commissure | Debated (see below) | Technically challenging | Consider if deep AC |
| T1b | Good option | Good option | SCPL-CHEP preferred |
| T2 with impaired mobility | Good option (higher dose) | If adequate exposure | SCPL-CHEP preferred |
| Patient preference for voice | Marginally better short-term | Acceptable | Lower voice quality |
| Access to repeated treatments | Single course | Repeatable | Single procedure |
| Cost consideration | Higher total cost | Lower cost | Intermediate |

## Radiotherapy for Early Glottic Cancer

### Technique

| Parameter | Standard Protocol |
|---|---|
| **Energy** | 6 MV photons (or cobalt-60) |
| **Technique** | Opposed lateral fields; 3D conformal or IMRT |
| **Patient positioning** | Supine, neck extended, thermoplastic mask immobilisation |
| **Field borders (T1)** | Superior: upper thyroid notch; Inferior: lower border of cricoid; Anterior: fall-off; Posterior: anterior border of vertebral body |
| **Field borders (T2)** | Slightly larger — superior to hyoid if supraglottic extension; inferior to 1st tracheal ring if subglottic extension |
| **Field size (T1)** | Typically 5 x 5 cm to 6 x 6 cm |

### Dose and Fractionation

| Scenario | Dose | Fractionation | OTT |
|---|---|---|---|
| **T1 — conventional** | 63-66 Gy | 2.0-2.25 Gy/fraction, 5 days/week | 6-6.5 weeks |
| **T1 — hypofractionation** | 56.25-58.08 Gy | 2.25 Gy/fraction, 5 days/week | 5-5.5 weeks |
| **T2 — conventional** | 65.25-70 Gy | 2.0-2.25 Gy/fraction | 6-7 weeks |
| **T2 — concomitant boost** | 72 Gy | 1.8 Gy/fraction AM + 1.5 Gy boost PM (last 12 fractions) | 6 weeks |

**Key RT principles**:
- **Hypofractionation** (2.25 Gy/fraction) is increasingly used for T1 glottic cancer — equivalent local control with shorter treatment time
- **Overall treatment time (OTT)** is critical — every day of prolongation beyond 6 weeks decreases local control by approximately 1.4% per day
- **No elective nodal irradiation** for T1N0 (nodal risk <2%)
- T2N0 — consider inclusion of level II-III if supraglottic extension

### RT Outcomes by T-stage (Mendenhall 2001)

| T-stage | 5-year Local Control | Ultimate Local Control (with salvage) |
|---|---|---|
| T1a | 85-94% | 95-98% |
| T1b | 75-85% | 90-95% |
| T2a (mobile cord) | 70-80% | 85-90% |
| T2b (impaired mobility) | 55-65% | 75-85% |

### Prognostic Factors for RT Outcome

1. **T-stage**: T1a > T1b > T2a > T2b
2. **Anterior commissure involvement**: Reduces local control by 5-15% (debated)
3. **Overall treatment time**: Prolongation >6 weeks decreases control
4. **Tumour bulk/volume**: Bulky T2 has worse outcomes
5. **Subglottic extension**: Negative prognostic factor
6. **Dose per fraction**: ≥2.0 Gy preferred (≥2.25 Gy for T1)

## Transoral Laser Microsurgery (TLM)

### Technique for Early Glottic Cancer

1. **Suspension microlaryngoscopy** with appropriate laryngoscope (Kleinsasser, Dedo, Lindholm)
2. **CO2 laser** coupled to the operating microscope (spot size 0.3-0.5 mm)
3. **Resection according to ELS cordectomy types** (see [[Answers/Larynx/Laser Surgery in Laryngeal Cancer|Laser Surgery in Laryngeal Cancer]])
4. **Frozen section margins** — aiming for ≥1 mm clear margins
5. **Specimen orientation and inking** for pathological assessment

### TLM Cordectomy Type Selection for Early Glottic Cancer

| Tumour | Recommended Cordectomy |
|---|---|
| Tis / superficial T1a (mid-cord) | Type I (subepithelial) or Type II (subligamental) |
| T1a involving vocal ligament | Type III (transmuscular) |
| T1a involving vocalis muscle | Type IV (total cordectomy) |
| T1b | Type Va (extended, contralateral cord) |
| T2 with subglottic extension | Type Vc (extended, subglottis) |

### TLM Outcomes

| T-stage | Local Control | Larynx Preservation | DSS |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1a (Canis 2015, n=404) | 86% | 97% | 98% |
| T1b (Huang 2017, pooled) | 82% | 93% | 95% |
| T2 (Canis, pooled) | 70-76% | 83-93% | 84-93% |

## Comparison: RT vs TLM vs Open Partial Laryngectomy

### Oncologic Outcomes

| Parameter | RT | TLM | Open (SCPL-CHEP) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **T1a local control** | 85-94% | 85-90% | 95%+ |
| **T1b local control** | 75-85% | 80-85% | 90-95% |
| **T2 local control** | 65-80% | 70-76% | 85-92% |
| **Ultimate larynx preservation** | 95-98% | 95-98% | 95% |
| **Salvage option after failure** | TLM or TL | RT or TL | RT or TL |
| **5-year OS (T1)** | 90-95% | 90-95% | 90-95% |

### Functional Outcomes

| Parameter | RT | TLM | Open (SCPL-CHEP) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Voice quality (short-term)** | Best (mild hoarseness) | Good (depends on cordectomy type) | Worst (rough, breathy) |
| **Voice quality (long-term)** | Stable or slight decline | Improves with healing | Stable neoglottic voice |
| **VHI score** | 10-20 (mild) | 15-30 (mild-moderate) | 30-50 (moderate) |
| **Swallowing** | Normal | Normal | Rehabilitation required |
| **Tracheotomy** | Not required | Rarely required | Always required (temporary) |
| **Treatment duration** | 5-7 weeks | 1-2 days | 10-21 days hospital |
| **Return to work** | 2-4 weeks after RT | 1-2 weeks | 4-8 weeks |

### Cost-Effectiveness

| Modality | Approximate Cost (single treatment) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| RT (conventional) | Higher (equipment, daily fractions over 5-7 weeks) | Includes planning, simulation, QA |
| TLM | Lowest (single OR procedure, short stay) | Requires specialised equipment and expertise |
| Open surgery | Intermediate | Longer hospitalisation, rehabilitation costs |

**Meta-analysis evidence (Feng 2011)**: No significant difference in local control between RT and TLM for T1-T2N0 glottic cancer (OR 0.90, 95% CI 0.54-1.49).

## T1a vs T1b Management

### T1a Glottic Cancer

- **First-line options**: RT or TLM — both equally effective
- **RT**: 63-66 Gy in 28-30 fractions (or hypofractionated 56.25 Gy in 25 fractions)
- **TLM**: Type I-IV cordectomy depending on depth of invasion
- **Local control**: 85-94% with either modality
- **Voice**: RT produces marginally better voice quality in the first 6-12 months; TLM voice improves over time and approaches RT quality by 12-24 months

### T1b Glottic Cancer

- **Management is more complex** — bilateral cord involvement
- **RT**: Wider fields required; local control 75-85%
- **TLM**: Type Va cordectomy (extended bilateral); technically demanding; local control 80-85%
- **SCPL-CHEP**: Excellent local control (90-95%); preferred by some European centres for T1b
- **Anterior commissure involvement**: Nearly universal in T1b — impacts both RT and TLM outcomes

## T2 Glottic Cancer Management

### T2a (Normal Cord Mobility, Supraglottic/Subglottic Extension)

- **RT**: 65-70 Gy; local control 70-80%
- **TLM**: Type V cordectomy (extended); local control 70-76%
- **SCPL-CHEP**: Local control 85-92%
- **CRT**: Generally not indicated for T2N0 (no survival benefit of adding chemotherapy to T2)

### T2b (Impaired Cord Mobility)

- **RT**: Local control drops to 55-65%; consider concomitant boost or hyperfractionation
- **TLM**: Limited role — impaired mobility suggests deeper invasion
- **SCPL-CHEP**: Preferred surgical option; local control 85-90%
- **Open partial laryngectomy**: Frontolateral laryngectomy for small T2 with limited extension

## Impact of Anterior Commissure (AC) Involvement

### Anatomic Significance

- **Broyles ligament**: At the AC, the internal perichondrium of the thyroid cartilage is absent; the vocal ligament inserts directly onto the cartilage
- This creates a potential pathway for **cartilage invasion** and **extralaryngeal spread**
- The AC is a narrow space, making both RT delivery and TLM resection challenging

### Impact on RT

| Study | AC Involved | AC Not Involved | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mendenhall 2001 | 5-year LC: 77% | 5-year LC: 93% | -16% |
| Other series | Variable — 5-15% reduction in local control | — | — |

**Controversy**: Some authors argue that the AC effect is due to higher T-stage rather than the AC itself. Modern imaging (CT, MRI) helps identify deep AC invasion.

### Impact on TLM

- AC involvement makes TLM technically challenging — limited visualisation, risk of thermal damage to the thyroid cartilage
- Requires **anterior commissurotomy** (Type Va or dedicated AC resection)
- Some centres report comparable outcomes with experienced surgeons

### Management Recommendations for AC Involvement

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Superficial AC involvement, T1a | RT or TLM (experienced surgeon) |
| Deep AC involvement, T1b/T2 | SCPL-CHEP (best surgical option) or RT with higher dose |
| AC involvement with cartilage erosion (CT) | SCPL or total laryngectomy; RT alone insufficient |

## Current Guidelines

### NCCN Guidelines (2024)

- **T1-T2 N0 glottic**: RT or conservation surgery (TLM or open partial)
- **Voice preservation** should be prioritised
- **No elective neck treatment** for T1N0 glottic (nodal risk <2%)
- **T2N0**: Consider elective neck RT if supraglottic extension

### NICE Guidelines

- RT is the most commonly offered modality in the UK for T1 glottic cancer
- TLM is an acceptable alternative in centres with expertise

## Follow-up Protocol

| Interval | Frequency | Investigations |
|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | Every 1-2 months | Flexible nasendoscopy; stroboscopy if voice concern |
| Year 2 | Every 2-3 months | Flexible nasendoscopy |
| Years 3-5 | Every 3-6 months | Flexible nasendoscopy; CT if clinical concern |
| After 5 years | Annually | Flexible nasendoscopy |

**Key follow-up principles**:
- Most recurrences occur within the first 2 years
- Post-RT oedema can persist for 6-12 months — distinguish from recurrence
- **NBI (narrow band imaging)** enhances detection of mucosal recurrence
- Biopsy any suspicious lesion; EUA for any post-RT lesion not resolving by 12 weeks

## Key Points for Exam

1. Early glottic cancer is T1-T2 N0 M0 — extremely low nodal metastasis risk for T1 (<2%)
2. RT, TLM, and open partial laryngectomy offer equivalent oncologic outcomes for T1 disease
3. RT dose for T1 glottic: 63-66 Gy in 2.0-2.25 Gy/fraction; hypofractionation is acceptable
4. Overall treatment time >6 weeks for RT decreases local control by ~1.4% per day
5. TLM cordectomy type depends on depth of invasion (ELS Types I-V)
6. T1b is managed differently from T1a — bilateral cord involvement reduces RT local control to 75-85%
7. Anterior commissure involvement reduces RT local control by 5-15% — Broyles ligament allows direct cartilage access
8. T2 with impaired cord mobility (T2b) has lowest RT control (55-65%); SCPL-CHEP preferred by some centres
9. Meta-analysis (Feng 2011) shows no significant difference in local control between RT and TLM
10. Voice quality after RT is marginally better short-term; TLM voice improves over 12-24 months; both modalities achieve acceptable long-term voice
