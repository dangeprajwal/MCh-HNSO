## Anatomy of the Anterior Commissure

The anterior commissure (AC) is the point where the two true vocal cords meet anteriorly at the midline of the thyroid cartilage. It is one of the most surgically and oncologically important landmarks in the larynx.

### Broyles Ligament

**Broyles ligament** (anterior commissure tendon) is the key anatomic structure at this site:

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Composition** | Dense fibrous tissue — tendon connecting the vocalis muscle to the inner surface of the thyroid cartilage |
| **Attachments** | From the vocal process of the arytenoid through the vocal ligament to the anterior midline of the thyroid cartilage |
| **Unique histology** | At this point, the **inner perichondrium of the thyroid cartilage is absent** |
| **Mucosal relationship** | The mucosa is in **direct contact with cartilage** through Broyles ligament — no intervening perichondrium |
| **Kirchner's observation** | Kirchner (1969) demonstrated histologically that Broyles ligament contains small blood vessels and lymphatics that traverse the thyroid cartilage, providing a direct route for extralaryngeal tumour spread |

### Clinical Significance of Broyles Ligament

1. **Direct pathway for extralaryngeal spread** — tumour at the AC can invade through Broyles ligament directly into the pre-epiglottic space or extralaryngeal soft tissues without needing to erode through cartilage
2. **Absence of inner perichondrium** — unlike other parts of the thyroid cartilage framework, the perichondrium does not act as a barrier at the AC
3. **Bilateral cord involvement** — tumour at the AC frequently involves both vocal cords, making staging T1b even for small lesions
4. **Anterior subcommissure extension** — the AC region extends inferiorly into the anterior subglottic area (anterior subcommissure), facilitating subglottic spread
5. **Pre-epiglottic space access** — superiorly, tumour at the AC can extend into the pre-epiglottic space via the thyroepiglottic ligament

### Anatomic Boundaries of the Anterior Commissure Region

| Direction | Boundary/Structure |
|---|---|
| **Superiorly** | Anterior attachment of the vestibular folds; thyroepiglottic ligament → pre-epiglottic space |
| **Inferiorly** | Anterior subcommissure (anterior subglottic region) — first 10 mm below the free edge |
| **Laterally** | Anterior one-third of each true vocal cord |
| **Anteriorly** | Thyroid cartilage (inner surface) — Broyles ligament attachment |
| **Posteriorly** | Glottic lumen |

## Staging Implications

Even small tumours involving the anterior commissure have significant staging implications:

| Scenario | T-stage | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Tumour limited to one cord touching but not crossing AC | T1a | AC involvement, but unilateral |
| Tumour involving both cords at the AC | **T1b** | Bilateral cord involvement |
| AC tumour with impaired cord mobility | **T2** | Impaired mobility criterion |
| AC tumour with thyroid cartilage erosion | **T3** | Paraglottic space invasion or minor thyroid cartilage erosion |
| AC tumour with through-and-through cartilage invasion | **T4a** | Extralaryngeal spread |

**Key point:** A seemingly small tumour at the AC may be **upstaged** due to bilateral cord involvement (T1b), subglottic extension (T2), or cartilage invasion through Broyles ligament (T3/T4a).

## Investigations

### Clinical Assessment

- **Indirect laryngoscopy** — initial assessment of tumour extent, cord mobility
- **Videostroboscopy** — assessment of mucosal wave; loss of mucosal wave at the AC suggests deep invasion into the vocal ligament or underlying Broyles ligament
- **Flexible nasopharyngolaryngoscopy** — dynamic assessment of cord mobility; if fixed, minimum T3

### Microlaryngoscopy (Direct Laryngoscopy under GA)

This is the **gold standard** investigation for anterior commissure tumours:

| Purpose | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Direct visualisation** | Binocular magnified view of AC under general anaesthesia |
| **Bimanual palpation** | Assessment of tumour depth, fixation, and submucosal extension |
| **Biopsy** | Multiple biopsies — from tumour centre, margins, and subglottic extension |
| **Anterior commissure laryngoscope** | Specially designed scope (Zeitels design) to expose the AC; standard laryngoscopes may not adequately visualise this region |
| **NBI assessment** | [[Answers/General/Narrow Band Imaging|NBI]] can delineate superficial mucosal extent and identify satellite lesions |
| **Contact endoscopy** | In-vivo assessment of cellular morphology at the AC |

### Imaging

| Modality | Role in AC Carcinoma |
|---|---|
| **CT neck with contrast** | Primary imaging modality; assess thyroid cartilage sclerosis or erosion at the AC; evaluate subglottic extension; detect nodal disease |
| **MRI** | Superior for detecting submucosal and soft-tissue extension; assess pre-epiglottic space involvement; better at differentiating tumour from inflammation; T2-weighted sequences with fat suppression are ideal |
| **PET-CT** | Limited role in early AC tumours; useful for advanced disease or suspected recurrence after treatment |

### CT Findings Specific to AC Involvement

- **Thyroid cartilage sclerosis** at the AC — reactive change suggesting tumour abutment; NOT equivalent to cartilage invasion
- **Erosion of inner cortex** at the AC — concerning for early invasion through Broyles ligament
- **Through-and-through destruction** — confirms T4a disease
- **Anterior subglottic soft-tissue thickening** — suggests subglottic extension
- **Pre-epiglottic fat obliteration** — suggests superior spread

**Caveat:** CT has limitations at the AC due to the thin cartilage and partial volume averaging effects. MRI may be more accurate for assessing AC involvement.

## Management Controversy

The management of anterior commissure carcinoma remains **one of the most debated topics** in laryngeal oncology. The debate centres on:

1. **Radiotherapy** — historically the standard treatment for early glottic cancer, but AC involvement reduces local control
2. **Endoscopic laser surgery** — increasingly used but technically challenging at the AC
3. **Open partial laryngectomy** — excellent oncologic control but more morbid than endoscopic approaches

### Why the Controversy?

- The AC is the one site in the glottis where **radiotherapy outcomes are consistently inferior** compared to other cord locations
- Endoscopic access to the AC is **technically difficult** — the anterior angle limits exposure
- Open surgery provides the best oncologic outcomes but with greater functional morbidity
- No randomised trials exist comparing treatment modalities specifically for AC carcinoma

## Radiotherapy for Anterior Commissure Carcinoma

### Technique

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Field design** | Lateral opposed fields with **anterior weighting** — the AC lies close to the anterior skin surface |
| **Dose** | **66 Gy in 33 fractions** (higher than standard T1 dose of 63 Gy) to compensate for reduced local control |
| **Field borders** | Superior: hyoid bone; Inferior: bottom of cricoid; Anterior: skin flash; Posterior: anterior vertebral body |
| **Bolus** | Anterior bolus may be needed to ensure adequate dose at the AC (build-up effect near skin surface) |
| **Wedge filters** | Used to compensate for the triangular cross-section of the larynx |

### Local Control Rates

| Site | 5-Year Local Control (RT alone) |
|---|---|
| T1a glottic (no AC involvement) | **90-95%** |
| T1a glottic (AC involvement) | **75-85%** |
| T1b glottic (AC involvement) | **70-80%** |
| T2 glottic (AC involvement) | **60-70%** |

### Failure Patterns after RT

- **Anterior recurrence** at or near the AC — most common pattern
- **Subglottic recurrence** — tumour extends inferiorly below the radiation field
- **Pre-epiglottic space recurrence** — through Broyles ligament superiorly
- **Cartilage necrosis** — may mimic or coexist with recurrence; requires differentiation

### Factors Contributing to Reduced RT Efficacy at AC

1. **Thin cartilage with no perichondrial barrier** — allows early extralaryngeal spread beyond the radiation field
2. **Build-up effect** — AC is close to the skin surface; dose may be suboptimal without bolus
3. **Bilateral disease** — T1b tumours have larger volume than T1a
4. **Submucosal extension** — difficult to assess clinically and may be undertreated

## Surgical Options

### Endoscopic Approaches

#### ELS Cordectomy Classification at the AC

| ELS Type | Description | AC Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Type V (extended)** | Includes the contralateral vocal cord | Standard for AC tumours crossing the midline |
| **Type Va** | Extended cordectomy encompassing the anterior commissure and the contralateral vocal fold | Specifically designed for AC tumours |
| **Type Vb** | Extended cordectomy including arytenoid | Not typically needed for isolated AC lesions |
| **Type Vc** | Extended cordectomy including subglottis | For AC tumours with subglottic extension |
| **Type Vd** | Extended cordectomy including ventricle | For AC tumours with supraglottic extension |

#### Technical Considerations for Endoscopic AC Surgery

- **Exposure is critical** — use of anterior commissure laryngoscope (Zeitels, Steiner) for adequate visualisation
- **En bloc resection preferred** — piecemeal resection increases risk of positive margins at this critical site
- **Must include Broyles ligament** — resection must extend to the inner surface of the thyroid cartilage perichondrium
- **Contralateral cord assessment** — must evaluate and resect contralateral disease if present
- **Subglottic margin** — at least 5 mm below the AC to ensure clear inferior margin

See also: [[Answers/Larynx/Laser Surgery in Laryngeal Cancer|Laser Surgery in Laryngeal Cancer]]

### Open Partial Laryngectomy Options

| Procedure | Description | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **Frontolateral vertical partial laryngectomy** | Resection of ipsilateral thyroid ala, true cord, false cord, and ventricle with the AC | T1a-T1b with AC involvement; limited subglottic extension |
| **Anterior commissure laryngectomy (Leroux-Robert)** | Resection of the anterior portion of both thyroid alae with the AC and adjacent cord segments; reconstruction with epiglottic flap | T1b tumours centred on the AC |
| **SCPL-CHEP** | Supracricoid partial laryngectomy with cricohyoidoepiglottopexy — resects thyroid cartilage, both true and false cords, paraglottic spaces; preserves cricoid, hyoid, and at least one arytenoid | T1b-T2 (even selected T3) with AC involvement |
| **SCPL-CHP** | Supracricoid partial laryngectomy with cricohyoidopexy — as above but includes the epiglottis | AC tumour with pre-epiglottic space involvement |

See also: [[Answers/Larynx/Supracricoid Laryngectomy and Conservation Surgery|Supracricoid Laryngectomy and Conservation Surgery]]

## Comparison of Treatment Outcomes by Approach

| Parameter | Radiotherapy | Endoscopic Laser (TLM) | Open Partial Laryngectomy | Total Laryngectomy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Local control (T1)** | 75-85% | 80-90% | 90-95% | ~100% |
| **Local control (T2)** | 60-70% | 70-85% | 85-95% | ~100% |
| **Larynx preservation** | 85-90% (including salvage) | 90-95% (including salvage) | 90-95% | 0% |
| **Voice quality** | Good (best short-term) | Variable (depends on extent) | Moderate (breathy but functional) | Alaryngeal only |
| **Swallowing** | Good | Good | Aspiration risk (esp. SCPL) | Requires stoma; no aspiration |
| **Treatment duration** | 6-7 weeks | Single procedure | Single procedure + recovery | Single procedure + recovery |
| **Salvage options** | Surgery available | Surgery available | TL if recurrence | N/A |
| **Cost** | High (multiple fractions) | Low (single session) | Moderate (hospitalisation) | Highest |

## Management of Carcinoma In Situ at the Anterior Commissure

CIS of the anterior commissure (Larynx Q60) requires a specific management approach:

### Diagnostic Considerations

1. **Excisional biopsy** — preferred over incisional biopsy as it serves both diagnostic and therapeutic purposes
2. **Multiple biopsies** — to rule out foci of microinvasion within the CIS
3. **Deep biopsy** — must include the subepithelial tissue to exclude invasion through the basement membrane
4. **Mapping biopsies** — to delineate the full extent of CIS, which may be multifocal

### Treatment Options for CIS

| Option | Description | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Laser stripping** | CO2 laser excision of the mucosa (ELS Type I subepithelial cordectomy) | Minimally invasive; good voice; tissue for histology | May miss deep extension |
| **Cold instrument excision** | Microflap technique with cold instruments | Precise tissue handling; clear margins | Technically demanding |
| **Observation after excisional biopsy** | If complete excision confirmed on histology | Least morbid | Risk of recurrence 15-30% |
| **Radiotherapy** | 60-63 Gy standard fractionation | Non-invasive; covers the entire field | Overtreatment for CIS; precludes future RT |
| **Repeat biopsies** | Serial microlaryngoscopy every 3-6 months | Documents disease behaviour | Repeated GA; patient anxiety |

### Recommended Approach for CIS at AC

1. **First step:** Excisional biopsy under microlaryngoscopy with cold instruments or CO2 laser
2. **If margins clear:** Close surveillance — laryngoscopy every 6-8 weeks for 6 months, then every 3 months for 2 years
3. **If margins positive or recurrence:** Repeat excision (laser stripping)
4. **If persistent/recurrent CIS despite multiple excisions:** Consider radiotherapy or frontolateral laryngectomy
5. **Smoking cessation** is **mandatory** — reduces recurrence risk significantly

## Prognostic Factors

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Cartilage invasion** | Upstages to T3/T4a; significantly worse prognosis; favours open surgery or total laryngectomy |
| **Subglottic extension** | >10 mm anteriorly or >5 mm posteriorly — reduces RT control; increases risk of cricoid invasion |
| **Bilateral cord involvement (T1b)** | Reduces RT local control by 10-15% compared to T1a |
| **Depth of invasion** | Superficial CIS/T1 has better prognosis than tumour invading into Broyles ligament |
| **Impaired cord mobility** | Indicates deep invasion into vocalis muscle or paraglottic space; minimum T2 |
| **Tumour volume** | Larger tumours at the AC have worse outcomes regardless of T-stage |
| **Pre-epiglottic space invasion** | Indicates superior extension through thyroepiglottic ligament; T3 |
| **Anterior subcommissure involvement** | Indicates inferior extension; increases risk of subglottic recurrence |

## Treatment Algorithm

```
AC Carcinoma
├── CIS (Q60)
│   ├── Excisional biopsy (laser/cold instruments)
│   ├── Margins clear → Surveillance (q3m × 2yr, then q6m)
│   ├── Margins positive → Re-excision
│   └── Persistent CIS → RT or open partial laryngectomy
│
├── T1a with AC involvement
│   ├── TLM (ELS Type Va) — if adequate exposure
│   ├── RT (66 Gy with anterior weighting) — if poor exposure
│   └── Frontolateral vertical partial laryngectomy — if TLM not feasible
│
├── T1b (bilateral at AC)
│   ├── TLM (ELS Type V/Va) — good exposure required
│   ├── SCPL-CHEP — preferred by many centres for T1b
│   └── RT (66 Gy) — inferior local control; consider if patient preference
│
├── T2 with AC involvement
│   ├── SCPL-CHEP or SCPL-CHP — optimal for conservation
│   ├── TLM — selected cases with adequate exposure and limited depth
│   └── RT — local control ~60-70%; consider CRT for T2
│
└── T3-T4a with AC involvement
    ├── Total laryngectomy ± adjuvant RT/CRT
    ├── SCPL — very selected T3 with mobile arytenoid
    └── CRT — organ preservation protocol (VA Larynx, RTOG 91-11 criteria)
```

## Key Exam Points

1. **Broyles ligament** is the anterior commissure tendon — connects vocalis muscle to thyroid cartilage; inner perichondrium is absent at this point, providing a direct route for extralaryngeal spread
2. AC involvement reduces RT local control to **75-85%** for T1 (vs 90-95% without AC involvement)
3. Endoscopic surgery requires **ELS Type V/Va cordectomy** with resection including Broyles ligament down to thyroid cartilage perichondrium
4. **SCPL-CHEP** is an excellent option for T1b-T2 AC tumours — removes the entire endolarynx while preserving at least one arytenoid
5. CIS at the AC should be managed with **excisional biopsy** first, not radiotherapy — RT is reserved for persistent/recurrent disease
6. CT/MRI is essential to assess **thyroid cartilage sclerosis/erosion** at the AC, which upstages the tumour
7. Smoking cessation is critical in CIS management — ongoing tobacco use increases malignant transformation and recurrence risk
