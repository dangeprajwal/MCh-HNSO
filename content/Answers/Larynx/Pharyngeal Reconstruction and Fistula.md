## Classification of Pharyngeal Defects After Laryngectomy

### By Circumferential Extent

| Defect Type | Definition | Percentage of Circumference | Typical Procedure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Partial (Type I) | Less than half the circumference | <50% | Partial pharyngectomy, hemilaryngopharyngectomy |
| Subtotal (Type II) | More than half but not complete | 50--75% | Extended partial pharyngolaryngectomy |
| Near-circumferential (Type III) | Only a narrow strip of mucosa remaining | 75--99% | Subtotal pharyngolaryngectomy |
| Circumferential (Type IV) | Complete removal of pharyngeal tube | 100% | Total pharyngolaryngectomy, pharyngolaryngo-oesophagectomy |

### By Anatomical Location

| Location | Common Indication | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| Hypopharyngeal | Pyriform fossa SCC | Proximity to cervical oesophagus; may require oesophageal resection |
| Laryngopharyngeal | Advanced laryngeal SCC with pharyngeal extension | Reconstruction must restore alimentary conduit |
| Cervical oesophageal | Post-cricoid SCC, cervical oesophageal SCC | May extend below thoracic inlet; gastric pull-up often required |
| Combined | Locally advanced tumours crossing subsites | Complex reconstruction; multidisciplinary planning essential |

## Reconstructive Options

### 1. Primary Closure

- **Indication:** Small partial defects (<30% circumference) with adequate residual mucosa
- **Technique:** Tension-free T-closure or linear closure in at least two layers (mucosa and muscle)
- **Advantages:** Simplest option; no donor-site morbidity; fastest recovery; best functional outcome when achievable
- **Limitations:** Risk of stricture if closed under tension; pharyngocutaneous fistula rate 5--15% (higher in irradiated fields)
- **Key principle:** The residual pharyngeal lumen must accommodate a 36 Fr bougie without tension

### 2. Regional Flaps

#### Pectoralis Major Myocutaneous Flap (PMMF)

- **Workhorse regional flap** for pharyngeal reconstruction before the free flap era; still widely used in resource-limited settings and as salvage
- **Blood supply:** Pectoral branch of the thoracoacromial artery
- **Indications:**
  - Partial pharyngeal defects when free flap expertise unavailable
  - Fistula repair and reinforcement in irradiated fields
  - Augmentation/buttressing over primary closure in salvage surgery
- **Technique:** Skin paddle designed over lower thorax; muscle tunnelled beneath clavicle and skin bridge into the neck
- **Advantages:** Reliable vascularity; no microsurgical expertise; short operative time; robust bulk for filling dead space
- **Limitations:** Excessively bulky for mucosal replacement; limited arc for high oropharyngeal defects; higher fistula and stricture rates than free flaps; cosmetic donor-site deformity
- **Fistula rate:** 10--25%
- **Stricture rate:** 15--30%

#### Deltopectoral Flap

- **Blood supply:** Perforators from the internal mammary artery
- **Historical significance:** First reliable fasciocutaneous flap for head and neck reconstruction (Bakamjian 1965)
- **Current role:** Largely replaced by PMMF and free flaps; still occasionally used as a secondary salvage option
- **Limitation:** Requires two-stage procedure (division and inset of pedicle at 3 weeks)

### 3. Free Flaps

#### Radial Forearm Free Flap (RFFF)

- **Blood supply:** Radial artery and venae comitantes
- **Skin paddle:** Thin, pliable, hairless forearm skin
- **Indications:** Partial pharyngeal defects; tubed RFFF for near-circumferential or circumferential defects
- **Tubed RFFF for circumferential defects:**
  - Skin paddle folded into a tube to create neopharynx
  - Minimum skin paddle width approximately 8 cm for adequate lumen
  - Higher stricture rate than jejunal flap (15--30%)
  - Superior speech outcomes compared to jejunal flap (permits TEP voice)
- **Advantages:** Thin and pliable (ideal for mucosal replacement); long vascular pedicle (15--20 cm); consistent anatomy; two-team approach feasible
- **Limitations:** Donor-site morbidity (forearm skin graft, tendon exposure, radial artery sacrifice); requires positive Allen test; limited tissue volume for large defects
- **Fistula rate:** 5--15%

#### Anterolateral Thigh Flap (ALT)

- **Blood supply:** Descending branch of the lateral circumflex femoral artery via septocutaneous or musculocutaneous perforators through vastus lateralis
- **Indications:** Partial or circumferential pharyngeal defects; currently the most versatile option
- **Tubed ALT for circumferential defects:**
  - Lower stricture rate than tubed RFFF (10--20%)
  - Better speech outcomes than jejunal flap
  - Shorter hospital stays than jejunal approach (Yu 2006)
- **Advantages:** Large skin paddle available (up to 25 x 15 cm); can be thinned; long pedicle; minimal donor-site morbidity (primary closure possible up to 8 cm width); versatile (can include vastus lateralis for bulk)
- **Limitations:** Variable perforator anatomy; may be too bulky in obese patients; learning curve for perforator dissection
- **Fistula rate:** 5--12%

#### Jejunal Free Flap

- **Blood supply:** Jejunal mesenteric arcade (branches of the superior mesenteric artery)
- **Indications:** Circumferential pharyngeal defects -- traditional gold standard for tube reconstruction
- **Advantages:** Pre-formed tube (no flap tubing required); secretory mucosa aids bolus transit; low stricture rate (5--15%)
- **Limitations:**
  - Requires laparotomy (ileus, adhesions, hernia -- donor-site morbidity)
  - Wet, secretory mucosa causes excessive secretions
  - Short vascular pedicle limits options for recipient vessel selection
  - Limited ischaemia tolerance (harvested segment must be placed rapidly)
  - **Poor speech outcomes with TEP** -- peristalsis interferes with voice prosthesis vibration
  - Cannot monitor flap externally (no skin paddle)
  - Higher perioperative mortality than fasciocutaneous flaps
- **Fistula rate:** 10--20%
- **Stricture rate:** 5--15%

### 4. Gastric Pull-Up (Gastric Transposition)

- **Indication:** Extensive pharyngolaryngo-oesophagectomy defects extending to or below the thoracic inlet; cervical oesophageal carcinoma
- **Technique:** Mobilise stomach on the right gastroepiploic arcade; divide left gastric and short gastric vessels; pull up through the posterior mediastinum; anastomose to the pharyngeal remnant
- **Advantages:** Single pharyngogastric anastomosis; pre-formed conduit; reliable blood supply; rapid restoration of alimentary continuity
- **Disadvantages:** Major operation with high morbidity/mortality (5--10%); mediastinitis risk; gastro-oesophageal reflux; dumping syndrome; long-term nutritional consequences; pulmonary complications
- **Fistula rate:** 10--25%

## Algorithm by Defect Size

### Partial Defect (<50% Circumference)

```
Small defect (<30%) --> Primary closure +/- PMMF buttress
                        |
                        v
Medium defect (30-50%) --> Free flap preferred:
                           1st choice: RFFF (thin, pliable)
                           2nd choice: ALT (if bulk needed)
                           3rd choice: PMMF (if free flap not available)
```

### Subtotal / Near-Circumferential Defect (50-99%)

```
Residual strip adequate (>3 cm) --> RFFF patch or ALT patch
                                    |
                                    v
Residual strip too narrow (<3 cm) --> Treat as circumferential defect
```

### Circumferential Defect (100%)

```
Short segment (<6 cm) --> 1st: Tubed ALT
                          2nd: Tubed RFFF
                          3rd: Jejunal free flap
                          |
Long segment (>=6 cm) --> 1st: Jejunal free flap
or extends below          2nd: Tubed ALT
thoracic inlet            3rd: Gastric pull-up
```

## Salivary Bypass Tubes

### Montgomery Salivary Bypass Tube (MSBT)

- **Design:** Silicone tube with a proximal funnel-shaped flange and a distal tapered tip
- **Principle:** Diverts saliva away from the pharyngeal suture line, reducing salivary contamination and promoting wound healing
- **Sizes:** Available in multiple diameters; most commonly 12 mm external diameter
- **Placement:** Inserted at the time of pharyngeal closure with the funnel seated in the oropharynx and the distal tip passing beyond the pharyngeal suture line into the oesophagus

### Bivona Salivary Bypass Tube

- **Alternative design** with similar purpose
- **Key difference:** Flexible silicone with softer material; may cause less mucosal irritation

### Indications for Salivary Bypass Tubes

1. **Prophylactic placement** during total laryngectomy in high-risk patients (prior RT, salvage surgery, diabetes)
2. **Circumferential pharyngeal reconstruction** to protect suture lines
3. **Treatment of established pharyngocutaneous fistula** to divert saliva

### Technique of MSBT Placement

1. Select appropriate tube size (should pass comfortably through the neopharyngeal lumen)
2. Insert the tube after completing the posterior pharyngeal closure
3. Position the proximal funnel in the oropharynx/tongue base region
4. Ensure the distal tip extends at least 2--3 cm below the lowest suture line
5. Secure with a suture through the nasal alar or cheek to prevent migration
6. Complete the anterior pharyngeal closure around the tube
7. Remove after 10--14 days when healing is satisfactory

### Outcomes

- Prophylactic MSBT placement reduces fistula rates from 15--30% to 5--10% in salvage settings
- Stricture prevention: maintains pharyngeal lumen patency during healing
- Complications: tube migration (most common), mucosal ulceration, aspiration around the tube

## Pharyngocutaneous Fistula (PCF)

### Incidence

- Overall incidence after total laryngectomy: 10--65% (wide range reflects variable definitions and populations)
- Primary total laryngectomy: 10--20%
- Salvage total laryngectomy (after RT/CRT failure): 25--60%
- After free flap reconstruction: 5--15%

### Risk Factors

| Category | Specific Risk Factors |
|---|---|
| Patient factors | Diabetes mellitus, hypothyroidism, malnutrition (albumin <3.0 g/dL), anaemia (Hb <12.5 g/dL), chronic liver disease, immunosuppression |
| Treatment-related | Prior radiotherapy (greatest single risk factor -- 2--3x increased risk), prior chemotherapy, prior tracheotomy |
| Surgical factors | Positive surgical margins, concurrent neck dissection (bilateral), extensive pharyngeal resection, circumferential defect, prolonged operative time |
| Tumour factors | Advanced T-stage (T3-T4), hypopharyngeal primary (vs laryngeal), subglottic extension |
| Postoperative | Wound infection, haematoma, poor nutritional status, continued smoking |

### Pathophysiology

1. **Impaired wound healing** from prior radiation (fibrosis, hypovascularity)
2. **Salivary contamination** of the suture line
3. **Tension on closure** from inadequate mucosa
4. **Infection** disrupting the healing process
5. **Ischaemia** from flap compromise or tight closure

### Prevention Strategies

1. **Preoperative optimisation:** Correct anaemia (Hb >12.5 g/dL), improve nutrition (albumin >3.0 g/dL), control diabetes (HbA1c <8%), thyroid replacement if hypothyroid
2. **Intraoperative:**
   - Tension-free pharyngeal closure (fundamental principle)
   - Vascularised tissue interposition -- free flap or pedicled flap buttress over suture lines in salvage surgery
   - [[Answers/General/Nutritional Assessment and PEG Feeding|Prophylactic PEG]] placement for prolonged NPO
   - Salivary bypass tube in high-risk patients
   - Meticulous haemostasis and avoidance of dead space
3. **Postoperative:** Closed suction drains; delay oral intake 7--14 days (minimum); early enteral feeding via PEG/NG; wound care; prophylactic antibiotics in high-risk patients

### Classification of PCF

| Grade | Description | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Grade I | Small (<1 cm), low output (<50 mL/day) | Conservative |
| Grade II | Medium (1--2 cm), moderate output | Conservative initially; surgical if persistent >4 weeks |
| Grade III | Large (>2 cm) or high output | Surgical repair with vascularised tissue |
| Grade IV | Exposed carotid artery | URGENT flap coverage |

### Management Algorithm

#### Conservative Management (Grade I-II)

1. **NPO** -- strict nil by mouth; enteral nutrition via PEG or NG
2. **Local wound care** -- daily dressing changes; debridement of necrotic tissue
3. **Negative pressure wound therapy** (VAC) in selected cases
4. **Antibiotics** -- broad-spectrum coverage; culture-directed therapy
5. **Proton pump inhibitor** -- reduce acid reflux contamination
6. **Nutritional optimisation** -- enteral feeding; correct deficiencies
7. Expected healing time: 4--8 weeks; success rate 60--80% for small fistulae

#### Surgical Management (Grade III-IV or persistent)

1. **PMMF repair** -- most commonly used for surgical fistula closure; provides bulk and vascularity
2. **Free flap repair** -- RFFF or ALT; preferred if PMMF previously used or inadequate
3. **Supraclavicular artery island flap** -- alternative regional option
4. **Carotid coverage** -- mandatory if vessel exposed; PMMF or sternocleidomastoid muscle flap

### Timing of Surgical Repair

- **Conservative trial:** Allow 4--6 weeks for spontaneous closure
- **Indications for early surgical intervention:** Large fistula, carotid exposure, rapid deterioration, failure of conservative management
- **Definitive repair:** After wound bed preparation; vascularised tissue interposition

## Key Points for Exam

1. **Defect classification** drives reconstructive choice: partial vs subtotal vs circumferential
2. **ALT is the most versatile free flap** for pharyngeal reconstruction -- usable for partial and circumferential defects with low donor-site morbidity
3. **Jejunal flap produces poor TEP voice** due to peristalsis -- avoid if tracheoesophageal [[Answers/General/Speech Rehabilitation after Laryngectomy|speech rehabilitation]] is desired
4. **Gastric pull-up** is reserved for defects extending below the thoracic inlet
5. **Prior radiation is the single greatest risk factor** for pharyngocutaneous fistula (2--3x increased risk)
6. **Salivary bypass tubes** (Montgomery) reduce fistula rates in high-risk patients
7. **Conservative management** succeeds in 60--80% of small, low-output fistulae
8. **Carotid exposure** in the setting of PCF is a surgical emergency requiring urgent vascularised tissue coverage
9. **Preoperative nutritional optimisation** (albumin >3.0 g/dL, Hb >12.5 g/dL) is essential for reducing wound complications
10. **Tension-free closure** is the most important technical principle in pharyngeal reconstruction
