## Overview of Radiation Therapy for Laryngeal Cancer

Radiation therapy (RT) is a cornerstone of laryngeal cancer treatment, used as:

1. **Definitive primary treatment** -- T1/T2 glottic and supraglottic cancers (organ preservation)
2. **Concurrent chemoradiation (CRT)** -- T3/T4a laryngeal cancers suitable for organ preservation
3. **Adjuvant post-operative RT** -- after total laryngectomy for adverse pathological features
4. **Palliative RT** -- symptom control in inoperable/metastatic disease

### Dose Prescriptions by Stage

| Stage | Treatment | Total Dose | Fractionation |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1a glottic | RT alone | 63--66 Gy | 2.25 Gy/fraction, 28--29 fractions |
| T1b glottic | RT alone | 65.25--66 Gy | 2.25 Gy/fraction |
| T2 glottic (mobile cord) | RT alone | 70 Gy | 2 Gy/fraction, 35 fractions |
| T2 glottic (impaired mobility) | RT +/- concurrent cisplatin | 70 Gy | 2 Gy/fraction |
| T1--T2 supraglottic | RT alone (include elective neck) | 70 Gy primary, 50 Gy neck | 2 Gy/fraction |
| T3 larynx | Concurrent CRT (RTOG 91-11) | 70 Gy + cisplatin 100 mg/m2 D1,22,43 | 2 Gy/fraction |
| T4a larynx (selected) | Concurrent CRT | 70 Gy + cisplatin | 2 Gy/fraction |
| Adjuvant (high risk) | Post-op CRT | 60--66 Gy + cisplatin | 2 Gy/fraction |

## Radiation Therapy Techniques

### 1. Conventional Radiation Therapy (Parallel Opposed Fields)

**Historical standard for laryngeal cancer:**

- Two lateral parallel opposed fields encompassing the primary tumour and regional nodes
- Field borders defined by bony landmarks
- Simple planning, widely available

**Field Design for T1 Glottic Cancer:**
- Superior border: upper thyroid notch (top of C3)
- Inferior border: lower border of cricoid cartilage (bottom of C6)
- Anterior border: skin flash (includes anterior commissure)
- Posterior border: anterior aspect of vertebral body
- Small fields (5 x 5 cm or 6 x 6 cm) -- no elective neck irradiation

**Advantages:**
- Simple setup and execution
- Reproducible
- Adequate for early glottic cancer (T1)

**Limitations:**
- Cannot spare critical structures (spinal cord, parotid, carotid)
- Non-conformal dose distribution
- Higher dose to normal tissues
- Inadequate for advanced disease requiring complex field arrangements

### 2. Three-Dimensional Conformal Radiation Therapy (3D-CRT)

- CT-based treatment planning with 3D tumour volume delineation
- Multiple beam arrangements shaped by multileaf collimators (MLC)
- Improved dose conformity compared to parallel opposed fields
- Better sparing of spinal cord
- Intermediate step between conventional RT and IMRT

### 3. Intensity-Modulated Radiation Therapy (IMRT)

**Current standard for advanced laryngeal cancer:**

IMRT uses computer-optimised inverse planning to modulate the intensity of each beam across its cross-section, enabling highly conformal dose distributions.

**Advantages in Laryngeal Cancer:**
- **Parotid gland sparing** -- reduced xerostomia (PARSPORT trial: grade >=2 xerostomia 29% vs 83%)
- **Spinal cord sparing** -- maintains cord dose within tolerance (< 45 Gy)
- **Carotid artery sparing** -- reduced dose to carotid, potentially lower long-term stroke risk
- **Constrictor muscle sparing** -- reduced long-term dysphagia
- **Dose escalation** to tumour volume while maintaining normal tissue constraints
- **Simultaneous integrated boost (SIB)** -- different dose levels to GTV, CTV, elective neck in single fraction

**Disadvantages and Concerns:**
- **Marginal miss risk** -- tight dose conformity increases risk of geographic miss if setup is inaccurate
- **Low-dose bath** -- larger volume of tissue receives low-dose radiation
- **Longer treatment delivery time** -- increased intrafraction motion
- **Complex planning** -- requires experienced physics team, quality assurance
- **Cost** -- higher infrastructure and staffing requirements

**IMRT Dose Constraints for Laryngeal Cancer:**

| Structure | Dose Constraint |
|---|---|
| Spinal cord | D_max < 45 Gy (50 Gy with 1 cm margin) |
| Brainstem | D_max < 54 Gy |
| Parotid gland | Mean dose < 26 Gy (at least one) |
| Pharyngeal constrictors | Mean dose < 50 Gy |
| Larynx (uninvolved) | Mean dose < 45 Gy (in non-laryngeal cases) |
| Carotid artery | Minimise (V35 < 50%) |
| Thyroid gland | Minimise (mean < 45 Gy) |
| Cochlea | Mean dose < 45 Gy |

### 4. Image-Guided Radiation Therapy (IGRT)

IGRT uses on-board imaging to verify patient setup immediately before each treatment fraction.

**Techniques:**
- **Cone-beam CT (CBCT)** -- volumetric imaging at treatment position, compared with planning CT
- **kV orthogonal portal images** -- bony anatomy verification
- **Surface-guided RT** -- optical surface monitoring systems
- **Electromagnetic transponders** -- implanted fiducial markers (rare in larynx)

**Rationale in Laryngeal Cancer:**
- Larynx moves with swallowing, breathing, weight loss
- IMRT requires tight margins -- any positional error risks marginal miss
- IGRT allows reduction of PTV margins (5 mm → 3 mm), further sparing normal tissue
- Daily CBCT verification recommended with IMRT for larynx

### 5. Volumetric Modulated Arc Therapy (VMAT)

- Advanced form of IMRT using continuous gantry rotation with simultaneous MLC modulation and dose rate variation
- Achieves similar dose distributions to step-and-shoot IMRT
- **Shorter treatment delivery time** (2--5 min vs 10--15 min for IMRT)
- Reduced intrafraction motion
- Currently the most commonly used IMRT delivery technique

### 6. Proton Beam Therapy (Emerging)

- Uses proton particles instead of photons
- **Bragg peak** -- dose deposited at specific depth with rapid falloff beyond target
- Reduced integral dose to surrounding tissues
- Potential for reduced xerostomia, dysphagia, carotid dose
- Limited availability, high cost
- Currently under investigation for head and neck cancer (not standard for larynx)

## Hyperfractionation and Altered Fractionation

### Radiobiological Rationale

**Accelerated repopulation** is a key phenomenon in head and neck SCC:
- After ~4 weeks of RT, surviving tumour clonogens undergo rapid proliferation
- Estimated tumour doubling time during treatment: 4--5 days
- Loss of ~0.6 Gy per day of treatment prolongation beyond 4 weeks
- **Rationale for altered fractionation**: overcome accelerated repopulation by reducing overall treatment time or increasing total dose within the same time frame

### Altered Fractionation Schedules

| Schedule | Fraction Size | Fractions/Day | Total Dose | Overall Time | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Standard** | 2 Gy | 1 | 70 Gy/35 fr | 7 weeks | Standard reference |
| **Hyperfractionation** | 1.2 Gy | 2 (6 hr gap) | 81.6 Gy/68 fr | 7 weeks | Higher total dose exploiting differential repair; same overall time |
| **Accelerated (pure)** | 2 Gy | 2 | 70 Gy/35 fr | 3.5 weeks | Shorter overall time to overcome repopulation |
| **Concomitant boost** | 1.8 Gy AM + 1.5 Gy PM (last 12 days) | 1--2 | 72 Gy | 6 weeks | Accelerated boost at end when repopulation maximal |
| **DAHANCA (6 fr/wk)** | 2 Gy | 1 (6 days/wk) | 66--68 Gy | 5.5 weeks | Modest acceleration |

### RTOG 9003 Trial (Fu 2000) -- Key Results

**Design:** 4-arm randomised trial (n = 1,073)
- Arm 1: Standard fractionation (70 Gy/35 fr/7 wk)
- Arm 2: Hyperfractionation (81.6 Gy/68 fr/7 wk, 1.2 Gy BID)
- Arm 3: Accelerated with split (67.2 Gy/42 fr/6 wk, with 2-wk rest)
- Arm 4: Concomitant boost (72 Gy/42 fr/6 wk)

**Key Outcomes:**

| Parameter | Standard | Hyperfractionation | Accelerated Split | Concomitant Boost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2-year LRC | 46% | **54.4%** (p=0.045) | 47.5% (NS) | **54.5%** (p=0.050) |
| Acute grade 3--5 toxicity | 32% | 40% | 30% | 40% |
| Late toxicity | Similar | Similar | Similar | Similar |

**Conclusions:**
- Hyperfractionation and concomitant boost significantly improved locoregional control
- Accelerated fractionation with split was NOT superior (split allowed repopulation)
- Increased acute but NOT late toxicity with hyperfractionation/concomitant boost
- **Hyperfractionation exploits differential repair** between tumour and late-responding normal tissues

### RTOG 91-11 (Forastiere 2003) -- Relevance to RT

The RT-alone arm of RTOG 91-11 provides the benchmark for radiation monotherapy in advanced laryngeal cancer:
- 70 Gy in 35 fractions over 7 weeks
- Laryngeal preservation rate: 67% (vs 84% for concurrent CRT)
- Established concurrent CRT as superior to RT alone for organ preservation

## Acute Complications of Radiation Therapy

### 1. Radiation Mucositis

**Grading (RTOG/CTCAE):**

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| Grade 1 | Erythema, mild soreness |
| Grade 2 | Patchy pseudomembranous mucositis, moderate pain, can eat modified diet |
| Grade 3 | Confluent pseudomembranous mucositis, severe pain, unable to eat solids |
| Grade 4 | Necrosis, ulceration, bleeding, life-threatening |
| Grade 5 | Death |

**Timeline:** Onset week 2--3, peak week 4--6, resolution 2--4 weeks post-RT

**Management:**
- Oral hygiene (soft toothbrush, saline/bicarbonate rinses)
- Pain management (topical lidocaine, systemic opioids for grade 2+)
- Nutritional support (PEG/NG tube for grade 3+)
- Benzydamine mouthwash (reduces severity if started prophylactically)
- Avoid alcohol-based mouthwashes, sharp/spicy foods
- No evidence for sucralfate, glutamine, or silver nitrate

### 2. Radiation Dermatitis

| Grade | Description | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 1 | Faint erythema, dry desquamation | Moisturisers, aqueous cream |
| Grade 2 | Bright erythema, patchy moist desquamation | Hydrogel dressings, topical steroids |
| Grade 3 | Confluent moist desquamation (>1.5 cm, not in folds) | Silver sulfadiazine, wound care |
| Grade 4 | Ulceration, haemorrhage, necrosis | Specialised wound management |

### 3. Acute Dysphagia

- Mucosal pain, oedema of pharyngeal constrictors
- Reduced tongue base retraction, impaired epiglottic inversion
- Aspiration risk increases during weeks 4--7 of RT
- Management: swallowing exercises (started before RT), dietary modification, PEG if needed
- **Prophylactic swallowing exercises** reduce long-term dysphagia (evidence from MD Anderson protocol)

### 4. Xerostomia (Acute Phase)

- Salivary output decreases ~50% after 10 Gy
- Near-complete loss by week 3--4 of RT
- Thick, ropy saliva (loss of serous acini first, mucous glands more resistant)
- Management: sipping water, saliva substitutes, pilocarpine (during RT)

### 5. Laryngeal Oedema

- Particularly common with supraglottic and transglottic tumours
- Peaks 2--4 weeks post-RT, may persist for months
- Risk factors: large tumour volume, supraglottic site, concurrent chemotherapy, pre-treatment biopsy
- Management: humidification, head elevation, short course dexamethasone, tracheostomy if severe airway compromise
- Must distinguish from persistent/recurrent tumour

### 6. Taste Alteration (Dysgeusia)

- Loss or distortion of taste beginning week 2--3
- Due to damage to taste bud epithelium (rapid turnover)
- Recovery over 2--12 months post-RT (often incomplete)
- Zinc supplementation may accelerate recovery

## Late Complications of Radiation Therapy

Late effects occur >90 days after completion of RT and are generally irreversible.

### 1. Chronic Xerostomia

- Most common long-term complaint after head and neck RT
- Parotid glands most radiosensitive (serous acini)
- **Mean parotid dose < 26 Gy** preserves function (PARSPORT trial threshold)
- IMRT significantly reduces incidence (PARSPORT: 29% vs 83%)
- **Management**: pilocarpine 5 mg TDS, cevimeline, saliva substitutes, sugar-free gum
- **Amifostine** (Brizel 2000): reduces acute xerostomia from 78% to 51%, chronic from 57% to 34%
  - Dose: 200 mg/m2 IV 15--30 min pre-RT
  - Side effects: hypotension, nausea
  - Not widely adopted due to toxicity profile and cost

### 2. Hypothyroidism

- Incidence: 20--50% after laryngeal RT (higher with concurrent chemotherapy)
- Mechanism: radiation damage to thyroid parenchyma and vasculature
- Onset: 6 months to several years post-RT (median 1.5 years)
- Screening: TSH levels every 6--12 months post-treatment
- Management: levothyroxine replacement

### 3. Carotid Stenosis

- RT accelerates atherosclerosis in irradiated carotid arteries
- Incidence: clinically significant stenosis in 12--30% at 10 years
- Risk of stroke 2--4x higher than age-matched controls
- Screening: carotid duplex ultrasound at 2--5 years post-RT in high-risk patients
- Management: antiplatelet therapy, stenting, endarterectomy for symptomatic stenosis

### 4. Chondroradionecrosis

- Necrosis of irradiated laryngeal cartilage (thyroid, cricoid, arytenoid)
- Incidence: 1--5% after definitive RT for laryngeal cancer
- Risk factors: T4 tumour, high dose (>70 Gy), large fraction size, concurrent chemotherapy, post-RT biopsy, continued smoking
- **Clinical features**: severe pain, dysphagia, foul odour, airway obstruction, cartilage exposure
- **Imaging**: CT shows cartilage fragmentation, gas within cartilage framework; PET-CT may show intense uptake (mimics recurrence)
- **Differential diagnosis**: must exclude recurrent tumour (biopsy essential)
- **Management**:
  - Conservative: antibiotics, proton pump inhibitors, humidification, steroids
  - Hyperbaric oxygen therapy (HBO) -- 20--30 sessions
  - Surgical: debridement, partial laryngectomy, total laryngectomy for refractory cases
  - See [[Answers/General/Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy|Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy]]

### 5. Pharyngeal/Laryngeal Fibrosis

- Progressive submucosal fibrosis of pharyngeal constrictors and laryngeal soft tissues
- Contributes to chronic dysphagia, aspiration, stenosis
- Onset: 6--24 months post-RT, progressive
- **Machtay 2008** identified severe late pharyngeal/laryngeal toxicity in 43% of patients after concurrent CRT
- Management: swallowing exercises (prophylactic and therapeutic), dilation for strictures
- See [[Answers/General/Trismus Prevention|Trismus Prevention]] for analogous fibrosis management

### 6. Osteoradionecrosis (ORN)

- Rare in laryngeal cancer (more common in oral cavity/mandible)
- Can affect hyoid bone, cervical spine
- See [[Answers/Oral Cavity/Osteoradionecrosis of the Mandible|Osteoradionecrosis of the Mandible]] for pathophysiology and management

### 7. Secondary Malignancies

- Increased risk of second primary cancers in irradiated field
- Estimated excess risk: 1--2% per decade
- Most common: thyroid cancer, soft tissue sarcomas
- Latency period: typically >10 years
- Contributes to importance of long-term surveillance

## Techniques to Limit Radiation Toxicity

| Strategy | Target Toxicity | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **IMRT** | Xerostomia, dysphagia, carotid dose | PARSPORT trial (Nutting 2011) |
| **Amifostine** | Xerostomia | Brizel 2000 (phase III) |
| **Prophylactic swallowing exercises** | Dysphagia, aspiration | MD Anderson protocol |
| **Dental care (pre-RT)** | Dental caries, ORN | Standard practice |
| **Fluoride trays** | Dental caries | Post-RT daily use |
| **IGRT/CBCT** | Marginal miss, excess normal tissue dose | Standard with IMRT |
| **Adaptive replanning** | Accounts for tumour shrinkage/weight loss | Emerging |
| **Pilocarpine during RT** | Xerostomia | Moderate evidence |
| **PEG tube placement** | Malnutrition during treatment | Selected patients |

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **RTOG 91-11** established concurrent CRT as standard for organ preservation in advanced laryngeal cancer (84% laryngeal preservation)
2. **RTOG 9003** showed hyperfractionation and concomitant boost improve LRC over standard fractionation -- exploits differential repair and overcomes accelerated repopulation
3. **PARSPORT trial** proved IMRT reduces xerostomia (29% vs 83%) -- parotid mean dose < 26 Gy is the threshold
4. **Amifostine** reduces xerostomia but has its own toxicity (hypotension, nausea) -- not universally adopted
5. **Machtay analysis** identified key predictors of severe late toxicity: older age, advanced T stage, larynx primary, neck dissection post-CRT
6. **Accelerated repopulation** begins at ~4 weeks of RT and is the rationale for altered fractionation
7. **IMRT marginal miss risk** necessitates IGRT with daily CBCT verification
8. **Chondroradionecrosis** must be distinguished from recurrent tumour -- biopsy is essential
9. **Hypothyroidism screening** (TSH every 6--12 months) is mandatory after laryngeal RT
10. **Prophylactic swallowing exercises** before RT reduce long-term dysphagia -- should be standard practice
