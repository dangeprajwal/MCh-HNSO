## Introduction

Laryngeal transplantation represents one of the most technically demanding procedures in reconstructive head and neck surgery. Total laryngectomy, while oncologically effective, results in the permanent loss of voice, normal swallowing, and airway physiology. Although tracheoesophageal puncture (see [[Answers/Larynx/TEP and Prosthetic Voice Rehabilitation|TEP and Prosthetic Voice Rehabilitation]]) and other alaryngeal speech methods (see [[Answers/Larynx/Alaryngeal Speech and Voice Restoration|Alaryngeal Speech]]) provide functional voice, they cannot restore the native laryngeal mechanism. Laryngeal transplantation aims to restore all three laryngeal functions — voice, airway protection during swallowing, and respiration — by replacing the excised organ with a cadaveric or donor allograft.

---

## Historical Background

### Animal Experiments (1960s-1990s)

The concept of laryngeal transplantation was first explored in animal models beginning in the 1960s:

| Year | Investigator | Model | Key Finding |
|------|-------------|-------|-------------|
| 1969 | Ogura et al. | Canine | First laryngeal autotransplantation in dogs; demonstrated vascular revascularization feasibility |
| 1972 | Silver et al. | Canine | Allotransplantation with immunosuppression; graft survival demonstrated |
| 1979 | Kluyskens & Ringoir | Canine | Orthotopic allotransplantation; documented rejection patterns |
| 1982 | Boles | Canine | Long-term survival with cyclosporine immunosuppression |
| 1990s | Strome et al. | Rat | Developed a heterotopic rat laryngeal transplant model; studied rejection and immunosuppression protocols systematically |
| 1992 | Strome & Stein | Rat | Demonstrated that cyclosporine monotherapy could prevent rejection in rat laryngeal allografts |
| 2011 | Birchall et al. | Minipig | MHC-matched laryngeal allotransplantation without immunosuppression; studied vascular and functional outcomes |

The rat model developed by Strome and colleagues at the Cleveland Clinic proved instrumental in understanding the immunobiology of laryngeal allografts and optimizing immunosuppression regimens before proceeding to human trials.

## First Human Laryngeal Transplant (Strome, 1998)

### Patient Selection

- **Patient:** 40-year-old male
- **History:** Total laryngectomy in 1996 for T3N0M0 squamous cell carcinoma of the larynx, followed by postoperative radiation therapy
- **Indication:** Permanent laryngostome with alaryngeal speech (electrolarynx user); desired functional voice restoration
- **Institution:** Cleveland Clinic Foundation, Cleveland, Ohio, USA
- **Date:** January 4, 1998
- **Disease-free interval:** Approximately 2 years post-laryngectomy

### Donor Matching

- **Donor:** Matched cadaveric donor (ABO-compatible, HLA partially matched)
- **Graft harvest:** Within 4 hours of brain death declaration
- **Organ preserved:** Cold ischemia in University of Wisconsin solution

### Surgical Technique

The transplanted composite allograft included:

#### Graft Components
- **Thyroid cartilage** (complete framework)
- **Hyoid bone**
- **Epiglottis**
- **Cricoid cartilage**
- **Five tracheal rings** (with intervening tracheal segments)
- **Bilateral thyroid gland lobes** (for parathyroid preservation)
- **Strap muscles** (for soft tissue coverage)
- **Mucosal lining** (endolaryngeal mucosa intact)

#### Vascular Anastomosis
- **Arterial inflow:** Superior thyroid artery (donor) to superior thyroid artery (recipient) — bilateral
- **Venous drainage:** Internal jugular vein (donor) to internal jugular vein (recipient) — bilateral
- **Cold ischemia time:** Approximately 5 hours

#### Neural Anastomosis
- **Motor innervation:** Recurrent laryngeal nerve (donor) to recurrent laryngeal nerve (recipient) — bilateral end-to-end neurorrhaphy using microsurgical technique
- **Sensory innervation:** Superior laryngeal nerve (internal branch) — bilateral anastomosis attempted

#### Framework Reconstruction
- **Hyoid suspension:** Donor hyoid fixed to recipient hyoid remnant/mandible
- **Tracheal anastomosis:** Donor trachea to recipient tracheal stump — end-to-end
- **Pharyngeal closure:** Neopharynx created by suturing donor pharyngeal mucosa to recipient pharyngeal remnants
- **Strap muscle reattachment:** For external support and soft tissue coverage

### Immunosuppression Protocol

Triple-drug regimen:

| Drug | Mechanism | Dosing |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Cyclosporine** | Calcineurin inhibitor (blocks IL-2 transcription) | Targeted trough levels 200-300 ng/ml initially, then 100-200 ng/ml |
| **Azathioprine** | Purine antimetabolite (inhibits DNA synthesis) | 1-2 mg/kg/day |
| **Prednisone** | Corticosteroid (broad immunosuppression) | Tapered from 1 mg/kg/day to maintenance 5-10 mg/day |

### Outcomes

#### Functional Results
- **Phonation:** First vocalization at 16 months post-transplant; progressive improvement in voice quality over 36 months
- **Swallowing:** Functional oral intake restored; required initial dietary modification
- **Airway:** Decannulated from tracheostomy; patent airway maintained
- **Sensory recovery:** Partial recovery of laryngeal sensation (cough reflex) by 24 months
- **Motor recovery:** Partial vocal fold mobility detected; full abduction not achieved (bilateral vocal fold fixation in paramedian position requiring tracheostomy intermittently)

#### Rejection Episodes
- **Acute rejection:** Multiple episodes detected by clinical signs (hoarseness, edema) and biopsy
- **Management:** Pulse methylprednisolone (1 g IV for 3 days) with successful reversal
- **Monitoring:** Serial direct laryngoscopy with biopsy; mucosal surveillance for rejection histopathology (lymphocytic infiltration, epithelial necrosis)

#### Long-term Outcome
- **Survival:** Patient died 14 years after transplantation (2012) from causes unrelated to the transplant — ischemic heart disease
- **Graft function:** The transplanted larynx remained functional until death
- **Explantation report:** Lorenz and Strome (2014) published the post-mortem explantation findings, confirming long-term graft viability with chronic immunosuppression

## Second Human Laryngeal Transplant (Farwell/Birchall, 2011)

### Patient and Indication

- **Patient:** 51-year-old female
- **History:** Acquired laryngotracheal stenosis from multiple self-extubations during prolonged intubation for renal failure (11 years tracheostomy-dependent)
- **Indication:** Non-malignant — benign laryngotracheal stenosis unresponsive to conventional reconstruction
- **Institution:** University of California Davis Medical Center, USA
- **Date:** 2011
- **Team:** D. Gregory Farwell, Peter Belafsky, Martin A. Birchall (collaborative international team)

### Technical Modifications

Several modifications were introduced compared to the first transplant:

1. **Composite tissue allograft (CTA) principles:** The procedure was reclassified under the emerging framework of vascularized composite allotransplantation (VCA), applying principles from face and hand transplants
2. **Expanded graft:** Included larynx, trachea, thyroid gland, and parathyroid glands as a composite unit
3. **Vascular technique:** Utilized the superior and inferior thyroid arteries and veins
4. **Immunosuppression protocol:** More modern regimen including tacrolimus (replacing cyclosporine), mycophenolate mofetil (replacing azathioprine), and prednisone
5. **Rejection surveillance:** Protocol-directed serial biopsies with standardized histological grading (adapted from Banff classification for VCA)

### Outcomes

- **Airway:** Patient remained tracheostomy-dependent but regained oral and nasal airflow for the first time in 11 years
- **Voice:** Vocalization restored (first phonation in over a decade)
- **Smell and taste:** Recovered with restoration of nasal airflow
- **Quality of life:** Significant improvement in patient-reported outcomes
- **Rejection:** Managed with protocol adjustments; no graft loss

## Vascularized Composite Allotransplantation (VCA) Classification

Laryngeal transplantation is now classified within the broader field of VCA, alongside face, hand, uterine, and penile transplants. Key principles of VCA that apply to laryngeal transplantation include:

### Characteristics of VCA

| Feature | Solid Organ Transplant | VCA (including Larynx) |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Life-saving** | Yes (heart, liver, kidney) | No (quality of life) |
| **Immunogenicity** | Moderate | High (skin component) |
| **Rejection monitoring** | Serum biomarkers, biopsy | Direct visual inspection, biopsy |
| **Donor matching** | ABO, HLA | ABO, HLA, size, gender (for voice) |
| **Ethical justification** | Life vs. death | Quality of life vs. immunosuppression risks |

### Immunological Considerations

- **Skin antigenicity:** The mucosal lining of the larynx is highly immunogenic, similar to skin in face transplants
- **Rejection patterns:** Acute rejection manifests as mucosal erythema, edema, and ulceration; chronic rejection may lead to fibrosis and functional decline
- **Surveillance biopsy:** Serial direct laryngoscopy with mucosal biopsy is the gold standard for rejection monitoring
- **Tolerance protocols:** Research is ongoing into protocols that may reduce or eliminate the need for lifelong immunosuppression (donor bone marrow infusion, co-stimulation blockade, regulatory T cells)

## Challenges and Limitations

### Lifelong Immunosuppression

The most significant ethical and clinical challenge is the requirement for lifelong immunosuppression for a non-life-threatening condition:

- **Infection risk:** Increased susceptibility to opportunistic infections (CMV, Pneumocystis, fungal)
- **Malignancy risk:** 3-5 fold increased risk of lymphoproliferative disorders and solid tumors (particularly skin cancer and PTLD)
- **Metabolic complications:** Diabetes mellitus, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, osteoporosis
- **Renal toxicity:** Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus) cause chronic nephrotoxicity
- **Risk-benefit analysis:** For patients who have undergone laryngectomy for cancer, immunosuppression risks include the theoretical concern of promoting recurrence of the original malignancy or new primary tumors

### Nerve Regeneration

- **Delay:** Recurrent laryngeal nerve regeneration occurs slowly (1-2 mm/day); functional vocal fold motion may take 12-36 months
- **Incomplete recovery:** Full bilateral vocal fold abduction has not been reliably achieved in either human case
- **Synkinesis:** Aberrant reinnervation may result in simultaneous contraction of abductors and adductors, limiting functional motion
- **Laryngeal pacing:** Functional electrical stimulation of laryngeal muscles is being investigated as an adjunct to overcome reinnervation deficits

### Donor Availability

- **Limited donor pool:** Suitable cadaveric donors must be ABO-compatible, size-matched, and ideally gender-matched (for voice characteristics)
- **Organ preservation:** Cold ischemia tolerance of laryngeal tissues is limited (optimally <6 hours)
- **Competing organ allocation:** Laryngeal grafts must not compromise the procurement of life-saving organs

### Rejection Surveillance

- **Invasive monitoring:** Requires serial direct laryngoscopy under anesthesia for biopsy
- **Lack of noninvasive biomarkers:** Unlike renal transplants (creatinine), no serum marker reliably indicates laryngeal graft rejection
- **Histological interpretation:** Standardized rejection grading systems for laryngeal allografts are still being validated

## Tissue Engineering Approaches

Given the limitations of transplantation, tissue engineering offers a potentially immunosuppression-free alternative to restore laryngeal form and function.

### Decellularized Scaffolds

- **Principle:** Native laryngeal tissue is processed to remove all cellular material while preserving the extracellular matrix (ECM) architecture, which retains growth factors and structural cues
- **Recellularization:** The acellular scaffold is seeded with autologous cells (chondrocytes, epithelial cells, muscle progenitors) derived from the patient
- **Advantages:** No immunosuppression required (autologous cells on ECM scaffold); preserves native 3D architecture
- **Challenges:** Achieving functional muscle regeneration, vascularization, and innervation within the scaffold

### Bioartificial Larynx

- **Concept:** Synthetic or hybrid scaffolds fabricated from biocompatible polymers (PGA, PLA, PCL) combined with biological materials (collagen, hyaluronic acid)
- **Cellular components:** Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs), induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs), chondrocytes
- **Functional requirements:** Must support cartilage regeneration, mucosal epithelialization, muscular contraction, and neural integration
- **Current status:** Proof-of-concept studies in animal models (rat, rabbit, canine); no human applications to date

### 3D Bioprinting

- **Technology:** Layer-by-layer deposition of bioinks containing cells and biomaterials to create patient-specific laryngeal constructs
- **Advantages:** Customized geometry matching CT/MRI scans; ability to incorporate multiple cell types in spatially defined regions
- **Materials:** Alginate, gelatin methacrylate (GelMA), PCL, collagen bioinks
- **Challenges:** Printing resolution for fine laryngeal structures, achieving functional mucosal surfaces, vascularization of thick constructs, innervation

### Comparison of Regenerative Approaches

| Approach | Immunosuppression | Functional Muscle | Innervation | Clinical Readiness |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Allotransplantation | Required (lifelong) | Donor muscle intact | Requires neurorrhaphy | 2 human cases |
| Decellularized scaffold | Not required | Must be regenerated | Must be regenerated | Preclinical |
| Bioartificial construct | Not required | Engineered | Engineered | Preclinical |
| 3D bioprinted larynx | Not required | Printed with cells | Theoretically possible | Early preclinical |

## Ethical Considerations

### Risk-Benefit Analysis

The central ethical dilemma in laryngeal transplantation is whether the benefits of restored voice, swallowing, and airway justify the risks of lifelong immunosuppression in a patient whose underlying condition (laryngectomy for cancer) is not immediately life-threatening:

- **Quality of life benefit:** Laryngectomees report significant impairment in social function, emotional well-being, and vocational participation; a functional transplant can dramatically improve QoL
- **Immunosuppression risk:** The cumulative burden of infections, metabolic disease, nephrotoxicity, and secondary malignancies may ultimately reduce life expectancy
- **Cancer recurrence:** For patients who underwent laryngectomy for SCC, immunosuppression theoretically increases the risk of disease recurrence or new primary tumors
- **Patient autonomy:** Fully informed, competent patients may rationally choose to accept immunosuppression risks for voice restoration

### Informed Consent

Patients must be counseled about:
- The experimental nature of the procedure
- The requirement for lifelong medication adherence
- The risk of graft failure and return to alaryngectomized state
- Alternative voice rehabilitation methods (TEP, electrolarynx, esophageal speech)
- The uncertain timeline for functional voice recovery (months to years)

### Selection Criteria

Based on the two reported cases and VCA consensus guidelines:

1. Non-malignant indications may be preferred (lower ethical barrier — no cancer recurrence risk)
2. Adequate time interval after cancer treatment (minimum 2 years disease-free for malignant indications)
3. Psychologically stable patient with realistic expectations
4. Demonstrated medication compliance
5. Absence of contraindications to immunosuppression (active infection, pre-existing malignancy, significant renal disease)

## Current Status and Future Directions

### Global Status (2024)

- **Total human cases:** Eleven laryngeal transplants have been performed globally across three continents (reported in Candelo et al., Laryngoscope 2024)
- **Outcomes:** Variable; the Strome case remains the longest documented success (14 years with functional graft)
- **No established program:** No center currently offers laryngeal transplantation as a routine clinical service
- **Research focus:** Shifting toward tissue engineering and regenerative medicine approaches

### Future Directions

1. **Tolerance induction:** Protocols to eliminate or minimize lifelong immunosuppression (donor bone marrow co-transplantation, regulatory T cell therapy, co-stimulation blockade)
2. **Laryngeal pacing:** Implantable electrical stimulation devices to restore vocal fold abduction in transplanted or reinnervated larynges
3. **Stem cell therapies:** Autologous iPSC-derived laryngeal tissue for scaffold seeding
4. **Xenotransplantation:** Genetically modified porcine laryngeal grafts (gene-edited to reduce immunogenicity)
5. **Composite approaches:** Combining decellularized scaffolds with autologous cell seeding and functional electrical stimulation
6. **Improved rejection monitoring:** Liquid biopsy, donor-derived cell-free DNA (dd-cfDNA), and advanced imaging biomarkers

> [!abstract] Key Points
> 1. The first human laryngeal transplant was performed by Strome in 1998 at the Cleveland Clinic in a 40-year-old male post-laryngectomy patient; the graft functioned for 14 years
> 2. The surgical graft included thyroid cartilage, hyoid, epiglottis, cricoid, 5 tracheal rings, bilateral thyroid lobes, and mucosa
> 3. Vascular anastomosis utilizes the superior thyroid artery and internal jugular vein bilaterally; neural anastomosis targets the recurrent laryngeal nerve
> 4. Triple immunosuppression (cyclosporine/tacrolimus, azathioprine/mycophenolate, prednisone) is required lifelong
> 5. The second transplant (Farwell/Birchall, 2011) was for benign laryngotracheal stenosis — demonstrating application beyond malignancy
> 6. Laryngeal transplantation is now classified as vascularized composite allotransplantation (VCA)
> 7. The central ethical challenge is lifelong immunosuppression risk for a non-life-threatening condition
> 8. Nerve regeneration is slow and incomplete — full bilateral vocal fold abduction has not been reliably achieved
> 9. Tissue engineering approaches (decellularized scaffolds, bioartificial larynx, 3D bioprinting) may offer immunosuppression-free alternatives in the future
> 10. Eleven total human laryngeal transplants have been reported globally; the procedure remains experimental with no established clinical program
