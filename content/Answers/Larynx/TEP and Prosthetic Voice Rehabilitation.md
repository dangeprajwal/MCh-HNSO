> Singer and Blom (1980) published the landmark description of the endoscopic technique for tracheoesophageal puncture and voice restoration after total laryngectomy, reporting fluent voice in 90% of 60 patients using a uniquely designed valved prosthesis that allowed pulmonary air to be shunted into the oesophagus for phonation while preventing aspiration. Hilgers et al. (1990) introduced the Provox voice prosthesis, a low-resistance self-retaining indwelling device placed in a retrograde fashion that achieved good speech quality in 91% of 79 patients, establishing the Provox system as a widely adopted alternative to the Blom-Singer device. Hotz et al. (2002) analysed success and predictability of Provox prosthesis voice rehabilitation in 87 patients, demonstrating that age, sex, tumour localisation, stage, and radiation therapy did not influence rehabilitation success, but that PE segment characteristics were the primary determinant. Luu et al. (2018) conducted a systematic review comparing primary vs secondary TEP for voice rehabilitation, finding no robust evidence that primary TEP is associated with poorer outcomes, supporting the trend toward primary TEP at the time of laryngectomy. Lewin et al. (2012) reported the results of a prospective trial on customisation of the voice prosthesis to prevent leakage from enlarged tracheoesophageal puncture, offering a practical clinical solution for one of the most common complications of TEP.

## Overview and History

Tracheoesophageal puncture (TEP) with voice prosthesis is the **gold standard** for voice rehabilitation after total laryngectomy. It was introduced by Singer and Blom in 1980 and has since evolved significantly. TEP voice restoration produces the most natural-sounding alaryngeal speech, with success rates of 70-90% across large series.

### Historical Timeline

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1932 | Guttman: first description of oesophageal speech |
| 1957 | Electrolarynx commercially available |
| 1972 | Amatsu: first surgical voice restoration shunt procedure |
| 1978 | Blom & Singer: first use of a valved prosthesis in a TE fistula |
| 1980 | Singer & Blom: published the endoscopic TEP technique |
| 1988 | Hilgers: introduced the Provox prosthesis (indwelling, retrograde insertion) |
| 1997 | Provox 2: improved design with anterograde and retrograde insertion capability |
| 2003 | Provox ActiValve: anti-fungal coating for extended device life |
| 2009 | Provox Vega: current generation with SmartInserter for easy anterograde placement |

## Mechanism of Voice Production

### Principle
After total laryngectomy, the trachea is separated from the pharynx/oesophagus (pharyngeal closure). A TEP creates a controlled fistula between the posterior tracheal wall and the anterior oesophageal wall. A one-way valve prosthesis is placed in this fistula.

### Voice Production Sequence
1. Patient occludes the tracheostoma (with finger or hands-free valve)
2. Expiratory air is redirected from the trachea through the prosthesis into the oesophagus
3. Air passes superiorly through the **pharyngoesophageal (PE) segment** (neopharynx)
4. The mucosa of the PE segment vibrates (analogous to vocal fold vibration)
5. This vibration produces a **sound source** that is articulated by the tongue, lips, and palate into speech
6. The one-way valve prevents food and liquid from entering the trachea

### The PE Segment
- The pharyngoesophageal segment is the **sound source** in tracheoesophageal speech
- Composed of the constrictor muscles (inferior constrictor, cricopharyngeus, upper oesophageal sphincter)
- Optimal PE segment characteristics: adequate tone (not too tight, not too flaccid), adequate mucosa, adequate blood supply
- **Hypertonic PE segment** produces strained, effortful voice or failure of TEP speech
- **Hypotonic PE segment** produces weak, breathy voice

## Primary vs Secondary TEP

### Primary TEP
- Performed at the time of total laryngectomy
- TEP created intraoperatively, usually at the conclusion of the laryngectomy
- Voice prosthesis may be placed immediately or after healing (with a catheter as placeholder)

### Secondary TEP
- Performed as a separate procedure weeks to months after laryngectomy
- Requires rigid oesophagoscopy and TEP needle puncture under general anaesthesia (or sometimes office-based with flexible endoscopy)

### Comparison

| Feature | Primary TEP | Secondary TEP |
|---|---|---|
| **Timing** | At laryngectomy | 6 weeks to months later |
| **Anaesthesia** | Already under GA for laryngectomy | Requires separate GA (or office-based) |
| **Advantages** | Single procedure; earlier voice restoration; no delay | PE segment characteristics can be assessed; pharyngeal closure healed |
| **Disadvantages** | Potential for fistula complications with early use; post-RT tissue may be compromised | Delay in voice rehabilitation; additional procedure |
| **Success rate** | 70-90% | 70-85% |
| **Complication rate** | Similar | Similar |
| **Current trend** | **Preferred** by most centres | Reserved for patients not undergoing primary TEP |

### Evidence (Luu 2018)
- Systematic review found **no robust evidence** that primary TEP is associated with poorer outcomes
- Most centres now perform primary TEP as the standard of care
- Secondary TEP is reserved for:
  - Patients who did not undergo primary TEP
  - Patients who initially declined TEP
  - Failed primary TEP requiring revision

## Voice Prosthesis Types

### Classification by Placement Method

| Type | Insertion | Change | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Non-indwelling (patient-changeable)** | Patient inserts/removes | Changed by patient daily or as needed | Blom-Singer duckbill, Blom-Singer low-pressure |
| **Indwelling (clinician-changeable)** | Clinician places with inserter | Changed by clinician in office (every 3-6 months) | Provox 2, Provox Vega, Blom-Singer Advantage |

### Major Prosthesis Systems

#### Blom-Singer System
- Original duckbill prosthesis (non-indwelling)
- Low-pressure prosthesis (non-indwelling, lower airflow resistance)
- Advantage prosthesis (indwelling, silver oxide-containing)
- Classic Indwelling prosthesis

#### Provox System (Atos Medical)
- Provox 1 (1988): retrograde placement only
- Provox 2 (1997): anterograde and retrograde; improved valve design
- Provox ActiValve (2003): magnetic valve with fluoroplastic coating to resist fungal colonization; indicated for patients with short prosthesis lifespan
- Provox Vega (2009): current standard; anterograde insertion with SmartInserter; improved seal, lower airflow resistance

### Prosthesis Selection Factors

| Factor | Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Patient dexterity** | Non-indwelling requires good manual skills; indwelling for patients with poor dexterity |
| **Visual acuity** | Poor vision favours indwelling prosthesis |
| **Device lifespan** | Provox Vega average 4-6 months; ActiValve up to 12 months |
| **Cost** | Non-indwelling cheaper per unit but changed more frequently |
| **Candidacy** | Most patients prefer indwelling (less maintenance, clinic-based changes) |
| **Fungal colonization** | Recurrent early failure --> consider ActiValve or antifungal strategies |

## Surgical Technique

### Primary TEP During Total Laryngectomy

1. **Completion of laryngectomy** and pharyngeal closure
2. **Site selection**: posterior tracheal wall, at the level of the tracheoesophageal party wall, approximately at the 12 o'clock position of the tracheostoma
3. **Puncture**: using a curved trocar or TEP needle, the posterior tracheal wall is punctured through and into the anterior oesophageal wall (under direct or endoscopic visualization)
4. **Guidewire passage**: a guidewire or catheter is passed through the puncture site
5. **Prosthesis placement**: the voice prosthesis is loaded onto the inserter, passed through the puncture, and seated with the oesophageal flange in the oesophageal lumen and the tracheal flange against the tracheal wall
6. **Sizing**: the tract length is measured (usually 8-10 mm initially; may change with healing and tissue changes)

### Key Technical Points
- The puncture should be placed **3-5 mm below the mucocutaneous junction** of the tracheostoma
- The ideal position is midline or slightly off-midline posteriorly
- The puncture should be made at approximately **30-45 degrees** posteriorly
- **Avoid injury** to the posterior oesophageal wall (risk of false passage or mediastinal injury)

## Complications and Management

### Overview of Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Leakage through prosthesis (central) | 50-80% (over prosthesis lifetime) | Late (weeks to months) |
| Leakage around prosthesis (peripheral) | 7-29% | Early or late |
| Fungal colonization | Universal (over time) | Late |
| Granulation tissue | 10-20% | Any time |
| TEP enlargement | 5-15% | Late |
| Prosthesis dislodgement/aspiration | 1-5% | Any time |
| PE segment spasm/hypertonicity | 10-20% | Early |
| Stomal stenosis | 5-10% | Late |

### Central Leakage (Through the Prosthesis)

**Cause**: Valve failure -- usually due to fungal colonization (Candida species) of the silicone valve flap, preventing complete closure.

**Management algorithm:**
1. **Step 1**: Clean the prosthesis with a brush (flush technique)
2. **Step 2**: If persistent, consider antifungal measures:
   - Nystatin oral suspension applied to prosthesis
   - Fluconazole systemic course
   - Miconazole oral gel
   - Dairy product supplementation (Lactobacillus competes with Candida)
3. **Step 3**: Replace the prosthesis
4. **Step 4**: If recurrent early failure (<3 months), switch to Provox ActiValve (fluoroplastic-coated, more resistant to fungal colonization)

### Peripheral Leakage (Around the Prosthesis)

**Cause**: Mismatch between prosthesis diameter and TEP tract diameter -- the tract is larger than the prosthesis.

**Mechanisms:**
- **Early**: tissue oedema resolution leads to a loose fit; simply resize
- **Late**: progressive TEP enlargement from mechanical stress, radiation effects, tumour recurrence, granulation tissue

**Management algorithm:**
1. **Step 1**: Confirm correct prosthesis size (measure tract length); resize if needed
2. **Step 2**: Try a **larger diameter** prosthesis
3. **Step 3**: If granulation tissue present, treat with silver nitrate cautery or steroid injection
4. **Step 4**: Temporary prosthesis removal with catheter placement for 2-4 weeks (allows tract to shrink)
5. **Step 5**: Injection augmentation around the TEP (e.g., Restylane, collagen, autologous fat)
6. **Step 6**: Customised prosthesis (Lewin 2012 technique -- enlarged flanges)
7. **Step 7**: If refractory, surgical closure of TEP with local tissue flap and creation of new TEP at a different site

### Fungal Colonization

- **Candida albicans** is the most common organism; also C. tropicalis, C. krusei
- Biofilm formation on silicone surfaces is the primary mechanism
- Mixed bacterial-fungal biofilm is typical
- Colonization is **universal** and is the primary determinant of prosthesis lifespan

**Prevention strategies:**
- Regular prosthesis cleaning (brush technique 2-3 times daily)
- Nystatin oral suspension rinse
- Dairy product supplementation (probiotics)
- Avoid refined sugar-rich diet
- Provox ActiValve for patients with recurrent early failure

### Granulation Tissue

- Forms around the TEP site, at the tracheal or oesophageal ends of the prosthesis
- Caused by mechanical irritation, infection, poorly fitted prosthesis
- **Management**: silver nitrate cautery, steroid injection, laser ablation, prosthesis resizing

### Pharyngoesophageal (PE) Segment Hypertonicity

**Definition**: excessive spasm or tone of the PE segment that prevents passage of air from the prosthesis into the oesophagus, producing a strained voice or no voice.

**Diagnosis:**
- Insufflation testing (Blom-Singer insufflation test or videofluoroscopic assessment)
- A tight PE segment produces high back-pressure and effortful or absent voice

**Management:**
1. **Botulinum toxin injection** into the PE segment (pharyngeal constrictor muscles):
   - 20-50 units of botulinum toxin A
   - Injected under EMG guidance or direct endoscopic visualization
   - Effects last 3-6 months; may require repeated injections
   - Success rate: 50-70%

2. **Pharyngeal constrictor myotomy**:
   - Surgical division of the inferior constrictor and cricopharyngeus muscles
   - Performed at the time of laryngectomy (prophylactic) or secondarily
   - Bilateral myotomy preferred over unilateral
   - Success rate: 60-80%

3. **Pharyngeal plexus neurectomy**:
   - Division of pharyngeal plexus branches
   - Reduces tone of the pharyngeal constrictors
   - May be combined with myotomy
   - Performed at the time of laryngectomy (prophylactic)

### PE Segment Hypotonicity

- Flaccid PE segment produces a weak, breathy voice with low volume
- More common after extensive pharyngeal reconstruction (free flap neopharynx)
- Management: digital pressure on the reconstructed segment during phonation; rarely amenable to surgical correction

## Prosthesis Sizing and Maintenance

### Sizing
- Prosthesis length (shaft length) typically ranges from **6 mm to 12.5 mm** (Provox system)
- Measured using a **sizing device** inserted through the TEP
- The correct size should allow the prosthesis to sit snugly without excessive compression (too short) or looseness (too long)
- **Too short**: tissue compression, pain, necrosis, embedding
- **Too long**: pistoning, central leak, poor valve function

### Maintenance Protocol

| Interval | Activity |
|---|---|
| **Daily** | Brush cleaning 2-3 times/day; stoma hygiene; HME filter use |
| **Weekly** | Visual inspection by patient; check for leakage |
| **Monthly** | Speech-language pathologist review (if available) |
| **Every 3-6 months** | Clinic visit for prosthesis assessment; change if leaking |
| **As needed** | Urgent review for aspiration, displacement, inability to speak |

### Heat and Moisture Exchanger (HME)

- A filter placed over the tracheostoma that warms and humidifies inspired air
- Compensates for the loss of nasal humidification after laryngectomy
- Reduces tracheal crusting, coughing, and pulmonary complications
- The **Provox HME** system can be combined with a hands-free valve (Provox FreeHands) for stoma-free phonation

## Outcomes

### Voice Quality
- **Success rate** (ability to produce conversational speech): 70-90% across major series
- Voice is rated as more natural than electrolarynx and more intelligible than oesophageal speech
- Mean fundamental frequency: ~80-110 Hz (lower than normal laryngeal voice)
- Mean phonation time: 10-20 seconds

### Quality of Life
- TEP voice users report significantly better communication-related quality of life than non-TEP users
- Voice handicap index (VHI) scores are better in TEP users vs. oesophageal speech and electrolarynx users
- Social reintegration is facilitated by TEP voice

### Factors Predicting Good TEP Voice Outcome
- Adequate PE segment tone (not hypertonic or hypotonic)
- Adequate pulmonary function
- Good manual dexterity (for stoma occlusion and prosthesis care)
- Motivated patient with realistic expectations
- Supportive speech-language pathology input
- Absence of significant pharyngeal stenosis or stricture

## Key Points for Exam

1. **Singer and Blom (1980)** introduced TEP; it is the **gold standard** for post-laryngectomy voice rehabilitation
2. **Primary TEP** (at time of laryngectomy) is now preferred over secondary TEP at most centres
3. The **PE segment** is the vibrating sound source in TEP speech -- its tone determines voice quality
4. **Central leak** (through prosthesis) is caused by valve failure due to **fungal colonization** -- managed by cleaning, antifungals, and prosthesis replacement
5. **Peripheral leak** (around prosthesis) is caused by TEP enlargement -- managed by resizing, injection augmentation, or surgical revision
6. **PE segment hypertonicity** is managed with **botulinum toxin injection**, pharyngeal constrictor myotomy, or pharyngeal plexus neurectomy
7. **Provox Vega** is the current standard indwelling prosthesis; **Provox ActiValve** for patients with recurrent early fungal failure
8. Prosthesis lifespan is primarily determined by **Candida biofilm** formation on the silicone valve
9. **Hands-free valves** (Provox FreeHands) allow phonation without manual stoma occlusion
10. TEP voice produces the **most natural-sounding** alaryngeal speech with the highest success rates
