> Pearson, Woods, and Hartman (1980) published the landmark description of the extended hemilaryngectomy (later termed near-total laryngectomy) for T3 glottic carcinoma, demonstrating that a dynamic myomucosal shunt could be preserved from the trachea to the neopharynx to provide pulmonary-driven voice while achieving oncologic clearance equivalent to total laryngectomy, reporting the first seven consecutive cases with clear surgical margins, local control, and satisfactory voice without significant aspiration. Pearson et al. (1998) reported the long-term results of 225 patients undergoing primary or salvage near-total laryngectomy for laryngeal and pyriform cancers, confirming that conversational voice was achieved in 85% of patients surviving beyond one year, with oncologic outcomes comparable to total laryngectomy. Pradhan et al. (2002) reported the Tata Memorial Hospital experience with 137 cases of near-total laryngectomy for advanced laryngeal and pyriform fossa cancers, demonstrating that 88.6% achieved communicable speech and 70.1% were alive and disease-free, establishing the TMH experience as one of the largest single-institution series worldwide. DeSanto et al. (1989) expanded the indications for near-total laryngectomy beyond glottic cancer to include supraglottic, pharyngeal, base-of-tongue, and other head and neck cancers, demonstrating the versatility of the procedure in patients where conventional conservation surgery was oncologically or physiologically unsafe. Pearson et al. (1998) further refined case selection criteria and established that the procedure could be safely applied as salvage surgery after radiation failure.

## Concept and History

### The Pearson Concept (1980)
Near-total laryngectomy (NTL) is a surgical procedure first described by **Bruce W. Pearson** in 1980 that achieves oncologic clearance equivalent to total laryngectomy while preserving a **dynamic myomucosal shunt** from the trachea to the neopharynx. This shunt enables **pulmonary-driven speech** without the need for any prosthetic device, which distinguishes it fundamentally from tracheoesophageal puncture (TEP) speech.

### Key Principle
The concept rests on the observation that in many advanced laryngeal cancers, the disease involves one side of the larynx more than the other. If the **contralateral arytenoid unit** (arytenoid cartilage, overlying mucosa, and its neurovascular supply) is uninvolved by tumour, it can be preserved and used to create a dynamic, innervated myomucosal shunt between the trachea and the reconstructed pharynx.

## Indications

### Primary Indications
- **T3 laryngeal cancer** with vocal fold fixation (provided contralateral arytenoid is uninvolved)
- **T4a laryngeal cancer** with limited extralaryngeal spread
- **Pyriform fossa cancer** (T2-T4a) with extension to the larynx -- provided one arytenoid unit is preserved
- **Salvage surgery** after radiation failure (selected cases)

### Essential Requirement
The **contralateral arytenoid unit must be completely uninvolved** by tumour and must have:
- Normal mobility (confirmed preoperatively by flexible laryngoscopy)
- Intact mucosa
- Intact nerve supply (RLN on the uninvolved side)
- Intact blood supply

### Extended Indications (TMH Experience)
- **Pyriform fossa cancers** requiring pharyngoplasty (extended NTL with pharyngeal reconstruction)
- **Post-cricoid cancers** (selected cases)
- Advanced cancers where TL+TEP would be the alternative

## Contraindications

| Absolute Contraindications | Relative Contraindications |
|---|---|
| Bilateral arytenoid involvement | Prior full-dose radiation therapy |
| Posterior commissure involvement | Subglottic extension >1 cm |
| Interarytenoid involvement | Fixed ipsilateral arytenoid with contralateral involvement |
| Bilateral vocal fold fixation | Poor pulmonary function (inadequate expiratory pressure for speech) |
| Fixed contralateral arytenoid | Significant aspiration risk (neurological comorbidity) |
| Thyroid cartilage invasion into soft tissue bilaterally | Advanced age with significant comorbidities |

## Case Selection Criteria (Detailed)

Careful case selection is **critical** to the success of NTL. The following criteria must be systematically assessed:

### Preoperative Assessment

1. **Clinical examination:**
   - Direct flexible laryngoscopy to assess vocal fold mobility, arytenoid mobility, and extent of mucosal disease
   - Confirm **normal contralateral arytenoid mobility**
   - Assess posterior commissure and interarytenoid area

2. **Imaging:**
   - CT neck with contrast: assess paraglottic space, pre-epiglottic space, cartilage invasion, extralaryngeal spread, lymph node status
   - MRI (optional): better soft tissue delineation, particularly for paraglottic space invasion

3. **Endoscopy under GA (EUA):**
   - Definitive assessment of tumour extent
   - Biopsy of suspicious areas (especially contralateral arytenoid, posterior commissure, subglottis)
   - Palpation of both arytenoid joints to confirm mobility

4. **Patient factors:**
   - Adequate pulmonary function for shunt-driven speech
   - Motivation and realistic expectations
   - No contraindications to general anaesthesia for prolonged surgery

### Ideal Candidate Profile
- Unilateral T3 or T4a glottic/transglottic carcinoma
- Mobile contralateral arytenoid
- No posterior commissure involvement
- No subglottic extension beyond 1 cm
- Adequate pulmonary reserve
- Motivated patient

## Surgical Technique

### Approach
The surgical approach is essentially that of a **total laryngectomy** with preservation of the contralateral arytenoid unit and creation of a dynamic tracheopharyngeal shunt.

### Step-by-Step Procedure

#### 1. Incision and Exposure
- Apron flap or horizontal neck incision
- Elevation of subplatysmal flaps
- Neck dissection if indicated (bilateral or unilateral)

#### 2. Laryngeal Skeleton Mobilisation
- Division of strap muscles
- Release of the thyrohyoid membrane
- Division of the superior laryngeal pedicles
- Division of the inferior constrictor muscles off the thyroid ala (on the tumour side)

#### 3. Tracheal Transection
- Trachea divided at the appropriate level (similar to total laryngectomy)
- Adequate inferior margin ensured

#### 4. Pharyngotomy and Resection
- The larynx is entered through the pharynx (usually through the vallecula or pyriform fossa on the tumour side)
- The tumour is resected with the bulk of the laryngeal framework
- **Key step:** The contralateral arytenoid unit is carefully preserved with its:
  - Mucosa intact
  - Posterior cricoarytenoid (PCA) muscle attachment intact
  - Interarytenoid muscle preserved on the uninvolved side
  - Recurrent laryngeal nerve on the uninvolved side preserved
  - Blood supply maintained

#### 5. Creation of the Dynamic Shunt
- The preserved arytenoid unit and its overlying mucosa are fashioned into a **tube or trough**
- This creates a narrow mucosal-lined channel from the **tracheal stump** to the **neopharynx**
- The shunt is approximately **4-5 mm in diameter**
- It is **dynamic** because the preserved arytenoid and its muscles can move, opening during phonation and closing during swallowing

#### 6. Pharyngeal Closure
- The neopharynx is reconstructed around the shunt
- Primary closure of the pharynx if adequate mucosa remains
- Pharyngoplasty using remaining pharyngeal mucosa

#### 7. Permanent Tracheostomy
- A permanent tracheostomy is fashioned (as in total laryngectomy)
- The tracheostomy is separate from the shunt

### TMH/Tata Memorial Modification (Subramanian)
- Modifications to the shunt design to improve reliability of voice production
- Emphasis on preserving an adequate cuff of mucosa around the arytenoid
- Refinements in pharyngeal closure technique to prevent fistula
- Extended pharyngeal resection with laryngoplasty (ENTLP) for pyriform fossa cancers requiring pharyngoplasty

## Mechanism of Speech

### Pulmonary-Driven Voice Production

1. Patient occludes the **tracheostoma** with a finger (digital occlusion)
2. Expiratory air is redirected upward through the **dynamic myomucosal shunt**
3. The shunt opens (because the arytenoid unit abducts under air pressure and neural control)
4. Air enters the **neopharynx**
5. Neopharyngeal mucosa vibrates (similar to the PE segment in TEP speech)
6. Sound is articulated into speech by the tongue, lips, palate, and teeth
7. During **swallowing**, the arytenoid unit adducts, closing the shunt and preventing aspiration

### Comparison with TEP Speech

| Feature | NTL (Shunt Speech) | TL + TEP (Prosthetic Speech) |
|---|---|---|
| **Sound source** | Neopharyngeal mucosa | PE segment mucosa |
| **Air pathway** | Via dynamic myomucosal shunt | Via voice prosthesis |
| **Prosthesis required** | No | Yes (voice prosthesis) |
| **Stoma occlusion** | Digital (finger over stoma) | Digital or hands-free valve |
| **Speech quality** | Comparable to or superior to TEP | Good; most natural alaryngeal speech |
| **Maintenance** | None | Regular prosthesis changes |
| **Complications** | Aspiration (early); shunt stenosis | Prosthesis-related (leakage, fungal colonization) |
| **Success rate** | 80-90% achieve functional speech | 70-90% achieve functional speech |

## Speech Outcomes

- **80-90%** of NTL patients achieve **functional conversational speech**
- Voice quality is **superior to oesophageal speech** and comparable to TEP speech
- Speech is **pulmonary-driven** -- louder and more sustained than oesophageal speech
- **No prosthesis required** -- this eliminates prosthesis-related complications and costs
- Voice rehabilitation begins 2-3 weeks postoperatively once the pharynx has healed
- The Pradhan (TMH) series reported **88.6% communicable speech** and **100% success in the ENTLP subgroup**

### Factors Affecting Speech
- Shunt size and patency
- Mucosal quality of the preserved arytenoid
- Pulmonary function and expiratory force
- Neopharyngeal tone (hypertonic segment impairs speech, similar to TEP)
- Patient motivation and speech therapy

## Swallowing Outcomes

- Most patients achieve **normal oral diet** within 2-4 weeks postoperatively
- **Aspiration** is the main concern in the early postoperative period
  - Usually mild and self-limiting
  - Due to incomplete shunt closure during swallowing
  - Managed with dietary modification, swallowing therapy, and time
- Severe persistent aspiration requiring shunt closure or completion laryngectomy is rare (<5%)
- Swallowing outcomes are generally comparable to total laryngectomy

## Oncologic Outcomes

### Local-Regional Control
- NTL achieves **oncologic clearance equivalent to total laryngectomy** when case selection is appropriate
- Local control rates: **70-85%** across major series
- The Pearson series (1998): local control comparable to TL for matched stages
- The TMH series (Pradhan 2002): 70.1% alive and disease-free at median follow-up of 35 months

### Survival

| Series | N | Follow-up | Disease-Free Survival | Speech Success |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pearson 1998 | 225 | Long-term | Comparable to TL | 85% |
| Pradhan 2002 (TMH) | 137 | Median 35 mo | 70.1% | 88.6% |
| DeSanto 1989 | Multi-site | Long-term | Equivalent to TL | >80% |

### Salvage After NTL Failure
- Completion total laryngectomy can be performed if local recurrence occurs
- Oncologic outcomes of salvage TL after NTL failure are comparable to primary salvage TL

## Comparison: NTL vs TL + TEP

| Parameter | NTL | TL + TEP |
|---|---|---|
| **Oncologic adequacy** | Equivalent (when well selected) | Gold standard |
| **Case selection** | Restricted (must have uninvolved arytenoid) | Broad (any stage) |
| **Technical complexity** | Higher | Standard |
| **Speech method** | Intrinsic shunt (no prosthesis) | Voice prosthesis |
| **Speech quality** | Comparable | Comparable |
| **Prosthesis costs** | None | Ongoing costs |
| **Prosthesis complications** | None | Leakage, fungal colonization, sizing |
| **Aspiration risk** | Higher (early postop) | Lower |
| **Revision rate** | Lower (no prosthesis issues) | Higher (prosthesis changes) |
| **Global applicability** | Excellent in resource-limited settings | Requires prosthesis availability |

### Advantages of NTL Over TL + TEP
1. **No prosthesis required** -- eliminates prosthesis-related costs and complications
2. **Particularly valuable in resource-limited settings** where prosthesis availability and replacement may be challenging
3. No risk of prosthesis-related leakage, fungal colonization, or sizing issues
4. Self-sustaining voice mechanism

### Disadvantages of NTL
1. **Technically demanding** -- requires experienced surgeon
2. **Limited indications** -- strict case selection criteria exclude many patients
3. **Higher early aspiration risk** compared to TL + TEP
4. Risk of shunt stenosis or dysfunction
5. If oncologic margins are compromised, the procedure fails both oncologically and functionally

## Current Status and Controversies

### Declining Global Usage
- NTL has seen **declining utilisation worldwide** over the past two decades
- Reasons include:
  - Success and widespread availability of **TEP with voice prosthesis**
  - Increasing use of **organ preservation protocols** (CRT, induction chemotherapy)
  - Strict case selection criteria limiting the eligible patient population
  - Technically demanding nature of the procedure
  - Variable results across centres (outcomes are surgeon-dependent)

### Continued Relevance
- NTL remains relevant in specific contexts:
  - **Resource-limited settings** where voice prostheses are unavailable or unaffordable
  - Patients who **cannot manage a voice prosthesis** (dexterity issues, compliance, follow-up limitations)
  - Centres with **extensive experience** in the technique (e.g., TMH Mumbai)
  - Patients who prefer a prosthesis-free voice solution

### TMH Experience
- Tata Memorial Hospital, Mumbai, remains one of the **largest centres for NTL worldwide**
- The TMH experience spans over 500 procedures with refined technique
- The **Subramanian modification** of the shunt design has improved speech outcomes
- TMH data supports NTL as a viable alternative to TL + TEP in appropriately selected patients in the Indian subcontinent

### Key Controversies
1. **Is NTL oncologically equivalent to TL?** -- Yes, when case selection is strict; no, if selection criteria are relaxed
2. **Is NTL speech superior to TEP speech?** -- Comparable in most studies; slight advantage in naturalness
3. **Should NTL be taught and practiced widely?** -- Debated; outcomes are surgeon-dependent
4. **Role after prior RT?** -- Controversial; increased complication rates but feasible in selected cases

## Key Points for Exam

1. **Pearson (1980)** described NTL: oncologic clearance of TL + pulmonary-driven speech via dynamic myomucosal shunt
2. **Essential requirement:** Uninvolved contralateral arytenoid with normal mobility, intact mucosa, nerve, and blood supply
3. **Contraindications:** Bilateral arytenoid involvement, posterior commissure involvement, interarytenoid involvement, subglottic extension >1 cm
4. The shunt is **dynamic** (innervated arytenoid), **mucosal-lined**, and approximately **4-5 mm in diameter**
5. **Permanent tracheostomy** is required (same as TL); digital occlusion for speech
6. **Speech success rate:** 80-90% achieve functional conversational voice -- **no prosthesis needed**
7. **TMH (Pradhan 2002):** 137 cases, 88.6% speech success, 70.1% disease-free survival
8. **NTL vs TL+TEP:** Oncologically equivalent when well selected; NTL eliminates prosthesis costs/complications; TL+TEP has broader indications
9. **Declining global use** due to TEP success and organ preservation protocols, but **remains relevant** in resource-limited settings
10. **Subramanian modification** (TMH) refined the shunt design for more reliable voice production
