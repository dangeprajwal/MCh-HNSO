## History of CO2 Laser in Laryngeal Surgery

### Key Milestones

| Year | Milestone | Surgeon/Group |
|---|---|---|
| 1964 | CO2 laser developed | Patel (Bell Labs) |
| 1972 | First use of CO2 laser in larynx (benign lesions) | Strong & Jako (Boston) |
| 1975 | CO2 laser coupled to operating microscope | — |
| 1979 | Steiner begins TLM for malignant tumours (Gottingen, Germany) | Wolfgang Steiner |
| 1993 | Steiner publishes landmark 240-patient series | Steiner |
| 2000 | ELS cordectomy classification (Remacle) | European Laryngological Society |
| 2007 | ELS revised classification (Types I-VIII) | Remacle et al. |

### CO2 Laser Properties

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Wavelength** | 10,600 nm (far infrared) |
| **Tissue interaction** | Absorbed by water in tissue → vaporisation |
| **Penetration depth** | 0.1-0.2 mm (superficial; highly precise) |
| **Haemostasis** | Effective for vessels <0.5 mm; larger vessels require bipolar cautery or clips |
| **Delivery** | Free beam through micromanipulator attached to operating microscope |
| **Modes** | Continuous, pulsed, superpulsed |
| **Spot size** | 0.3-0.8 mm (adjustable) |

### Advantages of CO2 Laser in Laryngeal Surgery

1. **Precise tissue cutting** with minimal thermal damage (0.1-0.2 mm lateral spread)
2. **Bloodless field** for vessels <0.5 mm
3. **Excellent visualisation** through the operating microscope
4. **Minimal oedema** post-operatively (compared to cold steel)
5. **Piecemeal resection** possible with specimen reconstruction — Steiner's principle of "cutting through tumour" for deep margins while maintaining oncologic safety
6. **No tracheotomy** required in most cases
7. **Short hospital stay** (1-3 days)

## ELS Classification of Endoscopic Cordectomies

### Original Classification (Remacle 2000) and Revision (2007)

| Type | Name | Depth of Resection | Indication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | Subepithelial cordectomy | Epithelium only (vocal cord stripping) | Dysplasia, Tis |
| **II** | Subligamental cordectomy | Epithelium + Reinke's space + vocal ligament | Tis, microinvasive T1a |
| **III** | Transmuscular cordectomy | Epithelium + ligament + part of vocalis muscle | T1a involving ligament |
| **IV** | Total cordectomy | Entire vocal cord (epithelium to inner perichondrium of thyroid cartilage) | T1a involving muscle depth |
| **Va** | Extended cordectomy — contralateral cord and anterior commissure | Bilateral resection including AC | T1b, T1a with AC involvement |
| **Vb** | Extended cordectomy — arytenoid | Ipsilateral cord + arytenoid | T1-T2 with arytenoid extension |
| **Vc** | Extended cordectomy — subglottis | Ipsilateral cord + subglottic extension | T2 with subglottic extension |
| **Vd** | Extended cordectomy — ventricle | Ipsilateral cord + ventricle + false cord | T2 with ventricular extension |
| **VI** | Extended cordectomy — anterior commissure | Bilateral anterior resection | AC-centred tumours |

### Relationship Between ELS Type and Voice Outcome

| Cordectomy Type | Voice Quality | VHI Score |
|---|---|---|
| I-II | Near normal | 5-15 (normal-mild) |
| III | Mild hoarseness | 10-20 (mild) |
| IV | Moderate hoarseness (breathy) | 20-35 (mild-moderate) |
| Va | Significant hoarseness | 30-45 (moderate) |
| Vb-Vd | Variable; often significant | 25-40 (moderate) |

## TLM Technique

### Pre-operative Evaluation for Laser Surgery

#### Stroboscopy

- Assesses **mucosal wave** and **vocal fold vibration**
- Loss of mucosal wave suggests deeper invasion (involvement of vocal ligament or muscle)
- Helps distinguish between superficial and deep lesions → guides cordectomy type selection
- **Findings suggesting deeper invasion**: Absent mucosal wave, adynamic segment, irregular edge

#### Narrow Band Imaging (NBI)

- Uses filtered light (415 nm blue, 540 nm green) to enhance visualisation of **superficial mucosal vasculature**
- **Intra-papillary capillary loops (IPCL)** classification (Ni classification):
  - Type I-III: Benign
  - Type IV: Dysplasia/Tis
  - Type Va-Vb: Invasive SCC
- Helps delineate tumour margins intra-operatively
- Improves detection of superficial extension and satellite lesions

#### Office Laryngoscopy

- Flexible nasendoscopy with white light and NBI
- Chip-on-tip endoscopes provide high-definition images
- Documents tumour extent, vocal cord mobility, arytenoid movement
- Video recording for multidisciplinary team review

#### Examination Under Anaesthesia (EUA)

- **Essential step before committing to TLM**
- Performed with rigid laryngoscopes (0° and 70° telescopes)
- Assess:
  - Tumour extent (superficial, deep, bilateral)
  - Anterior commissure involvement
  - Subglottic extension (measure with graduated probe)
  - Arytenoid mobility (palpation under anaesthesia)
  - Transoral access and exposure adequacy

### Instrumentation

| Equipment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Suspension laryngoscope | Exposure (Kleinsasser, Dedo, Lindholm, Weerda) |
| CO2 laser with micromanipulator | Tissue cutting |
| Operating microscope | Magnification and visualisation |
| Microlaryngeal instrument set | Grasping, suctioning, retraction |
| Bipolar cautery (fine tip) | Haemostasis for larger vessels |
| Laser-safe endotracheal tube | Airway protection (wrapped or specialised) |

### Cutting Principles (Steiner Technique)

1. **Block resection for small tumours** (≤1 cm): En bloc excision with margins
2. **Piecemeal resection for larger tumours**: Cutting through the tumour to assess deep extent, followed by resection of the deep margin under direct vision — the "Steiner principle"
3. **Deep margin checked first**, then lateral and mucosal margins
4. **Cutting with the laser on continuous mode** at 3-5 watts; superpulse mode for precise cuts near critical structures
5. **Char must be removed** from the specimen edges before pathological assessment

### Laser Safety

- **Eye protection**: All operating theatre personnel wear protective eyewear (specific for CO2 laser wavelength)
- **Laser-safe ETT**: Wrapped aluminium foil or dedicated laser-resistant tube; cuff filled with methylene blue-stained saline
- **Wet pledgets** around the surgical field to prevent fire
- **Smoke evacuation**: Suction near laser plume (viral DNA concerns)
- **Fire protocol**: Water syringe immediately available

## TLM for Glottic Cancer by T-stage

### T1a Glottic Cancer

| Parameter | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Cordectomy type | Type I-IV (depending on depth) |
| Local control | 85-90% |
| Larynx preservation | 95-98% |
| DSS | 96-98% |
| Tracheotomy | Rarely required (<5%) |
| Hospital stay | 1-2 days |

### T1b Glottic Cancer

| Parameter | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Cordectomy type | Type Va (bilateral + AC) |
| Local control | 80-85% |
| Larynx preservation | 90-95% |
| Voice outcome | Moderate-severe hoarseness |
| Consideration | Alternative: SCPL-CHEP for better local control |

### T2 Glottic Cancer

| Parameter | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Cordectomy type | Type Vc/Vd (extended) |
| Local control | 70-76% |
| Larynx preservation | 83-93% |
| Consideration | Higher recurrence rate; consider SCPL-CHEP for T2b |

### T3 Glottic Cancer (Selected Cases)

- **Highly selected patients only**: Small-volume T3 with impaired mobility but not fixed cord
- **Requires extensive experience** and willingness for close follow-up
- **Local control**: 60-70%
- **Alternative**: SCPL-CHEP or chemoradiation preferred for most T3

## TLM for Supraglottic Cancer (Endoscopic Supraglottic Laryngectomy)

### Patient Selection

| Criterion | Details |
|---|---|
| T-stage | T1-T2 (selected T3 in experienced centres) |
| Exposure | Adequate transoral access (EUA assessment essential) |
| Pre-epiglottic space | No invasion (or limited invasion in selected cases) |
| Base of tongue | No deep invasion |
| Arytenoid fixation | Absent |
| Vallecular/BOT access | Adequate for margin control |

### Technique

1. **Laryngoscope selection**: Weerda distending laryngoscope or large-bore Lindholm for supraglottic access
2. **Laser settings**: 5-8 watts, continuous or superpulse
3. **Resection sequence**: Pre-epiglottic space → epiglottis → AE fold(s) → false cord(s) → ventricle
4. **Margin assessment**: Frozen sections of deep margin in pre-epiglottic space
5. **Haemostasis**: Bipolar cautery for superior laryngeal artery branches
6. **Wound healing**: By secondary intention (no reconstruction needed)

### Outcomes for Endoscopic Supraglottic Laryngectomy

| Parameter | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Local control (T1-T2) | 85-95% |
| Larynx preservation | 90-95% |
| Aspiration rate | 15-25% (temporary); <5% long-term |
| Decannulation | >95% |
| 5-year DSS | 85-90% |

## Endoscopic Surgery for Hypopharyngeal Cancer

### Patient Selection

Hypopharyngeal carcinoma treated by TLM requires strict criteria (Vilaseca 2004):

| Criterion | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Subsite | Piriform sinus (medial wall), posterior hypopharyngeal wall |
| T-stage | T1-T2 (selected small T3) |
| Apex of piriform sinus | Not invaded |
| Post-cricoid region | Not invaded |
| Exposure | Adequate transoral access |
| Neck | Ipsilateral neck dissection for nodal staging |

### Technique

1. **Weerda or Lindholm distending laryngoscope** for exposure
2. **Laser resection** with piecemeal technique (Steiner principle)
3. **Resection of piriform sinus mucosa** from medial to lateral, with preservation of the laryngeal framework
4. **Bilateral neck dissection** (levels II-IV) routinely performed (high nodal risk >30% for N0 hypopharynx)
5. **Adjuvant RT** frequently indicated based on pathological staging

### Outcomes

| Parameter | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Local control (T1-T2) | 80-90% |
| Larynx preservation | 85-95% |
| 5-year OS | 55-70% |
| Swallowing preservation | >90% |
| Hospital stay | 3-7 days (including neck dissection) |

## Salvage TLM for Recurrent Disease

### After Prior Radiotherapy

| Consideration | Detail |
|---|---|
| Feasibility | Feasible for rT1-rT2 recurrences with adequate exposure |
| Local control | 50-70% (lower than primary TLM) |
| Larynx preservation | 60-80% |
| Complication rate | Higher — delayed healing, chondronecrosis risk |
| Selection | Post-RT oedema makes margin assessment challenging; use NBI |
| Alternative | SCPL or total laryngectomy for larger recurrences |

### Key Principles for Salvage TLM

1. **Biopsy-proven recurrence** — distinguish from post-RT changes
2. **CT/MRI** to assess cartilage status and rule out extralaryngeal disease
3. **PET-CT** at 12 weeks post-RT to detect persistent/recurrent disease
4. **Generous margins** — wider resection than in the primary setting
5. **Close follow-up**: Monthly for the first year after salvage TLM

## Specimen Handling and Processing

### Principles

Accurate pathological assessment after TLM is critical because of the **piecemeal resection technique**:

1. **Specimen orientation**: The surgeon must orient each specimen piece using marking sutures or India ink before removal from the patient
2. **Specimen mapping**: Draw a diagram of the resection showing the relationship of each piece to the tumour and surrounding structures
3. **Inking**: The deep margin is inked (surgeon and pathologist agree on orientation)
4. **Frozen sections**: Sent from the deep margin, lateral margins, and any area of concern
5. **Specimen reconstruction**: The pathologist reassembles the pieces on a template to reconstruct the original tumour geography
6. **Serial sectioning**: Perpendicular cuts every 2-3 mm for histological assessment

### Margin Assessment

| Margin | Definition | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Clear (R0)** | ≥1 mm (ideally ≥2 mm) free of tumour | Observation |
| **Close** | <1 mm from tumour to edge | Close follow-up or re-excision |
| **Positive (R1)** | Tumour at the inked margin | Re-excision or RT |
| **Thermal artefact** | Laser char obscures margin | Remove char before assessment; deeper sections |

### Steiner's Approach to Margins

- The **deep margin is the most important** — mucosal margins can be assessed by direct vision
- Cutting through tumour to assess deep extent is acceptable if the final deep margin is clear
- **"Positive mucosal margin"** after cutting through tumour is expected and does not indicate incomplete resection — the final circumferential resection provides the definitive margins

## Meta-analyses: TLM vs RT

### Key Findings

| Meta-analysis | Conclusion |
|---|---|
| Feng 2011 (T1-T2N0) | No significant difference in local control (OR 0.90, 95% CI 0.54-1.49) |
| Huang 2017 (T1b) | Overall DFS 85%; no modality significantly superior |
| Canis 2015 (T1a) | TLM: LC 86%, LP 97%, DSS 98% — comparable to RT benchmarks |

### Summary of Evidence

- **Level I evidence** supports treatment equivalence between TLM and RT for T1 glottic cancer
- **No randomised controlled trial** directly comparing TLM vs RT exists (VOICE trial initiated but closed due to recruitment difficulty)
- **Cost-effectiveness**: TLM is more cost-effective than RT in most health economic analyses (single procedure, short hospital stay)
- **Voice**: RT produces marginally better voice in the short term; TLM voice improves over 12-24 months

## Functional Outcomes After TLM

### Voice

| Cordectomy Type | Voice Outcome |
|---|---|
| Type I-II | Near-normal voice; minimal VHI elevation |
| Type III | Mild hoarseness; VHI 10-20 |
| Type IV | Moderate hoarseness (breathy); VHI 20-35 |
| Type V (extended) | Significant hoarseness; VHI 30-50 |

### Swallowing

- Swallowing is generally **well preserved** after glottic TLM
- **Aspiration risk** is low for cordectomy Types I-IV (<5%)
- **Supraglottic TLM**: Temporary aspiration in 15-25%; long-term aspiration <5%
- **Hypopharyngeal TLM**: Swallowing preservation >90%

### Quality of Life

- QoL measures (EORTC QLQ-H&N35, FACT-H&N) show good preservation of function after TLM
- Social function and emotional well-being are generally well maintained
- Return to work within 1-2 weeks for most glottic TLM patients

## Complications of TLM

### Intra-operative

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Haemorrhage | 2-5% | Bipolar cautery, clips, packing |
| Airway fire | <0.1% | Water syringe, protocol activation |
| Dental injury | 1-3% | Tooth guard, careful retractor placement |
| Incomplete exposure | 5-10% | Convert to external approach or abandon TLM |
| Laser damage to ETT | <0.5% | Laser-safe tube, wet pledgets |

### Early Post-operative (0-30 days)

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Oedema requiring re-intubation | 1-3% | Dexamethasone, re-intubation; rarely tracheotomy |
| Post-operative haemorrhage | 1-2% | Return to OR; bipolar cautery |
| Aspiration (supraglottic TLM) | 15-25% | Swallowing therapy; temporary NG feed |
| Surgical emphysema | 1-2% | Usually self-limiting |
| Infection | <1% | Antibiotics |

### Late Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Web formation (anterior glottis) | 5-10% (Type Va) | Lysis, silastic keel placement |
| Granulation tissue | 5-10% | Laser excision at follow-up |
| Dysphonia | Variable by type | Speech therapy; injection augmentation |
| Laryngeal stenosis | <2% | Dilation; stenting |
| Chondronecrosis (salvage TLM) | 2-5% | Debridement; antibiotics; hyperbaric oxygen |

## Key Points for Exam

1. CO2 laser was first used in the larynx by Strong and Jako in 1972; Steiner revolutionised oncologic TLM from 1979 onwards
2. CO2 laser wavelength is 10,600 nm; absorbed by water; penetration depth 0.1-0.2 mm
3. The ELS classification (Remacle 2000, revised 2007) defines cordectomy Types I-VI based on depth and extent of resection
4. Steiner's piecemeal technique allows cutting through tumour to assess deep margins — this is oncologically safe when the final deep margin is clear
5. NBI enhances mucosal visualisation and helps delineate tumour margins; IPCL classification (Ni) distinguishes benign from malignant vascular patterns
6. TLM for T1a glottic cancer: local control 85-90%, larynx preservation 95-98%, DSS 96-98%
7. Endoscopic supraglottic laryngectomy: feasible for T1-T2 supraglottic SCC with adequate exposure; aspiration is the main complication (temporary in most)
8. TLM for hypopharyngeal cancer requires strict selection: piriform sinus medial wall, T1-T2, no apex invasion
9. Specimen orientation, inking, and frozen sections are essential for accurate margin assessment after piecemeal TLM
10. Salvage TLM after RT: feasible for rT1-rT2; local control 50-70%; higher complication rate; close follow-up essential
