Sadri et al. ## Laryngeal Leukoplakia

### Definition

**Laryngeal leukoplakia** is a clinical (descriptive) term for a **white patch or plaque on the laryngeal mucosa** that cannot be rubbed off and cannot be classified clinically as any other defined lesion.

**Key point:** Leukoplakia is a **clinical diagnosis**, not a histological one. It encompasses a spectrum from benign hyperkeratosis to invasive squamous cell carcinoma.

### Clinical Appearance

| Morphology | Description | Malignant Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Homogeneous (flat/smooth)** | Uniformly white, thin, smooth surface | Low (5-10%) |
| **Verrucous (warty)** | Raised, irregular, papillomatous surface | Moderate (10-20%) |
| **Speckled (erythroleukoplakia)** | White patch with red areas interspersed | **High (25-40%)** |
| **Nodular** | White with raised nodules or excrescences | High (20-30%) |
| **Ulcerated** | White patch with areas of ulceration | **Highest** — suspect invasive SCC |

### Differential Diagnosis of White Laryngeal Lesion

| Condition | Features | Histology |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple hyperkeratosis** | Thin, flat, uniform white patch | Thickened keratin layer; no dysplasia |
| **Dysplasia (mild-severe)** | Variable appearance; may be verrucous or speckled | Architectural and cytological atypia |
| **Carcinoma in situ (CIS)** | Often indistinguishable from dysplasia clinically | Full-thickness atypia; intact basement membrane |
| **Invasive SCC** | Ulcerated, irregular, firm on palpation | Invasion through basement membrane |
| **Candidiasis** | White patches that can be scraped off; immunosuppressed patients | Fungal hyphae (PAS stain) |
| **Pachydermia laryngis** | Thickened, rough mucosa; posterior glottis; associated with LPR | Epithelial thickening; typically no dysplasia |
| **Vocal cord cyst/sulcus** | Submucosal white appearance; affects mucosal wave | Submucosal pathology; not true leukoplakia |

## Histological Classification of Laryngeal Dysplasia

### WHO 2017 Classification (Three-Tier)

The WHO 2017 classification (4th edition) uses a simplified three-tier system:

| Grade | Histological Features | Malignant Transformation Rate |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild dysplasia** | Architectural disturbance and cytological atypia limited to the **lower third** of the epithelium | **1-2%** |
| **Moderate dysplasia** | Atypia extending into the **middle third** of the epithelium | **5-15%** |
| **Severe dysplasia / CIS** | Atypia involving the **upper third or full thickness** (CIS = full thickness with intact basement membrane) | **15-30%** |

### Ljubljana Classification (Four-Tier)

| Grade | Equivalent (WHO) | Description | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Squamous (simple) hyperplasia** | No dysplasia | Increased cell layers; no atypia; orderly maturation | Benign; no increased risk |
| **Basal/parabasal cell hyperplasia** | Mild dysplasia | Increased basal/parabasal cell layers; minimal atypia | Low risk |
| **Atypical hyperplasia** (risky epithelium) | Moderate-severe dysplasia | Significant architectural and cytological atypia | **High risk** |
| **Carcinoma in situ** | CIS | Full-thickness atypia; intact basement membrane | **Highest risk** |

### Comparison of Classification Systems

| WHO 2017 (3-tier) | Ljubljana (4-tier) | SIN System (3-tier) | Risk Category |
|---|---|---|---|
| No dysplasia | Squamous hyperplasia | — | Benign |
| Mild dysplasia | Basal/parabasal hyperplasia | SIN 1 | **Low-risk SIL** |
| Moderate dysplasia | Atypical hyperplasia | SIN 2 | **High-risk SIL** |
| Severe dysplasia / CIS | Atypical hyperplasia / CIS | SIN 3 | **High-risk SIL** |

**Note:** The Ljubljana "atypical hyperplasia" category spans both moderate and severe dysplasia in the WHO system, reflecting the clinical reality that distinguishing moderate from severe dysplasia has poor inter-observer reproducibility.

### Malignant Transformation Rates by Grade

| Dysplasia Grade | Transformation Rate | Median Time to Transformation |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | 1-2% | Variable (years) |
| **Moderate** | 5-15% | 3-5 years |
| **Severe** | 15-25% | 1-3 years |
| **CIS** | 20-30% | 1-2 years |
| **Overall (all grades)** | ~8% (Karatayli-Ozgursoy 2015) | Variable |

## Risk Factors for Laryngeal Dysplasia and Cancer

### Primary Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Mechanism | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Smoking** | **Strongest risk factor** — >90% of laryngeal SCC patients are smokers; carcinogens (PAH, nitrosamines) cause DNA damage, p53 mutations, field cancerisation | Strong |
| **Alcohol** | Synergistic with smoking (multiplicative risk); direct mucosal irritant; acetaldehyde is a carcinogen | Strong |
| **GERD/LPR** | See detailed section below | Moderate (cofactor) |
| **Occupational exposures** | Asbestos, wood dust, cement dust, nickel, sulphuric acid fumes | Moderate |
| **HPV** | HPV 6, 11 associated with laryngeal papillomatosis (benign); HPV 16 rarely implicated in laryngeal SCC (unlike oropharynx) | Weak for larynx |

See also: [[Answers/General/Tobacco Carcinogenesis|Tobacco Carcinogenesis]]

## GERD and Laryngeal Cancer

### Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) and Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR)

| Feature | GERD | LPR |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Reflux of gastric contents into oesophagus | Reflux of gastric contents **above the upper oesophageal sphincter** into the pharynx and larynx |
| **Predominant symptom** | Heartburn, regurgitation | **Hoarseness**, throat clearing, globus, chronic cough |
| **Timing** | Predominantly nocturnal (supine) | Predominantly **upright/daytime** |
| **Oesophagitis** | Often present | Usually **absent** |
| **pH monitoring** | Distal probe positive | **Proximal (pharyngeal) probe** positive |

### Koufman's Contribution (1991)

Koufman's seminal 1991 study established the concept of **reflux laryngitis**:

- Studied 225 patients using ambulatory 24-hour double-probe pH monitoring
- Found that **62% of patients** with laryngeal/voice disorders had abnormal pharyngeal acid exposure
- Animal experiments showed that intermittent exposure to acid and pepsin (even 3 times/week) produced laryngeal mucosal damage:
  - **Acid + pepsin** → most severe injury (ulceration, granulation tissue)
  - **Acid alone** → moderate injury
  - **Pepsin alone at pH >4** → mild injury (but pepsin remains active up to pH 6-7)

### Mechanism: How LPR May Contribute to Laryngeal Carcinogenesis

```
Gastric acid + pepsin
        ↓
Refluxate reaches larynx (through UES)
        ↓
Laryngeal mucosa exposure
(larynx has minimal protective mechanisms
 compared to oesophagus — no peristalsis,
 no bicarbonate secretion, thin mucus layer)
        ↓
Chronic inflammation
        ↓
Molecular changes:
• NF-κB activation → pro-inflammatory cytokines
• COX-2 upregulation → prostaglandin synthesis
• Oxidative stress → reactive oxygen species → DNA damage
• Pepsin internalised into laryngeal epithelial cells
  (even at pH 7) → activates intracellular signalling
        ↓
Epithelial hyperplasia → Dysplasia → Carcinoma
(multi-step carcinogenesis)
```

### Pepsin — The Key Mediator

Recent research has highlighted the role of **pepsin** (not just acid) in laryngeal injury:

| Finding | Significance |
|---|---|
| Pepsin detected in laryngeal tissue of LPR patients | Direct evidence of refluxate reaching the larynx |
| Pepsin remains active at pH up to 6-7 | Damage occurs even in **non-acid** or **weakly acid** reflux |
| Pepsin can be **internalised** by laryngeal epithelial cells | Intracellular damage pathway — independent of extracellular acid |
| Pepsin at pH 7 causes **mitochondrial damage** and **DNA fragmentation** | Provides molecular mechanism for carcinogenesis |
| Pepsin activates **NF-κB** and upregulates **IL-8, IL-1β** | Pro-inflammatory and pro-carcinogenic signalling |

### Epidemiological Evidence

| Study Type | Findings | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Case-control studies** | Increased prevalence of GERD/LPR in laryngeal cancer patients | Supports association |
| **Population-based studies** | Mixed results — some show increased risk with GERD diagnosis; others do not after controlling for smoking/alcohol | **Confounding** by tobacco/alcohol is a major limitation |
| **Meta-analyses** | Modest increased risk (OR 1.5-2.0) for laryngeal cancer with GERD | Suggestive but not conclusive |
| **Mechanistic studies** | Pepsin, acid, and oxidative stress cause molecular changes consistent with carcinogenesis | Biologically plausible |

### The Debate: Causation vs Association

| Evidence Supporting Causation | Evidence Against Independent Causation |
|---|---|
| Biologically plausible mechanism (pepsin/acid → DNA damage) | Most GERD/LPR patients never develop laryngeal cancer |
| Pepsin found in laryngeal tissue of cancer patients | Cannot exclude confounding by smoking/alcohol in epidemiological studies |
| Animal models show laryngeal injury with acid/pepsin | No prospective randomised evidence that anti-reflux treatment prevents cancer |
| Temporal relationship (chronic LPR precedes cancer) | GERD prevalence is 20-40% in general population; laryngeal cancer is rare |

### Current Consensus

**GERD/LPR is likely a cofactor** in laryngeal carcinogenesis, acting synergistically with smoking and alcohol rather than as an independent causative agent. The strongest evidence supports LPR as a **promoter** of carcinogenesis in patients already exposed to primary carcinogens (tobacco, alcohol).

### Clinical Implications

1. **Screen for LPR** in patients with laryngeal leukoplakia/dysplasia — Reflux Symptom Index (RSI), Reflux Finding Score (RFS)
2. **Anti-reflux therapy** — empiric PPI (twice daily for 3-6 months) in patients with suspected LPR and laryngeal dysplasia
3. **Lifestyle modification** — head of bed elevation, dietary modifications (avoid late meals, caffeine, alcohol)
4. **Anti-reflux treatment alone does NOT replace cancer surveillance** — patients with dysplasia need continued endoscopic follow-up regardless of LPR treatment

## Management of Diffuse Leukoplakia with Grade III (Severe) Dysplasia

This is the core topic of Larynx Q93. The management of diffuse leukoplakia with severe dysplasia (Grade III / high-risk SIL / atypical hyperplasia) requires a systematic approach.

### Initial Evaluation

| Step | Detail |
|---|---|
| **History** | Smoking history (pack-years), alcohol use, voice changes, dysphagia, GERD symptoms, occupational exposures |
| **Flexible laryngoscopy** | Document extent, morphology (homogeneous vs speckled vs verrucous), bilateral involvement |
| **Stroboscopy** | Assess mucosal wave — absent/reduced wave suggests deeper invasion |
| **NBI (Narrow Band Imaging)** | Delineate superficial mucosal extent; identify satellite lesions and intraepithelial papillary capillary loops (IPCL); NBI enhances detection of dysplastic mucosa beyond white-light visualisation |
| **CT/MRI** | Usually not needed for pure dysplasia/CIS unless suspicion of invasion (abnormal stroboscopy, bulky lesion, impaired mobility) |

See also: [[Answers/General/Narrow Band Imaging|NBI]]

### Surgical Management

#### Excisional Biopsy — The Standard Approach

| Technique | Description | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cold instrument excision** | Microflap technique under operating microscope; subepithelial dissection | Best tissue preservation for histology; precise plane of dissection | Technically demanding |
| **CO2 laser excision** | Laser vaporisation/excision of the mucosa (ELS Type I-II cordectomy) | Haemostatic; good visualisation; can be precise | Thermal artefact at margins; may obscure histology |
| **KTP laser** | Angiolytic laser — targets submucosal vasculature | Office-based potential; good for superficial lesions | Less tissue for histology (photoangiolytic effect) |

#### Approach for Diffuse Leukoplakia

When leukoplakia is **diffuse** (involving both cords, supraglottis, or extensive glottic surface):

1. **NBI-guided mapping** — use NBI to delineate the true extent of dysplastic mucosa before excision
2. **Staged excision** — excise one side at a time to prevent anterior glottic web formation
   - First stage: Complete excision of one cord + anterior commissure if involved
   - Wait 4-6 weeks for healing
   - Second stage: Excision of contralateral cord
3. **Deep biopsies** — must sample the basement membrane zone to **rule out microinvasion** within the dysplastic field
4. **Complete mucosal stripping** — for diffuse involvement, strip the entire affected mucosa to the level of the vocal ligament
5. **Specimen orientation** — mark the specimen for the pathologist (anterior, posterior, superior, inferior margins)

#### Critical Points in Surgical Management

| Principle | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Excision, not just biopsy** | Incisional biopsy may miss invasive foci within a diffuse dysplastic field; excisional biopsy is both diagnostic and therapeutic |
| **Deep biopsies at multiple sites** | Severe dysplasia may harbour foci of microinvasion — especially in speckled or verrucous areas |
| **Staged bilateral excision** | Simultaneous bilateral cord stripping risks **anterior glottic web** — a devastating complication |
| **NBI-guided margins** | Standard white-light visualisation underestimates the extent of dysplasia; NBI improves margin assessment |
| **Frozen section** | Consider intraoperative frozen section if a suspicious area is encountered to guide extent of excision |

### Post-Excision Surveillance

| Time Interval | Frequency | Method |
|---|---|---|
| **First 6 months** | Every 6-8 weeks | Flexible laryngoscopy + stroboscopy |
| **6-24 months** | Every 3 months | Flexible laryngoscopy + NBI |
| **2-5 years** | Every 6 months | Flexible laryngoscopy |
| **Beyond 5 years** | Annually | Flexible laryngoscopy |
| **Any new suspicious lesion** | Immediate | Microlaryngoscopy + biopsy |

### If Recurrent or Persistent Severe Dysplasia

| Scenario | Management |
|---|---|
| **Focal recurrence** | Repeat laser excision (ELS Type I-II) |
| **Diffuse recurrence** | Repeat staged stripping; consider deeper excision (ELS Type III — transmuscular) |
| **Multiple recurrences (>3)** | Consider radiotherapy (definitive — 60-66 Gy) |
| **Progression to invasive SCC** | Manage as glottic cancer by T-stage — TLM, RT, or open surgery |

See also: [[Answers/Larynx/Early Glottic Cancer Management|Early Glottic Cancer]], [[Answers/Larynx/Laser Surgery in Laryngeal Cancer|Laser Surgery]]

## Molecular Markers for Progression Risk

| Marker | Significance | Clinical Utility |
|---|---|---|
| **p53 mutation/overexpression** | Most commonly mutated gene in laryngeal SCC; p53 overexpression in dysplasia associated with higher transformation risk | Moderate — IHC widely available |
| **Ki-67 (MIB-1)** | Proliferation marker; high Ki-67 index in dysplasia correlates with malignant transformation | Moderate — quantitative assessment |
| **LOH at 3p and 9p** | Loss of heterozygosity at chromosomes 3p14 and 9p21 — early events in head and neck carcinogenesis | High — strong predictor of progression (requires molecular analysis) |
| **LOH at 17p** | p53 locus — loss associated with advanced dysplasia and progression | High — but technically demanding |
| **DNA aneuploidy** | Abnormal DNA content — indicates genomic instability | Moderate — flow cytometry or image cytometry |
| **EGFR overexpression** | Epidermal growth factor receptor — increased in dysplasia progressing to SCC | Moderate |
| **Cyclin D1 overexpression** | Cell cycle regulator — amplified in a subset of progressing lesions | Low — not yet standard |

**Current status:** Molecular markers are **not standard clinical practice** for routine dysplasia management but are increasingly used in research settings to stratify risk. LOH at 3p/9p is the strongest molecular predictor of malignant transformation.

## Chemoprevention

| Agent | Evidence | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Retinoids (13-cis-retinoic acid)** | Hong et al. (1986) — partial responses in oral leukoplakia; subsequent studies showed high relapse on cessation and significant toxicity | **Not standard** — limited evidence in laryngeal dysplasia specifically |
| **Beta-carotene** | ATBC and CARET trials — no benefit; possible increased lung cancer risk in smokers | **Not recommended** |
| **Celecoxib (COX-2 inhibitor)** | Theoretical rationale (COX-2 upregulated in dysplasia); limited clinical data | Experimental |
| **EGFR inhibitors** | Preclinical rationale; no established clinical role in dysplasia | Experimental |

**Bottom line:** No chemopreventive agent has proven efficacy for laryngeal dysplasia. **Smoking cessation remains the single most important intervention** to reduce progression and recurrence.

## Counselling and Risk Reduction

| Intervention | Priority | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Smoking cessation** | **HIGHEST** — most important modifiable risk factor; reduces recurrence and progression risk | Strong |
| **Alcohol reduction** | High — synergistic carcinogen with tobacco | Strong |
| **Anti-reflux therapy** | Moderate — PPI + lifestyle modification if LPR suspected | Moderate |
| **Voice hygiene** | Supportive — reduce vocal abuse/misuse | Expert opinion |
| **Dietary modification** | Supportive — fruits, vegetables, antioxidants | Weak evidence |
| **Regular follow-up** | **ESSENTIAL** — lifelong surveillance for recurrence, new dysplasia, or progression | Strong |

## Key Exam Points

1. Laryngeal leukoplakia is a **clinical** diagnosis; histological grade determines management — ranges from benign hyperkeratosis to CIS/invasive SCC
2. **WHO 2017 three-tier classification** (mild/moderate/severe-CIS) is the current standard; Ljubljana classification (squamous hyperplasia/basal-parabasal hyperplasia/atypical hyperplasia/CIS) has the best prognostic data
3. Malignant transformation rates: mild 1-2%, moderate 5-15%, severe/CIS 15-30%; overall approximately 8% (Karatayli-Ozgursoy 2015)
4. **GERD/LPR** is a cofactor in laryngeal carcinogenesis — acts through pepsin-mediated mucosal damage, NF-kappaB activation, and oxidative stress; Koufman (1991) coined "reflux laryngitis"
5. **Pepsin** is active even at neutral pH (up to pH 7) and can be internalised by laryngeal epithelial cells — explains damage in non-acid reflux
6. Diffuse leukoplakia with Grade III dysplasia: **staged excisional biopsy** (one cord at a time to prevent web) with **NBI-guided mapping** and **deep biopsies** to rule out microinvasion
7. Post-excision surveillance: every 6-8 weeks for 6 months, then every 3 months for 2 years, then every 6 months
8. **Smoking cessation** is the single most important intervention — more impactful than any surgical or medical treatment for preventing progression
9. Molecular markers (p53, Ki-67, LOH at 3p/9p) can predict progression risk but are not yet standard clinical practice
10. No chemopreventive agent (retinoids, beta-carotene) has proven efficacy for laryngeal dysplasia
