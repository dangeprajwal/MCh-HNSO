## Overview

Subglottic and tracheal stenosis represents a spectrum of conditions characterised by narrowing of the airway at or below the level of the glottis. The most common aetiology in adults is **post-intubation injury**, accounting for approximately 90% of acquired cases. The clinical significance lies in the potentially life-threatening nature of airway compromise and the complexity of surgical management, which requires careful assessment and individualised treatment planning.

## Etiology

### Post-Intubation Stenosis (Most Common)

- **Mechanism:** Ischaemic necrosis of tracheal mucosa at the cuff site due to cuff pressure exceeding capillary perfusion pressure (>30 mmHg)
- **Cuff site stenosis:** Most common location -- at the level of the endotracheal tube cuff
- **Tube tip injury:** Ulceration at the point of contact of the tube tip with the tracheal wall
- **Subglottic stenosis:** Occurs at the level of the cricoid cartilage, the narrowest portion of the adult airway
- **Risk factors:** Prolonged intubation (>7-10 days), oversized tube, excessive cuff pressure, traumatic intubation, repeated intubation, hypotension, sepsis, diabetes, steroid use, GORD

### Post-Tracheostomy Stenosis

| Site | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Stomal stenosis | Granulation tissue at tracheostomy site |
| Suprastomal stenosis | Erosion of anterior tracheal wall by cannula tip or high-pressure cuff |
| Cuff-site stenosis | Ischaemic injury at cuff level (same as intubation) |
| Subglottic stenosis | Upward erosion by cannula into subglottis |

### Other Causes

- **Post-radiation:** Fibrosis and chondronecrosis leading to luminal narrowing
- **Idiopathic subglottic stenosis:** Predominantly affects middle-aged women; possible hormonal/autoimmune aetiology
- **Autoimmune:** Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA, formerly Wegener's), relapsing polychondritis, sarcoidosis, amyloidosis
- **Post-surgical:** After laryngeal surgery, cricotracheal resection, or prior airway reconstruction
- **Traumatic:** Blunt or penetrating laryngotracheal trauma
- **Neoplastic:** Benign (papilloma, haemangioma) or malignant (SCC, adenoid cystic carcinoma)
- **Infectious:** Tuberculosis, fungal infections, diphtheria (historically)

## Pathophysiology

The progression from mucosal injury to established stenosis follows a predictable sequence:

1. **Mucosal ischaemia** -- Pressure necrosis from tube or cuff exceeding capillary perfusion pressure
2. **Ulceration** -- Loss of mucosal integrity with exposure of underlying cartilage
3. **Perichondritis** -- Inflammation extends to cartilaginous framework
4. **Granulation tissue** -- Exuberant inflammatory response with vascular proliferation
5. **Fibrosis** -- Mature scar tissue contracts circumferentially
6. **Cicatricial stenosis** -- Fixed narrowing with loss of cartilaginous support

The **critical factor** is the duration and degree of ischaemia. Once the cartilaginous framework is involved, the stenosis becomes mature and unlikely to resolve without intervention.

## Myer-Cotton Classification

The Cotton-Myer grading system (1994) is the universally accepted classification for subglottic stenosis based on the percentage of airway obstruction:

| Grade | % Obstruction | Description | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | 0-50% | Mild | Often asymptomatic; may not require intervention |
| II | 51-70% | Moderate | Dyspnoea on exertion; may be managed endoscopically |
| III | 71-99% | Severe | Stridor at rest; near-complete obstruction; requires intervention |
| IV | 100% | Complete obstruction | No detectable lumen; requires tracheostomy |

### Key Points
- Assessment is based on **endoscopy** -- the smallest endotracheal tube that can be passed through the stenosis is compared to the age-appropriate tube size
- The grade determines the treatment algorithm
- The classification applies specifically to the subglottic region but is commonly extrapolated to tracheal stenosis

## Assessment

### History

- Duration of intubation and timing of symptom onset
- Nature of symptoms: stridor (inspiratory suggests supraglottic/glottic; biphasic suggests subglottic/tracheal), dyspnoea, voice changes, cough
- Previous airway interventions
- Comorbidities: diabetes, GORD, immunosuppression, autoimmune conditions
- Smoking and alcohol history

### Clinical Examination

- **Stridor assessment:** Inspiratory, expiratory, or biphasic
- **Voice quality:** Hoarseness suggests glottic involvement
- **Neck examination:** Presence of tracheostomy, surgical scars, neck masses

### Investigations

| Investigation | Role |
|---|---|
| Flexible nasolaryngoscopy | First-line assessment; evaluates supraglottis, glottis, vocal fold mobility, subglottis |
| CT airway (with 3D reconstruction) | Defines location, length, and degree of stenosis; relationship to surrounding structures |
| MRI | Soft tissue detail; rarely needed |
| Rigid bronchoscopy | Gold standard for assessment; allows measurement and biopsy; often combined with treatment |
| Pulmonary function tests (PFTs) | Flow-volume loop: characteristic flattening of inspiratory limb (extrathoracic) or expiratory limb (intrathoracic); fixed obstruction shows flattening of both limbs |
| LEMG (laryngeal EMG) | For bilateral vocal fold immobility -- distinguishes neuropathic from mechanical fixation |

### Flow-Volume Loop Patterns

- **Extrathoracic fixed stenosis:** Flattening of both inspiratory and expiratory limbs
- **Extrathoracic variable stenosis:** Flattening of the inspiratory limb (negative intrathoracic pressure collapses the extrathoracic segment during inspiration)
- **Intrathoracic variable stenosis:** Flattening of the expiratory limb
- The **Empey index** (FEV1/PEF) >10 mL/L/min suggests upper airway obstruction

## Management Algorithm by Grade

### Grade I (0-50% Obstruction)

- **Observation** in most cases
- Serial endoscopic surveillance
- Medical management of contributing factors (anti-reflux therapy, humidification)
- Intervention only if symptomatic

### Grade II (51-70% Obstruction)

- **Endoscopic management** is first-line:
  - Balloon dilation
  - Laser incision (CO2 or KTP)
  - Cold knife incision
  - Topical mitomycin-C application (0.4 mg/mL for 2-4 minutes)
  - Intralesional steroid injection (triamcinolone 40 mg/mL)
- May require repeated procedures
- Consider open surgery if endoscopic management fails after 2-3 attempts

### Grade III (71-99% Obstruction)

- **Open surgical management** usually required:
  - **Tracheal resection and anastomosis** (Grillo technique) for tracheal stenosis
  - **Laryngotracheal reconstruction (LTR)** for subglottic stenosis
  - **Cricotracheal resection (CTR)** for high subglottic stenosis
- Endoscopic treatment may be attempted as initial approach if stenosis is short (<1 cm), web-like, and without cartilage loss
- If unfit for open surgery: long-term stenting or serial dilations

### Grade IV (Complete Obstruction)

- **Tracheostomy** (if not already present) for immediate airway
- Definitive open surgery when stable:
  - Tracheal resection and anastomosis
  - LTR with cartilage grafting
  - CTR
- Long-term tracheostomy if patient is not a surgical candidate

## Endoscopic Techniques

### Balloon Dilation

- **Technique:** Controlled radial expansion (CRE) balloon or angioplasty-type balloon
- **Advantages:** Applies even radial force; less mucosal trauma than rigid dilation
- **Indications:** Soft, immature stenosis; adjunct to other endoscopic procedures
- **Limitations:** High recurrence rate as sole treatment (40-60%)

### Laser Treatment

| Laser | Wavelength | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| CO2 | 10,600 nm | Precise cutting; minimal thermal damage | Requires line-of-sight; cannot pass through flexible scope |
| KTP | 532 nm | Fibre-delivered; good for vascular lesions | More thermal spread |
| Diode | 980 nm | Fibre-delivered; portable | Less precise |
| Nd:YAG | 1,064 nm | Deep penetration; good haemostasis | Excessive thermal damage |

- **Technique:** Radial incisions at 3, 9, and 12 o'clock positions followed by balloon dilation
- CO2 laser is most commonly used for airway stenosis

### Cold Knife Techniques

- Radial incisions using sickle knife or micro-scissors
- Less thermal damage than laser
- Often combined with balloon dilation

### Adjuncts

- **Mitomycin-C:** Topical application (0.4 mg/mL for 2-4 minutes); inhibits fibroblast proliferation; reduces restenosis
- **Intralesional steroids:** Triamcinolone acetonide (40 mg/mL) injected into scar; reduces inflammation and fibrosis
- **Drug-eluting stents:** Under investigation

### Stenting

| Type | Material | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| Silicone (Dumon, Montgomery T-tube) | Silicone | Easy to remove/reposition; biocompatible | Migration; granulation at ends |
| Metallic (Ultraflex, Wallstent) | Nitinol/stainless steel | Self-expanding; thin wall | Difficult removal; granulation tissue ingrowth |
| Bioabsorbable | Polydioxanone | No need for removal | Limited availability; variable absorption |

- **Montgomery T-tube:** Useful for subglottic stenosis; provides stenting while allowing phonation and breathing through the upper airway
- **Indications for stenting:** Bridge to definitive surgery; palliation; patients unfit for open surgery
- Stents should generally be **temporary** -- long-term stenting is associated with granulation, migration, and infection

## Open Surgery: Tracheal Resection and Anastomosis

### Grillo Technique (Gold Standard for Tracheal Stenosis)

#### Principles
- **Maximum resectable length:** 4-6 cm (approximately 50% of tracheal length) in adults
- The trachea has limited ability to regenerate; the length of resection is the critical determinant of outcome
- **Tension-free anastomosis** is the single most important factor for success

#### Preoperative Assessment
- CT airway with 3D reconstruction to determine stenosis length and location
- Bronchoscopy to assess distal airway and confirm extent of disease
- Assessment of fitness for single-lung ventilation

#### Surgical Steps
1. **Cervical collar incision** (or with partial sternotomy for distal lesions)
2. Identification and preservation of **recurrent laryngeal nerves**
3. Circumferential dissection of the trachea limited to the stenotic segment (to preserve blood supply)
4. Transection of the trachea below the stenosis; intubation of the distal trachea across the surgical field
5. Transection above the stenosis; resection of the stenotic segment
6. **End-to-end anastomosis** with interrupted absorbable sutures (4-0 Vicryl or PDS)
7. Sutures placed through the full thickness of the tracheal wall with knots tied on the outside
8. Posterior membranous wall anastomosed first, then cartilaginous rings

#### Release Manoeuvres (to Reduce Tension)

| Manoeuvre | Technique | Length Gained |
|---|---|---|
| **Suprahyoid release** (Montgomery) | Division of suprahyoid muscles and thyrohyoid membrane; larynx drops caudally | 1-2 cm |
| **Hilar release** (Dedo-Fishman) | Inferior pulmonary ligament division and pericardial release around hilum | 1-2 cm |
| **Neck flexion** (guardian stitch) | Chin-to-chest suture maintained for 7-10 days postoperatively | 1-2 cm |

#### Postoperative Care
- **Guardian suture:** Chin-to-chest stitch to prevent neck extension for 7-10 days
- Avoid re-intubation if possible (risk of disrupting anastomosis)
- Flexible laryngoscopy to monitor for granulation
- PPI for GORD prophylaxis

#### Outcomes
- **Success rate:** 93-95% good to excellent results in experienced centres (Grillo series: 93.7%)
- **Mortality:** 1-2.4%
- **Complications:** Suture line granulations (9.7%), wound infection (3%), anastomotic dehiscence (<1%), restenosis (3-5%), RLN injury (2-4%)

## Laryngotracheal Reconstruction (LTR)

### Indications
- **Subglottic stenosis** where the cricoid cartilage is involved
- Stenosis with **cartilaginous framework loss**
- Failed endoscopic management
- Paediatric airway stenosis (most common indication in children)

### Technique

#### Anterior Cartilage Graft
- **Graft source:** Costal cartilage (rib), auricular cartilage, or thyroid ala
- Anterior cricoid split or midline laryngofissure
- Cartilage graft placed in the anterior cricoid defect to expand the lumen
- Secured with sutures; stented temporarily (with Montgomery T-tube or endotracheal tube)

#### Posterior Cartilage Graft
- For **posterior glottic/subglottic stenosis** or interarytenoid scarring
- Posterior cricoid lamina is split in the midline
- Costal cartilage graft interposed to widen the posterior airway
- More technically demanding; risk of injury to the party wall between the airway and oesophagus

#### Single-Stage vs Two-Stage
- **Single-stage:** No postoperative stent; patient extubated; higher risk of graft displacement
- **Two-stage:** Temporary stent (T-tube or endotracheal tube) for 4-6 weeks; higher success rate

### Cricotracheal Resection (CTR)
- For **high subglottic stenosis** involving the cricoid
- Partial resection of the anterior cricoid arc with anastomosis of the trachea to the remaining posterior cricoid plate and thyroid cartilage
- Higher success rate than LTR for severe subglottic stenosis (90-95%)
- Risk of injury to recurrent laryngeal nerves at the cricothyroid joint

## Bilateral Abductor Paralysis

### Definition
Bilateral vocal fold immobility in the paramedian (adducted) position due to bilateral recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) injury or fixation.

### Etiology

| Category | Causes |
|---|---|
| Iatrogenic (most common) | Thyroidectomy (most frequent), parathyroidectomy, anterior cervical spine surgery, carotid endarterectomy, oesophageal surgery |
| Neurological | CVA, brainstem lesion, Arnold-Chiari malformation, multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's disease |
| Idiopathic | No identifiable cause; accounts for 10-20% |
| Malignant | Thyroid cancer, oesophageal cancer, lung cancer with mediastinal invasion |
| Traumatic | Blunt or penetrating neck trauma |
| Post-intubation | Cricoarytenoid joint ankylosis after prolonged intubation |

### Clinical Presentation
- **Biphasic stridor** -- hallmark symptom
- Dyspnoea, particularly on exertion
- **Voice is often near-normal** (both folds are in the midline, allowing good closure for phonation)
- Poor exercise tolerance
- Recurrent respiratory infections

### Assessment

1. **Flexible laryngoscopy:** Demonstrates bilateral immobile vocal folds in paramedian position
2. **Distinguish paralysis from fixation:**
   - **LEMG (Laryngeal Electromyography):** Neuropathic changes (fibrillation potentials, decreased recruitment) confirm nerve injury; normal motor unit potentials suggest cricoarytenoid joint fixation
   - **Palpation under GA:** Direct palpation of arytenoid joints to assess passive mobility
3. **CT/MRI:** To identify the cause (skull base to thoracic inlet)
4. **Blood tests:** Thyroid function, inflammatory markers, ANCA (if GPA suspected)

### Management Algorithm

#### Acute Setting (Within 6-12 Months of Onset)
- **Observation** is recommended for 6-12 months (if cause is potentially reversible, e.g., post-thyroidectomy)
- **Tracheostomy** if airway is severely compromised
- Serial LEMG to monitor for reinnervation (polyphasic potentials suggest recovery)
- LEMG at 6 months: if no evidence of reinnervation, likely permanent

#### Permanent Bilateral Abductor Paralysis

| Procedure | Technique | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Posterior cordotomy** | Laser (CO2) transection of the vocal fold at the vocal process; creates a posterior glottic gap | Preserves anterior vibratory segment; better voice quality | May not provide sufficient airway in severe cases |
| **Medial arytenoidectomy** | Laser removal of the medial surface of one arytenoid | Good airway improvement | More voice deterioration than cordotomy |
| **Total arytenoidectomy** | Complete removal of one arytenoid (endoscopic or external approach) | Maximum airway improvement | Significant voice deterioration; aspiration risk |
| **Suture lateralisation** | Suture passed through vocal fold and tied externally to lateralise one fold | Reversible; can be adjusted | Temporary; suture may cut through tissue |
| **Tracheostomy** | Permanent tracheostomy | Reliable airway; no voice compromise | Patient must live with stoma; social/psychological impact |

#### Posterior Cordotomy (Dennis and Kashima Technique)
- **Most commonly performed procedure** for bilateral abductor paralysis
- CO2 laser used to make a C-shaped incision through the posterior vocal fold at the level of the vocal process
- The cut extends from the vocal process medially to create a triangular gap in the posterior glottis
- **Unilateral** cordotomy is performed first; bilateral only if insufficient airway
- Results: adequate airway in 80-90%; acceptable voice preservation in most patients

#### Selective Reinnervation (Crumley Technique)
- **Principle:** Reinnervation of the posterior cricoarytenoid (PCA) muscle using a branch of the ansa cervicalis nerve
- The ansa cervicalis nerve branch is anastomosed to the adductor branch of the RLN or directly implanted into the PCA muscle
- **Goal:** Restore abductor function while maintaining the innervated adductor muscles for phonation
- **Limitations:** Takes 4-6 months for reinnervation; outcomes are variable; technically demanding
- Best suited for **young patients** with bilateral RLN injury and intact laryngeal framework

#### Laryngeal Pacing
- Emerging technology; electrical stimulation of the PCA muscle via implanted electrodes
- Synchronised with respiratory cycle to provide abduction during inspiration
- Still largely experimental

### Outcomes

- **Posterior cordotomy:** Decannulation rate 85-95%; voice quality maintained in 70-80%
- **Arytenoidectomy:** Decannulation rate 90-95%; voice deterioration in 30-50%
- **Reinnervation:** Variable results; 60-80% achieve improved airway without tracheostomy
- **Revision rates:** 10-20% require repeat procedure

## Stridor After Prolonged Intubation: Clinical Approach

When a patient presents with stridor 4 weeks after extubation following prolonged intubation (as in Q15):

### Differential Diagnosis
1. **Subglottic/tracheal stenosis** (most common)
2. **Bilateral vocal fold paralysis** (from compression neuropathy or arytenoid fixation)
3. **Posterior glottic stenosis** (interarytenoid scarring)
4. **Granulation tissue** at the site of tube/cuff contact
5. **Tracheomalacia** (loss of cartilaginous support)

### Step-wise Management
1. **Secure the airway** if in respiratory distress (heliox, nebulised adrenaline, intubation, tracheostomy)
2. **Flexible laryngoscopy** -- assess vocal fold mobility, subglottis
3. **CT airway** -- define extent and location of stenosis
4. **Rigid bronchoscopy under GA** -- definitive assessment, grading, and possible initial endoscopic treatment
5. **Definitive management** based on grade and pathology (as outlined above)

## Key Points for Exam

1. **Post-intubation injury** is the most common cause of acquired tracheal stenosis (cuff pressure >30 mmHg causes mucosal ischaemia)
2. **Myer-Cotton classification** grades subglottic stenosis from I to IV based on percentage obstruction
3. **Grade I-II:** Endoscopic management (dilation, laser, mitomycin-C, steroids)
4. **Grade III-IV:** Open surgery (tracheal resection and anastomosis or LTR)
5. **Grillo technique:** Maximum 4-6 cm resection; tension-free anastomosis is paramount; release manoeuvres (suprahyoid, hilar) gain additional length
6. **Guardian suture** (chin-to-chest) is maintained for 7-10 days postoperatively
7. **Bilateral abductor paralysis:** Wait 6-12 months if potentially reversible; LEMG guides prognosis
8. **Posterior cordotomy** (Dennis-Kashima) is the preferred procedure for permanent bilateral abductor paralysis -- best balance of airway and voice
9. **Flow-volume loop** shows flattening of the inspiratory limb in extrathoracic variable stenosis
10. **Cricotracheal resection** has a higher success rate (90-95%) than LTR for high-grade subglottic stenosis
