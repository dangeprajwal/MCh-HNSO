## Epidemiology

- **Second most common** primary skull base tumour after meningioma
- Accounts for ~6% of all skull base tumours
- Rare overall: approximately 200 new cases per year in the USA
- Peak incidence: **30–60 years** (younger than chordoma)
- Slight **male predominance**

### Sites at the Skull Base
1. **Petroclival junction** (50%) — most common; at synchondrosis between petrous and occipital bone
2. **Parasellar / sphenooccipital synchondrosis** (20–30%)
3. **Temporal bone** (15%)
4. Other: clivus, foramen magnum, sphenoid

## Histopathology

### WHO 2021 Classification — Skull Base Chondrosarcoma Types

| Type | Histology | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| **Conventional (Grade I–II)** | Hyaline or myxoid matrix; chondrocytes in lacunae; lobular architecture | Most common at skull base; Grade I = indolent; Grade II = intermediate |
| **Conventional (Grade III)** | Increased cellularity, nuclear atypia, mitoses | More aggressive; rare at skull base |
| **Mesenchymal** | Bimorphic — undifferentiated small round cells + islands of cartilage | Most aggressive; early distant metastasis; worst prognosis |
| **Clear cell** | Clear cytoplasm, glycogen-rich | Low grade; rare |
| **Dedifferentiated** | High-grade spindle cell sarcoma adjacent to conventional chondrosarcoma | Worst prognosis; very rare at skull base |

**Grade I (well-differentiated)** is by far the most common at the skull base — explains the relatively good prognosis with appropriate treatment.

## Distinction from Chordoma

This is a classic exam question — chondrosarcoma vs chordoma.

| Feature | Chondrosarcoma | Chordoma |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin** | Cartilaginous remnants at synchondroses | Notochordal remnants |
| **Location** | Petroclival junction (lateral to midline) | **Midline** (clivus, sacrum, spheno-occipital) |
| **Cells** | Chondrocytes in lacunae; lobular | **Physaliferous cells** (vacuolated, bubbly) |
| **Immunohistochemistry** | **S100+**, **brachyury negative**, EMA± | **S100+**, **brachyury+**, EMA+, CK+, vimentin+ |
| **Key IHC distinction** | Brachyury **negative** | Brachyury **positive** (most specific) |
| **Calcification pattern on CT** | "Rings and arcs" (chondroid matrix) | Amorphous/irregular |
| **MRI T2** | Markedly **hyperintense** (water in cartilage) | Moderately hyperintense |
| **Prognosis** | Better (5-yr OS 70–90% conventional) | Worse (5-yr OS 50–65% with treatment) |
| **Radiation response** | Excellent with proton beam | Good with proton beam (but less than chondrosarcoma) |

## Radiology

### CT
- Calcification in 50–70%: classic **"rings and arcs"** pattern of enchondral calcification
- Bone erosion at petroclival synchondrosis
- Soft tissue component

### MRI
- **T1**: isointense or hypointense to brain
- **T2**: markedly **hyperintense** — characteristic; due to high water content in cartilaginous matrix (helps distinguish from meningioma which is isointense)
- **Gadolinium**: heterogeneous enhancement; septal enhancement pattern
- Useful for delineating relationship to ICA, cavernous sinus, brainstem, optic nerves

## Clinical Presentation

The cranial nerve affected depends on the location:

| Location | CN Affected | Clinical Sign |
|---|---|---|
| **Petroclival** (most common) | **CN VI** (most common — long intradural course, Dorello's canal) | Diplopia (lateral gaze palsy) |
| Cavernous sinus | CN III, IV, V1, VI | Cavernous sinus syndrome |
| Clivus central | CN VI, corticospinal tracts | Diplopia, long-tract signs |
| Jugular foramen | CN IX, X, XI | Jugular foramen syndrome |
| Foramen magnum | CN XII, C1–C2 | Hypoglossal palsy, neck pain |

**CN VI palsy** (abducens; horizontal diplopia) is the most common presentation of skull base chondrosarcoma — caused by the nerve's long intradural path along the clivus in Dorello's canal, making it vulnerable to any clival or petroclival mass.

## Treatment

### 1. Surgery
**Goal**: maximal safe surgical resection (gross total or near-total resection whenever possible)
- GTR rarely achieved due to proximity to ICA, brainstem, cranial nerves
- Surgical approaches depend on location:
  - **Transsphenoidal/endoscopic endonasal**: central clival / petroclival
  - **Middle cranial fossa** (Kawase approach): petroclival — see [[Answers/Skull Base/Middle Cranial Fossa Approaches|Middle Cranial Fossa Approaches]]
  - **Infratemporal fossa** (Fisch): lateral skull base extension
  - **Far lateral/transcondylar**: foramen magnum extension
- **Subtotal resection + proton RT** achieves similar local control to GTR in many series

### 2. Proton Beam Radiotherapy
**Standard of care** as adjuvant (or definitive) radiotherapy for skull base chondrosarcoma.

**Why proton beam?**
- Skull base chondrosarcoma requires **high radiation doses** (60–74 CGE) for local control
- Immediately adjacent critical structures: optic apparatus, brainstem, spinal cord, temporal lobes, pituitary
- Conventional photon RT cannot safely deliver these doses without unacceptable toxicity
- Proton beam exploits the **Bragg peak**: dose is deposited at a precise depth, then falls sharply — minimal exit dose
- Allows dose escalation to tumour while sparing adjacent normal structures

**Standard protocol**: 74 CGE in 37 fractions (2 CGE/fraction) — well-established dose

For full discussion of proton beam physics and indications, see [[Answers/Radiotherapy and Chemotherapy/Proton Beam Therapy in Head and Neck Cancer|Proton Beam Therapy in HNC]].

### 3. Chemotherapy
- **Not standard** for conventional chondrosarcoma
- **Mesenchymal chondrosarcoma**: may respond to Ewing-like chemotherapy regimens (ifosfamide + etoposide, or Ewing protocols)
- Dedifferentiated: high-grade sarcoma chemotherapy; poor prognosis regardless

## Prognosis

| Type | 5-Year Local Control | 5-Year OS |
|---|---|---|
| Conventional Grade I–II (proton RT) | 80–99% | 70–90% |
| Conventional Grade III | 60–70% | 50–70% |
| Mesenchymal | 40–50% | 25–50% |
| Dedifferentiated | <30% | <20% |

**Prognostic factors for conventional chondrosarcoma**:
- Surgical resection extent (GTR > STR)
- Proton RT dose (74 CGE > lower doses)
- Grade (Grade I > Grade II)
- Location (petroclival resectable vs parasellar complex)

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Skull base chondrosarcoma most commonly arises at the petroclival junction (50%), presents with CN VI palsy (diplopia) due to the nerve's proximity to the clivus in Dorello's canal, and shows "rings and arcs" calcification on CT with marked T2 hyperintensity on MRI. The critical distinction from chordoma is brachyury immunohistochemistry (negative in chondrosarcoma, positive in chordoma) and location (chondrosarcoma is lateral at petroclival synchondrosis; chordoma is midline). Treatment is maximal surgical resection followed by proton beam radiotherapy (74 CGE/37 fractions), achieving 5-year local control of 80–99% for conventional Grade I–II disease.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Skull base chondrosarcoma is the **second most common primary skull base tumour** (after meningioma); petroclival junction is the most common site.
> 2. **Brachyury** is the gold-standard IHC distinguisher: positive in chordoma, **negative** in chondrosarcoma — ask for it in every undifferentiated midline/paramedian skull base tumour.
> 3. "Rings and arcs" calcification = **enchondral matrix** = chondrosarcoma; amorphous calcification = chordoma.
> 4. **T2 hyperintensity** on MRI is characteristic of chondrosarcoma; no other skull base tumour is as bright on T2 (cartilage matrix contains ~70% water).
> 5. CN VI palsy (horizontal diplopia) is the **most common initial symptom** — because Dorello's canal runs along the clivus where chondrosarcoma originates.
> 6. Proton beam at **74 CGE** is standard — the Bragg peak prevents brainstem/optic nerve toxicity that conventional RT cannot avoid at these doses.
> 7. **Mesenchymal chondrosarcoma** is the most aggressive subtype; behaves like Ewing sarcoma and may respond to Ewing protocols; entirely different prognosis from conventional.
> 8. In the absence of a proton beam centre, **heavy ion (carbon ion) radiotherapy** offers equivalent or superior physical dose distribution and is an accepted alternative.
