## Anterior Skull Base Anatomy

### Bony Components
The anterior cranial fossa floor is formed by three bones:
- **Frontal bone** — orbital plates (fovea ethmoidalis posteriorly)
- **Ethmoid bone** — cribriform plate (crista galli, olfactory foramina)
- **Sphenoid bone** — planum sphenoidale (anterior to chiasmatic sulcus), lesser wing, anterior clinoid process

### Key Structures

| Structure | Location | Surgical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Cribriform plate** | Midline ethmoid; thinnest part of skull base | Olfactory filaments (CN I) pass through; CSF fistula and esthesioneuroblastoma arise here |
| **Crista galli** | Midline projection superior to cribriform plate | Falx cerebri attachment; landmark for midline |
| **Fovea ethmoidalis** | Roof of ethmoid air cells; part of frontal bone | More lateral and stronger than cribriform plate; important in FESS |
| **Planum sphenoidale** | Flat surface of sphenoid anterior to chiasmatic sulcus | Roof of sphenoid sinus; transplanum corridor in EEES |
| **Orbital plate (frontal)** | Roof of orbit | Thinnest superiorly; involved in orbital roof tumours |
| **Anterior ethmoidal artery** | Crosses skull base in anterior ethmoidal canal | Must be identified/ligated in skull base surgery; runs 7 mm posterior to frontal recess |
| **Posterior ethmoidal artery** | Runs in roof of posterior ethmoid | ~12 mm anterior to optic canal; important danger zone |

### Dura Mater Attachments
The dura is tightly adherent to the **cribriform plate** (through olfactory filaments) and at the **crista galli**. This tight adherence means tumours invading the cribriform plate have dural involvement earlier than elsewhere.

### Olfactory Filaments
Approximately 20 bundles of olfactory nerve fibres (fila olfactoria) pass through the foramina of the cribriform plate. Division in craniofacial resection or tumour invasion results in **anosmia**.

## Extended Endoscopic Endonasal Surgery (EEES)

### Concept
Developed by Amin Kassam, Carl Snyderman, and Ricardo Carrau (Pittsburgh group) in the 2000s. EEES uses the nose as a natural corridor to access the entire ventral skull base from the crista galli to the C2 vertebra — without any facial incision or brain retraction.

### Four-Hand Binostril Technique
- Two surgeons, one on each side of the patient
- One surgeon holds the endoscope; the other uses two working instruments
- Enables simultaneous bipolar coagulation and suction — critical for haemostasis near ICA

### EEES Corridors (Rostrocaudal Axis)

| Corridor | From | To | Key Structures Accessed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Transcribriform** | Crista galli | Posterior to cribriform plate | Cribriform plate, olfactory groove, ACF floor |
| **Transplanum** | Tuberculum sellae | Planum sphenoidale | Suprasellar cistern, optic chiasm (from above) |
| **Transsellar** | Anterior wall sella | Posterior wall sella | Pituitary gland, diaphragma sellae |
| **Transclival** | Dorsum sellae | Foramen magnum | Basilar artery, brainstem (ventral) |
| **Trans-odontoid** | C1–C2 level | Odontoid process | Craniocervical junction |

### Indication Criteria for EEES over Open CFR
Midline lesions are ideally suited to EEES when:
1. Lesion is **midline or paramedian**
2. No extension lateral to **optic nerve**
3. No significant **cavernous sinus** or **ICA** encasement
4. No **lateral dural invasion** beyond the parasellar region
5. Surgeon has advanced endoscopic skills and neuronavigation

## Nasoseptal (Hadad-Bassagasteguy) Flap

### Anatomy
- **Mucoperiosteal/mucoperichondrial flap** of nasal septum
- Pedicle: **nasoseptal artery** (terminal branch of posterior septal artery, from sphenopalatine artery → branch of internal maxillary artery)
- Pedicle enters nasal cavity through **sphenopalatine foramen**, then runs along inferior nasal septum
- Harvest: incisions along floor of nose, along choana, superiorly along sphenoid face, and along dorsal septum

### Dimensions
- Average surface area: **12–14 cm²** — sufficient to cover most anterior skull base defects including cribriform plate and planum sphenoidale

### Technique
- Pedicle must be identified and preserved **before** drilling the sphenoid face
- Flap is rotated 180° to cover the defect
- Multi-layer closure: fat graft (inlay) → fascia lata → nasoseptal flap (onlay)
- Fibrin glue + nasal packing

### Indication
Primary reconstruction after:
- EEES for pituitary macroadenoma with significant suprasellar extension
- Transcribriform resection (olfactory groove meningioma, esthesioneuroblastoma)
- CSF fistula after previous surgery
- Clival tumour resection

### Alternatives When NSF Unavailable
(Prior septal surgery, prior sphenoid surgery, or tumor involving pedicle)
- Inferior turbinate flap (pedicle: anterior branch of inferior turbinate artery)
- Middle turbinate flap
- Free mucosal graft
- Pericranial flap via small frontal trephination

## Recent Advances in Endoscopic Skull Base Surgery

| Technology | Application | Advantage |
|---|---|---|
| **Intraoperative neuronavigation** | Real-time instrument tracking in 3D | Reduces orientation errors; see [[Answers/Skull Base/Neuronavigation in Skull Base Surgery\|Neuronavigation]] |
| **4K/3D endoscopes** | High-resolution stereoscopic vision | Depth perception; reduces reliance on hand-eye coordination |
| **Angled endoscopes** (45°, 70°) | Visualising lateral recesses, cavernous sinus | Avoids blind spots from 0° endoscope |
| **Exoscope** | Surgeon comfort; digital display | No direct eyepiece contact; ergonomic |
| **5-ALA fluorescence** | Tumour-specific fluorescence (glioma, some pituitary adenomas) | Improves extent of resection |
| **Intraoperative MRI** | Updating neuronavigation after brain shift | Corrects for brain shift during resection |
| **Ultrasonic aspirator (CUSA)** | Soft tissue tumour debulking | Reduces blood loss in highly vascular lesions |

## Complications of EEES

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **CSF leak** (most common) | 5–15% | Multi-layer closure; nasoseptal flap; lumbar drain |
| **Meningitis** | 1–5% | Prophylactic antibiotics; CSF leak control |
| **ICA injury** | ~1% | Most feared; immediate packing, Fogarty balloon; interventional radiology |
| **Anosmia** | Near-universal with transcribriform | Counsel pre-op; permanent if olfactory filaments divided |
| **Vision loss** | <1% | From optic nerve traction or ICA injury; neuronavigation reduces risk |
| **Pneumocephalus** | Rare | Tension pneumocephalus — needle aspiration via burr hole; avoid >30° head up position |
| **Epistaxis** | 2–5% | Usually from sphenopalatine territory; embolisation if severe |
| **Nasal crusting/synechia** | 20–30% | Regular saline irrigation; endoscopic debridement |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The anterior skull base consists of the cribriform plate, planum sphenoidale, and fovea ethmoidalis, with the cribriform plate being the thinnest and most vulnerable segment through which olfactory filaments pass. Extended Endoscopic Endonasal Surgery (EEES) uses a binostril four-hand technique to access the entire ventral skull base through defined corridors (transcribriform, transplanum, transsellar, transclival) without facial incision or brain retraction. The Hadad-Bassagasteguy nasoseptal flap, pedicled on the nasoseptal artery, is the workhorse reconstruction and has reduced post-EEES CSF leak rates from ~15% to ~5%.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The cribriform plate is the **thinnest part** of the anterior skull base (0.2 mm) and most susceptible to CSF fistula; it is also the origin of esthesioneuroblastoma.
> 2. The **nasoseptal artery** must be identified and preserved before any drilling of the sphenoid face — once cut, the Hadad flap cannot be used.
> 3. EEES is preferred over open CFR for **midline lesions without lateral extension beyond the optic nerve** — same oncological clearance, less morbidity.
> 4. The **anterior ethmoidal artery** crosses the skull base ~7 mm posterior to the frontal recess; the posterior ethmoidal artery is ~12 mm anterior to the optic canal — critical danger zones.
> 5. **Pneumocephalus** after EEES is managed by keeping the patient flat (not 30° head-up) and needle aspiration if tension develops.
> 6. ICA injury (~1%) is the most feared complication; surgical team must have a rehearsed protocol including immediate packing and interventional radiology on standby.
> 7. **5-ALA fluorescence** is most useful in gliomas and some adenomas but not yet standard for all skull base tumours.
> 8. Intraoperative MRI corrects for **brain shift** — a key limitation of standard neuronavigation that worsens as CSF is drained and tumour is resected.
