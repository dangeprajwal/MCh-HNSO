## Concept and Rationale

### The Problem with Transcranial Approaches
Traditional transcranial approaches to the anterior skull base (frontal craniotomy, orbitofrontal/transbasal approach) require:
1. Removal of frontal bone from the **calvarium** (top of skull)
2. **Frontal lobe retraction** (brain pulled posteriorly) to visualise the skull base floor
3. This retraction risks **frontal lobe contusion**, cerebral oedema, and neurocognitive sequelae

### The Subcranial Solution
Raveh's innovation: instead of approaching the skull base from **above** (via a craniotomy), remove the **floor** of the anterior cranial fossa as an osteotomy — accessing the skull base from **below**, without retracting the frontal lobes at all.

**Key principle**: *The frontal lobes are allowed to fall away from the skull base by gravity* when the anterior cranial floor is removed — no retractor required.

## Surgical Technique

### Patient Positioning
- Supine, head slightly extended (10–15°)
- Three-pin fixation (Mayfield) or gel horseshoe

### Incision
- **Bicoronal scalp flap** from preauricular to preauricular regions, across the vertex
- Pericranium elevated and **preserved as a separate layer** for later dural/skull base repair
- Scalp flap reflected anteriorly to orbital rims

### Osteotomy: The Frontonasoorbital Frame

The subcranial osteotomy removes a **composite bone segment** including:

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Supraorbital bar** | Horizontal cut through frontal bone above both orbits |
| **Nasofrontal junction** | Through nasion (nasofrontal suture area) |
| **Frontomaxillary suture** | Lateral orbital rims |
| **Nasal bones** | As required for inferior access |

This creates an **en bloc frontonasoorbital segment** that is removed as one piece.

### What Is Exposed
Once the frontonasoorbital frame is removed:
- The **entire anterior cranial fossa floor** is visible from crista galli → sella turcica → bilateral orbits → parasellar region
- The **dura** over the frontal lobes falls posteriorly under gravity — no brain retraction needed
- Direct visualisation of cribriform plate, planum sphenoidale, ethmoids, sphenoid sinus, bilateral orbital roofs

### Dural Repair
- Watertight primary dural closure or dural substitute
- Pericranial flap turned down to cover the skull base defect
- Multi-layer closure critical to prevent CSF fistula

### Reconstruction of the Osteotomy
- Frontonasoorbital bone segment replaced at end of procedure
- Fixed with titanium miniplates and screws
- Normal facial contour restored

## Exposure Compared to Other Anterior Skull Base Approaches

### Subcranial vs Transcranial Approaches

| Feature | Subcranial (Raveh) | Orbitofrontal/Transbasal (Derome) | Classic Bifrontal Craniotomy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Craniotomy** | No (floor osteotomy only) | Yes (bifrontal bone flap from calvarium) | Yes (large bifrontal) |
| **Brain retraction** | None — gravity fall of dura | Moderate — frontal lobe retracted posteriorly | Significant |
| **Frontal lobe risk** | Very low | Moderate | Higher |
| **Exposure** | Entire ACF floor, bilateral orbits | Similar but less panoramic | Limited without retraction |
| **Access to sella/parasellar** | Excellent | Good | Limited |
| **Best for** | Bilateral central lesions; large fibrous dysplasia | Midline lesions with moderate bilateral spread | Limited anterior skull base lesions |

### Subcranial vs EEES (Endoscopic)
- Subcranial approach is **open surgery** — indicated when:
  - Lesion is extensive with bilateral bony involvement
  - En bloc resection required (oncological)
  - EEES not feasible (lateral extension, major bony reconstruction needed)
- EEES preferred for midline lesions without lateral spread; see [[Answers/Skull Base/Anterior Skull Base Anatomy and Endoscopic Surgery|Anterior Skull Base and EEES]]

## Indications for the Raveh Subcranial Approach

1. **Extensive anterior skull base tumours** with bilateral spread:
   - Large olfactory groove meningioma
   - Fibrous dysplasia of the anterior cranial base
   - Fronto-orbital meningioma extending through floor
2. **Post-traumatic deformities**:
   - Frontobasilar fractures with CSF fistula and wide dural defects
   - Orbital dystopia correction
   - Complex nasofrontal fractures requiring osteotomy and reconstruction
3. **Extensive sinonasal malignancy** invading the entire cribriform plate and bilateral ethmoid complex
4. **Parasellar lesions** with anterior extension (craniopharyngioma, large sellar meningioma)
5. **Fibrous dysplasia** of the craniofacial skeleton with vision-threatening orbital narrowing

---

## Complications

| Complication | Notes |
|---|---|
| **CSF leak** | Most common; critical watertight dural and pericranial closure |
| **Anosmia** | Division of olfactory filaments; counsel pre-operatively |
| **Frontal hypoaesthesia** | Injury to supraorbital nerve during osteotomy; usually temporary |
| **Enophthalmos/diplopia** | Orbital involvement of osteotomy; careful periorbital handling |
| **Plate/hardware prominence** | Titanium miniplates visible if thin skin; exchangeable if symptomatic |
| **Frontal sinus complications** | Mucocele formation if frontal sinus opened; obliteration recommended |
| **Osteotomy non-union** | Rare; risk with previous radiotherapy |

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The Raveh subcranial approach, described in 1993, accesses the entire anterior cranial base by removing the frontonasoorbital bony frame as an osteotomy — without a formal craniotomy. The frontal lobes fall posteriorly under gravity, eliminating the need for brain retraction and avoiding the retraction injury inherent to transcranial approaches. It is ideally suited to bilateral, central anterior skull base lesions (large meningioma, fibrous dysplasia, post-traumatic deformities) where conventional bifrontal craniotomy would require unacceptable brain retraction. Key complications include CSF leak, anosmia from olfactory filament division, and temporary supraorbital hypoaesthesia.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. The subcranial approach removes the **floor** of the anterior cranial fossa, not the frontal calvarium — this is the conceptual distinction from all other transcranial approaches.
> 2. **No brain retraction** is the cardinal advantage — brain falls away under gravity when the skull base floor is removed.
> 3. The pericranium must be **elevated and preserved before the osteotomy** — it is the primary dural/skull base reconstruction material.
> 4. The frontonasoorbital segment is replaced at closure and **fixed with titanium miniplates** — excellent cosmetic restoration of facial contour.
> 5. The approach gives the **best bilateral exposure** of any anterior skull base approach — from crista galli to sella, including both orbits simultaneously.
> 6. **Anosmia is near-universal** when the cribriform plate is approached — the olfactory filaments are divided; counsel patients pre-operatively.
> 7. **Frontal sinus** encountered during the osteotomy should be exenterated and obliterated (with fat or osteoplastic flap) to prevent mucocele.
> 8. Compared with [[Answers/Skull Base/Craniofacial Resection|classic CFR]] (which combines craniotomy with nasal approach), the subcranial approach avoids entry into the calvarium entirely.
