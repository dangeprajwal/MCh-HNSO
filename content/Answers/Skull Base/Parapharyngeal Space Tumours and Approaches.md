## Parapharyngeal Space Anatomy

### Shape and Boundaries
The parapharyngeal space (PPS) is an **inverted pyramid**:
- **Apex**: at the hyoid bone
- **Base**: at the skull base (foramen ovale, foramen spinosum)
- **Medial wall**: superior constrictor muscle (pharyngobasilar fascia)
- **Lateral wall**: medial pterygoid muscle, parotid fascia (deep lobe of parotid)
- **Posterior wall**: prevertebral fascia
- **Superior boundary**: skull base

### The Styloid Process Divides PPS into Two Compartments

| | Prestyloid Compartment | Retrostyloid Compartment |
|---|---|---|
| **Position relative to styloid** | Anteromedial | Posterolateral |
| **Main contents** | Deep lobe parotid, minor salivary gland tissue, fat, ascending pharyngeal artery branches | Carotid artery (ICA), internal jugular vein, CN IX, X, XI, XII, sympathetic chain |
| **Common tumours** | Deep-lobe parotid pleomorphic adenoma (40–50%), minor salivary gland tumours, lipoma, branchial cyst | Vagal paraganglioma, schwannoma (CN IX–XII), sympathetic chain schwannoma |
| **Clinical sign** | Medial displacement of tonsil | Posteromedial displacement + cranial nerve palsies |

## Clinical Presentation

### General Features
- Most PPS tumours present as **asymptomatic neck mass** or **medial oropharyngeal bulge** (para-tonsillar fullness)
- **Trismus**: masseter/pterygoid involvement
- **Dysphagia/dysphonia**: retrostyloid CN involvement
- **Pulsatile mass**: paraganglioma (do NOT biopsy)

### Differentiating Prestyloid from Retrostyloid

| Feature | Prestyloid | Retrostyloid |
|---|---|---|
| Tonsillar displacement | Anteromedial (forward) | Posteromedial |
| CN involvement | Rare | CN IX–XII; Horner's (sympathetic) |
| Vascular | Non-vascular (except hemangioma) | Flow void on MRI (paraganglioma) |
| MRI fat triangle | Fat plane displaced medially; mass in fat | No fat triangle; mass posterior to styloid |

## Imaging

### CT
- Delineates bony involvement, foramen involvement (skull base extension)
- Differentiates solid vs cystic
- Identifies origin (parotid tail vs separate mass)

### MRI
- **Best modality** for PPS
- T1: fat is bright (prestyloid fat); compressed fat in prestyloid tumour pushes inward
- T2: paraganglioma — **salt and pepper** appearance (flow voids + haemorrhage)
- Gadolinium: enhancing, highly vascular lesion (paraganglioma)
- **Fat triangle sign**: prestyloid fat plane on axial MRI; displaced medially = prestyloid mass
- **Flow void**: characteristic of paraganglioma (high flow)

### Angiography / MR Angiography
- Mandatory for suspected paraganglioma
- Identifies feeding vessels (ascending pharyngeal artery for vagal paraganglioma)
- Pre-operative embolisation 24–48 hours before surgery for large vascular tumours

### FNAC
- Avoid FNA for suspected paraganglioma (vascular; risk of haematoma)
- Transorally: generally avoided (risk of pseudocapsule rupture)
- Transcervical ultrasound-guided FNA: acceptable for solid, non-vascular lesions

## Surgical Approaches

### 1. Transcervical Approach
- **Most common approach** for PPS tumours
- Indication: benign tumours (prestyloid or retrostyloid) without skull base extension; mass extending to or below the hyoid
- Incision: standard neck dissection incision (horizontal cervical crease)
- Dissection: dissect along posterior belly of digastric, identify ICA, IJV, CN X; retrostyloid contents visualised
- **Adequate for**: most schwannomas, vagal paragangliomas below jugular foramen, benign prestyloid tumours

### 2. Transparotid-Cervical Approach
- **Indication**: tumours of deep lobe parotid origin (prestyloid)
- Incision: standard parotidectomy incision + cervical extension (Blair/modified Blair)
- **Facial nerve identification** is mandatory — retracted anteriorly
- Deep lobe delivered through parotid bed after facial nerve exposure and retraction
- Combined cervical exposure for inferior pole
- Avoids blind delivery through restricted space

### 3. Transoral Approach
- **Indication**: small benign prestyloid tumours without skull base extension
- Access through ipsilateral oropharynx
- **Disadvantages and dangers**:
  - Blind approach — cannot visualise feeding vessels or cranial nerves
  - High risk of pseudocapsule rupture → spillage → recurrence
  - Cannot control haemorrhage easily
  - **Contraindicated** for malignant tumours, paraganglioma, or any retrostyloid tumour
- Reserved for small (<2.5 cm), clearly defined, clearly benign prestyloid tumours in experienced centres

### 4. Infratemporal Fossa (Fisch) Approach — Type A/B
- **Indication**: PPS tumours with **skull base extension** (foramen jugulare, carotid canal involvement)
- Type A: primarily jugular foramen/temporal bone; ICA dissection and carotid mobilisation
- Type B: more anterior; pterygoid plates, foramen ovale
- Provides full exposure of the infratemporal fossa, petrous ICA, and clivus
- Requires facial nerve rerouting (Type A) — risk of facial palsy
- Used for jugular paraganglioma, extensive vagal paraganglioma, chordoma, ACC
- See also [[Answers/Skull Base/Middle Cranial Fossa Approaches|Middle Cranial Fossa Approaches]] for superior extension

### 5. Combined Transcervical-Transparotid Approach
- **Indication**: large (>4 cm) tumours, malignant PPS tumours, tumours at or near skull base
- Combines best elements of both exposures
- Allows neck dissection if malignant

### Approach Selection Summary

| Tumour | Preferred Approach |
|---|---|
| Benign prestyloid (<4 cm, no skull base) | Transcervical |
| Deep-lobe parotid tumour | Transparotid-cervical |
| Small benign prestyloid (<2.5 cm) | Transoral (selected centres) |
| Retrostyloid schwannoma/paraganglioma (below skull base) | Transcervical |
| Any tumour with skull base involvement | Infratemporal fossa (Fisch) ± cervical |
| Malignant PPS tumour | Combined transcervical-transparotid + neck dissection |

## Special Considerations: Vagal Paraganglioma

- Most common retrostyloid PPS paraganglioma
- Origin: glomus cells in vagal body (at jugular foramen or nodose ganglion)
- Presents: pulsatile neck mass, CN X palsy (hoarseness, dysphagia) — **Singer's syndrome**
- MRI: flow voids; salt and pepper
- Management: pre-op embolisation (24–48 h before) → surgery with CN X sacrifice (anticipated)
- Post-op: voice rehabilitation (medialisaton laryngoplasty); swallowing therapy

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The parapharyngeal space is an inverted pyramid divided by the styloid process into prestyloid (deep parotid, minor salivary gland tumours — cause anterior tonsillar displacement) and retrostyloid (paraganglioma, schwannoma — cause posteromedial displacement with cranial nerve palsies) compartments. MRI with gadolinium is the imaging modality of choice, with the fat triangle sign identifying prestyloid masses and flow voids indicating paraganglioma. The transcervical approach is the most common, while transparotid-cervical is used for deep-lobe parotid tumours; the infratemporal fossa approach (Fisch Type A/B) is reserved for tumours extending to the skull base.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Pulsatile oropharyngeal mass** in a patient with hoarseness and dysphagia = vagal paraganglioma — do NOT biopsy without angiography.
> 2. The **fat triangle sign** on axial MRI: displaced medially = prestyloid mass pushing fat inward; absent = retrostyloid.
> 3. Deep-lobe parotid tumours are the **most common PPS tumour** (40–50%); they require facial nerve exposure before delivery.
> 4. **Transoral approach** is the highest-risk route — pseudocapsule rupture, haemorrhage, incomplete removal; avoid for anything other than small benign prestyloid lesions.
> 5. Pre-operative embolisation of paraganglioma should be done **24–48 hours** before surgery — earlier and oedema may increase difficulty.
> 6. Vagal paraganglioma resection almost always sacrifices CN X — counsel for post-operative hoarseness and dysphagia; plan voice rehabilitation.
> 7. **Fisch Type A** approach (for jugular foramen extension) requires **anterior transposition of the facial nerve** — highest risk of facial palsy among all approaches.
> 8. Retrostyloid schwannomas may arise from CN IX, X, XI, or XII, or the sympathetic chain; pre-op MRI helps predict the parent nerve and anticipated deficit.
