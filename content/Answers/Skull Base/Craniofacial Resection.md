## Indications for Craniofacial Resection

### Histological Types
CFR is indicated for sinonasal and skull base malignancies with anterior skull base involvement (T3–T4):
- **Esthesioneuroblastoma (olfactory neuroblastoma)** — most common primary CFR tumour; arises from olfactory epithelium at cribriform plate
- **Squamous cell carcinoma** of ethmoid/nasal cavity
- **Adenocarcinoma** (intestinal-type ITAC — occupational: wood dust, leather)
- **Adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC)** — perineural spread; requires wide dural margins
- **Sinonasal undifferentiated carcinoma (SNUC)** — high-grade, poor prognosis; often requires multimodal treatment
- **Chondrosarcoma / chordoma** — see [[Answers/Skull Base/Skull Base Chondrosarcoma|Skull Base Chondrosarcoma]]
- **Meningioma** extending through the skull base floor

### AJCC/Staging Criteria for CFR
- **T3** — Invasion of periorbita, medial wall of orbit, pterygoid plates, or cribriform plate (without dural breach)
- **T4a** — Invasion of dura, brain, orbital apex, cavernous sinus, or skin of nose/cheek (resectable with CFR)
- **T4b** — Massive disease: bilateral orbit, cavernous sinus bilaterally, nasopharynx, clivus, encasement of ICA (unresectable)

---

## Absolute Contraindications to CFR

| Contraindication | Reason |
|---|---|
| **Bilateral cavernous sinus involvement** | Cannot achieve negative margins; bilateral CN III–VI loss unacceptable |
| **Bilateral orbital invasion** | Bilateral exenteration incompatible with function |
| **Brain parenchyma invasion** (beyond superficial dural attachment) | Incomplete resection; oncologically unacceptable |
| **Encasement of ICA** | Vascular injury; no safe margin |
| **Distant metastases** | Locoregional surgery not curative |
| **Medical inoperability** | Prolonged combined procedure not tolerated |

## Classic Ketcham Craniofacial Resection

### Team Composition
- **Neurosurgeon**: cranial component — bicoronal craniotomy, anterior cranial fossa exposure, dural dissection
- **Head and Neck Surgeon / Rhinologist**: nasal component — rhinotomy or midfacial degloving, maxillectomy if required
- Combined team present simultaneously in most centres

### Step-by-Step Technique

**1. Positioning and Incision**
- Supine, head in three-pin fixation (Mayfield)
- **Bicoronal scalp flap**: from preauricular region, across vertex, coronal plane
- Pericranium elevated separately and preserved (for reconstruction)

**2. Cranial Phase (Neurosurgeon)**
- Bifrontal craniotomy: bilateral frontal bone flaps
- Careful elevation of frontal lobe dura off the anterior cranial fossa floor
- Identification and preservation of olfactory tracts (if uninvolved) or planned division at bulbs
- Dural incision **anterior to coronal suture**, behind frontal sinus
- Circumferential dural cuts around the planned skull base resection margins
- En bloc removal of cribriform plate ± planum sphenoidale with dura

**3. Nasal Phase (HNS)**
- **External rhinotomy (lateral rhinotomy)** or **midfacial degloving** for wider exposure
- Total ethmoidectomy, sphenoidectomy, and partial maxillectomy as needed
- The nasal specimen joins the cranial specimen en bloc at the cribriform plate
- Orbital periosteum excised if T3 orbital involvement

**4. Dural Reconstruction**
- Primary watertight dural closure if sufficient dura available
- **Pericranial flap** (see below) as primary dural substitute and skull base floor coverage
- Fibrin glue reinforcement
- Cranial bone flap replaced and fixed with titanium plates/miniplates

**5. Closure**
- Galeal-pericranial flap turned down over defect
- Scalp closure in layers
- Nasal cavity packed; drain placed if large dead space

## Pericranial Flap: The Workhorse of Anterior Skull Base Reconstruction

### Anatomy
- Composed of **periosteum** overlying the calvarium + galea aponeurotica
- **Blood supply** (dominant pedicle): **supratrochlear** and **supraorbital arteries** (branches of ophthalmic artery) entering inferiorly at the orbital rim
- Secondary supply: superficial temporal artery (posterolateral)
- Arc of rotation: pivots on the supraorbital/supratrochlear pedicle at the orbital rim

### Dimensions
- Length: typically 12–15 cm (sufficient to reach cribriform plate and planum sphenoidale)
- Width: 8–12 cm; harvested across full coronal width

### Harvest
- During the bicoronal incision, the **pericranium is elevated separately**, starting posterior and advancing anteriorly
- Avoid cutting the pedicle at the orbital rim
- Flap tunnelled under the skull base or through a frontal sinus opening into the anterior cranial fossa

### Applications
1. **Primary dural substitute** — covers skull base defect; vascularised coverage reduces infection
2. **Separates intracranial from sinonasal cavity** — prevents ascending meningitis
3. Combines with split-thickness skin graft or allowed to epithelialise

## Post-Operative Complications After CFR

### Scenario: Drowsy Patient + Headache After CFR

> **Differential Diagnosis** (in order of priority):
> 1. **Intracranial haematoma** (extradural/subdural/intracerebral) — EMERGENCY
> 2. **Tension pneumocephalus** — air under pressure; "Mount Fuji sign" on CT
> 3. **CSF leak** with ascending meningitis
> 4. **Cerebral oedema** / retraction injury
> 5. Metabolic (hyponatraemia — SIADH post-CFR)

> **Immediate Action:**
> - **CT head without contrast** — first and urgent
> - If haematoma: return to theatre for evacuation
> - If tension pneumocephalus: needle aspiration via burr hole; avoid N₂O anaesthesia
> - If meningitis suspected: LP + blood cultures + IV antibiotics

### Full Complication List

| Complication | Incidence | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **CSF leak** | 10–25% | Inadequate dural/pericranial closure; manage with lumbar drain |
| **Meningitis** | 5–10% | Bacterial (Gram-positive commonest); prophylaxis perioperative |
| **Intracranial haematoma** | 2–5% | Extradural from bone edges; subdural from retraction |
| **Tension pneumocephalus** | Rare | Prevents venous drainage; urgent decompression |
| **Frontal lobe contusion** | 5–10% | From retraction; bilateral frontal approach minimises |
| **Anosmia** | Near-universal | Division of olfactory filaments |
| **Orbital complications** | 5% | Diplopia, enophthalmos, epiphora |
| **Wound infection** | 10–15% | Dead space, nasal communication; flap reconstruction reduces |
| **Skin flap necrosis** | 2–5% | Pedicle injury during scalp elevation |
| **Delayed seizures** | 5% | Frontal lobe involvement; prophylactic anticonvulsants |

## Oncological Outcomes

| Tumour Type | 5-Year OS | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Esthesioneuroblastoma | 60–70% | Best prognosis among CFR tumours; Kadish staging |
| Sinonasal SCC | 40–55% | Depends on dural and orbital involvement |
| Adenocarcinoma (ITAC) | 50–60% | Better with wide margins |
| SNUC | 25–40% | High-grade; combined chemoradiotherapy |
| Overall CFR | 50–60% | Landmark multicenter studies |

**Prognostic Factors (Poor)**:
- Positive surgical margins
- Dural involvement / brain invasion
- Orbital exenteration required
- Node-positive disease

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> Craniofacial resection (CFR), introduced by Ketcham in 1963, is the combined intracranial and extracranial en bloc resection of anterior skull base tumours invading the cribriform plate and ethmoid complex, performed jointly by a neurosurgeon and head-and-neck surgeon. Absolute contraindications include bilateral cavernous sinus or bilateral orbital involvement and brain parenchyma invasion. A drowsy patient with headache after CFR requires urgent CT head to exclude intracranial haematoma or tension pneumocephalus — both requiring immediate surgical intervention. The pericranial flap, pedicled on supratrochlear and supraorbital arteries, is the workhorse of skull base floor reconstruction, providing vascularised barrier between the intracranial and sinonasal compartments.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Ketcham (1963) established the principle: **en bloc resection** of the cribriform plate + sinonasal tumour is the only curative approach for anterior skull base malignancy.
> 2. **Esthesioneuroblastoma** has the best prognosis (60–70% 5-year OS) among tumours requiring CFR; SNUC has the worst (~25–40%).
> 3. **Drowsy + headache post-CFR = CT head immediately**; do not delay for CSF/glucose testing before imaging.
> 4. The pericranial flap pedicle (supratrochlear + supraorbital arteries) is at the **orbital rim** — protect it during bicoronal incision.
> 5. Positive surgical margins are the **strongest predictor of recurrence** — R1/R0 distinction drives adjuvant radiotherapy decision.
> 6. **Tension pneumocephalus** (Mount Fuji sign on CT) is distinguished from simple pneumocephalus by mass effect and midline shift; requires urgent burr hole aspiration.
> 7. Midfacial degloving gives better cosmesis than lateral rhinotomy for the nasal phase of CFR, with equivalent surgical exposure.
> 8. Endoscopic CFR (combined craniotomy + endoscopic nasal phase) now offers reduced morbidity in selected patients; compare with [[Answers/Skull Base/Anterior Skull Base Anatomy and Endoscopic Surgery|EEES]].
