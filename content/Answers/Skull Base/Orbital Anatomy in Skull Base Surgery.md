## Orbital Walls — Anatomy

The orbit is a four-walled pyramid with its **apex pointing posteriorly** and base (orbital rim) anteriorly.

### Roof (Superior Wall)
- **Orbital plate of frontal bone** (anterior 2/3)
- **Lesser wing of sphenoid** (posterior 1/3; forms the orbital apex roofward)
- Adjacent to: frontal sinus anteriorly; anterior cranial fossa above
- **Surgical relevance**: most commonly involved in anterior cranial fossa tumours (meningioma, CFR specimens); see [[Answers/Skull Base/Craniofacial Resection|Craniofacial Resection]]
- Thin centrally — lacrimal fossa (for lacrimal gland) in anterolateral roof
- Trochlea (fibrocartilaginous pulley of superior oblique) located at anteromedial roof

### Floor (Inferior Wall)
- **Orbital plate of maxilla** (largest component)
- **Zygomatic bone** (anterolateral)
- **Orbital process of palatine bone** (posteromedial, small)
- Contains: **inferior orbital canal** (transmits infraorbital nerve and artery — V2)
- Thin medially — **classic site of blow-out fracture** (inferior blow-out)
- Adjacent below: maxillary sinus

### Medial Wall
- **Frontal process of maxilla** (anteroinferior)
- **Lacrimal bone** (contains lacrimal fossa and nasolacrimal duct)
- **Lamina papyracea** (orbital plate of ethmoid) — central; thinnest wall (0.2–0.4 mm)
- **Body of sphenoid** (posterior)
- Adjacent medially: ethmoid air cells, sphenoid sinus
- **Surgical relevance**: most commonly breached in FESS, ethmoid tumours, orbital decompression
- **Lamina papyracea** — paper-thin; first barrier to orbital invasion by ethmoid tumours; key landmark in FESS

### Lateral Wall
- **Zygomatic bone** (anterior)
- **Greater wing of sphenoid** (posterior)
- **Thickest orbital wall** — strongest; least commonly fractured
- Contains: **inferior orbital fissure** (between floor and lateral wall) — transmits V2 (infraorbital nerve continuing from foramen rotundum), zygomatic nerve, inferior ophthalmic vein
- **Zygomaticotemporal** and **zygomaticofacial** foramina on lateral wall surface

## Orbital Apex and Key Foramina

### Superior Orbital Fissure (SOF)
- Between **greater wing** (below) and **lesser wing** (above) of sphenoid
- Transmits (medial to lateral):
  1. **CN III** (oculomotor — all extraocular muscles except SO and LR)
  2. **CN IV** (trochlear — superior oblique)
  3. **CN V1 branches**: nasociliary, frontal, lacrimal
  4. **CN VI** (abducens — lateral rectus)
  5. **Superior ophthalmic vein**
  6. **Sympathetic fibres** (from ICA plexus)
- SOF syndrome: total ophthalmoplegia + CN V1 sensory loss + proptosis

### Optic Canal
- In the **lesser wing of sphenoid**
- Transmits: **CN II** (optic nerve) + **ophthalmic artery** (from ICA)
- Located medial and slightly superior to SOF
- Orbital apex invasion involving optic canal = **T4b disease** (unresectable) in paranasal sinus staging

### Annulus of Zinn (Common Tendinous Ring)
- Fibrous ring at orbital apex encircling the optic canal and medial part of SOF
- Origin of all four recti muscles + superior oblique (partial)
- CN III (superior and inferior divisions), CN VI, and nasociliary nerve (V1) pass **through** the ring
- CN IV and lacrimal/frontal branches of V1 pass **outside** the ring

## Orbital Exenteration

### Definition
Surgical removal of all orbital contents (globe, extraocular muscles, orbital fat, eyelids) within the periorbita (periosteum).

### Types of Exenteration

| Type | What Is Removed | When Used |
|---|---|---|
| **Total exenteration** | All orbital contents + periorbita + eyelids | Tumour invades eyelid skin; most extensive |
| **Subtotal (eyelid-sparing) exenteration** | All orbital contents + periorbita; eyelid skin preserved | Malignant disease not involving eyelid skin; eyelids used to line socket |
| **Extended exenteration** | Contents + adjacent structures (maxilla, ethmoid, temporal bone, skin) | Advanced disease with bony or cutaneous extension |
| **Radical exenteration** | Total + adjacent skin + bony walls of orbit | Very rarely performed; for extensive primary orbital malignancy |

### Indications for Orbital Exenteration

**Primary orbital malignancy** (rare):
- Lacrimal gland adenoid cystic carcinoma (most common primary orbital malignancy requiring exenteration)
- Orbital rhabdomyosarcoma (children — usually chemotherapy first)
- Primary orbital melanoma

**Secondary invasion** (more common):
- Paranasal sinus carcinoma invading orbital fat or extraocular muscles
- Lid malignancy (SCC, BCC, Merkel cell carcinoma) with deep orbital invasion
- Recurrent BCC of medial canthus/lower eyelid
- Conjunctival melanoma with orbital extension

**Other**:
- Refractory chronic orbital infection (invasive fungal sinusitis, mucormycosis)

## Orbital Preservation vs Exenteration in Paranasal Sinus Malignancy

This is a critical and frequently examined oncological decision.

### T-Stage Correlation (AJCC 8th Edition, Maxillary Sinus)

| T Stage | Orbital Involvement | Surgical Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **T3** | Invasion of **floor or medial wall** of orbit (periorbita); floor of orbit (lamina papyracea) | Orbital **preservation possible** — resect periorbita; strip medial wall; observe orbital fat |
| **T4a** | Invasion of **orbital fat**, extraocular muscles, or anterior orbital contents | **Borderline** — exenteration often needed; some select cases may be observed if periorbita intact after resection |
| **T4b** | Invasion of **orbital apex**, dura, brain, middle cranial fossa, or cribriform plate | **Unresectable** — orbital apex involvement; exenteration would not achieve R0 |

### Decision Framework
1. **Periorbita (periosteum) intact**: orbital contents can be preserved regardless of bony wall removal
2. **Periorbita breached with macroscopic fat involvement**: exenteration recommended
3. **Extraocular muscle invasion**: exenteration (diplopia post-op if spared; oncological clearance)
4. **Apex involvement** (optic canal): unresectable (T4b)

## Reconstruction After Orbital Exenteration

| Technique | Description | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Split-thickness skin graft (STSG)** | Applied directly to periorbital socket | Simple; allows surveillance for recurrence | Delayed healing; no bulk; poor cosmesis |
| **Temporalis muscle flap** | Rotated through zygomatic arch window | Good bulk; obliterates dead space; regional | Limited arc of rotation; forehead depression |
| **Temporoparietal fascia flap** | Thin fascial flap; vascularised by STA | Thin coverage; allows prosthetic fitting | Less bulk than temporalis |
| **Free flap** (ALT, rectus abdominis) | For extensive defects or previously irradiated fields | Large volume; reliable; any defect size | Complex microsurgery |
| **Prosthetic rehabilitation** | Ocular prosthesis ± osseointegrated implants for retention | Excellent cosmesis; patient preference | Requires intact socket lining; maintenance |

The **temporalis muscle flap** is most commonly used after orbital exenteration for sinonasal malignancy (proximity, reliable pedicle, good bulk to obliterate the socket).

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> The medial orbital wall (lamina papyracea — thinnest, 0.2–0.4 mm) and roof (frontal bone + lesser wing of sphenoid) are most relevant to skull base surgery; the lateral wall is the thickest and most resistant. The orbital apex contains the optic canal (CN II, ophthalmic artery) and superior orbital fissure (CN III, IV, V1, VI, superior ophthalmic vein). In paranasal sinus malignancy, periorbital (T3) invasion permits orbital preservation by periosteal stripping; invasion of orbital fat/muscles (T4a) generally mandates exenteration; orbital apex involvement (T4b) renders the case unresectable. Reconstruction of the exenterated socket is most commonly achieved with the temporalis muscle flap combined with a split-thickness skin graft.

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. **Lamina papyracea** (medial orbital wall) is the thinnest orbital wall (0.2–0.4 mm) and the first barrier breached by ethmoid tumours — its integrity determines orbital preservation.
> 2. The **lateral wall is the thickest** — least affected by sinonasal disease; most resistant to blow-out fractures.
> 3. SOF syndrome (total ophthalmoplegia + V1 sensory loss) = CN III, IV, V1, VI damage at the superior orbital fissure — distinguishable from orbital apex syndrome by presence/absence of vision loss (optic canal involved in apex syndrome).
> 4. **Periorbita intact = orbital preservation** regardless of bony erosion; periorbita breached + fat invasion = exenteration required.
> 5. Orbital **apex** involvement = T4b = **unresectable** — important to confirm on MRI before planning surgery.
> 6. **Annulus of Zinn** is the origin of the recti muscles; CN VI (abducens) enters the lateral rectus through the annulus; CN IV runs outside the ring and over the superior oblique.
> 7. **Temporalis muscle flap** is the workhorse for orbital socket reconstruction — rotated through a window in the zygomatic arch; bulk fills the orbit and prevents meningocele.
> 8. Post-exenteration patients should be referred for **osseointegrated implant-retained ocular prosthesis** — superior retention and cosmesis vs adhesive prosthesis.
