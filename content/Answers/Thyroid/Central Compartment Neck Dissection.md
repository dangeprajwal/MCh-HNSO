## Anatomy of the Central Compartment (Level VI)

The central compartment of the neck, designated level VI in the classification of cervical lymph nodes, is the primary echelon nodal basin for thyroid carcinoma. It is bounded by the following anatomical landmarks:

| Boundary | Landmark |
|---|---|
| Superior | Hyoid bone |
| Inferior | Innominate (brachiocephalic) artery / suprasternal notch |
| Lateral (bilateral) | Medial border of the carotid sheath |
| Anterior | Superficial layer of the deep cervical fascia |
| Posterior | Prevertebral fascia / deep layer of deep cervical fascia |

### Nodal Subgroups Within Level VI

The central compartment contains the following distinct lymph node groups, as defined by the ATA Surgery Working Group (Randolph 2009):

1. **Prelaryngeal (Delphian) nodes** -- located anterior to the cricothyroid membrane and upper tracheal rings; sentinel nodes for thyroid and subglottic laryngeal cancers
2. **Pretracheal nodes** -- located anterior to the trachea, below the thyroid isthmus and above the innominate artery
3. **Right paratracheal nodes** -- located in the tracheoesophageal groove on the right side, extending from the inferior border of the right thyroid lobe to the innominate artery
4. **Left paratracheal nodes** -- located in the tracheoesophageal groove on the left side, extending from the inferior border of the left thyroid lobe to the aortic arch

### Critical Structures at Risk During CCND

- **Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN)** -- both nerves traverse the central compartment in or near the tracheoesophageal groove
- **Inferior parathyroid glands** -- typically located within the central compartment anterior to the RLN
- **Superior parathyroid glands** -- located posterior to the RLN, usually above level VI but may descend into the central compartment
- **Thoracic duct** (left side) -- variable but may extend into the lower central compartment on the left
- **Innominate artery and vein** -- define the inferior limit of dissection

## Incidence of Central Compartment Nodal Metastases

Central compartment nodal involvement in differentiated thyroid cancer (DTC) is extremely common:

| Characteristic | Incidence |
|---|---|
| Microscopic (occult) central nodal metastases in cN0 PTC | 40--60% |
| Clinically apparent central nodal metastases | 20--30% |
| Bilateral paratracheal involvement | 15--25% |
| Skip metastases (lateral without central) | 5--20% |
| Central nodal metastases in follicular thyroid cancer | 5--10% |

The high rate of occult nodal involvement forms the basis of the argument for prophylactic CCND, while the generally indolent clinical course of many occult metastases forms the basis of the argument against it.

## Detection of Central Compartment Nodes

### Preoperative Ultrasonography

- **Sensitivity for central compartment nodes:** 40--50% (significantly lower than for lateral compartment, where sensitivity is 70--80%)
- The limited sensitivity is due to acoustic shadowing from the thyroid gland itself, tracheal air artefact, and the deep location of paratracheal nodes
- **Specificity** remains high at 85--95%
- Suspicious features: round shape, loss of fatty hilum, peripheral vascularity, heterogeneous echogenicity, calcifications, cystic change

### CT Imaging

- **Role:** Adjunct to ultrasound, particularly for evaluating retrotracheal, retro-oesophageal, and mediastinal extension
- Contrast-enhanced CT provides better anatomic detail but interferes with subsequent radioiodine therapy (iodine load requires 4--8 week washout)
- Sensitivity for central compartment nodes is 50--60%

### Intraoperative Assessment

- **Visual and palpatory assessment:** Often underestimates disease; surgeon's visual detection of positive central nodes has sensitivity of approximately 60%
- **Frozen section:** Can be used for suspicious nodes identified intraoperatively
- **Intraoperative ultrasound:** Emerging modality with potential to improve detection

## Definitions: Therapeutic vs Prophylactic CCND

### Therapeutic Central Compartment Neck Dissection

- Performed when central lymph node metastases are clinically apparent (cN1a)
- Identified preoperatively on imaging or intraoperatively by the surgeon
- **Universally recommended** by all guidelines (ATA, NCCN, BTA, ETA)
- Should include bilateral central compartment dissection (both paratracheal basins, plus prelaryngeal and pretracheal nodes)

### Prophylactic (Elective) Central Compartment Neck Dissection

- Performed in clinically node-negative (cN0) patients
- No clinical or imaging evidence of central nodal involvement
- **Controversial** -- the most debated topic in thyroid cancer surgery
- Goal: improve staging accuracy, reduce Tg levels, possibly reduce recurrence, and potentially avoid reoperation in the central compartment

## Indications for Prophylactic CCND

### ATA 2015 Guidelines (Haugen 2016)

The ATA provides the following recommendations:

- **Prophylactic CCND (ipsilateral or bilateral)** should be considered for:
  - T3 and T4 tumours (extrathyroidal extension or tumours >4 cm)
  - Clinically involved lateral neck nodes (cN1b) -- perform ipsilateral CCND at minimum
  - Patients in whom staging information will guide RAI decision-making
- **Prophylactic CCND is NOT routinely recommended** for:
  - Small (T1/T2), non-invasive, clinically node-negative papillary thyroid cancers
  - Follicular thyroid cancer (FTC rarely metastasises to cervical nodes)

### Japanese and Korean Practice

- **Routine bilateral CCND** is standard of care in many Japanese and Korean centres, even for small PTC
- Justification: high rates of occult nodal involvement, improved staging, lower regional recurrence rates in institutional series
- The Japanese Association of Endocrine Surgery recommends prophylactic CCND for all PTC patients undergoing total thyroidectomy

### NCCN Guidelines

- Prophylactic CCND may be considered for T1--T2 PTC without evidence of nodal metastases
- Should be performed when staging information may change the plan for RAI ablation

## Evidence For Prophylactic CCND

### Arguments Supporting Prophylactic CCND

1. **Accurate staging** -- Upstages 30--40% of patients from N0 to N1a; reclassifies ATA risk category in 10--20% of patients
2. **Reduces stimulated thyroglobulin levels** -- Lower postoperative Tg facilitates biochemical surveillance
3. **May reduce locoregional recurrence** -- Some institutional series show reduced central compartment recurrence (3--5% vs 5--9% without CCND)
4. **Avoids reoperation in the central compartment** -- Revision central compartment surgery carries substantially higher complication rates (RLN injury 5--10%, permanent hypoparathyroidism 10--20%)
5. **Guides RAI decision-making** -- Knowledge of nodal status helps determine necessity and dose of RAI
6. **Low additional morbidity in expert hands** -- When performed by high-volume thyroid surgeons, the added morbidity of prophylactic CCND is minimal

### Supporting Data

- Barczyński et al. (2013): Prospective randomised trial of 214 patients; prophylactic CCND improved staging but did not affect short-term recurrence
- Sywak et al. (2006): Prophylactic bilateral CCND reduced central compartment recurrence from 3.1% to 0% at 4.5-year follow-up
- Multiple institutional series from Japan and Korea report central compartment recurrence rates of <2% with routine CCND

## Evidence Against Prophylactic CCND

### Arguments Against Prophylactic CCND

1. **No proven survival benefit** -- No randomised trial has demonstrated improved disease-specific survival with prophylactic CCND
2. **Clinical significance of occult metastases is uncertain** -- Many occult micrometastases may never become clinically relevant
3. **Increased morbidity** -- Higher rates of transient and permanent hypoparathyroidism (Viola 2015: permanent hypoparathyroidism 19.4% vs 8.0% in non-CCND group)
4. **Staging benefit only** -- The Zetoune 2010 meta-analysis found that prophylactic CCND did not significantly reduce locoregional recurrence (OR 0.60, 95% CI 0.34--1.05, p = 0.07)
5. **Recurrence in the central compartment is salvageable** -- Central compartment recurrence can be detected on surveillance imaging and managed surgically, although with increased morbidity

### Key Trial Data (Viola 2015)

| Outcome | TT alone (n=88) | TT + pCCND (n=93) | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nodal metastases found | N/A | 53% | -- |
| Transient hypoparathyroidism | 14.8% | 30.1% | <0.05 |
| Permanent hypoparathyroidism | 8.0% | 19.4% | <0.05 |
| Transient RLN palsy | 2.3% | 3.2% | NS |
| Permanent RLN palsy | 0% | 1.1% | NS |
| Recurrence at 5 years | Similar | Similar | NS |

## Bilateral vs Ipsilateral CCND

### Ipsilateral (Unilateral) CCND

- Removes prelaryngeal, pretracheal, and ipsilateral paratracheal nodes
- Lower morbidity -- preserves contralateral parathyroid glands and RLN from surgical trauma
- Appropriate for unilateral tumours without contralateral disease
- May miss contralateral paratracheal metastases (present in 25--30% of bilateral paratracheal disease)

### Bilateral CCND

- Comprehensive dissection of all central compartment node groups
- Recommended for:
  - Bilateral thyroid tumours
  - Isthmus tumours
  - Clinically apparent central node metastases (therapeutic CCND)
  - Large tumours (T3/T4)
  - When contralateral nodes are palpable or visible on imaging
- Higher risk of bilateral RLN injury and hypoparathyroidism

## Surgical Technique

### Patient Positioning

- Supine with neck extension using shoulder roll
- General anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation (non-reinforced tube allows nerve monitoring)
- Intraoperative nerve monitoring (IONM) strongly recommended for CCND

### Key Technical Steps

1. **Total thyroidectomy is completed first** -- The thyroid gland is mobilised to expose the central compartment
2. **Identification and preservation of RLN** -- The nerve is identified at the Berry ligament or in the tracheoesophageal groove; IONM confirms function
3. **Identification and preservation of parathyroid glands** -- Superior and inferior parathyroid glands are identified and preserved on their vascular pedicle; devascularised glands are autotransplanted into the sternocleidomastoid or forearm
4. **En bloc dissection of fibrofatty tissue** -- All lymph node-bearing tissue is removed from the hyoid bone superiorly to the innominate artery inferiorly, from carotid sheath to carotid sheath
5. **Special attention to the left paratracheal region** -- The thoracic duct and left RLN (which has a more variable course than the right) require careful identification
6. **Specimen orientation and labelling** -- Separate labelling of prelaryngeal, pretracheal, right paratracheal, and left paratracheal specimens for accurate staging

### Parathyroid Gland Management During CCND

- At least one parathyroid gland with intact blood supply should be preserved in situ
- Devascularised parathyroid glands should be confirmed by frozen section and then autotransplanted (minced 1 mm fragments into the sternocleidomastoid muscle)
- Intraoperative PTH measurement may guide the need for autotransplantation

## Morbidity of CCND

### Comparison of Complication Rates

| Complication | TT alone | TT + CCND | Revision CCND |
|---|---|---|---|
| Transient hypoparathyroidism | 10--20% | 20--40% | 30--60% |
| Permanent hypoparathyroidism | 1--3% | 3--8% | 10--20% |
| Transient RLN palsy | 2--5% | 3--6% | 5--12% |
| Permanent RLN palsy | <1% | 1--2% | 3--5% |
| Chyle leak | Rare | 1--2% | 2--5% |

The significantly higher complication rates with revision CCND form one of the strongest arguments for upfront comprehensive CCND during the index operation, particularly in high-risk patients.

## Oncologic Outcomes

### Impact on Recurrence

- **Central compartment recurrence** after total thyroidectomy without CCND: 5--9%
- **Central compartment recurrence** after total thyroidectomy with prophylactic CCND: 1--5%
- This difference, while suggestive, has not reached statistical significance in randomised trials

### Impact on Survival

- **No randomised trial has demonstrated a survival benefit** for prophylactic CCND
- Disease-specific survival for differentiated thyroid cancer exceeds 95% at 10 years regardless of prophylactic CCND
- The excellent overall prognosis of DTC makes it extremely difficult to demonstrate a survival benefit from prophylactic CCND (would require thousands of patients with prolonged follow-up)

### Impact on Thyroglobulin Surveillance

- Prophylactic CCND significantly reduces postoperative stimulated Tg levels
- Lower Tg facilitates interpretation of biochemical surveillance and may reduce the need for RAI

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **Level VI boundaries** -- hyoid to innominate artery, medial carotid sheath to medial carotid sheath -- this is a frequently tested anatomical question
2. **Occult central nodal metastases** occur in 40--60% of cN0 PTC -- but most are clinically insignificant
3. **Prophylactic CCND is the most controversial topic** in thyroid cancer surgery -- examiners expect a balanced discussion of both sides
4. **Therapeutic CCND is universally recommended** -- never controversial
5. **Viola 2015** is the landmark RCT: similar oncologic outcomes but higher permanent hypoparathyroidism with prophylactic CCND
6. **Revision CCND** carries 3--5x higher complication rates than primary CCND -- this is the strongest argument for upfront comprehensive dissection
7. **Always rule out pheochromocytoma before thyroidectomy** in familial/MTC cases
8. **US sensitivity for central nodes is only 40--50%** -- a negative preoperative US does not reliably exclude central nodal metastases
