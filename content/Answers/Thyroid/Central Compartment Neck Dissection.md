## Anatomy of the Central Compartment (Levels VI and VII)

The central compartment of the neck, designated level VI and VII in the classification of cervical lymph nodes, is the primary echelon nodal basin for thyroid carcinoma. Randolph (Ch 38) emphasises that the central neck lymph nodes include both level VI and level VII, based on the seven-compartment neck nomenclature.

### Level VI Boundaries

| Boundary | Landmark |
|---|---|
| Superior | Hyoid bone |
| Inferior | Suprasternal notch |
| Lateral (bilateral) | Medial border of the carotid sheath |
| Anterior | Undersurface of the sternothyroid muscle (superficial layer of the deep cervical fascia) |
| Posterior | Prevertebral fascia / deep layer of deep cervical fascia |

### Level VII (Superior Mediastinum)

Level VII lymph nodes lie in the compartment **inferior to level VI**, bordered by the innominate (brachiocephalic) artery superiorly and the brachiocephalic vein inferiorly (Randolph Ch 38). On the left side, the innominate artery does not extend into the paratracheal region; therefore the corresponding plane of the innominate crossing the trachea on the right defines the inferior border for the left side. Importantly, the sternal notch and the innominate artery have a **variable relationship**, with the artery rising above the notch in **25% of cadaveric dissections** -- vigilance for a high-riding innominate artery is needed, and variability in the surgically approachable caudal central neck exists.

### Nodal Subgroups Within the Central Compartment

The central compartment contains the following distinct lymph node groups, as defined by the ATA Surgery Working Group (Randolph 2009):

1. **Prelaryngeal (Delphian) nodes** -- located anterior to the cricothyroid membrane and upper tracheal rings; sentinel nodes for thyroid and subglottic laryngeal cancers. Nodal tissue may reside in the indented cricothyroid membrane, requiring deep dissection in this area (Randolph Ch 38)
2. **Pretracheal nodes** -- located anterior to the trachea, below the thyroid isthmus and above the innominate artery
3. **Right paratracheal nodes** -- located in the tracheoesophageal groove on the right side, extending from the inferior border of the right thyroid lobe to the innominate artery. Randolph describes a unique anatomy here: the right RLN ascends obliquely (inferolateral to superomedial) because the subclavian artery is ventral, displacing the nerve ventrally. This creates a **three-dimensional compartment** divisible into upper lateral and lower medial triangles, with nodes potentially lying **deep to the right RLN** -- requiring **360-degree nerve dissection** in some cases
4. **Left paratracheal nodes** -- located in the tracheoesophageal groove on the left side, extending from the inferior border of the left thyroid lobe to the aortic arch. The left RLN ascends more vertically in the tracheoesophageal groove from the ligamentum arteriosum, making this a **flatter, more two-dimensional compartment** compared with the right

The number of nodes in the paratracheal basins ranges from **3 to 30**, with an average of **8 nodes** on pathologic examination of a central neck dissection specimen (Randolph Ch 38).

### Sublevel Classification of the Right Paratracheal Region (Randolph)

Randolph specifically describes dividing the right paratracheal region into:
- **Upper lateral triangle** -- between the RLN and the carotid sheath
- **Lower medial triangle** -- between the RLN and the trachea
- The nerve divides the compartment into **anterior and posterior compartments**, and nodes may be hidden deep (posterior) to the right RLN because of the increased depth of this region compared with the left

### Critical Structures at Risk During CCND

- **Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN)** -- both nerves traverse the central compartment in or near the tracheoesophageal groove; the right RLN is more ventral at the base of the paratracheal region than the left (Randolph Ch 38)
- **Inferior parathyroid glands** -- typically located within the central compartment anterior to the RLN; most at risk during paratracheal dissection
- **Superior parathyroid glands** -- located posterior to the RLN, usually above level VI but may descend into the central compartment
- **Thoracic duct** (left side) -- variable but may extend into the lower central compartment on the left
- **Innominate artery and vein** -- define the inferior limit of dissection; the brachiocephalic vein on the left is particularly vulnerable during pretracheal dissection (Randolph Ch 38)
- **Thymus** -- lies at the lower limit of dissection; rarely contains metastatic nodes but may harbour ectopic parathyroid tissue, so is generally left in situ (Randolph Ch 38)

## Terminology (ATA Consensus -- Randolph 2009)

The ATA Surgery Working Group consensus statement (Randolph 2009) established critical terminology:

- **Therapeutic dissection** -- removal of nodal metastases identified preoperatively or intraoperatively as containing metastases (clinically N1)
- **Elective/prophylactic dissection** -- removal of lymph nodes that do not appear to contain metastases (clinically N0)
- **Unilateral CCND** -- comprehensive removal of pretracheal and prelaryngeal node-bearing tissue along with unilateral paratracheal node removal
- **Bilateral CCND** -- removal of pretracheal, prelaryngeal, and bilateral paratracheal nodal basins
- Comprehensive CCND (unilateral or bilateral) is a **compartmental dissection** and is distinguished from **node plucking (berry picking)** of only macroscopically involved nodes, which is associated with higher recurrence rates (Randolph Ch 38)

## AJCC 8th Edition Nodal Staging (Randolph Ch 38)

| N Category | Criteria |
|---|---|
| NX | Regional lymph nodes cannot be assessed |
| N0 | No evidence of locoregional lymph node metastasis |
| N0a | One or more cytologically or histologically confirmed benign lymph nodes |
| N0b | No radiologic or clinical evidence of locoregional lymph node metastasis |
| N1 | Metastasis to regional nodes |
| N1a | Metastasis to level VI or VII (pretracheal, paratracheal, prelaryngeal/Delphian, or upper mediastinal) -- unilateral or bilateral |
| N1b | Metastasis to unilateral, bilateral, or contralateral lateral neck (levels I--V) or retropharyngeal lymph nodes |

Under the AJCC 8th edition, patients with N1 disease who are **<55 years old** are classified as **Stage I**; patients aged 55 years or older are reclassified as **Stage II**. This reflects data showing that lymph node metastasis increases disease-specific mortality most significantly in elderly patients (Randolph Ch 38).

## Incidence of Central Compartment Nodal Metastases

Central compartment nodal involvement in differentiated thyroid cancer (DTC) is extremely common:

| Characteristic | Incidence |
|---|---|
| Occult central nodal metastases in cN0 PTC (2016 meta-analysis of 37,655 patients, Randolph Ch 38) | 26.4% |
| Microscopic (occult) central nodal metastases in cN0 PTC (range across studies) | 40--60% (up to 90% in some series) |
| Clinically apparent central nodal metastases | 20--30% |
| Bilateral paratracheal involvement | 15--25% |
| Skip metastases to lateral neck without central involvement | 5--22% |
| Central nodal metastases in follicular thyroid cancer | 5--10% |

The high rate of occult nodal involvement forms the basis of the argument for prophylactic CCND, while the generally indolent clinical course of many occult metastases forms the basis of the argument against it. Randolph notes that occult or micrometastases may be present in up to **90%** of WDTC in some series, though a 2016 meta-analysis of 37,655 patients with PTC found a pN1a rate of **26.4%** in cN0 necks.

### Risk Factors for Occult Central Metastases (Randolph Ch 38)

- **Young age (<40 years)** and **male sex** -- more frequently associated with large-volume lymph node metastasis even in cN0 necks
- **Extrathyroidal extension** (T3 or T4)
- **Aggressive histologic subtypes** -- diffuse sclerosing, insular, poorly differentiated
- **Bilateral or multifocal tumours**
- **Lateral cervical neck disease (cN1b)**

## Detection of Central Compartment Nodes

### Preoperative Ultrasonography

- **Sensitivity for central compartment nodes:** often **less than 25%** when the thyroid is present (Randolph Ch 38 cites this lower figure due to acoustic shadowing), up to 40--50% in some series
- The limited sensitivity is due to acoustic shadowing from the thyroid gland itself, tracheal air artefact, and the deep location of paratracheal nodes
- **Specificity** remains high at 85--95%
- Suspicious features: hypoechogenicity, hypervascularisation, loss of hilar architecture (hilar stripe), cystic appearance, microcalcifications, absence of hilum, peripheral vascularisation (Randolph Ch 38)
- Pre-operative US has **83.5% sensitivity and 97.7% specificity** for nodal metastases overall (including lateral) and may identify abnormal nodes not found on physical examination in up to 20% of patients (Randolph Ch 38)

### CT Imaging

- **Role:** Adjunct to ultrasound, particularly for evaluating retrotracheal, retro-oesophageal, and mediastinal extension
- Contrast-enhanced CT provides better anatomic detail but interferes with subsequent radioiodine therapy (iodine load requires 4--8 week washout)
- Combined CT + US significantly improves sensitivity for central node metastases (48% vs 28% for US alone) and accuracy (69% vs 63%) (Randolph Ch 38, Kim et al.)

### Intraoperative Assessment

- **Visual and palpatory assessment:** Often underestimates disease; surgeon's visual detection of positive central nodes has sensitivity of approximately 60%
- Metastatic lymph nodes at surgery often have a **dark blue or black appearance** -- must be distinguished from anthracotic nodes, which have a powdery, specked, charcoal-like particulate appearance (Randolph Ch 38)
- Central lymph nodes **>1 cm** are suspicious, but size is less reliable in coexisting thyroiditis -- frozen section is helpful in such cases (Randolph Ch 38)
- **Frozen section:** Can be used for suspicious nodes identified intraoperatively, and also to distinguish parathyroid from lymph node tissue
- **Intraoperative ultrasound:** Emerging modality with potential to improve detection

### Preoperative Laryngeal Examination (Randolph Ch 38)

A preoperative laryngeal examination should be performed to assess and document the integrity of the RLN. If the vocal fold is immobile, this may indicate prior iatrogenic injury or tumour invasion. This information is critical for surgical planning -- one can more confidently resect an invaded RLN if contralateral nerve function has been confirmed.

## Definitions: Therapeutic vs Prophylactic CCND

### Therapeutic Central Compartment Neck Dissection

- Performed when central lymph node metastases are clinically apparent (cN1a)
- Identified preoperatively on imaging or intraoperatively by the surgeon
- **Universally recommended** by all guidelines (ATA, NCCN, BTA, ETA) -- **strong recommendation** by 2015 ATA guidelines (Randolph Ch 38)
- Should include bilateral central compartment dissection (both paratracheal basins, plus prelaryngeal and pretracheal nodes)
- Rationale: comprehensively remove all grossly evident nodal disease along with any adjacent subclinical disease to minimise risk of recurrent/persistent disease and morbidity from reoperation for initially undertreated disease (Randolph Ch 38)
- When clinically apparent nodes are detected during surgery, a **comprehensive compartmental dissection** (not berry picking) is warranted (Randolph Ch 38)

### Prophylactic (Elective) Central Compartment Neck Dissection

- Performed in clinically node-negative (cN0) patients
- No clinical or imaging evidence of central nodal involvement
- **Controversial** -- the most debated topic in thyroid cancer surgery
- Goal: improve staging accuracy, reduce Tg levels, possibly reduce recurrence, and potentially avoid reoperation in the central compartment
- Also indicated when there is a diagnosis of **medullary thyroid carcinoma** (Randolph Ch 38)

## Indications for Prophylactic CCND

### ATA 2015 Guidelines (Haugen 2016) as Summarised by Randolph Ch 38

The ATA provides the following recommendations:

- **Prophylactic CCND (ipsilateral or bilateral)** should be considered for:
  - T3 and T4 tumours (extrathyroidal extension or tumours >4 cm)
  - Clinically involved lateral neck nodes (cN1b) -- perform ipsilateral CCND at minimum
  - Patients in whom staging information will guide RAI decision-making
  - More aggressive histologic subtypes (diffuse sclerosing, insular, poorly differentiated)
- **Prophylactic CCND is NOT routinely recommended** for:
  - Small (T1/T2), non-invasive, clinically node-negative papillary thyroid cancers -- **strong recommendation against** (Randolph Ch 38)
  - Follicular thyroid cancer (FTC rarely metastasises to cervical nodes)
- **Revision prophylactic CCND** after initial thyroidectomy is **strongly discouraged** (Randolph Ch 38)
- If prophylactic dissection is planned, it should be performed at **initial surgical management**, not as a staged procedure

### European Society of Endocrine Surgeons (ESES) Consensus (Randolph Ch 38)

Recommended prophylactic CCND for:
- Larger tumours (T3 or T4)
- Patients >45 or <15 years of age
- Male patients
- Bilateral or multifocal tumours
- Known lateral cervical neck disease

### Japanese and Korean Practice

- **Routine bilateral CCND** is standard of care in many Japanese and Korean centres, even for small PTC
- Justification: high rates of occult nodal involvement, improved staging, lower regional recurrence rates in institutional series
- The Japanese Association of Endocrine Surgery recommends prophylactic CCND for all PTC patients undergoing total thyroidectomy

### NCCN Guidelines

- Prophylactic CCND may be considered for T1--T2 PTC without evidence of nodal metastases
- Should be performed when staging information may change the plan for RAI ablation
- T3 and T4 tumours: **category 2B recommendation** for prophylactic CCND (Randolph Ch 38)

### Prophylactic CCND in the Context of Lobectomy (Randolph Ch 38)

With updated ATA guidelines accepting lobectomy for tumours up to 4 cm, an elective **ipsilateral CCND** (ipsilateral paratracheal, pretracheal, and prelaryngeal basins) can be considered at lobectomy to determine whether completion thyroidectomy is warranted for adjuvant RAI in patients found to have occult metastasis.

## Evidence For Prophylactic CCND

### Arguments Supporting Prophylactic CCND

1. **Accurate staging** -- Upstages 30--40% of patients from N0 to N1a; reclassifies ATA risk category in 10--20% of patients
2. **Reduces stimulated thyroglobulin levels** -- Lower postoperative Tg facilitates biochemical surveillance
3. **May reduce locoregional recurrence** -- A recent meta-analysis (Zhao et al. 2017, cited in Randolph Ch 38) demonstrated that prophylactic CCND with thyroidectomy appears to **significantly lower locoregional recurrence** compared with total thyroidectomy alone
4. **Avoids reoperation in the central compartment** -- Revision central compartment surgery carries substantially higher complication rates (RLN injury 5--10%, permanent hypoparathyroidism 10--20%)
5. **Guides RAI decision-making** -- Knowledge of nodal status helps determine necessity and dose of RAI; confirmation of cN0 through pathologic staging may allow **avoidance of routine RAI** for cNx necks (Randolph Ch 38)
6. **Low additional morbidity in expert hands** -- When performed by high-volume thyroid surgeons, the added morbidity of prophylactic CCND is minimal

### Supporting Data

- Barczyński et al. (2013): Prospective randomised trial of 214 patients; prophylactic CCND improved staging but did not affect short-term recurrence
- Sywak et al. (2006): Prophylactic bilateral CCND reduced central compartment recurrence from 3.1% to 0% at 4.5-year follow-up
- Multiple institutional series from Japan and Korea report central compartment recurrence rates of <2% with routine CCND

## Evidence Against Prophylactic CCND

### Arguments Against Prophylactic CCND

1. **No proven survival benefit** -- No randomised trial has demonstrated improved disease-specific survival with prophylactic CCND
2. **Clinical significance of occult metastases is uncertain** -- Many occult micrometastases may never become clinically relevant; small-volume microscopic disease found by prophylactic CCND may have **little clinical relevance** (Randolph Ch 38)
3. **Increased morbidity** -- Higher rates of transient and permanent hypoparathyroidism (Viola 2015: permanent hypoparathyroidism 19.4% vs 8.0% in non-CCND group); transient RLN injury also higher (Randolph Ch 38)
4. **Staging benefit only** -- The Zetoune 2010 meta-analysis found that prophylactic CCND did not significantly reduce locoregional recurrence (OR 0.60, 95% CI 0.34--1.05, p = 0.07)
5. **Recurrence in the central compartment is salvageable** -- Central compartment recurrence can be detected on surveillance imaging and managed surgically, although with increased morbidity
6. **Number needed to treat** -- One study demonstrated that **31 patients** need to undergo prophylactic CCND to prevent one recurrence (Randolph Ch 38, Wang et al. 2013)
7. **Estimated risk of central recurrence in unoperated cN0 neck is only ~6%** (Randolph Ch 38)
8. **Low-volume surgeon concern** -- 82% of thyroid surgeries in the United States are performed by low-volume surgeons (1--2 thyroid operations per year); prophylactic CCND must be applied in the context of available surgical expertise (Randolph Ch 38)
9. **Reoperative complication rates at experienced centres may be similar** to initial dissection (Randolph Ch 38, Shen et al. 2010)

### Key Trial Data (Viola 2015)

| Outcome | TT alone (n=88) | TT + pCCND (n=93) | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nodal metastases found | N/A | 53% | -- |
| Transient hypoparathyroidism | 14.8% | 30.1% | <0.05 |
| Permanent hypoparathyroidism | 8.0% | 19.4% | <0.05 |
| Transient RLN palsy | 2.3% | 3.2% | NS |
| Permanent RLN palsy | 0% | 1.1% | NS |
| Recurrence at 5 years | Similar | Similar | NS |

## Bilateral vs Ipsilateral CCND

### Ipsilateral (Unilateral) CCND

- Removes prelaryngeal, pretracheal, and ipsilateral paratracheal nodes
- **Preferred for elective CCND with total thyroidectomy** due to lower risk of hypoparathyroidism (Randolph Ch 38) -- unless the patient has bilateral paratracheal nodal disease or bilateral multifocal primary tumours
- Lower morbidity -- preserves contralateral parathyroid glands and RLN from surgical trauma
- Appropriate for unilateral tumours without contralateral disease
- May miss contralateral paratracheal metastases (present in 25--30% of bilateral paratracheal disease)
- Can also be performed at **lobectomy** to guide decision-making for completion thyroidectomy and RAI (Randolph Ch 38)

### Bilateral CCND

- Comprehensive dissection of all central compartment node groups
- Recommended for:
  - Bilateral thyroid tumours
  - Isthmus tumours
  - Clinically apparent central node metastases (therapeutic CCND)
  - Large tumours (T3/T4)
  - When contralateral nodes are palpable or visible on imaging
  - High-risk features: extranodal extension, 5 or more nodes involved (Randolph Ch 38)
- Higher risk of bilateral RLN injury and hypoparathyroidism
- If clinically detectable nodal metastasis is identified during elective/prophylactic CCND, **conversion to therapeutic dissection** is warranted (Randolph Ch 38)

### Staged Bilateral Strategy (Randolph Ch 38)

Randolph describes a strategy to mitigate morbidity of bilateral paratracheal dissection: perform a **more aggressive ipsilateral paratracheal dissection** (potentially sacrificing the inferior parathyroid on that side with planned autotransplantation) combined with a **less aggressive contralateral paratracheal dissection** that is more parathyroid- and nerve-preserving, provided the pattern of nodal disease permits this approach.

## Surgical Technique

### Patient Positioning

- Supine with neck extension using shoulder roll
- General anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation (non-reinforced tube allows nerve monitoring)
- Intraoperative nerve monitoring (IONM) strongly recommended for CCND

### Incision and Exposure (Randolph Ch 38)

- Standard thyroidectomy incision is used for CCND
- Skin flaps raised superiorly to the **thyroid notch** and inferiorly to the **sternal notch**
- The median raphe of the strap muscles is identified and split vertically from the thyroid notch to the sternal notch -- **incomplete opening of this raphe hinders exposure** of the inferior pretracheal and paratracheal regions (Randolph Ch 38)
- Use of **loupes magnification** is associated with lower incidence of inadvertently removed parathyroid glands during thyroidectomy and paratracheal dissection (Randolph Ch 38)

### Key Technical Steps

1. **Total thyroidectomy is completed first** -- The thyroid gland is mobilised to expose the central compartment. Metastatic lymph nodes with extranodal extension strongly adherent to the thyroid should be kept on the specimen for **en bloc removal** (Randolph Ch 38)
2. **Step 1 -- Prelaryngeal dissection (Delphian nodes):** Fibrofatty tissue containing lymph nodes anterior to the cricothyroid muscle extending superiorly to the thyroid cartilage is removed. The pyramidal lobe is dissected and reflected down with the thyroid specimen. Dissection in the cricothyroid membrane area must extend deeply to recognise and excise Delphian nodes residing in the membrane indentation. Superior limit is the hyoid/thyroid notch. Care is taken to avoid injury to cricothyroid muscle, cricothyroid membrane, and perichondrium of thyroid cartilage (Randolph Ch 38)
3. **Step 2 -- Pretracheal dissection:** Begins at the inferior extent of the isthmus on the anterior trachea. The lower limit is the innominate artery (palpated to define extent). Care is taken to avoid the vulnerable brachiocephalic vein on the left. If performed separately from paratracheal dissection, staying pretracheal avoids RLN injury (Randolph Ch 38)
4. **Step 3 -- Paratracheal dissection:** The dissection begins at the cricoid with **retrograde RLN dissection** -- the RLN is identified and must be kept in view during the entire dissection. The nerve is followed until its disappearance under the innominate crossing the trachea, signifying the lower limit. The trachea is retracted medially and the carotid laterally to expose the compartment. Nodes intimately associated with the RLN are dissected gently without traction on the nerve itself (Randolph Ch 38)
5. **Right paratracheal specifics:** The RLN is followed obliquely toward the "corner" where the innominate bends into the carotid. Because the subclavian artery crosses ventral to the trachea, the right nerve is displaced ventrally. Nodes may be hidden deep to the nerve, requiring **360-degree RLN dissection** (Randolph Ch 38). Fibrofatty tissue is dissected off the prevertebral fascia and trachea
6. **Left paratracheal specifics:** The nerve traverses the tracheoesophageal groove in a more medial caudal-to-cranial direction. An arbitrary horizontal line from where the innominate crosses the trachea defines the inferior limit (unless gross disease is evident). The thoracic duct and left RLN (more variable course than right) require careful identification. The thymus at the lower limit is generally left in situ (Randolph Ch 38)
7. **Specimen orientation and labelling** -- Separate labelling of prelaryngeal, pretracheal, right paratracheal, and left paratracheal specimens for accurate staging

### Parathyroid Gland Management During CCND

- **Superior parathyroids** can generally be left in situ along with their vascular supply (Randolph Ch 38)
- **Inferior parathyroid glands** should be actively searched for and **reflected laterally with the vascular pedicle** (Randolph Ch 38)
- Some surgeons advocate marking inferior glands with clips to help identification, though clips may cause scatter on subsequent CT or MRI (Randolph Ch 38)
- At least one parathyroid gland with intact blood supply should be preserved in situ
- Devascularised parathyroid glands should be confirmed by frozen section (to protect against accidental implantation of pathologic lymph nodes) and then autotransplanted -- minced 1 mm fragments into the sternocleidomastoid muscle or anterior strap muscle (Randolph Ch 38)
- **Parathyroid autofluorescence and angiography** are new tools to optimally care for and preserve parathyroid glands and their vascular supply (Randolph Ch 38)
- Intraoperative PTH measurement may guide the need for autotransplantation

### Intraoperative Nerve Monitoring (IONM) During CCND (Randolph Ch 38)

IONM is strongly recommended during CCND, particularly bilateral dissections:

- Before contralateral nerve dissection, the **ipsilateral vagus must be stimulated** and shown to have robust EMG activity and laryngeal twitch -- this implies good ongoing function and that contralateral dissection should not result in bilateral cord paralysis
- **Proximal RLN or vagal stimulation** at the completion of one side of surgery for neural prognostication is an **important and basic tenet** in RLN monitoring (Randolph Ch 38)
- At the conclusion of the case, the RLN should be checked for integrity by stimulating the nerve proximally or the vagus nerve within the carotid sheath
- This staged neuromonitoring approach is particularly important for bilateral paratracheal dissection where bilateral RLN injury would result in airway compromise

### Wound Closure and Drainage (Randolph Ch 38)

- Meticulous haemostasis with vigilance to location of nerves and parathyroid glands
- Haemostatic agents placed in paratracheal compartments near the nerves where bipolar cautery is to be avoided
- **Routine drainage is unnecessary** -- a prospective randomised study demonstrated that absence of drains facilitates shorter hospital stays for patients undergoing total thyroidectomy with or without CCND (Randolph Ch 38)

## Morbidity of CCND

### Comparison of Complication Rates

| Complication | TT alone | TT + CCND | Revision CCND |
|---|---|---|---|
| Transient hypoparathyroidism | 10--20% | 20--40% | 30--60% |
| Permanent hypoparathyroidism | 1--3% | 3--8% | 10--20% |
| Transient RLN palsy | 2--5% | 3--6% | 5--12% |
| Permanent RLN palsy | <1% | 1--2% | 3--5% |
| Chyle leak | Rare | 1--2% | 2--5% |

The significantly higher complication rates with revision CCND form one of the strongest arguments for upfront comprehensive CCND during the index operation, particularly in high-risk patients.

### Bilateral CCND-Specific Morbidity (Randolph Ch 38)

- Bilateral paratracheal dissection has the potential for **substantial morbidity** in terms of both parathyroid and bilateral nerve injury
- The risk of bilateral RLN injury mandates staged neuromonitoring (stimulate ipsilateral vagus before contralateral dissection)
- The more aggressive ipsilateral / less aggressive contralateral strategy described above can mitigate morbidity

### Reoperative CCND Morbidity (Randolph Ch 38)

- Patients may present for reoperation with **pre-existing hypoparathyroidism or RLN injury**, creating additional challenges
- Reoperative surgery involves removal of persistent or recurrent nodal disease in a compartment-oriented fashion
- Evidence from experienced centres (Shen et al. 2010) suggests that complication rates at high-volume centres may be **similar** when reoperative central neck surgery is compared with initial dissection -- though this does not apply to the wider surgical community

## Oncologic Outcomes

### Impact on Recurrence

- **Central compartment recurrence** after total thyroidectomy without CCND: 5--9% (estimated ~6% in cN0 unoperated neck per Randolph Ch 38)
- **Central compartment recurrence** after total thyroidectomy with prophylactic CCND: 1--5%
- This difference, while suggestive, has not reached statistical significance in randomised trials
- The Zhao et al. 2017 meta-analysis (cited in Randolph Ch 38) did show a statistically significant reduction in locoregional recurrence with prophylactic CCND

### Impact on Survival

- **No randomised trial has demonstrated a survival benefit** for prophylactic CCND
- Disease-specific survival for differentiated thyroid cancer exceeds 95% at 10 years regardless of prophylactic CCND
- The excellent overall prognosis of DTC makes it extremely difficult to demonstrate a survival benefit from prophylactic CCND (would require thousands of patients with prolonged follow-up)
- Lymph node metastases may increase disease-specific mortality in all patients but **most significantly in the elderly** (rationale for AJCC 8th edition age threshold change to 55 years per Randolph Ch 38)

### Impact on Thyroglobulin Surveillance

- Prophylactic CCND significantly reduces postoperative stimulated Tg levels
- Lower Tg facilitates interpretation of biochemical surveillance and may reduce the need for RAI

## Summary of Indications (Randolph Ch 38 -- Box 38.1)

| Therapeutic CCND | Elective/Prophylactic CCND |
|---|---|
| To remove clinically apparent disease (cN1) | To provide accurate pathologic staging to guide adjuvant therapy |
| To minimise risk of morbidity from reoperation for initially undertreated disease | To reduce regional recurrence |
| | To minimise risk of morbidity from reoperation for recurrent/persistent disease |
| | Medullary thyroid carcinoma |

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **Level VI boundaries** -- hyoid to suprasternal notch, medial carotid sheath to medial carotid sheath; **Level VII** lies below VI, bordered by the innominate artery and brachiocephalic vein -- this distinction is frequently tested
2. **Right paratracheal anatomy** is three-dimensional with nodes potentially deep to the RLN; left paratracheal is flatter and more two-dimensional -- a Randolph-specific concept important for technique questions
3. **Occult central nodal metastases** occur in 26--60% of cN0 PTC (depending on the study) -- but most are clinically insignificant
4. **Prophylactic CCND is the most controversial topic** in thyroid cancer surgery -- examiners expect a balanced discussion of both sides
5. **Therapeutic CCND is universally recommended** -- never controversial
6. **Node plucking/berry picking is condemned** -- always perform compartmental dissection (Randolph Ch 38)
7. **Viola 2015** is the landmark RCT: similar oncologic outcomes but higher permanent hypoparathyroidism with prophylactic CCND
8. **Revision CCND** carries 3--5x higher complication rates than primary CCND -- this is the strongest argument for upfront comprehensive dissection
9. **NNT of 31** for prophylactic CCND to prevent one recurrence (Wang et al., cited in Randolph Ch 38)
10. **IONM with staged vagal stimulation** before contralateral dissection is a basic tenet in bilateral CCND (Randolph Ch 38)
11. **Always rule out pheochromocytoma before thyroidectomy** in familial/MTC cases
12. **US sensitivity for central nodes is <25% when thyroid is present** (Randolph Ch 38) -- a negative preoperative US does not reliably exclude central nodal metastases
13. **AJCC 8th edition**: N0a (confirmed benign nodes) vs N0b (no radiologic/clinical evidence), and age threshold changed to 55 years
