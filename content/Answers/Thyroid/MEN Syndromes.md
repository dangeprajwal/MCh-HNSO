## Introduction

Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN) syndromes are autosomal dominant inherited cancer predisposition syndromes characterised by the occurrence of tumours in two or more endocrine glands. For the head and neck surgeon, MEN2A and MEN2B are of paramount importance because medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) is the most consistent and often the earliest manifestation. Understanding the genotype-phenotype correlations, screening protocols, and management algorithms — particularly the timing of prophylactic thyroidectomy — is essential for optimal outcomes.

Hereditary MTC is unique among hereditable cancer syndromes (Randolph Ch 27): it affects up to 25% of all MTC patients (more frequent than many common hereditary tumours); it displays a strong genotype-phenotype correlation utilised worldwide for risk assessment; and disease progression from C-cell hyperplasia to MTC is a stochastic, age-dependent sequence of events requiring the acquisition of additional somatic mutations ("second hits") beyond the germline RET mutation. Serum calcitonin better reflects the gene carrier's stage of C-cell disease than the underlying germline mutation alone, allowing individualised timing of surgical intervention.

## Classification of MEN Syndromes

### Overview

| Syndrome | Gene | Chromosome | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| MEN1 | Menin (MEN1) | 11q13 | Parathyroid adenomas, pituitary adenomas, pancreatic NETs |
| MEN2A | RET proto-oncogene | 10q11.2 | MTC, phaeochromocytoma, primary hyperparathyroidism |
| MEN2B | RET proto-oncogene | 10q11.2 | MTC (earliest/aggressive), phaeochromocytoma, mucosal neuromas, marfanoid habitus |
| MEN4 | CDKN1B (p27) | 12p13 | MEN1-like phenotype; parathyroid, pituitary, gonadal tumours |
| FMTC | RET proto-oncogene | 10q11.2 | MTC only (no other endocrinopathies); now classified as MEN2A variant |

### FMTC — Now an Abortive Form of MEN2 (Randolph)

Originally defined as an entity requiring a minimum of four affected kindreds, FMTC is now conceived as an abortive form of MEN2 that frequently converts to a full MEN2 phenotype with the subsequent development of phaeochromocytoma or hyperparathyroidism during the patient's lifetime. Conversion is the rule for highest-risk codon 918 (ATA HST) and high-risk codon 634 (ATA H), common for moderate-to-high-risk codons 620 and 618, and rare for low-to-moderate-risk codons 768, 790, 804, and 891. Some 6.5% to 9.5% of patients with seemingly sporadic MTC harbour germline RET mutations that cannot be recognised unless all relevant exons are screened.

### MEN1 — Wermer Syndrome (Brief Overview)

- **Gene:** Menin — tumour suppressor gene (loss-of-function mutations)
- **Inheritance:** Autosomal dominant with >95% penetrance by age 50
- **Classic triad:** Parathyroid hyperplasia (95%), pancreatic islet cell tumours (40-70%), pituitary adenomas (30-40%)
- **3 P's mnemonic:** Parathyroid, Pancreas, Pituitary
- **Head and neck relevance:** Parathyroid surgery often required; rare thymic or bronchial carcinoids

### MEN4

- Recently identified syndrome caused by CDKN1B mutations (encodes p27Kip1, a cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor)
- Phenotypically resembles MEN1 but without MEN1 gene mutations
- Rare — fewer than 100 cases reported
- Features: hyperparathyroidism, pituitary adenomas, adrenal, gonadal, and renal tumours

## MEN2A — Sipple Syndrome

### Clinical Features

| Component | Prevalence | Typical Age at Onset | Details |
|---|---|---|---|
| MTC | ~100% | 5-25 years | Bilateral, multifocal; preceded by C-cell hyperplasia |
| Phaeochromocytoma | ~50% | 20-40 years | Often bilateral (50-80%); rarely malignant (<5%); catecholamine-secreting |
| Primary hyperparathyroidism | 20-30% | 20-40 years | Multiglandular hyperplasia (not adenoma); usually mild |
| Cutaneous lichen amyloidosis | 5-10% (codon 634) | Variable | Pruritic scapular pigmented skin lesion; pathognomonic of codon 634 |
| Hirschsprung disease | 2-5% | Neonatal | Associated with specific RET exon 10 mutations |

**Randolph — Additional MEN2A Detail:**
- MTC represents the usual first component; annual primary tumour growth is estimated at 0.4-0.5 mm in node-negative carriers and 1.2 mm (moderate-risk mutations) to 2.6 mm (high-risk mutations) in node-positive carriers
- Multifocal tumour growth is far more common in hereditary than sporadic MTC (65% vs 8%)
- PCC coexists synchronously with MTC at diagnosis in 35% of codon 634 carriers; PCC is rarely the initial MEN2 component (4% of moderate-to-high-risk carriers, 0% of highest-risk carriers)
- HPT prevalence is 19.1% among codon 634 carriers; mean manifestation age is 34 years (earliest 10 years) for high-risk and 32-54 years for moderate-risk mutations
- CLA occurs in ~9% of MEN2A families, almost always associated with codon 634; itching is the first manifestation, located in dermatomes C4-T5 at the interscapular region; CLA often precedes clinical MTC

### Hirschsprung Disease in MEN2A — "Janus Genes" (Randolph)

Some 6-16% of RET families with moderate-to-high-risk mutations in exon 10 (codons 620, 618, and more rarely 611, 609) develop a peculiar phenotype combining a "gain of function" MEN2A phenotype with a "loss of function" Hirschsprung phenotype. These genotypes have been dubbed "Janus genes" (referencing the Roman god of doorways facing either way). The mechanism involves trapping of precursor RET receptor proteins in the endoplasmic reticulum, preventing transport to the cell membrane. Decreased cell surface RET receptor concentrations cause premature arrest of the craniocaudal migration of enteric neurons during weeks 5-12 of gestation, resulting in aganglionosis. HSCR frequently precedes MTC and PCC clinically.

### RET Mutations in MEN2A

| Codon | Exon | Risk Category (ATA 2015) | Penetrance |
|---|---|---|---|
| 609, 611, 618, 620 | 10 | Moderate | Lower penetrance; later onset MTC |
| 630 | 11 | Moderate | Similar to exon 10 mutations |
| 634 | 11 | High | Most common MEN2A mutation (85%); highest penetrance for all three components |

The cysteine-rich extracellular domain mutations (codons 609, 611, 618, 620, 630, 634) cause ligand-independent dimerisation and constitutive activation of the RET tyrosine kinase receptor.

## MEN2B

### Clinical Features

| Component | Prevalence | Details |
|---|---|---|
| MTC | ~100% | **Earliest onset and most aggressive** of all MEN2 subtypes; can present in infancy |
| Phaeochromocytoma | ~50% | Bilateral; catecholamine-secreting |
| Mucosal neuromas | >95% | Lips, tongue, eyelids, conjunctivae — PATHOGNOMONIC; present from birth |
| Marfanoid habitus | >95% | Tall, thin, long limbs, high-arched palate, pectus excavatum |
| Ganglioneuromatosis | >95% | Intestinal; causes constipation, megacolon, feeding difficulties |
| **NO hyperparathyroidism** | — | Key distinguishing feature from MEN2A |

### Detailed MEN2B Phenotype (Randolph Ch 27)

MEN2B is defined by the combination of MTC and PCC; the absence of HPT; and the presence of ocular, oral, intestinal, and musculoskeletal neuromas, neurofibromas, ganglioneuromas, and unique musculoskeletal disorders. The nonendocrine stigmata develop in an age-dependent fashion and frequently precede the clinical manifestation of MTC:

**Oral stigmata:** Nodules on lips, anterior tongue, and buccal mucosa; bumpy lips; prominent labial frenula; dental irregularities. Mucosal neuromas and neurofibromas are the pathological substrate.

**Ocular stigmata:** Conjunctival nodules with recurrent conjunctivitis; thickened corneal nerves (corneal fibres) visible on slit lamp examination (found as early as age 3 months); entropium (infolding of eyelid margins); reduced tear production causing conjunctivitis sicca. "Tearless crying" in MEN2B infants — nearly all parents notice low tear production early on; this should prompt RET screening.

**Intestinal ganglioneuromatosis:** ~90% of MEN2B patients develop intestinal symptoms — excessive bloating, flatulence, abdominal distention, constipation, recurrent abdominal pain ("pseudo-Hirschsprung disease"). Ganglioneuromatosis in the submucosal and myenteric plexuses is almost exclusively seen in MEN2B. More than 70% of patients aged 6 years or younger at diagnosis had prior evidence of intestinal ganglioneuromatosis. ATA guidelines recommend RET gene analysis on intestinal tissue showing ganglioneuromatosis.

**Musculoskeletal stigmata:** Marfanoid habitus with elongated extremities, hyperflexible joints, muscle weakness, mid-face hypergnathism, pectus deformities, scoliosis, slipped capital femoral epiphysis, and foot abnormalities (pes cavus, pes excavatus). Caused by thickened nerve sheaths reducing nerve conduction velocity and action potential amplitudes.

**Age-dependent emergence of MEN2B stigmata within the first year of life (Randolph, Table 27.3 — M918T carriers):**

| Stigma | Present by Age 1 Year | On Follow-Up |
|---|---|---|
| Dry eyes | 86% | 91% |
| Chronic nasal congestion | 61% | 71% |
| Pes cavus | 30% | 75% |
| Conjunctivitis | 24% | 55% |
| Hyperflexible joints | 19% | 52% |
| Bumpy lips | 17% | 96% |
| Oral neuromas | 17% | 92% |
| Skeletal disorders | 0% | 38% |
| Phaeochromocytoma | 0% | 28% |

### Early Clinical Clues in MEN2B Infants (Randolph)

Because >90% of MEN2B germline mutations arise de novo, without any family history, clinical recognition is the only route to early diagnosis. Two critical early clues in MEN2B infants are:
1. **"Tearless crying"** — reduced tear production noted by parents within the first months of life
2. **"Pseudo-Hirschsprung disease"** — chronic constipation, failure to thrive, suckling weakness beginning months after birth

These clinical signs precede the more characteristic MEN2B stigmata and should prompt immediate RET screening. Sadly, even though classic stigmata are often obvious, many patients are not recognised as having MEN2B in a timely manner. The window of surgical curability closes rapidly — most MEN2B patients older than 4 years at diagnosis are not curable.

### RET Mutations in MEN2B

| Codon | Exon | Risk Category (ATA 2015) | Details |
|---|---|---|---|
| 918 (M918T) | 16 | **Highest** | >95% of MEN2B; most aggressive; MTC can develop in first year of life |
| 883 (A883F) | 15 | High | Rare; slightly later onset than M918T |

The M918T mutation affects the intracellular tyrosine kinase domain, causing constitutive kinase activation without requiring dimerisation.

**Significance of M918T (Randolph):** This is the single most aggressive RET mutation. MTC develops by the time of birth in carriers of this highest-risk mutation. PCC coexists synchronously with MTC at diagnosis in 60% of codon 918 carriers. A truly "prophylactic" thyroidectomy (before MTC develops) is exceptional because of the extremely early onset. Exceptionally, a 6-month-old infant with M918T was found free of neoplastic C-cell disease, and another MEN2B patient showed only C-cell hyperplasia at 14 months — illustrating the very narrow window for cure.

**Atypical MEN2B mutations (<5% of cases):** Include A883F (single mutation, less aggressive), and rare double mutations V804M/E805K, V804M/Y806C, and V804M/S904C. These tend to be less aggressive and less penetrant than M918T, consistent with in vitro studies.

### Distinguishing MEN2A from MEN2B

| Feature | MEN2A | MEN2B |
|---|---|---|
| MTC aggressiveness | Moderate — onset 5-25 years | Severe — onset infancy/early childhood |
| Phaeochromocytoma | 50% | 50% |
| Hyperparathyroidism | 20-30% | **ABSENT** |
| Mucosal neuromas | Absent | Present — pathognomonic |
| Marfanoid habitus | Absent | Present |
| Ganglioneuromatosis | Absent | Present (pseudo-Hirschsprung) |
| Hirschsprung disease | Present (exon 10, true aganglionosis) | Absent (ganglioneuromatosis instead) |
| Cutaneous lichen amyloidosis | Present (codon 634) | Absent |
| Most common mutation | C634R (codon 634) | M918T (codon 918) |
| Prophylactic thyroidectomy | By age 5 (high risk) | **Within first 6 months of life** (highest risk) |
| De novo mutations | ~5% | ~50% (no family history) |
| Thickened corneal nerves | Rarely present | Commonly present |

## Diagnostic Workup

### Universal RET Testing

The 2015 ATA guidelines mandate RET mutation testing for **all patients with MTC**, regardless of family history:
- Up to 6-7% of apparently sporadic MTC harbour germline RET mutations (Elisei 2019); Randolph cites 6.5-9.5%
- Identification triggers cascade screening of all first-degree relatives
- Testing should include sequencing of exons 5, 8, 10, 11, 13, 14, 15, and 16
- DNA-based screening has no false-positive findings (barring sample/laboratory error) and has largely replaced calcitonin-based screening for early diagnosis of gene carriers (Randolph)

### Comprehensive MEN2 Workup

| Investigation | Purpose | Details |
|---|---|---|
| Serum calcitonin | MTC marker (basal) | Elevated in MTC; correlates with tumour burden |
| Stimulated calcitonin | Early MTC detection | Pentagastrin or calcium stimulation test |
| CEA | MTC prognostic marker | Elevated in advanced MTC; rapid rise indicates dedifferentiation |
| Plasma/urine metanephrines | Phaeochromocytoma screen | **MUST be performed before any surgery** — undiagnosed phaeo is lethal |
| 24-hour urine catecholamines | Phaeochromocytoma confirmation | VMA, metanephrines, normetanephrines |
| Serum calcium and PTH | Hyperparathyroidism screen | Only in MEN2A |
| Neck ultrasound | Thyroid nodule assessment | Identify MTC, assess lymph nodes |
| CT chest/abdomen | Staging | If MTC confirmed |
| Adrenal CT/MRI | Phaeochromocytoma localisation | If biochemically positive |
| MIBG scan | Functional phaeo imaging | If CT/MRI equivocal |

**Calcitonin in very young children (Randolph):** Mean serum calcitonin levels are physiologically higher in the first year of life (9.81 pg/mL; range 2.0-48.9) and second year (4.56 pg/mL; range 2.0-14.7) than in adults. This must be considered when interpreting paediatric calcitonin results. Calcitonin screening is superior to neck ultrasonography in predicting MTC.

### Critical Rule: Phaeo First — Detailed Workup (Randolph)

> **ALWAYS exclude and treat phaeochromocytoma BEFORE thyroidectomy.** Manipulation of the thyroid during surgery can precipitate a catecholamine crisis in an undiagnosed phaeochromocytoma, causing hypertensive emergency, cardiac arrhythmia, and potentially death. The management sequence is: **screen for phaeo → treat phaeo if present → then proceed with thyroidectomy.**

**Randolph — Phaeochromocytoma evaluation detail:**
- MEN2-associated PCC displays impaired terminal differentiation and produces **epinephrine more frequently than norepinephrine** — this is why more carriers sustain **paroxysmal** rather than sustained hypertension
- When diagnosis is delayed, minor triggers can provoke excessive catecholamine secretion, igniting an often lethal hypertensive crisis (typically around the 4th-5th decade); this was historically the most common cause of death among RET gene carriers
- With routine use of sensitive screening tests, PCC is increasingly diagnosed at an asymptomatic stage before clinical crises occur
- PCC develops from adrenal medullary hyperplasia upon allelic loss at chromosome 1p or deletion of the VHL gene
- **No manipulation of these tumours should be undertaken before effective alpha-receptor blockade is in place**
- PCC is rarely seen before puberty in MEN2B; youngest reported MEN2B patient with PCC was 12 years old
- PCC can develop as early as ages 8-10 years in highest/high-risk mutations (ATA HST/H) and as early as age 20 in moderate-risk mutations

### Synchronous MTC and PCC — Surgical Sequencing (Randolph)

When MTC and PCC coexist, two surgical options:
1. **Subtotal "tissue-sparing" adrenalectomy** — minimises risk of steroid dependency while leaving the possibility of a second PCC in the adrenal remnant
2. **Total adrenalectomy** — for tumours occupying most or all of the adrenal gland

Followed by total thyroidectomy either:
- **One-stage:** Adrenalectomy then thyroidectomy in the same session for limited neck disease
- **Two-stage:** Adrenalectomy first, then thyroidectomy as a separate procedure for advanced neck disease

**Alpha-receptor blockade must be in place before adrenalectomy in all cases.**

## Management of MTC in MEN2

### Prophylactic Thyroidectomy — Timing Based on RET Risk Category

The 2015 ATA guidelines stratify RET mutations into three risk categories that determine the timing of prophylactic thyroidectomy:

| ATA Risk Category | Mutations | Prophylactic Thyroidectomy Timing | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Highest** | M918T (codon 918) | Within first 6 months of life; ideally by 6 months | MTC can develop in infancy; metastatic MTC reported in children <1 year |
| **High** | C634R, A883F | By age 5 years | High penetrance; MTC develops in childhood; lymph node mets by age 5-10 years |
| **Moderate** | Codons 609, 611, 618, 620, 630, V804M, others | May delay beyond age 5 if: annual calcitonin normal AND annual US normal | Lower penetrance; later MTC onset; individualised approach |

### Integrated DNA-Biochemical Strategy for Prophylactic Surgery (Randolph)

Randolph Ch 27 describes a refinement of purely age-based timing: an integrated DNA-based/biochemical concept that may indicate the extent of C-cell disease more effectively than age-related risk assessment alone:
- In 308 RET gene carriers, lymph node metastases were found **only with increased basal calcitonin levels** typically exceeding 30 pg/mL
- Carriers with **normal basal calcitonin** may safely forego lymph node dissection, sparing them the morbidity of central neck dissection (hypoparathyroidism, RLN palsy)
- Prophylactic thyroidectomy can be postponed beyond the standard age recommendation if **stimulated calcitonin levels are normal and basal calcitonin remains within normal limits**
- This is particularly relevant for very young children with smaller, more delicate anatomy and less compliance with thyroxine substitution
- The combined DNA-biochemical strategy offers more leeway than a purely age-based concept, with less potential for undertreatment or overtreatment

**For MEN2B:** Most patients have elevated basal calcitonin at diagnosis, necessitating total thyroidectomy and compartment-oriented lymph node dissection without delay. Surgery should be performed as early in life as possible but may also improve survival in late-onset patients.

### Surgical Extent for MTC in MEN2

| Scenario | Surgery |
|---|---|
| Prophylactic (calcitonin normal, US normal) | Total thyroidectomy ALONE (no neck dissection) |
| Calcitonin elevated or US abnormal | Total thyroidectomy + bilateral central compartment neck dissection (level VI) |
| Clinically apparent MTC | Total thyroidectomy + bilateral CCND + ipsilateral/bilateral lateral neck dissection (levels II-V) |
| Calcitonin >200 pg/mL | Consider bilateral lateral ND in addition to CCND |

**Randolph — Extent of lymph node dissection refined by calcitonin:**
- Patients with MTC and no evidence of cervical or distant metastases: total thyroidectomy + level VI dissection
- Lateral compartment dissection (levels II-V) may be considered based on calcitonin levels
- When imaging is positive in the ipsilateral lateral compartment but negative contralaterally, contralateral neck dissection should be considered if basal calcitonin >200 pg/mL (above this threshold, bilateral lymph node metastases and distant metastases are increasingly present)
- Compartment-oriented microdissection removes tumour deposits from the vicinity of vital neck structures, aiding in prevention of tumour-related complications and supporting systemic targeted therapies

### Reoperation for Persistent/Recurrent Hereditary MTC (Randolph)

- Systematic lymph node dissection for persistent MTC can be curative after removal of no more than 5 lymph node metastases at initial operation and when pre-reoperation calcitonin is <=1000 pg/mL
- Above these thresholds, biochemical cure is not possible; strategy shifts to locoregional tumour control
- Bilateral compartment-oriented surgery can achieve biochemical cure in at least half the patients with pretherapeutic basal calcitonin <=1000 pg/mL, but not in those with levels >10,000 pg/mL
- For detection of recurrent metastatic MTC: 18F-DOPA PET/CT or FDG-PET/CT is better than conventional imaging

### Parathyroid Management During Thyroidectomy

- Identify all four parathyroid glands
- If devascularised: autotransplant into non-dominant forearm (MEN2A) or sternocleidomastoid (preferable if future surgery unlikely)
- In MEN2A with hyperparathyroidism: subtotal parathyroidectomy or total parathyroidectomy with autotransplantation
- Cryopreserve resected parathyroid tissue

### PHPT Management in MEN2A — Randolph Approach

Randolph Ch 27 provides important nuances on parathyroid management in MEN2A:
- HPT in MEN2A is usually **mild**, with often no more than one or two parathyroid glands involved (unlike MEN1 where all four are typically hyperplastic)
- **Diagnosis:** Should not be based solely on elevated intact PTH in the absence of hypercalcaemia; pseudonodular parathyroid hyperplasia may be present histopathologically even with normal calcium/PTH levels
- **Surgical strategy:** Removal of **grossly enlarged parathyroid glands only** (limited parathyroidectomy), rather than subtotal or total parathyroidectomy with autografting
- **Total parathyroidectomy with autografting has been abandoned** because it carries a 6% risk of permanent hypoparathyroidism
- Normal parathyroids should be **preserved in situ** wherever feasible — this is standard practice in neck surgery for hereditary MTC
- Only in the event of **complete devascularisation** should normal parathyroids be autografted to minimise postoperative hypoparathyroidism

## Management of Phaeochromocytoma

### Surgical Approach

- **Laparoscopic adrenalectomy** is preferred for tumours <6 cm
- Cortical-sparing (partial) adrenalectomy increasingly favoured to prevent lifelong steroid dependence in bilateral disease
- Preoperative alpha-blockade mandatory: phenoxybenzamine (10-20 mg BID, titrate up) or doxazosin for 10-14 days pre-operatively
- Beta-blockade added AFTER adequate alpha-blockade if tachycardia persists (NEVER beta-block first — precipitates unopposed alpha-mediated crisis)
- Adequate volume resuscitation preoperatively

### Screening and Surveillance

- Annual biochemical screening from age 8 in MEN2A moderate risk, from age 5 in high risk, and from birth in highest risk
- If initial screen negative, annual plasma metanephrines

## Screening of Family Members

### Cascade Genetic Screening

1. **Index case identified** — confirm specific RET mutation
2. **Screen all first-degree relatives** for the same mutation
3. **Mutation-positive relatives:** Enter age-appropriate surveillance and prophylactic surgery pathway
4. **Mutation-negative relatives:** No further surveillance needed (excluded from the kindred)

**Randolph — Population-level impact of systematic RET screening:**
Widespread use of systematic RET gene analysis in all patients with a personal or family history of MEN2 or MTC results in: (1) depletion of the reservoir of symptomatic, previously unrecognised gene carriers (index patients); (2) eradication of MTC, notably node-positive MTC, among gene carriers from known RET families (nonindex patients); (3) increased biochemical cure among nonindex patients; and (4) a fall in patient age at thyroidectomy among nonindex patients.

### DNA-Based Screening — The Diagnostic Gold Standard (Randolph)

- DNA-based screening of "hot spot" regions in exons 10, 11, 13, 14, 15, and 16 of the RET gene is the diagnostic gold standard
- Barring sample/laboratory error, there are no false-positive findings with DNA-based screening
- Has largely replaced calcitonin-based screening for early diagnosis of gene carriers
- Biochemical screening with calcitonin can occasionally yield false-positive findings due to nonneoplastic ("reactive") C-cell hyperplasia, which prompted unnecessary thyroidectomies in the pre-molecular era

### Screening Protocol by Age

| Age | Investigations |
|---|---|
| Birth (MEN2B) | RET testing if family history or clinical features |
| 3-5 years (MEN2A High) | Annual calcitonin, annual neck US, annual metanephrines |
| 5-10 years (MEN2A Moderate) | Annual calcitonin, annual neck US, annual metanephrines |
| >10 years | Add calcium/PTH screening (MEN2A only); continue above |
| Lifelong | Annual metanephrines (even after thyroidectomy — phaeo can appear at any age) |

## Genetic Counseling

### Key Principles

- **Autosomal dominant** — 50% risk to each offspring
- Pre-test and post-test counseling mandatory
- Discuss implications for insurance, employment, psychological impact
- Reproductive counseling: preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) available
- Minors: parents consent for RET testing; in MEN2B, testing should be done as early as possible (ideally at birth)

### Documentation of MEN2 Pedigree

- Three-generation family tree
- Record all endocrine tumours, ages at diagnosis, outcomes
- Identify at-risk individuals who have not been tested
- Maintain a family registry for longitudinal follow-up

## Complications and Outcomes After Prophylactic Surgery (Randolph)

In a large series of 167 children undergoing prophylactic thyroidectomy (Randolph Ch 27):
- Early prophylactic thyroidectomy was viable in experienced hands, sparing older children the morbidity of delayed surgery
- **Without central node dissection:** Transient hypoparathyroidism 4%, permanent hypoparathyroidism 0% (children <=3 years)
- **With central node dissection:** Permanent hypoparathyroidism 0% (149 children, mean age 6 years)
- Transient hypoparathyroidism was more frequent in **older** children (32% in the oldest age group vs 3% in the youngest) — likely because delayed surgery necessitated more extensive dissection
- Three children developed transient RLN palsy, all of whom had undergone central node dissection; all resolved within 6 months
- Postoperative calcitonin normalisation achieved in 99.1% (114/115) of children with raised preoperative values
- No residual structural disease or recurrence observed

**Key surgical principles to minimise complications:**
1. Refrain from central node dissection in gene carriers with normal basal calcitonin levels
2. Use in situ preservation of normal parathyroid glands
3. After prophylactic surgery, ensure careful follow-up for recurrence, sufficient thyroxine substitution, and psychosocial care

## Surveillance Protocols After Thyroidectomy

### Post-Thyroidectomy Monitoring

| Marker | Frequency | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Serum calcitonin | Every 6-12 months | Undetectable = biochemical cure; rising = recurrence |
| CEA | Every 6-12 months | Rising CEA suggests dedifferentiation/aggressive recurrence |
| Neck US | Annually | Detect local/regional recurrence |
| Plasma metanephrines | Annually | Screen for phaeo (lifelong) |
| Calcium/PTH | Annually (MEN2A) | Monitor for hyperparathyroidism |
| CT chest/abdomen | If calcitonin rising | Detect distant metastases |
| Calcitonin doubling time | Calculate at each visit | <6 months: aggressive; 6-24 months: intermediate; >24 months: indolent |

### Management of Persistent/Recurrent Hypercalcitoninemia

| Calcitonin Level | Approach |
|---|---|
| <150 pg/mL, stable | Observe with imaging |
| >150 pg/mL, rising | Intensive imaging: CT neck/chest/abdomen, bone scan, liver MRI |
| Rising with negative imaging | Consider DOPA-PET/CT or FDOPA-PET (most sensitive for MTC) |
| Localised recurrence | Compartment-oriented revision neck dissection |
| Metastatic, progressive | Systemic therapy (vandetanib, cabozantinib, selpercatinib) |

## Genotype-Phenotype Correlation — Detailed

### Machens et al. (2003) Key Findings

This European multicenter study of 207 RET mutation carriers aged <=20 years who underwent prophylactic thyroidectomy demonstrated:

| RET Codon | C-Cell Hyperplasia (median age) | MTC (median age) | Lymph Node Mets (median age) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 918 (MEN2B) | Birth | <1 year | ~2 years |
| 634 (MEN2A) | ~2 years | ~5 years | ~10 years |
| 611, 618, 620 | ~5 years | ~10 years | ~15-20 years |

This data provides the evidence base for the risk-stratified timing of prophylactic thyroidectomy.

## Exam-Relevant Key Points

> [!tip] High-Yield Points
> - MEN2B has the most aggressive MTC — can metastasise in the first year of life; prophylactic thyroidectomy within 6 months
> - MEN2B has NO hyperparathyroidism — this is a classic exam question distinguishing it from MEN2A
> - Mucosal neuromas (lips, tongue, eyelids) are pathognomonic of MEN2B
> - 50% of MEN2B cases are de novo mutations with no family history — clinical recognition is essential
> - **"Tearless crying" and "pseudo-Hirschsprung disease" are the earliest clues to MEN2B in infants** (Randolph) — should prompt immediate RET screening
> - ALWAYS screen for and treat phaeochromocytoma BEFORE thyroidectomy
> - MEN2-associated PCC produces epinephrine > norepinephrine, causing **paroxysmal** rather than sustained hypertension (Randolph)
> - Alpha-blockade BEFORE beta-blockade for phaeo management — never reverse the order
> - Universal RET testing is mandatory in ALL MTC patients — 6-9.5% of "sporadic" cases are actually hereditary
> - Codon 634 is the most common MEN2A mutation and has the highest penetrance for all three MEN2A components
> - FMTC (familial MTC) is now classified as a variant of MEN2A, not a separate syndrome — it frequently converts to full MEN2 phenotype
> - **M918T is the single most aggressive RET mutation** — MTC develops by the time of birth; >90% arise de novo; most patients older than 4 years at diagnosis are not curable (Randolph)
> - HPT in MEN2A is usually mild; limited parathyroidectomy (removal of enlarged glands only) is preferred; total parathyroidectomy with autografting has been abandoned (6% permanent hypoparathyroidism risk) (Randolph)
> - **Calcitonin-guided surgical strategy:** Lymph node metastases found only with basal calcitonin >30 pg/mL; patients with normal calcitonin can safely forego neck dissection (Randolph)
> - Calcitonin doubling time is the most important prognostic marker in follow-up
