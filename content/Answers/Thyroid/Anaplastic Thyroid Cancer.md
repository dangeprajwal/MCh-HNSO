## Epidemiology

- **Incidence:** 1-2% of all thyroid carcinomas; incidence has remained largely stable over time despite rising DTC rates (Randolph Ch 28)
- **Most aggressive** thyroid malignancy -- accounts for 14-39% of thyroid cancer deaths despite low incidence; Randolph notes this figure is 20-50%
- **Median age at diagnosis:** 60-65 years (Randolph); some series report 65-70 years
- **Female:Male ratio:** 1.5-2:1 (female preponderance in population-based studies)
- **Association with pre-existing thyroid disease:**
  - ~20-50% have a history of pre-existing or co-existing DTC (PTC or FTC)
  - Nearly half of ATCs show coexistent DTC on surgical pathology (Randolph)
  - ~20-30% arise in longstanding goitre
  - Suggests **de-differentiation** from pre-existing well-differentiated thyroid carcinoma
- **No clear regional differences** in incidence; conflicting evidence on iodine deficiency and ATC pathogenesis (Randolph)
- Rarely, ATC can present in younger patients in the 2nd or 3rd decade of life

## Pathogenesis -- De-Differentiation Model

ATC is believed to arise through a **stepwise de-differentiation process** from well-differentiated thyroid carcinoma:

```
Normal thyroid follicular cell
    ↓ (BRAF V600E or RAS mutation)
Well-differentiated thyroid carcinoma (PTC/FTC)
    ↓ (+ TERT promoter mutation)
Poorly differentiated thyroid carcinoma (PDTC)
    ↓ (+ TP53 mutation, + PI3K/AKT pathway activation)
Anaplastic thyroid carcinoma (ATC)
```

- The high frequency of BRAF V600E in ATC (25-41%) suggests origination from pre-existing papillary cancers that commonly carry this alteration (Randolph)
- Additional mutations (TP53, TERT promoter) accumulate and are associated with incremented mutational burden

### Molecular Landscape of ATC

| Gene/Pathway | Frequency | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **BRAF V600E** | 25-45% (25-41% per Randolph) | Therapeutic target (dabrafenib + trametinib); suggests PTC origin |
| **TP53** | 50-80% (29-73% per Randolph) | Most common mutation; hallmark of de-differentiation; may show robust p53 staining on IHC |
| **TERT promoter** | 65-75% (54-73% per Randolph) | Early event in progression; associated with incremented mutational burden |
| **RAS** (NRAS > HRAS > KRAS) | 15-25% | Alternative to BRAF as driver |
| **PIK3CA** | 10-25% | PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway activation |
| **PTEN** (loss) | 10-20% | PI3K/AKT pathway disinhibition |
| **NF1/NF2** | 5-10% | RAS pathway regulators |
| **CDKN2A/2B** | 20-30% | Cell cycle deregulation |
| **Methyltransferases (KMT2A, 2C, 2D)** | Variable | Epigenetic regulation (Randolph) |
| **SWI/SNF complex** | 10-20% | Chromatin remodelling |
| **MMR deficiency (MSH2, MSH6)** | 5-10% | Potential immunotherapy target (Randolph) |
| **High TMB** | 10-15% | Immunotherapy response predictor |
| **PD-L1 expression** | 20-70% | Immunotherapy biomarker; ATCs infiltrated with tumour-associated macrophages/neutrophils suggesting stimulated but restrained immune microenvironment (Randolph) |

**Key Co-Occurring Mutations:**
- BRAF V600E + TP53 -- most common combination in ATC
- TERT + BRAF or TERT + RAS -- associated with aggressive behaviour
- TP53 + PIK3CA -- synergistic effect on de-differentiation

## Pathology -- Morphologic Variants (Randolph)

ATC has **different morphologic variants**, confounding morphologic assessment. Classification is not always possible on FNA analysis. Importantly, **different morphologic variants do not affect clinical management** (Randolph):

| Variant | Key Features |
|---|---|
| **Sarcomatoid (spindle cell)** | Spindle-shaped cells; may stain focally for vimentin; resembles sarcoma |
| **Squamoid** | Squamous-appearing epithelial cells; must be distinguished from primary thyroid SCC |
| **Giant cell** | Pleomorphic tumour giant cells; historically "giant cell carcinoma of thyroid" |
| **Osteoblastic** | Osteoid/bone formation within tumour |
| **Paucicellular** | Sparse cellularity with extensive fibrosis; can mimic Riedel's thyroiditis |
| **Rhabdoid** | Rhabdoid-type cells with eccentric nuclei and eosinophilic cytoplasm |
| **Carcinosarcoma** | Combined epithelial and mesenchymal differentiation |

### IHC Profile (Randolph)
- ATC usually shows **minimal to no staining** for thyroglobulin, calcitonin, and TTF-1
- May show **robust p53 and PAX8 staining** -- PAX8 is especially useful in pointing to thyroid origin
- May stain for **cytokeratin** and show **focal vimentin staining** (especially spindle cell variants)
- Poorly differentiated cancers lose IHC staining for nearly all but cytokeratin markers, making it difficult to distinguish ATC from metastatic cancers from other sites

**Immunohistochemistry Profile of ATC:**

| Marker | ATC | Lymphoma | MTC |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cytokeratin (AE1/AE3) | +/- (variable) | - | + |
| TTF-1 | Usually - (lost) | - | + |
| Thyroglobulin | Usually - (lost) | - | - |
| PAX8 | +/- (may retain; often positive -- Randolph) | - | + |
| p53 | Robust staining (Randolph) | - | - |
| CD20, CD45 | - | + | - |
| Calcitonin | - | - | + |
| CEA | - | - | + |
| Chromogranin | - | - | + |
| Ki-67 | Very high (>30%) | Variable | Low |

**Molecular Testing:**
- BRAF V600E testing is **mandatory** -- identifies patients eligible for dabrafenib + trametinib
- Comprehensive genomic profiling (NGS panel) recommended when available
- Assess MSI/TMB for immunotherapy candidacy

## Clinical Presentation

### Typical Presentation
- **Rapidly enlarging neck mass** -- the hallmark of ATC
  - Doubling time: days to weeks (compared to months for DTC)
  - Often in a patient with known longstanding goitre or previous thyroid cancer
  - Occasionally, ATC is diagnosed **incidentally** after thyroidectomy for biopsy-proven DTC (Randolph)
- **Compressive symptoms:**
  - Dyspnoea and stridor (tracheal compression/invasion)
  - Dysphagia (oesophageal compression/invasion)
  - Hoarseness (RLN involvement)
  - Superior vena cava syndrome (rare, mediastinal extension)
- **Fixed, hard thyroid mass** on palpation
- **Cervical lymphadenopathy** in 40-50% (up to 75% -- Randolph)
- **Skin involvement** -- erythema, ulceration, fungation
- **Pain** -- more common than in DTC; neck discomfort is a frequent early symptom
- **Occasional bleeding** from the mass (Randolph)

### Sites of Distant Metastases
- **Lung:** 40-50% (most common)
- **Bone:** 5-15%
- **Brain:** 5-10%
- **Liver:** 5-10%
- **Adrenal:** Rare
- Note: Distant metastases are present at diagnosis in **20-50%** of patients

### Natural History if Untreated (Randolph)
- Death occurs most commonly from **local tumour invasion, particularly asphyxiation** -- over half of untreated patients die from suffocation from an invasive neck tumour
- In patients treated with chemoradiation +/- surgery, death is primarily from **distant metastases**, suggesting progress in locoregional tumour control
- ATC is inherently a **systemic disease at diagnosis** -- the majority, if not all patients, will eventually develop distant metastases

## Staging -- All ATC is Stage IV

ATC has a **unique staging system** in the AJCC 8th edition -- **all ATC is classified as Stage IV** regardless of tumour size or nodal status. Randolph notes that T designations in AJCC 8th edition are now consistent with DTC T designations, but all ATC remains Stage IV:

### AJCC 8th Edition ATC Staging (Randolph)

| T Stage | N Stage | M Stage | AJCC Stage |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1-T3a | N0/NX | M0 | **IVA** |
| T1-T3a | N1 | M0 | **IVB** |
| T3b | Any N | M0 | **IVB** |
| T4 | Any N | M0 | **IVB** |
| Any T | Any N | M1 | **IVC** |

### Simplified Stage Description

| Stage | Definition | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **IVA** | Intrathyroidal, N0 | Tumour confined to the thyroid without nodal disease; potentially resectable |
| **IVB** | Extrathyroidal extension OR N1 | Gross extrathyroidal extension, or any nodal metastases; locally advanced |
| **IVC** | Distant metastases | Any T, any N, M1 |

### Staging Rationale
- Even small intrathyroidal ATC carries extremely poor prognosis
- The staging emphasises **resectability** and **extent of disease** rather than tumour size
- Treatment approach differs significantly between IVA, IVB, and IVC
- Note: In AJCC 8th edition, **N1 disease now upstages to IVB** (even with intrathyroidal tumour), unlike earlier editions

## Diagnostic Workup

### Biopsy and Pathological Diagnosis

**Core Needle Biopsy (CNB):**
- Preferred over FNA for suspected ATC -- provides tissue architecture (Randolph emphasises core biopsy is preferred as FNA may be inadequate for morphologic analysis)
- FNA may be insufficient due to tumour necrosis and heterogeneity; cellularity varies depending on diagnosis and extent of necrosis
- FNA may be inadequate if additional IHC stains or genomic interrogation is needed (Randolph)
- **Critical distinction required** from:
  - **Thyroid lymphoma** -- rapidly enlarging thyroid, Hashimoto's background; flow cytometry, CD20+; morphology and lymphoid markers initially differentiate (Randolph)
  - **Medullary thyroid carcinoma** -- calcitonin+, CEA+, TTF-1+, synaptophysin+; negative calcitonin helps exclude MTC (Randolph)
  - **Metastatic carcinoma** to thyroid -- poorly differentiated cancers lose IHC staining for nearly all but cytokeratin markers (Randolph)
  - **Squamous cell carcinoma** of adjacent structures (extremely rare primary thyroid SCC)
  - **Sarcoma**
- If distant disease is present at diagnosis, **biopsy of metastatic lesion** helps determine accurate staging, as metastatic DTC and ATC may coexist (Randolph)
- **Expert pathologic evaluation** cannot be overestimated; an accurate and expeditious diagnosis is required (Randolph)

### Imaging Workup

1. **CT neck, chest, abdomen, and pelvis** (with contrast) -- extent of local disease, systemic staging; neck ultrasound alone has limited role in ATC due to limited evaluation of disease posterior to/invading the trachea (Randolph)
2. **PET-CT** -- full systemic staging; ATC is uniformly and intensely FDG-avid; associated with necrosis in some patients (Randolph)
3. **Brain MRI** (preferred) or contrast-enhanced CT brain -- recommended in all ATC patients (Randolph)
4. **Laryngoscopy** -- vocal cord function and upper aerodigestive tract assessment; critical for surgical planning (Randolph)
5. **Bronchoscopy** -- if airway invasion suspected; assess subglottic and upper tracheal regions
6. **Oesophagoscopy** -- consider to assess oesophageal invasion (Randolph)

**Timeline:** Comprehensive evaluation should be completed within **a few days** so that emergent treatment can be started; delays may permit ATC to progress from resectable to unresectable (Randolph)

## Management -- Multimodal Approach

### Multidisciplinary Team (Randolph)

Most centres of excellence specifically **expedite** the care of ATC patients. The multidisciplinary treatment team should be assembled rapidly and include: surgery, medical oncology, endocrinology, radiation oncology, pathology, and palliative care specialists. Treatment is directed at the most imminently threatening tumour component (ATC); treatment of concomitant DTC is deferred until ATC is effectively treated.

### Treatment Algorithm by Stage

#### Stage IVA -- Resectable Intrathyroidal Disease

**Recommended Approach: Surgery + Adjuvant CRT**

1. **Surgery:**
   - Total thyroidectomy with central neck dissection
   - Therapeutic lateral neck dissection if clinically positive nodes
   - Goal: **R0 or R1 resection** (complete gross resection) -- Randolph stresses that R0/R1 resection is the critical surgical goal
   - **Debulking surgery leaving gross residual disease is unacceptable** and should not be undertaken except under rare circumstances (Randolph)
   - If R0 is not achievable, consider neoadjuvant therapy first
   - Surgical resection is a **strong predictor of overall survival** in ATC (Randolph)

2. **Adjuvant Chemoradiation (CRT):**
   - Begin within **1-4 weeks** of surgery (Randolph); prolonged convalescence must be avoided as it delays adjuvant treatment
   - EBRT: **60-70 Gy** in 30-35 fractions using IMRT
   - Concurrent chemotherapy: **Weekly doxorubicin** (10-20 mg/m2) or **taxane-based** regimen
   - Randolph/Mayo Clinic protocol: docetaxel and doxorubicin (15-20 mg/m2 each, weekly) or paclitaxel and carboplatin (50 mg/m2 and AUC 2, respectively, weekly) concurrently with IMRT to 60-66 Gy
   - Alternative: hyperfractionated RT (1.6 Gy BID) with concurrent doxorubicin

3. **Targeted Therapy:**
   - If BRAF V600E positive: consider **neoadjuvant dabrafenib + trametinib** to facilitate resection
   - Continue targeted therapy post-operatively

#### Stage IVB -- Locally Advanced (Extrathyroidal / N1)

**Recommended Approach: CRT +/- Surgery**

1. **Primary Chemoradiation:**
   - EBRT: 60-70 Gy with concurrent chemotherapy
   - Taxane-based or doxorubicin-based concurrent chemotherapy
   - IMRT technique to spare uninvolved structures

2. **Surgery:**
   - May be considered **after neoadjuvant CRT** if disease becomes resectable
   - Goal remains R0/R1 resection; debulking leaving gross residual disease is not recommended (Randolph)
   - Procedures requiring laryngectomy, major arterial/tracheal resection, or permanent tracheostomy are **not advisable** as the morbidity is seldom justified given overall prognosis (Randolph)
   - If BRAF V600E+, neoadjuvant dabrafenib/trametinib may convert unresectable to resectable disease

3. **BRAF-Directed Therapy (if BRAF V600E positive):**
   - **Neoadjuvant dabrafenib (150 mg BID) + trametinib (2 mg daily)**
   - May convert unresectable to resectable disease
   - Response rates: ~69% in BRAF V600E-mutant ATC

#### Stage IVC -- Distant Metastases

**Recommended Approach: Systemic Therapy +/- Palliative RT**

1. **Systemic Therapy (first-line options):**
   - BRAF V600E positive: **Dabrafenib + trametinib** (strongest evidence; FDA-approved)
   - BRAF wild-type: **Lenvatinib** or **clinical trial**
   - Consider **immunotherapy** (see below)
   - Traditional chemotherapy: doxorubicin-based (limited efficacy -- 16% response rate as single agent; 26% with cisplatin combination per Randolph)
   - Paclitaxel: 21-50% response rate, median OS 6.7 months (Randolph)
   - Randolph notes: surgical resection in the presence of extensive metastatic ATC may **delay systemic therapy and worsen outcomes**

2. **Palliative Radiation:**
   - For symptomatic local disease
   - High-dose IMRT (60-70 Gy) for stage IVC with low-volume disease may be considered but may have limited survival benefit and greater toxicity (Randolph)
   - Airway stabilisation
   - Bone metastases pain control

3. **Best Supportive Care:**
   - Should be offered concurrently
   - Early palliative care referral improves quality of life
   - Patient goals of care must guide therapy election (Randolph)

## Role of Surgery in ATC (Randolph)

### Surgical Assessment -- Pre-Surgical Checklist (Randolph Box 28.1)

Before surgery is considered, the following must be addressed:

1. **Immediate airway evaluation** -- does patient require emergent tracheostomy for stridor?
2. **Detailed surgical airway evaluation:**
   - Fibreoptic evaluation including vocal cord status, laryngeal, subglottic, and upper tracheal regions
   - Contrast-enhanced axial scanning (CT or MRI preferred over US)
   - Consider oesophagoscopy and bronchoscopy to assess invasion
3. **Is R0/R1 resection realistically achievable?**
   - Without prohibitive collateral damage (no laryngectomy, arterial/tracheal resection, no permanent tracheostomy)
   - Is the assembled surgical team optimal for the tasks at hand?
4. **Multidisciplinary evaluation** -- surgery, endocrinology, radiation oncology, medical oncology, pathology, palliative care
5. **Confirmed pathologic diagnosis** -- core biopsy, negative calcitonin, expert pathology review
6. **Complete radiographic staging** -- including brain imaging
7. **Patient comorbidities and fitness** for surgery
8. **Patient decision-making capacity and goals of care**

### Exclusionary Conditions for Surgery (Randolph)
- Laryngectomy expected/required to achieve R0/R1
- Bone, arterial, tracheal resections required
- Anticipated prolonged postoperative recovery incompatible with timely adjuvant CRT
- Unacceptably high risk of bilateral vocal cord paralysis
- Apparent distant metastatic ATC (stage IVC)
- Patient condition or goals of care unsuitable for surgery

### Surgical Approach (Randolph)
- **Traditional open approach** is appropriate (not minimally invasive); adequately wide incision for thorough dissection
- Resection of strap musculature (especially ipsilateral sternothyroid) or division of SCM may improve exposure
- Approach the area of greatest resection challenge first
- Placing a **bougie dilator or ETT in the oesophageal lumen** aids identification and avoidance of luminal violation
- Metastatic cervical lymphadenopathy occurs in ~75% of ATC patients, necessitating nodal dissections
- ATC nodal metastases are frequently **extracapsular** with blood vessel wall adventitia involvement -- may require vein resection
- Greater care needed to avoid phrenic nerve and sympathetic ganglia injury due to common posterior invasion
- Sternotomy or limited manubrial split may be needed for upper mediastinal extension
- **Surgical complications** (haemorrhage, wound infection, chylous fistulae, vocal cord paralysis, dysphagia, hypoparathyroidism) must be recognised early as delayed adjuvant therapy worsens outcomes

### Neoadjuvant Dabrafenib/Trametinib Before Surgery (Randolph)
- In a pilot study of 11 BRAF V600E+ ATC patients with initially unresectable disease:
  - Neoadjuvant dabrafenib + trametinib was feasible in **6/11 patients (55%)**
  - **Complete resection achieved in all 6**, with R0 in 4/6
  - Post-surgical pathology showed **<5% tumour viability** in 4/6 patients
  - Complications: wound infection, temporary unilateral vocal cord palsy, pulmonary embolism (one each)
- Demonstrates feasibility of neoadjuvant targeted therapy to convert unresectable to resectable disease

## BRAF V600E-Directed Therapy

### Dabrafenib + Trametinib (FDA Approved)

**ROAR Basket Study (Subbiah et al. 2018):**
- **Design:** Phase II, open-label, multicentre
- **Population:** 16 patients with BRAF V600E-mutant locally advanced or metastatic ATC
- **Regimen:** Dabrafenib 150 mg BID + trametinib 2 mg daily
- **Results:**
  - Confirmed ORR: **69%** (11/16)
  - Complete response: 6% (1/16)
  - Partial response: 63% (10/16)
  - 12-month PFS: 79%
  - 12-month OS: 80%
  - Median DOR, PFS, and OS: not reached at time of publication
- **Updated Analysis (2022):**
  - Expanded cohort of 36 patients
  - ORR: 56%
  - Median OS: 14.5 months
  - Durable responses in many patients

**Clinical Application:**
- BRAF V600E testing is mandatory in all ATC patients
- Can be used in neoadjuvant, adjuvant, or metastatic setting
- Rapid responses (often within 2-4 weeks) enable surgical conversion
- Continuation until progression, toxicity, or cure
- Randolph notes: BRAF inhibitors as single agents show mostly brief responses; **combination with MEK inhibitor is essential**

**Common Adverse Effects:**
- Pyrexia (most common)
- Fatigue
- Nausea
- Skin toxicity (rash, photosensitivity)
- Peripheral oedema
- Decreased LVEF (monitor echocardiography)

### Lenvatinib

- **Multi-kinase TKI** targeting VEGFR1-3, FGFR1-4, PDGFR-alpha, RET, KIT
- Some efficacy in ATC (data from small series and expanded access)
- Randolph notes: MKI treatment of ATC has been **disappointing** overall compared to targeted BRAF/MEK inhibition
- Considered for BRAF wild-type patients
- Dose: 24 mg daily (dose reductions common)
- AE profile: hypertension, diarrhoea, fatigue, palmar-plantar erythrodysaesthesia

### Other Targeted Agents (Randolph)
- **ALK inhibitors** -- extremely rare cases of ATC demonstrate ALK fusions that respond exquisitely to ALK inhibitors
- **Efatutazone** (PPAR-gamma agonist) -- under investigation
- **Pazopanib** -- investigated in RTOG 0912 (addition to initial CRT)

## Immunotherapy

### Rationale for Immunotherapy in ATC
- High **tumour mutational burden (TMB)** in some ATC cases
- **PD-L1 expression** in 20-70% of ATC
- ATCs are infiltrated with **tumour-associated macrophages and neutrophils**, suggesting a stimulated but restrained immune microenvironment (Randolph)
- Some ATC cases show **MSI-high** status (MMR deficiency -- MSH2, MSH6 per Randolph)

### Pembrolizumab
- Anti-PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor
- **FDA-approved** for MSI-H/TMB-high tumours (tumour-agnostic approval)
- Case series and retrospective data show responses in selected ATC patients
- Most promising when combined with targeted therapy or chemoradiation
- Consider for:
  - MSI-H or TMB >=10 mut/Mb
  - PD-L1 CPS >=1
  - After progression on targeted therapy
  - In combination with dabrafenib/trametinib (emerging data)

### Combination Approaches (Emerging)
- **Dabrafenib + trametinib + pembrolizumab** -- being studied in clinical trials
- **Lenvatinib + pembrolizumab** -- under investigation
- **Atezolizumab + vemurafenib + cobimetinib** -- BRAF-mutant tumours

## Airway Management in ATC (Randolph)

Maintenance of airway patency is **critical and challenging** in ATC.

### Tracheostomy -- Controversial and Ethically Complex

**ATA guidelines do not recommend routine tracheostomy** in ATC patients with a stable airway, as it neither improves survival outcomes nor quality of life (Randolph).

### Indications for Tracheostomy (Randolph)
- **Imminent suffocation** in patients with unresectable tumours whose dyspnoea does not respond to high-dose corticosteroids
- **Impending airway compromise** at initial evaluation requiring clinical stabilisation before treatment planning
- Patients undergoing chemoradiotherapy (not primary surgical resection) with significant and **progressing airway compromise**
  - In this scenario, patient should also be presented with the option of palliative management of dyspnoea without tracheostomy (Randolph)

### Arguments For Tracheostomy
- Prevents death from acute airway obstruction
- May allow time for systemic therapy to take effect (especially dabrafenib/trametinib)
- Required for perioperative airway management in some cases
- Improves quality of life if airway compromise is the primary symptom

### Arguments Against Tracheostomy
- **Does not alter disease course** in most patients
- May prolong suffering in patients with terminal disease
- Tracheostomy complications in ATC: tumour growth through stoma, bleeding, infection
- Risk of creating a **fungating tracheostomy site**
- An overlying tumour makes tracheostomy technically difficult (Randolph)

### Technical Considerations for Tracheostomy in ATC (Randolph)
- Better performed **under general anaesthesia** with the patient intubated, not emergently under less-controlled conditions
- Experienced anaesthesiologist is critical for airway management during induction
- Intubation options: direct laryngoscopy, flexible bronchoscope, fibreoptic glide-type laryngoscope
- Typically palpated **landmarks may be obscured** by tumour -- preoperative analysis of imaging is essential
- Incision is placed over discerned tracheal location (rarely midline); portion of tumour may need to be resected to expose trachea
- **Extended-length tracheostomy tubes** are frequently required (increased skin-to-trachea distance due to tumour)
- **Tracheal stents** may provide airway stabilisation as a last resort, but placement is often very problematic

### Current Recommendation (ATA 2021)
- **Individualised decision** based on:
  - Resectability and treatment intent
  - Expected response to systemic therapy (BRAF V600E status)
  - Patient's wishes and goals of care
  - Overall performance status and prognosis
- If tracheostomy is performed, it should be in the context of a **treatment plan with realistic goals**
- Prophylactic tracheostomy before starting targeted therapy in BRAF V600E-positive patients may be reasonable

### Maintenance of Enteral Access (Randolph)
- Dysphagia is common; may result in malnutrition, weight loss, dehydration
- **Gastrostomy placement** should permit critical fluid and nutritional support, particularly when CRT is planned
- ~75% of patients undergoing CRT will eventually need **temporary feeding tube placement** (Randolph)
- Percutaneous gastrostomy may require variation in technique due to narrowed oesophagus and airway compromise at thoracic inlet

## Radiation Therapy in ATC

### Curative-Intent EBRT
- **IMRT has become a cornerstone** of locoregional treatment in ATC (Randolph)
- **Dose:** 60-70 Gy in 30-35 fractions; Randolph/Mayo Clinic: 66-70 Gy in 33-35 fractions over 6.5-7 weeks
- 66 Gy in 30 fractions for small-volume disease or as adjuvant dose after R0 resection (Randolph)
- **Total radiation dose >40-50 Gy** has been consistently associated with improved survival (Randolph)
- **Concurrent chemotherapy** improves local control
- **Hyperfractionated RT:** 1.6 Gy BID to 57.6 Gy with concurrent doxorubicin -- some data suggest improved local control but toxicity is high with uncertain benefit (Randolph)
- **Target volume:** thyroid bed, involved nodes, at-risk regions
- About **20% of ATC patients survive 2 years or beyond** after multimodality therapy (Randolph)

### Concurrent Chemoradiotherapy Regimens (Randolph)

No consensus on optimal chemotherapy agent. Selection based on side effects, patient fitness, and institutional preference:

| Regimen | Dose | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Doxorubicin + docetaxel** (Mayo Clinic) | 15-20 mg/m2 each, weekly | Randolph/Mayo preferred regimen |
| **Paclitaxel + carboplatin** | 50 mg/m2 + AUC 2, weekly | Alternative Mayo Clinic regimen |
| **Doxorubicin alone** | 10-20 mg/m2/week | Historical regimen (Kim protocol) |
| **Doxorubicin + cisplatin** | 60 mg/m2 + 90-120 mg/m2, q4w | French protocol (Schlumberger, de Crevoisier) |

| Crevoisier (1999-2000) | 30 | 24 | 40 | Doxorubicin+cisplatin | 10 | 47 |
| Sherman/Mayo (1984-07) | 37 | 51 | 58 | Doxorubicin | 6 | 50 |
| Prasongsook/Mayo (2003-15) | 30 | 90 | 66 | Doxorubicin+docetaxel | **21** | **93** |

- The Mayo Clinic expanded cohort (48 patients) with doxorubicin+docetaxel+IMRT to 60-66 Gy + surgery confirmed: **median OS 21 months with multimodal therapy vs 4 months with palliative/BSC**; locoregional relapse in only 2/27 evaluable patients (Randolph)

### Palliative RT
- **Dose:** 20-30 Gy in 5-10 fractions
- For symptomatic relief of local compression
- Bone metastases palliation
- Brain metastases (whole brain RT or stereotactic radiosurgery)

### RAI Therapy
- **NOT effective** in ATC -- tumour loses NIS expression and RAI avidity
- Should not be used as primary treatment
- No role for RAI in ATC except in cases with a dominant DTC tumour metastatic component requiring RAI therapy itself (Randolph)

## Prognosis

### Overall Survival
- **Median survival:** 3-6 months (historical data; Randolph cites 3-6 months)
- **1-year OS:** 11-35% (historical/population-based) to 50-59% (modern era with targeted therapy)
  - NCDB 2017 study: 1-year survival still only 11% in population-based data (Randolph)
  - British Columbia population-based study: 1-year OS 19%; almost all patients not referred for treatment died within 1 month (Randolph)
- **5-year OS:** <5% (historical)

### Prognostic Factors

| Factor | Favourable | Adverse |
|---|---|---|
| Stage | IVA (intrathyroidal) | IVC (distant metastases) |
| Age | <60 years | >70 years |
| Tumour size | <5 cm | >7 cm |
| Completeness of resection | R0 resection | Unresectable |
| DTC component | Present (coexistent DTC) | Absent (Randolph) |
| BRAF status | BRAF V600E (targeted therapy available) | BRAF wild-type |
| WBC count | Normal | Leukocytosis (>10,000) |
| Multimodal treatment | Received surgery + CRT | Single modality |
| Performance status | ECOG 0-1 | ECOG >=2 |
| RT dose | >40-50 Gy (Randolph) | <40 Gy |

- SEER multivariate analysis: only age <60, lesser disease extent, and combined surgery + EBRT were independent prognostic factors (Randolph)
- NCDB multivariate analysis: age, distant metastasis, chemotherapy, surgical resection, and radiation dose were independently associated with survival (Randolph)
- **Younger age, limited stage disease (IVA/IVB), and multimodality treatment** are consistently associated with better survival (Randolph)

### Improving Outcomes (Maniakas et al. 2020)

The MD Anderson experience (479 patients, 2000-2019) demonstrated:

| Time Period | 1-Year OS | Median OS |
|---|---|---|
| 2000-2013 | 35% | 0.67 years |
| 2014-2016 | 47% | 0.88 years |
| 2017-2019 | 59% | 1.31 years |

This improvement is attributed to:
- Introduction of BRAF-directed therapy (dabrafenib + trametinib)
- Immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors)
- Multimodal treatment approaches
- Clinical trial participation
- Early multidisciplinary team involvement

### Failure Patterns (Randolph)
- Treatment with multimodal approaches results in long-term survivors in stage IVA and sometimes stage IVB
- Local recurrence rates have decreased with multimodal therapy
- ATC is inherently a **systemic disease** -- majority of patients will eventually develop distant metastases
- Both locoregional and distant recurrences can be **rapid and fatal**
- Follow-up should be at a **much shorter schedule** than other cancers, using regular physical examinations and imaging (FDG-PET or CT)

## Clinical Trials and Future Directions

### Ongoing Areas of Investigation
1. **Combination targeted + immunotherapy** -- dabrafenib/trametinib + pembrolizumab
2. **Novel targets:** RET inhibitors (selpercatinib), NTRK inhibitors (larotrectinib, entrectinib) for fusion-positive ATC; ALK inhibitors for ALK fusion-positive ATC (Randolph)
3. **Re-differentiation strategies** -- attempting to restore RAI avidity
4. **CAR-T cell therapy** -- early preclinical data
5. **HDAC inhibitors** -- epigenetic modulation
6. **Neoadjuvant targeted therapy** to convert unresectable to resectable disease
7. **RTOG 0912** -- randomised study examining addition of pazopanib to initial CRT (Randolph)
8. **Efatutazone** (PPAR-gamma agonist) -- under investigation (Randolph)

## Key Examination Points

1. ATC represents **1-2% of thyroid cancers** but 20-50% of thyroid cancer deaths (Randolph)
2. **All ATC is Stage IV** -- IVA (intrathyroidal N0), IVB (extrathyroidal or N1), IVC (distant metastases)
3. **BRAF V600E testing is mandatory** -- dabrafenib + trametinib achieves 69% ORR; BRAF inhibitor monotherapy shows only brief responses (Randolph)
4. **TP53 mutation** is the hallmark of de-differentiation (29-73% per Randolph; up to 80% in some series)
5. **Morphologic variants** (sarcomatoid/spindle cell, squamoid, giant cell, osteoblastic, paucicellular, rhabdoid, carcinosarcoma) -- do NOT affect clinical management (Randolph)
6. Must **distinguish from lymphoma** and MTC -- PAX8 positivity supports thyroid origin; calcitonin negativity excludes MTC (Randolph)
7. **Stage IVA: surgery + CRT** is the standard approach; R0/R1 resection is the surgical goal
8. **Stage IVB: CRT +/- surgery** (neoadjuvant targeted therapy may convert to resectable); laryngectomy is rarely appropriate in ATC (Randolph)
9. **Stage IVC: systemic therapy** (targeted > immunotherapy > chemotherapy); surgical resection in extensive metastatic disease may delay systemic therapy and worsen outcomes (Randolph)
10. **Debulking leaving gross residual disease is unacceptable** in ATC (Randolph)
11. Median survival historically 3-6 months; Mayo Clinic multimodal therapy achieves **21 months median OS** (Randolph)
12. **Tracheostomy** should be individualised, not routine -- only for imminent airway compromise; technically difficult due to tumour (Randolph)
13. RAI is **NOT effective** in ATC
14. Pembrolizumab may be effective in **MSI-H/TMB-high** ATC
15. ~75% of patients undergoing CRT will need **temporary feeding tube** placement (Randolph)
16. **RT dose >40-50 Gy** is consistently associated with improved survival (Randolph)
17. If untreated, **over half of patients die from suffocation** from invasive neck tumour (Randolph)
