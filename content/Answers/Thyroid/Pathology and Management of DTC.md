## WHO Classification of Thyroid Tumours

The WHO Classification of Tumours of Endocrine Organs (5th edition, 2022) provides the definitive pathological framework for thyroid neoplasms. The classification has undergone significant revisions, most notably the introduction of NIFTP (Nikiforov 2016) and the separation of high-grade follicular cell-derived carcinomas. Per Randolph (Ch 41, Baloch & LiVolsi), the WHO classification introduced several important borderline categories including "Follicular Tumour of Uncertain Malignant Potential" (FTUMP) and "Well-Differentiated Tumour of Uncertain Malignant Potential" (WDTUMP), and Hurthle cell carcinoma is now classified as a separate entity distinct from FTC.

### Overview of Major Categories

| Category | Key Entities |
|---|---|
| **Benign follicular cell-derived** | Follicular adenoma, oncocytic (Hurthle cell) adenoma |
| **Borderline/precursor** | NIFTP, hyalinizing trabecular tumour, FTUMP, WDTUMP |
| **Malignant -- low grade** | Papillary thyroid carcinoma (classical and non-aggressive variants), follicular thyroid carcinoma (minimally invasive) |
| **Malignant -- high grade** | Poorly differentiated thyroid carcinoma, high-grade differentiated thyroid carcinoma |
| **Malignant -- undifferentiated** | Anaplastic thyroid carcinoma |
| **Malignant -- C-cell derived** | Medullary thyroid carcinoma |
| **Other** | Thyroid lymphoma, sarcoma, metastatic tumours |

### Borderline/Precursor Categories (Randolph Ch 41)

- **FTUMP (Follicular Tumour of Uncertain Malignant Potential):** Encapsulated follicular-patterned tumour with partial capsular invasion that does not traverse the entire capsule thickness, lacking PTC-type nuclei. The great majority behave as benign adenomas
- **WDTUMP (Well-Differentiated Tumour of Uncertain Malignant Potential):** Partial capsular invasion with abnormal nuclear features not completely characteristic of PTC. Clinicians may find it difficult to establish treatment strategies, as data lack complete clinical follow-up
- **Hyalinizing Trabecular Neoplasm:** Follicular-derived lesion growing in nests surrounded by dense hyaline stroma with PTC-like nuclear features; positive for thyroglobulin and CK19; some harbour RET rearrangements suggesting a relationship to PTC, but all cases with classic histology behave in a benign fashion

## Papillary Thyroid Carcinoma (PTC)

PTC is the most common thyroid malignancy, accounting for 80--85% of all thyroid cancers. It is characterised by distinctive nuclear features rather than papillary architecture. Per Randolph (Ch 19, Sipos & Haugen), the incidence has tripled over the past three decades in the United States, with 87% of newly diagnosed cancers measuring 2 cm or smaller and 49% being <1 cm, although cancers of all sizes are increasing, indicating that overdiagnosis is not solely to blame. Approximately 5% of nonmedullary thyroid cancers are hereditary, associated with syndromes such as familial adenomatous polyposis, Gardner syndrome, Cowden disease, Carney complex type 1, and Werner syndrome (Randolph Ch 19).

### Diagnostic Nuclear Features (WHO Criteria)

Per Randolph (Ch 41, Baloch & LiVolsi), the diagnosis of PTC rests on nuclear morphology rather than architectural pattern:

1. **Nuclear enlargement** with overlapping and crowding -- nuclei are relatively large and irregular in shape; cytoplasm concentrated in apical or basal portions causes neighbouring nuclei to overlap
2. **Nuclear clearing** ("Orphan Annie eyes" / ground-glass nuclei) -- nuclear heterochromatin is concentrated near the membrane, causing the central portion to appear pale and empty
3. **Nuclear grooves** (longitudinal folds in nuclear membrane)
4. **Intranuclear cytoplasmic inclusions** (pseudoinclusions) -- cytoplasmic indentations into the nucleus
5. **Irregular nuclear contours** with moulding -- the nucleolus is often inconspicuous and eccentric, lying near the nuclear membrane

### Psammoma Bodies (Randolph Ch 41)

One third to one half of papillary carcinomas form laminated calcific spherules (psammoma bodies), measuring 5--100 micrometres, appearing in stroma or lymphatics. A psammoma body found in normal thyroid tissue, cervical lymph nodes, or perithyroidal soft tissue should prompt a search for PTC. These should be distinguished from calcified inspissated colloid in adenomas/adenomatous nodules.

### PTC Variants

#### Classical PTC

- Papillary and follicular architecture with well-developed fibrovascular cores covered by a single layer of epithelial cells (Randolph Ch 41)
- Characteristic nuclear features
- Accounts for 55--65% of all PTC
- Frequent BRAF V600E mutation (36--69%; Randolph Ch 19)
- Generally favourable prognosis

#### Follicular Variant of PTC (FVPTC)

- Follicular growth pattern but PTC-type nuclear features
- The most common histologic variant after the classical type (Randolph Ch 41)
- Per Randolph (Ch 41), at least four distinct subtypes have been described:
  1. **Infiltrative FVPTC:** Unencapsulated, resembles classic PTC growth pattern but entirely composed of follicles; spreads and behaves as ordinary PTC with multifocal involvement, lymphatic and nodal metastases
  2. **Diffuse follicular variant:** Total replacement of thyroid by tumour; both lymph node and distant (pulmonary) metastases are common
  3. **Encapsulated FVPTC:** If noninvasive, reclassified as **NIFTP** (Nikiforov 2016); if invasive (capsular/vascular invasion), follicular variant of PTC with behaviour more akin to follicular carcinoma
  4. **Macrofollicular variant:** Composed almost entirely of distended colloid-filled follicles resembling adenomatoid nodules; rare papillae and occasional focus of infiltration confirm diagnosis
- **Molecular features (Randolph Ch 41, Ch 22):** RAS mutations are seen exclusively in FVPTC and not in classic PTC; RET translocations and BRAF mutations common in classic PTC are rare in FVPTC. Gene expression profiling and TCGA analysis show FVPTC chromosomal aberrations are more comparable to follicular adenoma/carcinoma than to classical PTC
- **Hybrid theory (Randolph Ch 41):** Encapsulated FVPTC may represent a hybrid of papillary carcinoma and follicular adenoma/carcinoma -- encapsulated FVPTC without invasion (NIFTP) behaves as follicular adenoma; those with invasion behave as follicular carcinoma

#### Aggressive Variants

| Variant | Frequency | Key Features (Randolph) | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tall cell** | ~10% (Randolph Ch 41) | Cells 3x taller than wide; often oncocytic; strong BRAF V600E association; ETE and vascular invasion in 20--25%; average 20 years older than usual PTC patients | Aggressive -- mortality rate of 25% (Randolph Ch 41) |
| **Hobnail** | 1--3% | Apical nuclear bulging ("hobnail" pattern); micropapillary growth pattern similar to aggressive subtypes in ovary, breast, bladder, kidney, lung (Randolph Ch 41) | Very aggressive -- high mortality |
| **Columnar cell** | <1% | Tall columnar cells with marked nuclear stratification; CDX2 expression | Originally reported as aggressive; recent data show more indolent course when confined within the thyroid (Randolph Ch 41) |
| **Diffuse sclerosing (DSV)** | 0.7--6.6% (Randolph Ch 41) | Diffuse involvement of one or both lobes; dense sclerosis, extensive squamous metaplasia, diffuse lymphocytic infiltration, numerous psammoma bodies, tumour deposits in intraglandular lymphatics; background chronic lymphocytic thyroiditis; young children and young adults | Virtually always accompanied by LN metastases; often pulmonary metastases; however acceptable prognosis with aggressive treatment |
| **Solid/trabecular** | 1--3% | Solid and trabecular growth; associated with radiation exposure; follicles or papillae may be rare in primary but present in LN metastases (Randolph Ch 41) | In middle-aged/elderly, associated with loss of differentiation suggesting aggressive neoplasm; in young people, prognostic impact less clear |

#### Warthin-Like Variant (Randolph Ch 41)

Composed of oxyphilic cells (oncocytes/Hurthle cells) arising in thyroid altered by lymphocytic thyroiditis, with central cystic change. Named for morphologic resemblance to parotid Warthin tumour. Behaves similar to conventional PTC.

### Noninvasive Follicular Thyroid Neoplasm with Papillary-Like Nuclear Features (NIFTP)

- **Definition:** Encapsulated/well-demarcated follicular-patterned thyroid neoplasm with PTC-type nuclei, WITHOUT invasion (capsular or vascular) or high-grade features
- **Significance:** Reclassified from "encapsulated noninvasive FVPTC" (a cancer) to NIFTP (a borderline/precursor lesion)
- **Histopathologic criteria (Randolph Ch 41):** The Endocrine Pathology Society (EPS) Working Group evaluated a large cohort of FVPTC with 10--26 years of follow-up, confirming that noninvasive encapsulated FVPTC were free of recurrences and any metastatic involvement. The definition requires a noninvasive follicular-patterned neoplasm with PTC nuclear cytology, lack of necrosis, and absence of papillary architecture
- **Clinical impact:** Patients do NOT require total thyroidectomy, RAI, or TSH suppression
- **Management:** Lobectomy alone is sufficient (equivalent to thyroid adenoma management)
- **Outcome:** Nikiforov 2016 reported 0% recurrence in 109 patients at 13-year median follow-up

### Immunohistochemistry of PTC (Randolph Ch 41)

- Almost all PTCs express TTF-1 and thyroglobulin
- **CK19** can be helpful in diagnosis of PTC (different cytokeratin profile from normal thyroid)
- **HBME-1** expressed in up to 68% of PTCs
- Other markers: Galectin-3, S-100, CD44, CD57, CA-125
- Despite extensive immunohistochemical studies, morphology remains the single most important diagnostic tool

## Follicular Thyroid Carcinoma (FTC)

Accounts for 10--15% of thyroid cancers. Unlike PTC, FTC does NOT have PTC-type nuclear features and is defined by the presence of capsular and/or vascular invasion. Per Randolph (Ch 22, Durante & Filetti), the incidence of FTC has remained stable or decreased over time, in contrast to the rising incidence of PTC, due to (1) reclassification of many tumours previously diagnosed as FTC to follicular variant of PTC following the 1977 paradigm shift by Chen and Rosai, and (2) increasing iodine supplementation worldwide.

### Molecular Pathogenesis (Randolph Ch 22, Ch 41)

| Marker | Role in FTC |
|---|---|
| **RAS mutations** (NRAS, HRAS, KRAS) | Found in 49% of FTCs; constitutively activates MAPK pathway; associated with clinically aggressive FTCs (Randolph Ch 41) |
| **PAX8-PPARgamma** | Present in 36% of FTCs, 16% of FVPTC, 11% of follicular adenomas; 100% of radiation-associated FTCs harbour this rearrangement (Randolph Ch 22) |
| **Combined** | RAS mutations or PAX8-PPARgamma present in 88% of FTCs (Randolph Ch 22) |
| **PI3K-Akt pathway** | FTC is more dependent on PI3K-Akt than MAPK pathway, distinguishing it from PTC (Randolph Ch 22) |
| **TERT promoter** | Listed among molecular markers of FTC; associated with aggressive behaviour |
| **PTEN, PIK3CA, TSHR** | Additional molecular markers identified in FTC (Randolph Ch 22) |

### Classification by Degree of Invasion

Per Randolph (Ch 41, Baloch & LiVolsi and Ch 22, Durante & Filetti), follicular carcinomas are divided into three categories based on the pattern and extent of invasion:

| Type | Definition (Randolph) | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Minimally invasive (capsular invasion only)** | Tumour penetration or disruption of the capsule in at least one area WITHOUT vascular invasion. Multiple sections of the capsule-tumor interface necessary (most diagnosed on 10 sections). Per Randolph Ch 22, carries ~5% risk of persistent/recurrent disease | Excellent -- near 100% 10-year survival; rarely recurs or spreads to distant sites |
| **Encapsulated angioinvasive** | Encapsulated follicular neoplasm that invades veins in or adjacent to tumour capsule. The WHO classification distinguishes this from capsular invasion alone because vascular invasion increases chances of recurrence/metastases. <4 vs >=4 foci of vascular invasion is predictive of overall and disease-free survival (Randolph Ch 22) | Intermediate -- 10-year DSS ~85--95% |
| **Widely invasive** | Diffuse infiltration of vascular structures and/or adjacent thyroid or extrathyroidal tissues; capsule often incomplete; areas of haemorrhage and necrosis are frequent (Randolph Ch 22). Multiple fingers of neoplastic cells extending into surrounding thyroid or extensive replacement of the gland and soft tissues of the neck (Randolph Ch 41) | Aggressive -- 10-year DSS 45--70%; completion thyroidectomy mandatory after lobectomy |

### Key Differences from PTC

| Feature | PTC | FTC |
|---|---|---|
| Lymph node metastases | Common (40--60%) | Uncommon (5--10%); FTC has little tendency to invade lymphatics (Randolph Ch 41) |
| Haematogenous metastases | Uncommon (2--5%) | More common (10--20%); distant metastases present in 15--27% at diagnosis (Randolph Ch 22) |
| Common sites of distant metastases | Lung (50%) > bone (25%) > both (20%) (Randolph Ch 19) | Bone > lung; bone metastases carry 25% 10-year survival vs 63% for pulmonary (Randolph Ch 22) |
| Molecular drivers | BRAF V600E (36--69%), RET/PTC | RAS (49%), PAX8-PPARgamma (36%); combined 88% (Randolph Ch 22) |
| Fine needle aspiration | Diagnostic (nuclear features) | Cannot distinguish from adenoma -- FNA identifies follicular neoplasm but cannot assess capsular/vascular invasion (Randolph Ch 22) |
| Lateral neck dissection | Often needed | Rarely needed |
| Tumour size at presentation | Variable | 75--100% larger than PTC (Randolph Ch 22) |

### Angioinvasion in FTC

- The number of foci of vascular invasion is the most important prognostic factor in FTC
- Per Randolph (Ch 22), the WHO classification distinguishes encapsulated angioinvasive FTC (referred to by some as "moderately invasive" FTC) from FTCs with capsular invasion alone, because the presence of vascular invasion increases the chances of recurrence and metastases
- **<4 foci:** Generally favourable; may be managed with lobectomy alone if other risk factors absent. Even minimally invasive FTC with fewer than 4 foci of disruption/penetration in the capsule or blood vessels has a relapse-free survival benefit (Randolph Ch 22)
- **>=4 foci:** Significantly higher risk of distant metastases and disease-specific mortality; total thyroidectomy and RAI recommended. Predictive of both overall and disease-free survival (Randolph Ch 22)
- **Pseudoinvasion (Randolph Ch 41):** Post-FNA changes can mimic capsular invasion and penetration. Clues to differentiating true from pseudoinvasion include the geographic linear pattern of FNA-induced pseudoinvasion, granulation tissue, haemosiderin-laden macrophages, cholesterol clefts, fibrosis, and rarely well-formed granulomas

### FTC Prognosis (Randolph Ch 22)

| Risk Factor | Impact (Randolph) |
|---|---|
| **Age** | Strongest independent prognostic factor; >=45 years carries OR 4.87--19.77 for mortality; age cutoff raised to 55 in AJCC 8th edition. For FTC specifically, a threshold at 50 years may outperform the current system (NTCTCS data) |
| **Distant metastases at presentation** | 67% mortality; 47-fold increased relative risk on multivariate analysis |
| **Tumour size** | OR 2.84 for mortality; >4 cm is a significant cutoff across multiple studies |
| **Extrathyroidal extension** | >3-fold risk of mortality |
| **Angioinvasion** | Strongest predictor of distant metastases and disease-free survival |
| **RAI avidity** | 92% of well-differentiated FTCs are RAI-avid vs 53% of poorly differentiated FTC; positive RAI uptake associated with better prognosis |

### Hurthle Cell (Oncocytic) Carcinoma -- Now a Separate Entity (Randolph Ch 41)

Per the WHO classification and Randolph (Ch 41), Hurthle cell carcinoma is now classified separately from FTC:

- Approximately one third of oncocytic thyroid tumours show invasion (i.e., are cancers), compared with only 2--5% of nononcocytic follicular tumours
- Can metastasise to regional lymph nodes as well as haematogenously (unlike conventional FTC, which rarely involves lymph nodes)
- Large oncocytic tumours (>=4 cm) have an 80% risk of malignancy
- Molecular markers differ from FTC: H-RAS mutations, EIF1AX, PTEN, TP53, copy number alterations, and mitochondrial DNA deletions/amplifications (Randolph Ch 22, Ch 41)

## High-Grade Thyroid Carcinomas (Randolph Ch 41)

Per Randolph (Ch 41), a category of "high-grade thyroid carcinoma" includes malignant tumours with recognisable differentiated thyroid cancer architecture (usually papillary) but with features placing them in a high-grade category:

- **Histology:** Classic PTC architecture but with marked nuclear pleomorphism, unequivocal tumour necrosis, and numerous mitotic figures
- **Behaviour:** Often large, with multifocal vascular invasion and gross extrathyroidal extension; associated with regional and distant metastases
- **Molecular:** A subset shows RAS mutations, TERT promoter mutations, and BRAF V600E
- **Clinical significance:** Bridges the gap between well-differentiated and poorly differentiated/anaplastic carcinoma; an important recognition for risk stratification

## Micropapillary Thyroid Carcinoma

### Definition

- PTC measuring <=1 cm in greatest dimension
- Accounts for up to 50% of newly diagnosed PTCs in some series due to incidental detection
- Per Randolph (Ch 19), 49% of newly diagnosed thyroid cancers are <1 cm
- Per Randolph (Ch 41), the prevalence in autopsy studies ranges from 6% to 36%; they may appear as small irregular firm scars, soft foci of discolouration, or tiny calcified lesions. They may be encapsulated or infiltrative

### Active Surveillance vs Surgery

This is an evolving area, pioneered by Japanese centres (Kuma Hospital, Ito/Miyauchi):

**Active Surveillance Criteria (Selected Patients):**
- Unifocal tumour <=1 cm
- No extrathyroidal extension
- No lymph node metastases
- Not adjacent to trachea or recurrent laryngeal nerve
- Not aggressive histological variant on cytology
- Patient willingness for regular surveillance

**Japanese Experience (Ito 2014):**
- 1235 patients followed with active surveillance
- Only 8% showed tumour enlargement (>=3 mm) over 10 years
- Only 3.8% developed nodal metastases over 10 years
- No disease-specific mortality
- Rescue surgery was successful in all cases requiring intervention

**Indications for Surgery in Microcarcinoma:**
- Patient preference after counselling
- Tumour growth on surveillance (>=3 mm increase)
- New lymph node metastases
- Location adjacent to trachea or RLN
- Aggressive cytological features
- Younger patients (<40 years) with long life expectancy and surveillance burden

## Differentiated Thyroid Cancer in Children

### Epidemiology

- Thyroid cancer accounts for ~3% of all paediatric malignancies
- Incidence increasing, likely due to improved detection
- Peak incidence in adolescents (15--19 years)
- Female:male ratio ~4:1 (similar to adults)
- >90% are PTC; FTC is uncommon in children
- Per Randolph (Ch 19), most thyroid cancers in the paediatric population are papillary cancers, and the incidence is rising across all age groups including children and adolescents

### Presentation Compared to Adults

| Feature | Children | Adults |
|---|---|---|
| Tumour size at diagnosis | Often larger | Variable |
| Extrathyroidal extension | More common (30--40%) | Less common (10--20%) |
| Cervical lymph node metastases | Very common (60--80%); up to 80% (Randolph Ch 19) | Common (30--50%) |
| Pulmonary metastases | 10--25% at diagnosis; 15--20% (Randolph Ch 19) -- nearly double adult rates | 2--5% at diagnosis |
| Bilateral/multifocal disease | More common | Less common |
| Disease-specific mortality | Extremely low (<1--2%); 2.7% in systematic review of those with pulmonary metastases (Randolph Ch 19) | Low (~5% at 25 years) |

### Paradox: More Advanced Disease, Better Prognosis

- Children present with more advanced disease (larger tumours, more nodal/distant metastases)
- Yet disease-specific mortality is extremely low (<1--2%)
- Per Randolph (Ch 19), in one systematic review of paediatric patients with pulmonary metastases, complete response to RAI therapy was seen in up to 50% and disease-specific mortality was only 2.7%
- Possible explanations:
  - More differentiated tumours with better RAI uptake
  - Better NIS expression in paediatric DTC
  - More robust immune response
  - Fewer concurrent comorbidities
  - Less dedifferentiation (rare TERT mutations in children)
  - Postpubertal children with microscopic and iodine-avid distant metastases have the best survival subsets (Randolph Ch 19)

### Management Considerations (Francis 2015 ATA Paediatric Guidelines)

1. **Surgery:** Total thyroidectomy with therapeutic CCND is the standard. Prophylactic bilateral CCND is recommended for most paediatric DTC. Lateral neck dissection for N1b disease. Surgery should be performed by a **high-volume thyroid surgeon** (minimum 30 thyroidectomies/year) given the higher complication rates in children
2. **RAI:** Indicated for most children with DTC given the higher rate of nodal and pulmonary metastases. Dose is activity-based (not empiric fixed dose), considering body size
3. **TSH suppression:** Recommended with TSH <0.5 mU/L initially, then adjusted based on response
4. **Follow-up:** Long-term surveillance is essential given the decades of remaining life and potential for very late recurrence
5. **Active surveillance** is NOT currently recommended for paediatric microcarcinomas (insufficient data)

## Management Algorithm for DTC

### Extent of Surgery: Total Thyroidectomy vs Lobectomy

The extent of surgery has become more nuanced with the ATA 2015 guidelines. Per Randolph (Ch 19, Sipos & Haugen), the recent trend to less radioiodine therapy has obviated the need for total thyroidectomy in many patients with low-risk thyroid cancer, and the risk of complications with total thyroidectomy is double that of lobectomy regardless of surgeon experience level. Multiple retrospective studies reveal equivalent outcomes for low-risk disease treated with lobectomy vs total thyroidectomy when controlled for tumour size and extent of disease.

#### Lobectomy Alone May Be Sufficient For:

- Unifocal intrathyroidal PTC 1--4 cm without ETE, cN0, no aggressive features
- Tumours <1 cm lacking metastatic disease or ETE (Randolph Ch 19)
- Unifocal intrathyroidal FTC (minimally invasive, no angioinvasion)
- Minimally invasive FTC -- likely adequately treated with hemithyroidectomy, especially if contralateral lobe and lymph nodes are normal on US (Randolph Ch 22)
- Patient preference to avoid lifelong thyroid hormone replacement

#### Total Thyroidectomy Recommended For:

- Tumours >4 cm
- Bilateral/multifocal disease
- Extrathyroidal extension (T3b or T4)
- Clinically apparent nodal metastases (cN1)
- Aggressive histological variants
- Known distant metastases
- History of childhood radiation exposure
- When RAI is planned
- Widely invasive FTC on histopathology after lobectomy -- completion thyroidectomy required (Randolph Ch 22)
- Cytologic or US findings suggestive of increased malignancy risk: lesions >4 cm, somatic mutations specific for carcinoma, family history, advanced age (Randolph Ch 22)

#### Surgeon Volume and Outcomes (Randolph Ch 19, Ch 22)

- Per Randolph (Ch 22), FTC surgery should ideally be performed by a surgeon with high-volume experience (>100 thyroid-related procedures per year), associated with significantly lower complication rates
- Per Randolph (Ch 19), surgeon case volume predicts patient outcomes: those performing <10 cases vs >100 cases/year had complication rates of 24% vs 14.5%
- Complication rates for completion thyroidectomy are comparable to total thyroidectomy in experienced hands (Randolph Ch 19)

### The 1.8 cm PTC Debate

A frequently examined clinical scenario: How to manage a 1.8 cm PTC?

**Arguments for Lobectomy:**
- Falls within the 1--4 cm range where lobectomy is acceptable per ATA 2015
- Lower complications (no risk of bilateral RLN injury, lower hypoparathyroidism)
- Disease-specific survival equivalent to total thyroidectomy in low-risk patients
- Avoids lifelong levothyroxine replacement
- Quality-of-life advantages
- Per Randolph (Ch 19), a large long-term follow-up study of lobectomy patients (14.6% with multifocal disease) demonstrated recurrence-free 20-year survival of 95% in the opposite lobe, 91% for LN recurrence, and DSS of 97.8%
- Only 7% of lobectomy patients required completion thyroidectomy at 10 years (Randolph Ch 19)

**Arguments for Total Thyroidectomy:**
- Enables RAI if needed based on final pathology (e.g., unexpected aggressive features, nodal metastases)
- Facilitates Tg surveillance (not reliable with remaining thyroid lobe)
- Treats potential contralateral occult disease (bilateral PTC in 20--30%; per Randolph Ch 19, 32--45% have cancer elsewhere in ipsilateral or contralateral lobe)
- Standard approach if preoperative findings suggest intermediate/high risk features
- Required if postoperative RAI is planned

**Decision-Making Approach:**
The decision depends on preoperative risk assessment:
- **Clearly low-risk** (unifocal, intrathyroidal, cN0, no aggressive features on biopsy): Lobectomy is appropriate
- **Any concerning feature** (multifocality, family history, radiation exposure, aggressive cytology, bilateral nodularity): Total thyroidectomy preferred
- Per Randolph (Ch 19), the decision to pursue more extensive surgery should include consideration of tumour histology, the potential for contralateral lobe malignancy, presence of abnormal nodes, desire for postoperative RAI, and patient preference
- **Patient factors:** Individual preferences, comorbidities, access to experienced surgeon, willingness for surveillance without Tg monitoring

### Lymph Node Dissection (Randolph Ch 19)

- PTC has a high predilection for LN spread, occurring in up to 40--90% when prophylactic dissection is performed
- However, recurrence-free survival is NOT affected by removal of sonographically normal, microscopically diseased nodes
- **Prophylactic central neck dissection** may be individually considered for T3/T4 tumours or in the presence of lateral neck metastases
- **Therapeutic dissection** warranted for clinically suspicious or biopsy-proven nodal disease
- **"Berry picking" is NOT recommended** -- selective removal of suspicious individual nodes is associated with significantly higher recurrence rates; systematic compartmental dissection is the standard (Randolph Ch 19)
- Number of involved nodes matters: >5 nodes carries recurrence risk of 7--21% vs 3--8% for fewer nodes
- Nodal metastases associated with increased mortality only in patients >45 years in SEER analysis, though more recent data show association even in younger patients with increasing node numbers

### Risk-Adapted Management Algorithm

```
DTC diagnosed on FNA
    |
    ├── Tumour <=1 cm, unifocal, intrathyroidal, cN0
    |       → Consider active surveillance OR lobectomy
    |
    ├── Tumour 1-4 cm, unifocal, intrathyroidal, cN0, no aggressive features
    |       → Lobectomy OR total thyroidectomy (discuss with patient)
    |
    ├── Tumour >4 cm, OR multifocal, OR bilateral, OR cN1, OR aggressive variant
    |       → Total thyroidectomy + therapeutic CCND if cN1a
    |       → Consider prophylactic CCND if T3/T4
    |
    ├── Total thyroidectomy performed → ATA risk stratification
    |       |
    |       ├── Low risk → No RAI or low-dose RAI (30 mCi)
    |       ├── Intermediate risk → RAI (30-150 mCi)
    |       └── High risk → RAI (100-200 mCi)
    |
    └── Follow-up with dynamic risk stratification
            → Reclassify at each visit based on response to therapy
```

### Indications for RAI (Randolph Ch 19)

Per Randolph (Ch 19), the use of RAI is clarified based on initial ATA risk stratification:

| ATA Risk | RAI Indication | Evidence (Randolph) |
|---|---|---|
| **Low risk, T1a N0** | NOT recommended | No benefit for disease-specific or disease-free survival |
| **Low risk, 1--4 cm, N0** | NOT routinely used | No mortality benefit; consider only if aggressive histology or vascular invasion |
| **Intermediate risk** | Case-by-case | Conflicting data; individualise decision |
| **High risk** | Routinely recommended | Demonstrated benefit in mortality and disease-free survival |

## Prognostic Factors in DTC

### Clinicopathological Factors

| Factor | Impact | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Age** | Most important prognostic factor; >=55 years = worse prognosis. Per Randolph Ch 19: incremental increase in 10-year mortality by 30--50% per 5-year increment beginning at age 35. BRAF-mutated tumours show age as a strong, continuous, independent mortality risk factor, but not BRAF wild-type (Randolph Ch 19) | Very high |
| **Tumour size** | >4 cm associated with higher recurrence and mortality. Cancer-specific mortality ranges from 2% for <1 cm to 19% for >8 cm (Randolph Ch 19) | High |
| **Extrathyroidal extension** | Gross ETE (T4) significantly worsens prognosis. Minimal ETE: recent meta-analysis shows no influence on mortality but small increase in recurrence risk (absolute increase 2.2% to 3.5% for N0 patients; Randolph Ch 19). Minimal ETE removed from AJCC 8th edition staging | Very high |
| **Nodal status** | >5 nodes or any >=3 cm increases risk. Per Randolph Ch 19: microscopic deposits in prophylactically dissected nodes do not significantly alter recurrence risk | High |
| **Distant metastases** | Present in ~5% of PTC; 15--27% of FTC; significantly worsens prognosis. Survival related to iodine avidity and FDG-PET uptake (Randolph Ch 19) | Very high |
| **Completeness of resection** | R2 resection has worst outcomes | High |
| **Histological variant** | Tall cell, hobnail worst; FVPTC/classical best. Per Randolph Ch 19: aggressive variants present at older age with more advanced disease | High |
| **Vascular invasion** | >4 foci in FTC is the strongest prognostic factor for FTC | Very high |
| **Multifocality** | 32--45% of PTC patients have cancer elsewhere in ipsilateral/contralateral lobe (Randolph Ch 19); bilateral tumours associated with lower survival in some studies | Moderate |

### Molecular Factors

| Marker | Prognostic Impact |
|---|---|
| **BRAF V600E alone** | Prevalence 36--69% in PTC (Randolph Ch 19); associated with higher-risk clinicopathologic features (LN metastases, ETE, recurrence, age-associated mortality), but independent prognostic utility remains in question due to high prevalence and excellent overall outcomes |
| **BRAF + TERT** | Synergistic -- highest risk of recurrence and mortality (Randolph Ch 19: meta-analysis confirmed more aggressive clinical course) |
| **TERT promoter** | Found in 9% of low-risk PTC, 51% of advanced PTC, 40% of PDTC, 54--73% of ATC (Randolph Ch 19); reactivates telomerase leading to immortalisation via unrestricted proliferation |
| **RAS** | 49% of FTC; also exclusive to FVPTC among PTC variants; associated with PI3K-Akt pathway activation |
| **PAX8-PPARgamma** | 36% of FTC; 100% of radiation-associated FTC (Randolph Ch 22) |
| **TP53** | Associated with dedifferentiation and anaplastic transformation |
| **RET/PTC rearrangements** | Generally associated with younger patients and favourable prognosis; also found in some hyalinizing trabecular neoplasms (Randolph Ch 41) |

### Utility of BRAF for Therapeutic Targeting (Randolph Ch 19)

Beyond prognostication, BRAF mutation may guide management of radioiodine-refractory tumours. A clinical trial using the BRAF-inhibitor dabrafenib showed 60% of patients exhibited new iodine uptake on diagnostic whole-body scans, with partial responses and stable disease achieved after RAI treatment. This represents a "redifferentiation" strategy for noniodine-avid tumours.

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **PTC nuclear features** -- clearing, grooves, pseudoinclusions, overlapping -- these define PTC, not papillary architecture
2. **NIFTP** is NOT cancer -- lobectomy alone is sufficient with no RAI or TSH suppression needed
3. **Tall cell variant** is the most commonly tested aggressive PTC variant -- cells 3x taller than wide, BRAF V600E associated, 25% mortality (Randolph Ch 41)
4. **Angioinvasion** (not capsular invasion alone) determines prognosis in FTC -- >=4 foci is the cutoff; WHO now separates encapsulated angioinvasive FTC as a distinct category
5. **Children with DTC** -- more advanced at presentation but excellent prognosis (<2% mortality) -- this paradox is a favourite examination topic; complete RAI response in up to 50% of those with pulmonary metastases (Randolph Ch 19)
6. **1--4 cm PTC** -- lobectomy is now acceptable (ATA 2015); the 1.8 cm PTC scenario requires balanced discussion; 97.8% DSS with lobectomy in properly selected patients (Randolph Ch 19)
7. **Age 55** is the most important single prognostic factor (changed from 45 in AJCC 8th edition); for FTC, a threshold at 50 years may outperform the current system (Randolph Ch 22)
8. **BRAF + TERT** is the molecular signature of highest-risk PTC; TERT found in 9% of low-risk vs 51% of advanced PTC (Randolph Ch 19)
9. **Active surveillance** for microcarcinoma is an emerging paradigm from Japan but NOT yet standard of care for children
10. **Francis 2015 paediatric guidelines** recommend total thyroidectomy with CCND by a high-volume surgeon as standard for paediatric DTC
11. **Berry picking is not acceptable** -- systematic compartmental dissection is the standard for nodal disease in PTC (Randolph Ch 19)
12. **Pseudoinvasion** after FNA can mimic capsular invasion in FTC -- look for geographic linear pattern, granulation tissue, haemosiderin-laden macrophages (Randolph Ch 41)
13. **Hurthle cell carcinoma** is now a separate entity from FTC (WHO); one third of oncocytic tumours are malignant vs 2--5% of nononcocytic follicular tumours; >=4 cm has 80% malignancy risk (Randolph Ch 41)
14. **FVPTC molecular profile** is closer to follicular carcinoma than classical PTC -- RAS mutations exclusive to FVPTC, BRAF/RET rare; encapsulated FVPTC may represent a "hybrid" tumour (Randolph Ch 41)
15. **Surgeon volume matters** -- <10 vs >100 cases/year: complication rates 24% vs 14.5% (Randolph Ch 19); FTC surgery ideally by surgeon with >100 procedures/year (Randolph Ch 22)
