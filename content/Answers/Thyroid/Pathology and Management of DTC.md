## WHO Classification of Thyroid Tumours

The WHO Classification of Tumours of Endocrine Organs (5th edition, 2022) provides the definitive pathological framework for thyroid neoplasms. The classification has undergone significant revisions, most notably the introduction of NIFTP (Nikiforov 2016) and the separation of high-grade follicular cell-derived carcinomas.

### Overview of Major Categories

| Category | Key Entities |
|---|---|
| **Benign follicular cell-derived** | Follicular adenoma, oncocytic (Hurthle cell) adenoma |
| **Borderline/precursor** | NIFTP, hyalinizing trabecular tumour |
| **Malignant -- low grade** | Papillary thyroid carcinoma (classical and non-aggressive variants), follicular thyroid carcinoma (minimally invasive) |
| **Malignant -- high grade** | Poorly differentiated thyroid carcinoma, high-grade differentiated thyroid carcinoma |
| **Malignant -- undifferentiated** | Anaplastic thyroid carcinoma |
| **Malignant -- C-cell derived** | Medullary thyroid carcinoma |
| **Other** | Thyroid lymphoma, sarcoma, metastatic tumours |

## Papillary Thyroid Carcinoma (PTC)

PTC is the most common thyroid malignancy, accounting for 80--85% of all thyroid cancers. It is characterised by distinctive nuclear features rather than papillary architecture:

### Diagnostic Nuclear Features (WHO Criteria)

1. **Nuclear enlargement** with overlapping and crowding
2. **Nuclear clearing** ("Orphan Annie eyes" / ground-glass nuclei)
3. **Nuclear grooves** (longitudinal grooves in nuclear membrane)
4. **Intranuclear cytoplasmic inclusions** (pseudoinclusions)
5. **Irregular nuclear contours** with moulding

### PTC Variants

#### Classical PTC

- Papillary and follicular architecture
- Characteristic nuclear features
- Accounts for 55--65% of all PTC
- Frequent BRAF V600E mutation (40--60%)
- Generally favourable prognosis

#### Follicular Variant of PTC (FVPTC)

- Follicular growth pattern but PTC-type nuclear features
- **Encapsulated FVPTC:**
  - If noninvasive: reclassified as **NIFTP** (Nikiforov 2016)
  - If invasive (capsular/vascular invasion): follicular variant of PTC
- **Infiltrative FVPTC:** aggressive behaviour similar to classical PTC
- RAS mutations more common than BRAF

#### Aggressive Variants

| Variant | Frequency | Key Features | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tall cell** | 5--12% | Cells 3x taller than wide; strong BRAF V600E association; older patients | Aggressive -- higher recurrence and mortality |
| **Hobnail** | 1--3% | Apical nuclear bulging ("hobnail" pattern); micropapillary architecture | Very aggressive -- high mortality |
| **Columnar cell** | <1% | Tall columnar cells with pseudostratified nuclei; CDX2 expression | Aggressive when widely invasive |
| **Diffuse sclerosing** | 1--5% | Diffuse bilateral involvement; psammoma bodies; squamous metaplasia; young patients | Higher nodal metastases but acceptable prognosis with treatment |
| **Solid/trabecular** | 1--3% | Solid and trabecular growth; associated with radiation exposure | Intermediate prognosis; higher recurrence |

### Noninvasive Follicular Thyroid Neoplasm with Papillary-Like Nuclear Features (NIFTP)

- **Definition:** Encapsulated/well-demarcated follicular-patterned thyroid neoplasm with PTC-type nuclei, WITHOUT invasion (capsular or vascular) or high-grade features
- **Significance:** Reclassified from "encapsulated noninvasive FVPTC" (a cancer) to NIFTP (a borderline/precursor lesion)
- **Clinical impact:** Patients do NOT require total thyroidectomy, RAI, or TSH suppression
- **Management:** Lobectomy alone is sufficient (equivalent to thyroid adenoma management)
- **Outcome:** Nikiforov 2016 reported 0% recurrence in 109 patients at 13-year median follow-up

## Follicular Thyroid Carcinoma (FTC)

Accounts for 10--15% of thyroid cancers. Unlike PTC, FTC does NOT have PTC-type nuclear features and is defined by the presence of capsular and/or vascular invasion.

### Classification by Degree of Invasion

| Type | Definition | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Minimally invasive** | Capsular invasion only (no vascular invasion) | Excellent -- near 100% 10-year survival |
| **Minimally invasive with angioinvasion** | <4 foci of vascular invasion | Intermediate -- 10-year DSS ~85--95% |
| **Widely invasive** | >=4 foci of vascular invasion OR extensive capsular penetration OR extrathyroidal extension | Aggressive -- 10-year DSS 45--70% |

### Key Differences from PTC

| Feature | PTC | FTC |
|---|---|---|
| Lymph node metastases | Common (40--60%) | Uncommon (5--10%) |
| Haematogenous metastases | Uncommon (2--5%) | More common (10--20%) |
| Common sites of distant metastases | Lung > bone | Bone > lung |
| Molecular drivers | BRAF V600E, RET/PTC | RAS, PAX8-PPARgamma |
| Fine needle aspiration | Diagnostic (nuclear features) | Cannot distinguish from adenoma |
| Lateral neck dissection | Often needed | Rarely needed |

### Angioinvasion in FTC

- The number of foci of vascular invasion is the most important prognostic factor in FTC
- **<4 foci:** Generally favourable; may be managed with lobectomy alone if other risk factors absent
- **>=4 foci:** Significantly higher risk of distant metastases and disease-specific mortality; total thyroidectomy and RAI recommended

## Micropapillary Thyroid Carcinoma

### Definition

- PTC measuring <=1 cm in greatest dimension
- Accounts for up to 50% of newly diagnosed PTCs in some series due to incidental detection

### Active Surveillance vs Surgery

This is an evolving area, pioneered by Japanese centres (Kuma Hospital, Ito/Miyauchi):

**Active Surveillance Criteria (Selected Patients):**
- Unifocal tumour <=1 cm
- No extrathyroidal extension
- No lymph node metastases
- Not adjacent to trachea or recurrent laryngeal nerve
- Not aggressive histological variant on cytology
- Patient willingness for regular surveillance

**Japanese Experience (Ito 2014):**
- 1235 patients followed with active surveillance
- Only 8% showed tumour enlargement (>=3 mm) over 10 years
- Only 3.8% developed nodal metastases over 10 years
- No disease-specific mortality
- Rescue surgery was successful in all cases requiring intervention

**Indications for Surgery in Microcarcinoma:**
- Patient preference after counselling
- Tumour growth on surveillance (>=3 mm increase)
- New lymph node metastases
- Location adjacent to trachea or RLN
- Aggressive cytological features
- Younger patients (<40 years) with long life expectancy and surveillance burden

## Differentiated Thyroid Cancer in Children

### Epidemiology

- Thyroid cancer accounts for ~3% of all paediatric malignancies
- Incidence increasing, likely due to improved detection
- Peak incidence in adolescents (15--19 years)
- Female:male ratio ~4:1 (similar to adults)
- >90% are PTC; FTC is uncommon in children

### Presentation Compared to Adults

| Feature | Children | Adults |
|---|---|---|
| Tumour size at diagnosis | Often larger | Variable |
| Extrathyroidal extension | More common (30--40%) | Less common (10--20%) |
| Cervical lymph node metastases | Very common (60--80%) | Common (30--50%) |
| Pulmonary metastases | 10--25% at diagnosis | 2--5% at diagnosis |
| Bilateral/multifocal disease | More common | Less common |
| Disease-specific mortality | Extremely low (<1--2%) | Low (~5% at 25 years) |

### Paradox: More Advanced Disease, Better Prognosis

- Children present with more advanced disease (larger tumours, more nodal/distant metastases)
- Yet disease-specific mortality is extremely low (<1--2%)
- Possible explanations:
  - More differentiated tumours with better RAI uptake
  - Better NIS expression in paediatric DTC
  - More robust immune response
  - Fewer concurrent comorbidities
  - Less dedifferentiation (rare TERT mutations in children)

### Management Considerations (Francis 2015 ATA Paediatric Guidelines)

1. **Surgery:** Total thyroidectomy with therapeutic CCND is the standard. Prophylactic bilateral CCND is recommended for most paediatric DTC. Lateral neck dissection for N1b disease. Surgery should be performed by a **high-volume thyroid surgeon** (minimum 30 thyroidectomies/year) given the higher complication rates in children
2. **RAI:** Indicated for most children with DTC given the higher rate of nodal and pulmonary metastases. Dose is activity-based (not empiric fixed dose), considering body size
3. **TSH suppression:** Recommended with TSH <0.5 mU/L initially, then adjusted based on response
4. **Follow-up:** Long-term surveillance is essential given the decades of remaining life and potential for very late recurrence
5. **Active surveillance** is NOT currently recommended for paediatric microcarcinomas (insufficient data)

## Management Algorithm for DTC

### Extent of Surgery: Total Thyroidectomy vs Lobectomy

The extent of surgery has become more nuanced with the ATA 2015 guidelines.

#### Lobectomy Alone May Be Sufficient For:

- Unifocal intrathyroidal PTC 1--4 cm without ETE, cN0, no aggressive features
- Unifocal intrathyroidal FTC (minimally invasive, no angioinvasion)
- Patient preference to avoid lifelong thyroid hormone replacement

#### Total Thyroidectomy Recommended For:

- Tumours >4 cm
- Bilateral/multifocal disease
- Extrathyroidal extension (T3b or T4)
- Clinically apparent nodal metastases (cN1)
- Aggressive histological variants
- Known distant metastases
- History of childhood radiation exposure
- When RAI is planned

### The 1.8 cm PTC Debate

A frequently examined clinical scenario: How to manage a 1.8 cm PTC?

**Arguments for Lobectomy:**
- Falls within the 1--4 cm range where lobectomy is acceptable per ATA 2015
- Lower complications (no risk of bilateral RLN injury, lower hypoparathyroidism)
- Disease-specific survival equivalent to total thyroidectomy in low-risk patients
- Avoids lifelong levothyroxine replacement
- Quality-of-life advantages

**Arguments for Total Thyroidectomy:**
- Enables RAI if needed based on final pathology (e.g., unexpected aggressive features, nodal metastases)
- Facilitates Tg surveillance (not reliable with remaining thyroid lobe)
- Treats potential contralateral occult disease (bilateral PTC in 20--30%)
- Standard approach if preoperative findings suggest intermediate/high risk features
- Required if postoperative RAI is planned

**Decision-Making Approach:**
The decision depends on preoperative risk assessment:
- **Clearly low-risk** (unifocal, intrathyroidal, cN0, no aggressive features on biopsy): Lobectomy is appropriate
- **Any concerning feature** (multifocality, family history, radiation exposure, aggressive cytology, bilateral nodularity): Total thyroidectomy preferred
- **Patient factors:** Individual preferences, comorbidities, access to experienced surgeon, willingness for surveillance without Tg monitoring

### Risk-Adapted Management Algorithm

```
DTC diagnosed on FNA
    |
    ├── Tumour <=1 cm, unifocal, intrathyroidal, cN0
    |       → Consider active surveillance OR lobectomy
    |
    ├── Tumour 1-4 cm, unifocal, intrathyroidal, cN0, no aggressive features
    |       → Lobectomy OR total thyroidectomy (discuss with patient)
    |
    ├── Tumour >4 cm, OR multifocal, OR bilateral, OR cN1, OR aggressive variant
    |       → Total thyroidectomy + therapeutic CCND if cN1a
    |       → Consider prophylactic CCND if T3/T4
    |
    ├── Total thyroidectomy performed → ATA risk stratification
    |       |
    |       ├── Low risk → No RAI or low-dose RAI (30 mCi)
    |       ├── Intermediate risk → RAI (30-150 mCi)
    |       └── High risk → RAI (100-200 mCi)
    |
    └── Follow-up with dynamic risk stratification
            → Reclassify at each visit based on response to therapy
```

## Prognostic Factors in DTC

### Clinicopathological Factors

| Factor | Impact | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Age** | Most important prognostic factor; >=55 years = worse prognosis | Very high |
| **Tumour size** | >4 cm associated with higher recurrence and mortality | High |
| **Extrathyroidal extension** | Gross ETE (T4) significantly worsens prognosis | Very high |
| **Nodal status** | >5 nodes or any >=3 cm increases risk | High |
| **Distant metastases** | Present in 2--5%; significantly worsens prognosis | Very high |
| **Completeness of resection** | R2 resection has worst outcomes | High |
| **Histological variant** | Tall cell, hobnail worst; FVPTC/classical best | High |
| **Vascular invasion** | >4 foci in FTC is the strongest prognostic factor for FTC | Very high |

### Molecular Factors

| Marker | Prognostic Impact |
|---|---|
| **BRAF V600E alone** | Modest -- does not independently worsen prognosis in most studies |
| **BRAF + TERT** | Synergistic -- highest risk of recurrence and mortality |
| **TERT promoter** | Independent adverse prognostic marker in DTC |
| **RAS** | Intermediate risk profile |
| **TP53** | Associated with dedifferentiation and anaplastic transformation |
| **RET/PTC rearrangements** | Generally associated with younger patients and favourable prognosis |

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **PTC nuclear features** -- clearing, grooves, pseudoinclusions, overlapping -- these define PTC, not papillary architecture
2. **NIFTP** is NOT cancer -- lobectomy alone is sufficient with no RAI or TSH suppression needed
3. **Tall cell variant** is the most commonly tested aggressive PTC variant -- cells 3x taller than wide, BRAF V600E associated
4. **Angioinvasion** (not capsular invasion alone) determines prognosis in FTC -- >=4 foci is the cutoff
5. **Children with DTC** -- more advanced at presentation but excellent prognosis (<2% mortality) -- this paradox is a favourite examination topic
6. **1--4 cm PTC** -- lobectomy is now acceptable (ATA 2015); the 1.8 cm PTC scenario requires balanced discussion
7. **Age 55** is the most important single prognostic factor (changed from 45 in AJCC 8th edition)
8. **BRAF + TERT** is the molecular signature of highest-risk PTC
9. **Active surveillance** for microcarcinoma is an emerging paradigm from Japan but NOT yet standard of care for children
10. **Francis 2015 paediatric guidelines** recommend total thyroidectomy with CCND by a high-volume surgeon as standard for paediatric DTC
