## Part 1: Bone Metastases in Follicular Thyroid Cancer

### Epidemiology and Pathophysiology

Bone is the **most common site of distant metastasis** in follicular thyroid cancer (FTC), and the second most common site (after lung) in differentiated thyroid carcinoma (DTC) overall.

**Frequency by Histology:**
- Follicular thyroid carcinoma: bone metastases in **10-20%** of cases
- Papillary thyroid carcinoma: bone metastases in **2-5%** of cases
- Hurthle cell carcinoma: bone metastases in **5-15%** of cases

**Pathophysiology:**
- FTC has a propensity for **haematogenous spread** (unlike PTC which preferentially spreads via lymphatics)
- FTC commonly demonstrates **vascular invasion** -- the defining feature distinguishing it from follicular adenoma
- Venous invasion leads to dissemination via the vertebral venous plexus (Batson's plexus) and systemic circulation
- Bone metastases from thyroid cancer are typically **osteolytic** (destructive) rather than osteoblastic
- Mechanism: thyroid cancer cells produce **RANKL, IL-6, PTHrP**, and **MMP** which activate osteoclasts

### Common Sites of Bone Metastases

| Site | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Vertebral column | 35-40% | Most common site; thoracic > lumbar > cervical |
| Pelvis (ilium, sacrum) | 20-30% | Often incidental finding on RAI scan |
| Ribs | 10-15% | May present as pathologic fracture |
| Long bones (femur, humerus) | 10-15% | Risk of pathologic fracture; weight-bearing bones critical |
| Skull/calvarium | 5-10% | Pulsatile, hypervascular mass |
| Sternum | 5% | Rare, locally destructive |
| Scapula | 3-5% | Uncommon |

### Clinical Presentation

- **Pathologic fracture** -- may be the presenting complaint (10-15% of cases)
- **Bone pain** -- chronic, progressive, often nocturnal
- **Spinal cord compression** -- medical emergency; vertebral metastases with epidural extension
- **Hypercalcaemia** -- from extensive osteolysis (uncommon in thyroid cancer)
- **Pulsatile mass** -- particularly in skull metastases (thyroid cancer is hypervascular)
- **Incidental finding** -- on RAI whole body scan, PET-CT, or other imaging

### Diagnostic Workup

**1. Radioiodine Whole Body Scan (RAI WBS):**
- Diagnostic RAI WBS (2-5 mCi I-131 or I-123) under TSH stimulation
- RAI-avid bone metastases: amenable to RAI therapy
- Non-avid lesions: require alternative imaging and treatment strategies
- Post-therapy WBS (after 100-200 mCi) may reveal additional lesions

**2. Bone Scintigraphy (Tc-99m MDP):**
- Detects osteoblastic response to metastases
- Less sensitive for purely osteolytic thyroid cancer metastases
- Useful as an adjunct, not primary imaging modality

**3. FDG PET-CT:**
- Critical for non-iodine-avid bone metastases
- SUVmax correlates with prognosis
- "Flip-flop phenomenon": loss of RAI avidity often corresponds to increased FDG avidity
- Robbins et al. (2006): FDG-positive metastases have significantly worse survival than FDG-negative

**4. MRI:**
- **Most sensitive** imaging modality for bone metastases
- Whole-spine MRI for suspected spinal cord compression
- Detects marrow infiltration before cortical destruction
- T1-weighted: low signal; T2/STIR: high signal

**5. CT Scan:**
- Evaluates cortical bone destruction
- Assesses fracture risk
- CT-guided biopsy if histological confirmation needed
- Essential for surgical planning

**6. Serum Thyroglobulin:**
- Elevated in the presence of residual/metastatic DTC
- Correlates with tumour burden
- Monitored serially to assess treatment response
- Stimulated Tg (under TSH elevation) is more sensitive than suppressed Tg

### Management of Bone Metastases

#### Radioactive Iodine (RAI) Therapy
- **First-line treatment** for RAI-avid bone metastases
- Dosing: 100-200 mCi I-131 (or dosimetry-based approach)
- May require multiple treatments at 6-12 month intervals
- Responses: complete response in ~5-10%, partial response in ~30-40%, stable disease in ~30%
- Best responses in: small lesions, young patients, RAI-avid disease
- RAI alone rarely achieves complete remission of macroscopic bone metastases

#### Surgery for Bone Metastases
**Indications:**
- **Solitary bone metastasis** -- potentially curative resection
- **Impending pathologic fracture** -- prophylactic stabilisation (>50% cortical destruction in long bones)
- **Pathologic fracture** -- stabilisation and pain relief
- **Spinal cord compression** -- emergent decompression
- **Symptomatic lesions** not responding to RAI or radiation
- **Skull base metastases** -- if causing cranial nerve compression

**Surgical Principles:**
- **Preoperative embolization** is essential for hypervascular thyroid metastases (reduces intraoperative blood loss by 50-80%)
- Embolization should be performed **24-48 hours** before surgery
- Wide resection with negative margins for solitary metastasis
- Internal fixation ± cementation for long bone fractures
- Vertebroplasty/kyphoplasty for vertebral compression fractures

#### External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT)
- Pain palliation -- **effective in 70-80%** of patients
- Dose: 20-30 Gy in 5-10 fractions for palliation
- Higher doses (40-50 Gy) for non-RAI-avid lesions with curative intent
- Stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) for oligometastatic disease
- Particularly useful for non-iodine-avid lesions

#### Bone-Targeted Agents

**Bisphosphonates:**
- Zoledronic acid (4 mg IV every 3-4 weeks)
- Reduces skeletal-related events (SREs): fractures, cord compression, need for surgery/RT
- Inhibits osteoclast activity
- Monitor renal function; risk of osteonecrosis of the jaw

**Denosumab:**
- RANKL inhibitor (120 mg SC every 4 weeks)
- Superior to zoledronic acid in preventing SREs (RANKL pathway trials)
- No renal dose adjustment required
- Risk of hypocalcaemia -- supplement calcium and vitamin D

#### Embolization (Interventional Radiology)
- **Preoperative embolization** before surgical resection of hypervascular metastases
- **Palliative embolization** for painful, hypervascular lesions not amenable to surgery
- Agents: PVA particles, gelfoam, coils, onyx
- Particularly valuable for skull, pelvic, and vertebral metastases

#### Systemic Therapy
- **Tyrosine kinase inhibitors** (lenvatinib, sorafenib) for progressive RAI-refractory bone metastases
- **TSH suppression** (target TSH <0.1 mIU/L) to reduce tumour stimulation
- **Combination approaches** may be required for refractory disease

### Prognosis of Bone Metastases

| Factor | Favourable | Adverse |
|---|---|---|
| Age at diagnosis | <45 years | >45 years |
| Number of lesions | Solitary | Multiple |
| RAI avidity | Avid | Non-avid (FDG-positive) |
| Histology | PTC | FTC, PDTC |
| Complete resection | Achieved | Not possible |
| Response to RAI | Responsive | Refractory |

- **5-year OS** with bone metastases: 50-70% (RAI-avid) vs 20-40% (non-avid)
- **10-year OS:** 20-40% overall
- Solitary, resectable, RAI-avid bone metastasis in a young patient: best prognosis
- Multiple, non-avid, unresectable lesions in an elderly patient: worst prognosis

## Part 2: PET-Detected Thyroid Incidentalomas

### Definition and Incidence

A **thyroid incidentaloma** is a thyroid nodule discovered incidentally on imaging performed for a non-thyroid indication.

**FDG PET-Detected Thyroid Incidentaloma:**
- Incidence of focal thyroid FDG uptake on PET-CT: **1-4%** of all PET-CT scans
- Diffuse uptake: usually **thyroiditis** (Hashimoto's) -- lower malignancy risk
- Focal uptake: represents a nodule -- **significantly higher malignancy risk**

### Patterns of FDG Uptake

| Pattern | Incidence | Malignancy Risk | Most Common Aetiology |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Focal uptake** | 1-2% | 25-50% (mean ~33%) | Thyroid nodule (benign or malignant) |
| **Diffuse uptake** | 2-3% | <5% | Hashimoto's thyroiditis |
| **Focal + diffuse** | Uncommon | Variable | Nodule in thyroiditis background |

### Malignancy Risk -- Soelberg Meta-Analysis (2012)

Key findings from the systematic review of 22 studies (125,754 subjects):
- **Focal thyroid incidentalomas** on FDG PET: pooled malignancy risk of **~33%**
- SUVmax was **significantly higher** in malignant nodules vs benign nodules
- However, **no reliable SUVmax cutoff** exists to distinguish benign from malignant
- Most common malignancy: **papillary thyroid carcinoma** (>80% of malignant incidentalomas)
- Size of nodule and SUVmax are imperfect predictors

### Histological Spectrum of Malignant PET Incidentalomas

| Histology | Frequency Among Malignant Incidentalomas |
|---|---|
| Papillary thyroid carcinoma | 70-85% |
| Follicular thyroid carcinoma | 5-10% |
| Hurthle cell carcinoma | 2-5% |
| Medullary thyroid carcinoma | 1-3% |
| Thyroid lymphoma | 1-3% |
| Metastasis to thyroid | 1-5% (especially in patients with known primary cancer) |
| Poorly differentiated/Anaplastic | Rare but important |

### Management Algorithm

```
FDG PET thyroid incidentaloma detected
    ↓
Assess uptake pattern
    ↓
FOCAL uptake ──────────────────── DIFFUSE uptake
    ↓                                  ↓
Thyroid ultrasound              Check TSH, anti-TPO
    ↓                                  ↓
Nodule identified?              Hashimoto's thyroiditis?
    ↓                                  ↓
Apply ACR TI-RADS              Manage thyroid function
    ↓                                  ↓
TI-RADS 3-5 and >=1 cm        No further workup for
    ↓                          diffuse uptake alone
FNAC (US-guided)
    ↓
Bethesda classification → Management per guidelines
```

### Step-by-Step Management

**Step 1: Thyroid Ultrasound**
- Mandatory for all focal FDG-avid thyroid incidentalomas
- Characterise the nodule: size, composition, echogenicity, margins, calcifications
- Apply ACR TI-RADS classification
- Evaluate for additional non-FDG-avid nodules

**Step 2: FNAC Recommendations**
- **Perform FNAC if:**
  - Focal FDG uptake with a nodule >=1 cm on US
  - Suspicious US features (TI-RADS 4-5) even if <1 cm
  - Patient has risk factors (radiation history, family history, known cancer)
- **Defer FNAC if:**
  - Purely cystic nodule (virtually no malignancy risk)
  - Diffuse uptake pattern consistent with thyroiditis
  - Very small nodule (<1 cm) with benign US features (TI-RADS 1-2)
  - Limited life expectancy from primary malignancy

**Step 3: Clinical Considerations**
- **Context of primary cancer:** FDG-avid thyroid nodule in a patient being staged for another malignancy must be evaluated to rule out:
  - Primary thyroid malignancy (most common)
  - Metastasis TO the thyroid from the primary cancer (rare, but occurs with lung, breast, renal, melanoma)
- **Patient life expectancy:** May influence aggressiveness of workup
- **Impact on management of primary cancer:** A concurrent thyroid malignancy may affect treatment sequencing

### ATA/ACR Guidance

- ATA 2015 guidelines recommend US evaluation of incidental FDG-avid thyroid nodules
- ACR TI-RADS provides standardised nodule risk stratification
- FNAC thresholds should follow standard TI-RADS size criteria
- The high malignancy rate (~33%) supports active workup in patients with reasonable life expectancy

## Part 3: TENIS Syndrome

### Definition

**TENIS syndrome** = **T**hyroglobulin-**E**levated **N**egative **I**odine **S**can

This refers to the clinical scenario in a patient with differentiated thyroid cancer who has:
1. **Elevated serum thyroglobulin** (Tg) -- indicating persistent/recurrent disease
2. **Negative radioiodine whole body scan** (WBS) -- no RAI-avid disease identified
3. Previous total thyroidectomy and RAI ablation

### Prevalence
- Occurs in approximately **10-25%** of DTC patients during follow-up
- More common in:
  - Patients with aggressive histological subtypes (tall cell, columnar cell variants)
  - Poorly differentiated components
  - Higher initial stage disease
  - Long disease course (de-differentiation over time)

### Pathophysiology -- NIS Downregulation

The sodium-iodide symporter (NIS) is the key transporter responsible for iodine uptake in thyroid cells. In TENIS syndrome, the cancer cells have **lost NIS expression** while retaining the ability to produce thyroglobulin.

**Mechanism of NIS Loss:**
1. **Epigenetic silencing** -- promoter methylation of the NIS gene (SLC5A5)
2. **Transcriptional downregulation** -- loss of PAX8 or TTF-1 binding to NIS promoter
3. **Aberrant MAPK pathway activation** -- BRAF V600E and RAS mutations suppress NIS expression via MEK/ERK signalling
4. **Dedifferentiation** -- progressive loss of thyroid-specific gene expression
5. **Post-translational defects** -- NIS protein produced but not trafficked to cell membrane

**Key Molecular Insight:**
- BRAF V600E mutation is strongly associated with **loss of RAI avidity**
- MAPK pathway hyperactivation leads to decreased NIS, TPO, and Tg gene expression
- This understanding has led to **redifferentiation strategies** (MEK inhibitors, BRAF inhibitors to restore NIS)

### Diagnostic Workup for TENIS Syndrome

The goal is to **localise the source of thyroglobulin elevation** using alternative imaging:

#### Step 1: Confirm Elevated Thyroglobulin
- **Stimulated Tg:** Under TSH stimulation (rhTSH or THW), Tg >10 ng/mL is highly suggestive of structural disease
- **Suppressed Tg:** Rising trend is significant even at lower levels
- **Tg doubling time:** <1 year indicates aggressive disease
- **Anti-thyroglobulin antibodies:** Must be checked -- can cause falsely low Tg in immunometric assays
- Note: Some labs use mass spectrometry-based Tg assays that are not affected by TgAb

#### Step 2: Neck Ultrasound
- **First-line imaging** in TENIS syndrome
- Evaluates thyroid bed for recurrence and cervical lymph nodes
- US-guided FNAC of suspicious lesions with **Tg washout** from the aspirate
- Sensitivity: 70-90% for cervical disease
- Limitations: cannot detect distant metastases

#### Step 3: 18F-FDG PET-CT
- **Most important imaging modality** in TENIS syndrome
- Detects non-iodine-avid disease that would be missed by RAI WBS
- **Flip-Flop Phenomenon:** As tumour de-differentiates, it loses RAI avidity (NIS expression) and gains FDG avidity (increased glucose metabolism)
- Best performed under **TSH stimulation** (improves sensitivity by 10-20%)
- Detection rate: 50-90% depending on Tg level
  - Tg <10 ng/mL: detection rate ~50%
  - Tg 10-100 ng/mL: detection rate ~70%
  - Tg >100 ng/mL: detection rate ~85-90%
- Robbins et al. (2006): FDG-positive disease is an independent predictor of cancer-specific mortality

#### Step 4: Diagnostic CT
- **CT neck and chest** (with contrast) -- complementary to PET-CT
- Detects small pulmonary metastases (miliary pattern may be missed on PET)
- Mediastinal lymphadenopathy
- Note: Iodinated contrast delays any subsequent RAI therapy by 6-8 weeks

#### Step 5: Other Imaging
- **MRI:** For suspected bone or CNS metastases
- **Bone scintigraphy:** If bone metastases suspected
- **Diagnostic RAI WBS with SPECT-CT:** May detect subtle RAI-avid disease not seen on planar imaging

### Management of TENIS Syndrome

#### 1. Surgery for Localisable Disease
- **If disease is localised** (neck recurrence, isolated metastasis) -- surgical resection is the preferred approach
- Reoperation for cervical recurrence or lymph node metastases
- Resection of solitary distant metastasis if feasible
- Surgery offers the best chance of cure or significant Tg reduction

#### 2. External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT)
- For unresectable locoregional disease
- Non-iodine-avid disease in the neck or mediastinum
- Bone metastases (palliation or local control)
- Dose: 60-66 Gy for gross disease; 50-54 Gy for microscopic disease
- SBRT for oligometastatic disease

#### 3. Empiric RAI Therapy
- **Controversial in TENIS syndrome** -- by definition, diagnostic scan is negative
- Rationale: Higher therapeutic doses may detect and treat lesions not visible on diagnostic doses
- Typical empiric dose: 100-150 mCi I-131
- Post-therapy WBS may reveal previously occult disease in ~25-30% of cases
- **ATA 2015 guidelines:** empiric RAI may be considered if Tg is rising and no structural disease is identified
- Diminishing returns with repeated empiric courses
- Generally not recommended if previous post-therapy WBS was also negative

#### 4. Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors (TKIs)
- For progressive, RAI-refractory, structurally evident disease
- **Lenvatinib:** Multi-kinase TKI; SELECT trial -- PFS 18.3 months vs 3.6 months placebo
- **Sorafenib:** Multi-kinase TKI; DECISION trial -- PFS 10.8 months vs 5.8 months placebo
- Indicated when disease is:
  - Radiologically progressive (RECIST criteria)
  - Symptomatic
  - Threatening critical structures
- Not indicated for biochemical-only disease (elevated Tg without structural disease)

#### 5. Redifferentiation Therapy (Emerging)
- **Concept:** Restore NIS expression and RAI avidity using targeted agents
- **Selumetinib** (MEK inhibitor) -- Ho et al. 2013 (NEJM): restored RAI avidity in 12/20 RAI-refractory patients; 8 met dosimetry thresholds for RAI therapy, 5/8 had partial response to RAI
- **Dabrafenib** (BRAF inhibitor) -- pilot data in BRAF V600E-mutant RAI-refractory DTC
- **Trametinib** (MEK inhibitor) -- under investigation
- **Combination BRAF + MEK inhibition** -- may be more effective for redifferentiation
- This is one of the most exciting frontiers in thyroid cancer management

#### 6. Surveillance
- For patients with:
  - Low-level Tg elevation (<10 ng/mL)
  - No structural disease on imaging
  - Stable or slowly rising Tg
- Strategy: serial Tg monitoring + periodic imaging (US, PET-CT)
- Active treatment initiated if:
  - Structural disease develops
  - Tg rises rapidly (doubling time <1 year)
  - Symptoms develop

### TENIS Syndrome -- Decision Framework

| Scenario | Recommended Approach |
|---|---|
| Localised neck disease on US/PET | Surgery |
| Solitary distant metastasis | Surgery if feasible, or SBRT |
| Oligometastatic disease | Surgery/SBRT + RAI trial |
| Widespread metastatic disease, symptomatic | TKI (lenvatinib/sorafenib) |
| Rising Tg, no structural disease | Surveillance or empiric RAI |
| Stable low Tg, no structural disease | Surveillance with periodic imaging |
| BRAF V600E-mutant, RAI-refractory | Consider redifferentiation (BRAF/MEK inhibitor) |

## Key Examination Points

1. **FTC metastasises via haematogenous route** -- bone is the most common distant site
2. **Preoperative embolization** is essential before surgical resection of hypervascular thyroid bone metastases
3. 10-year OS for bone metastases: **20-40%** overall; better if RAI-avid and young age
4. **PET thyroid incidentaloma** with focal uptake carries **~33% malignancy risk** (Soelberg 2012)
5. Focal PET uptake requires **US evaluation and FNAC** per TI-RADS guidelines
6. **Diffuse PET uptake** = usually thyroiditis, low malignancy risk
7. TENIS = elevated Tg + negative RAI scan -- due to **NIS downregulation**
8. **Flip-flop phenomenon:** loss of RAI avidity corresponds to gain in FDG avidity
9. **PET-CT under TSH stimulation** improves detection rate in TENIS syndrome
10. **Redifferentiation therapy** (selumetinib, dabrafenib) is an emerging strategy to restore RAI avidity
11. **Surgery** is the preferred treatment for localised TENIS syndrome disease
12. TKIs (lenvatinib, sorafenib) are reserved for progressive, structurally evident, RAI-refractory disease
