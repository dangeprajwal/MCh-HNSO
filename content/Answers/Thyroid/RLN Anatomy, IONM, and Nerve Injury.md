## Surgical Anatomy of the Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve

### Origin and Course

**Right RLN**:
- Branches from the vagus nerve at the level of the right subclavian artery
- Loops posteriorly around the right subclavian artery (4th branchial arch derivative)
- Ascends the neck, enters the thoracic inlet, emerges from under the common carotid artery, and tracks laterally to medially as it travels superiorly (Randolph Ch 36)
- Course is more oblique and lateral than the left RLN; Shindo et al. showed both RLNs typically form an angle of 15-30 degrees relative to the trachea, with the right being more oblique (Randolph Ch 36)
- Enters the larynx at the cricothyroid joint (posterior to the inferior cornu of the thyroid cartilage)

**Left RLN**:
- Branches from the vagus nerve at the level of the aortic arch (6th arch, ligamentum arteriosus)
- Loops posteriorly around the aortic arch just lateral to the obliterated ductus arteriosus
- Ascends in the tracheo-oesophageal groove more vertically
- Enters the larynx at the cricothyroid joint
- Course is more medial and vertical than the right RLN (more consistently in the TOG)

**Dimensions (Randolph Ch 36)**: The vagus diameter is approximately 4 mm with epineurium (3 mm without). The RLN diameter averages approximately 2 mm (range 1-3 mm). The RLN length from vagal takeoff to laryngeal entry point is 8.5 cm on the right and 10 cm on the left, accounting for the different EMG latencies with vagal stimulation on each side.

### Visual Appearance (Randolph Ch 36)

- The RLN is white and approximately 2 mm wide
- Virtually always has a vessel running on its surface (vasa nervosum), visible as a ventral "red strip"
- May be less apparent if the nerve has been attenuated (e.g., massive goiter or nerve on stretch)
- Sympathetic chain branches can mimic the RLN or a non-recurrent nerve -- electrical stimulation is essential to differentiate (sympathetic branches do not yield laryngeal EMG activity)

### Key Anatomical Relationships

| Structure | Relationship to RLN |
|---|---|
| Tracheo-oesophageal groove | RLN lies within or adjacent to the TOG (right RLN more variable) |
| Inferior thyroid artery (ITA) | 6 variations -- RLN may pass anterior, posterior, or between ITA branches |
| Ligament of Berry | RLN enters the larynx at or just posterior to the ligament of Berry -- most vulnerable point |
| Tubercle of Zuckerkandl | RLN lies deep (dorsal) to the TOZ in 93% of cases; ventral in 7% (high risk) (Randolph Ch 36) |
| Thyroid gland | RLN lies deep (posterior) to the thyroid parenchyma |
| Simon triangle (Beahr triangle) | Triangle bounded by CCA, ITA, and RLN -- landmark for nerve identification |
| Inferior parathyroid gland | Typically anterior to RLN |
| Superior parathyroid gland | Typically posterior to RLN (more constant in position) |

### Entry Point -- Ligament of Berry

- The ligament of Berry (LOB) is a posterior condensation of pretracheal fascia attaching the thyroid gland to the trachea at the level of the 1st-2nd tracheal rings. James Berry first described the clinical significance in 1888, noting that "most of the difficulty in the separation of the tumor has occurred in the region of these ligaments" (Randolph Ch 36)
- **Dimensions (Randolph Ch 36)**: Length 11.5 mm (range 8-14 mm), width 4.4 mm (range 2-7 mm) (Sasou)
- The LOB is both dense and well vascularised, with a branch along its inferior edge from the ITA that can cause troublesome haemorrhage during the final phase of dissection (Randolph Ch 36)
- The RLN enters the larynx at or within 5 mm of this ligament
- **This is the most dangerous point** during thyroid surgery for RLN injury
- Berlin (1935) found that in 30% of cases the RLN courses through the ligament itself rather than posterior to it; in 10-15% of cases the nerve penetrates the thyroid gland at the LOB (Randolph Ch 36)
- Thyroid tissue may infuse the substance of the LOB and in so doing approximate the RLN, making dissection especially hazardous
- Snyder et al. showed through post-injury mapping that stretch at the LOB during lobar retraction is the most common mechanism for neuropraxic nerve injury, with injury most commonly occurring in the distal 2 cm leading to the laryngeal entry point (Randolph Ch 36)

### Tubercle of Zuckerkandl (Randolph Ch 36)

- Described by Zuckerkandl in 1904 as the "processus posterior glandulae" -- a lobule of thyroid tissue at the posterolateral margin of the thyroid lobe, believed to represent the point of fusion between the lateral anlage (4th pouch) and the medial thyroid elements
- **Grading**: Grade 1 (<5 mm), Grade 2 (5-10 mm), Grade 3 (>1 cm). Significant TOZ (grade 3) present in 14-61% of patients
- **Relationship to RLN**: The nerve is deep (dorsal) to the TOZ in 93% of cases; it "points to the nerve" as the nerve interacts with the LOB and laryngeal entry point
- In 7% of cases, the nerve may ride ventral to the tubercle -- an extremely vulnerable position at surgery (Hisham found anterior position in 6%, more common in revision patients)
- The nerve may be entrapped in a cleft between the TOZ's deep surface and the adjacent thyroid lobe
- **Surgical relevance**: When the TOZ is present, it can obscure the distal RLN course; however, its variable presence from patient to patient has hindered its reliability as a sole landmark for nerve identification
- The TOZ has a relatively constant relationship to the ITA (dorsal) and the superior parathyroid gland (lateral or superolateral margin)

### Branching Pattern

| Pattern | Incidence |
|---|---|
| Single trunk (no branching before laryngeal entry) | 55-65% |
| Bifurcation (2 branches) | 30-40% |
| Trifurcation or more | 5-10% |

- Various series show the RLN branches before the laryngeal entry point in 30-78% of cases; most branching occurs in the distal 2 cm, with 90% above the ITA intersection (Randolph Ch 36)
- The anterior (motor) branch innervates the thyroarytenoid, lateral cricoarytenoid, and interarytenoid muscles
- The posterior (sensory) branch innervates the subglottic mucosa (Galen anastomosis with SLN)
- **Critical point (Randolph Ch 36)**: Adductor and abductor fibres lack spatial segregation at the laryngeal entry point; they are diffusely distributed throughout the nerve. The RLN contains 2-4 times as many adductor fibres as abductor fibres. No consistent segregation into functionally discrete anterior (adductor) and posterior (abductor) branches exists -- therefore all extralaryngeal branches must be identified and preserved
- **Clinical relevance**: Branched nerves are at approximately twice the risk of transient VCP compared with unbranched nerves (Sancho et al.: 15.8% vs 8%); Cassella found paralysis risk 7-12 times higher in branched RLNs (Randolph Ch 36)

### Galen's Anastomosis (Randolph Ch 36)

- A robust anastomotic connection between the posterior branch of the RLN and the internal branch of the SLN on the posterior surface of the PCA muscle
- Found in 100% of 90 human microdissected larynges by Sanudo
- Traditionally considered primarily sensory, but newer evidence suggests it may have some motor function
- Four functionally important RLN-SLN connections exist: (1) Galen's anastomosis, (2) Human communicating nerve (EBSLN-distal RLN anastomosis through the cricothyroid membrane to the anterior thyroarytenoid, present in 41-85%), (3) Interarytenoid anastomosis, (4) SLN internal branch-RLN thyroarytenoid region anastomosis
- The human communicating nerve may partially explain ongoing TA EMG activity after RLN transection and contribute to reinnervation after RLN injury

## RLN-ITA Anatomical Variations

### The Six Classic Variations

| Type | Description | Incidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | RLN passes anterior to the ITA trunk | 15-20% |
| 2 | RLN passes posterior to the ITA trunk | 25-35% |
| 3 | RLN passes between branches of the ITA | 20-30% |
| 4 | ITA absent (RLN without ITA relationship) | 2-5% |
| 5 | RLN intertwined with ITA branches | 5-10% |
| 6 | Non-recurrent laryngeal nerve | 0.5-1% (right), <0.1% (left) |

### Clinical Significance

- Hollingshead found the RLN deep to ITA in approximately 50%, between branches in 25%, and anterior in 25%. Reed found 28 different RLN-ITA patterns and noted bilateral symmetry in only 17% (Randolph Ch 36)
- The ITA is NOT a reliable sole landmark for identifying the RLN; the highly variable patterns of nerve-artery crossing, the varying course of the ITA, and the potential for its absence (0.2-5.9%) and parallel course to the RLN make it a poor landmark for routine RLN identification (Randolph Ch 36)
- **Best practice**: Identify the RLN in the tracheo-oesophageal groove inferiorly and trace it superiorly to its entry point, rather than relying on the ITA relationship
- Ligation of the ITA close to the thyroid capsule (not on the trunk) reduces risk of parathyroid devascularisation

### Approaches to RLN Identification (Randolph Ch 36)

| Approach | When to Use | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lateral** (midpolar level with medial thyroid retraction) | Routine cases | Protects parathyroid vascular supply; limits length of RLN dissection | Not available with large masses or scarring; extralaryngeal branches may occur at this level; must consider non-recurrence on the right |
| **Inferior** (thoracic inlet) | Revision or large cervical goiters | RLN found in loose areolar bed as a single trunk before branching; outside previous surgical scarring | Long RLN segment dissected; may devascularise inferior parathyroid |
| **Superior** (laryngeal entry point/LOB) | Large cervical/substernal goiters; suspected NRLN; when other approaches fail | Most constant RLN position in the neck; inferior cornu of thyroid cartilage can be palpated | LOB is fibrous and bleeds easily; superior pole must be taken down first; technically more challenging |

## Parathyroid Anatomy in Relation to RLN

### Superior Parathyroid Glands

- **Embryological origin**: 4th pharyngeal pouch (with thyroid C cells)
- **Position**: Posterior to the RLN in >80% of cases
- **Location**: Usually at the level of the cricoid cartilage, posterior aspect of the upper thyroid pole
- **Blood supply**: Posterior branch of the superior thyroid artery or ITA
- **Consistency of position**: More constant than inferior glands (shorter embryological migration)
- **Relationship to TOZ (Randolph Ch 36)**: The superior parathyroid gland (PIV) typically lies on the lateral or superolateral margin of the tubercle of Zuckerkandl

### Inferior Parathyroid Glands

- **Embryological origin**: 3rd pharyngeal pouch (with thymus)
- **Position**: Anterior to the RLN in >60% of cases
- **Location**: More variable -- from the angle of the mandible to the mediastinum
- **Common locations**: Lower pole of thyroid, thyrothymic ligament, thymus
- **Blood supply**: Inferior thyroid artery (usually the inferior branch)
- **Clinical relevance**: More frequently removed inadvertently due to variable position

### Mnemonic

- **Superior parathyroid (4th pouch)**: dorsal/Posterior to RLN, Position more constant
- **Inferior parathyroid (3rd pouch)**: ventral/Anterior to RLN, more variable position (paradox of descent -- 3rd pouch descends further than 4th)

## Non-Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (NRLN)

### Definition

A non-recurrent laryngeal nerve is one that does not loop around the subclavian artery (right) or aortic arch (left) but instead passes directly from the vagus nerve to the larynx at the level of the thyroid gland, without a recurrent course.

### Incidence

| Side | Incidence | Associated Vascular Anomaly |
|---|---|---|
| Right | 0.5-1.0% | Aberrant right subclavian artery (arteria lusoria) |
| Left | <0.04% | Right-sided aortic arch / situs inversus (extremely rare) |

- A study of 31 patients at risk of NRLN demonstrated a 12.9% paralysis rate, underscoring the elevated injury risk (Randolph Ch 36)

### Embryological Explanation

- The RLN recurs around the 6th aortic arch derivative (right subclavian or aortic arch)
- If the right 4th aortic arch fails to develop, the right subclavian artery arises aberrantly from the descending aorta (arteria lusoria), and there is no structure around which the nerve can loop
- The nerve therefore takes a direct transverse course from the vagus to the larynx

### Classification of NRLN

**Avisse Classification (Randolph Ch 36)**:

| Type | Course |
|---|---|
| Type I | High takeoff from the vagus, travelling in association with the superior pole vessels |
| Type IIA | Derived from the vagus at the level of the laryngotracheal junction, travelling along the path of the ITA |
| Type IIB | Derived from the vagus at the laryngotracheal junction, then travels in a downward looping course below the level of the ITA before extending to the laryngeal entry point |

**Henry Classification**:

| Type | Course |
|---|---|
| Type 1 | Direct transverse course from vagus to larynx (most common NRLN pattern) |
| Type 2A | Descends briefly before entering the larynx (parallel to vagus, then turns medially) |
| Type 2B | Ascends from the vagus after origin, then enters the larynx |

- The NRLN may be bifid or have multiple branches in 18-40% of cases (Randolph Ch 36)
- May coexist with a second, smaller right RLN in the normal position (Katz found this in 5 of 11 NRLNs); the finding of a small narrow right RLN should alert the surgeon to search for a larger NRLN trunk (Randolph Ch 36)
- The retroesophageal subclavian artery is associated with Kommerell's diverticulum in 60% and with other aortic, cardiac, and vertebral artery abnormalities (Randolph Ch 36)

### Preoperative Identification

- **CT neck/thorax**: Aberrant right subclavian artery can be identified on preoperative CT
  - Look for the last great vessel arising from the aortic arch, crossing posterior to the oesophagus
  - If arteria lusoria is seen, suspect right NRLN
- **Other imaging (Randolph Ch 36)**: Barium swallow, ultrasonography, MRI, and angiography have all been shown to identify the associated vascular anomaly
- **Chest X-ray**: Absent right brachiocephalic trunk shadow (unreliable)
- **Intraoperative IONM algorithm (Randolph Ch 36)**: Positive EMG response to proximal vagal stimulation (superior border of thyroid cartilage) and absent EMG response to distal vagal stimulation (below 4th tracheal ring) reliably identifies NRLN before any dissection in the area. This algorithm considers all three Avisse anatomic variations (Kamani & Randolph, Laryngoscope 2015)

### Surgical Significance

- The NRLN is at extreme risk of injury because it lies in an unexpected position
- It may cross the operative field transversely at the level of the inferior or middle thyroid
- The nerve may be mistaken for a vessel or connective tissue strand
- Large cervical sympathetic-RLN anastomotic branches (found in 1.5% of cases, more frequent than NRLN) may be confused with a true NRLN; electrical stimulation differentiates them (Randolph Ch 36)
- **Key prevention**: Awareness of the variant, preoperative CT screening, routine IONM

## External Branch of the Superior Laryngeal Nerve (EBSLN)

### Anatomy

- **Origin**: Superior laryngeal nerve (branch of vagus at nodose ganglion), divides into internal and external branches about 2-3 cm above the superior pole (Randolph Ch 35, 36)
- **Course**: Descends on the surface of the inferior pharyngeal constrictor muscle within Joll's space (sternothyroid-laryngeal triangle: medially the inferior constrictor and CTM, anteriorly the sternothyroid muscle, laterally the superior thyroid pole) (Moosman & DeWeese, 200 cadavers; Randolph Ch 35)
- **Dimensions (Randolph Ch 35)**: Approximately 0.8 mm wide, total length 8.0-8.9 cm
- **Function**: Motor to the cricothyroid muscle (tensor and elongator of the vocal fold)
- **Human communicating nerve (Randolph Ch 35, 36)**: In 41-85% of specimens, the EBSLN continues through the cricothyroid membrane to innervate the anterior thyroarytenoid muscle (vocalis), providing a documented SLN-RLN motor connection. This has implications for IONM: EBSLN stimulation yields vocal cord EMG activity in approximately 70-80% of patients
- **Subfascial course**: In approximately 20% of cases, the EBSLN runs subfascially on the inferior constrictor and may not be directly visualised but can be stimulated electrically (Randolph Ch 36)
- **Innervation loss**: Inability to produce high-pitched sounds, voice fatigue, reduced projection

### Cernea Classification (Randolph Ch 35)

| Type | Description | Incidence (small goiter) | Incidence (large goiter) | Risk of Injury |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Type 1 | EBSLN crosses >1 cm above upper edge of superior pole | 68% | 23% | Low |
| Type 2a | EBSLN crosses <1 cm above upper edge of superior pole | 18% | 15% | Moderate |
| Type 2b | EBSLN crosses below the upper border of the superior thyroid pole | 14% | 54% | High |

- **Key point (Randolph Ch 35)**: Type 2b is significantly more prevalent in patients with large goiters (54%) than small goiters (14%). Shorter stature and increased glandular volume are independent predictors of type 2b anatomy (Furlan et al.)
- **Kierner classification** (4-type system) adds a Type 4 where the EBSLN runs dorsally (posterior) to the superior thyroid pedicle, observed in 13% of dissections (Randolph Ch 35)
- **Friedman classification** focuses on the relationship to the inferior constrictor: Type 1 (superficial throughout), Type 2 (penetrates lower portion), Type 3 (dives under superior fibres and remains covered) (Randolph Ch 35)

### Prevention of EBSLN Injury

1. **Individual ligation of superior thyroid vessels**: Ligate branches individually, close to the thyroid capsule (not en masse clamping of the superior pedicle). The superior thyroid vessels usually divide into three branches embracing the superior pole; two anterior and one dorsal (Randolph Ch 35)
2. **Dissect on the thyroid capsule**: Stay on the surface of the thyroid gland during superior pole mobilisation
3. **Expose Joll's space completely** before any superior pole suture is placed (Randolph Ch 35)
4. **Visual identification**: Identify the nerve on the inferior constrictor before ligating vessels (successful in 80-90% with dedicated effort)
5. **IONM**: Cricothyroid muscle twitch/EMG response confirms EBSLN integrity. A novel endotracheal tube with anterior surface electrodes allows quantifiable EBSLN EMG activity in 100% of cases (Randolph Ch 36)
6. **Avoid traction**: Excessive downward traction on the superior pole stretches the nerve
7. **Sternothyroid division (Randolph Ch 36)**: The laryngeal head of the sternothyroid muscle is an excellent landmark for the EBSLN because it tracks along the nerve's descending path on the lateral aspect of the inferior constrictor

### Clinical Consequences of EBSLN Injury

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Voice change | Subtle -- loss of high-pitched phonation, voice fatigue |
| Singing voice | Significantly affected (devastating for professional singers) -- "the nerve of Amelita Galli-Curci" (Randolph Ch 35) |
| Examination | Bowing of the affected vocal fold, rotation of the posterior commissure |
| Often unrecognised | Called "the forgotten nerve" -- routine VLS may miss subtle changes |
| Bilateral injury | More significant voice changes, but airway intact |

## Intraoperative Nerve Monitoring (IONM)

### Principles

IONM uses electrical stimulation of the vagus nerve or RLN with electromyographic recording from the vocalis muscle via surface electrodes on the endotracheal tube. IONM is currently used in approximately 80% of thyroid surgeries performed by head and neck surgeons and over 65% by general surgeons in the United States (Randolph Ch 36). A survey of recently trained endocrine and head and neck surgeons reveals that 95% routinely use IONM.

### Equipment Setup

| Component | Specification |
|---|---|
| EMG endotracheal tube | Surface electrodes at glottic level (NIM tube) |
| Nerve stimulator probe | Monopolar or bipolar, 1-2 mA stimulation current |
| Recording unit | Real-time EMG display with audio signal |
| Ground electrode | Placed on chest/shoulder |
| Stimulation current | 1-2 mA for nerve stimulation; 0.5 mA for mapping |

**Stimulator selection (Randolph Ch 36)**: Monopolar probes are most sensitive at a distance and through overlying fascia -- best for initial neural mapping. Bipolar probes are most specific (reduce false-positive stimulation) but need to be close to the nerve and require higher current. Stimulation dissectors provide dissection and stimulation in one instrument.

### Standardised IONM Protocol (Randolph 2011, INMSG)

**The L1-V1-R1-R2-V2-L2 Algorithm**:

| Step | Action | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **L1** | Preoperative laryngeal examination | Document baseline vocal cord function |
| **V1** | Stimulate ipsilateral vagus before thyroid dissection | Confirm system function, baseline signal, and enable neural mapping (a negative stimulation can be relied on as a true negative only after V1 is positive) (Randolph Ch 36) |
| **R1** | Stimulate RLN at first identification | Confirm nerve identity and baseline RLN signal |
| **R2** | Stimulate RLN after thyroid dissection complete | Confirm intact RLN signal after dissection |
| **V2** | Stimulate vagus after complete dissection | Most accurate prognostic test for postoperative glottic function -- higher sensitivity, specificity, and predictive value than RLN stimulation alone (Randolph Ch 36) |
| **L2** | Postoperative laryngeal examination | Final confirmation of nerve integrity and outcome measure |

- **Vagal stimulation technique (Randolph Ch 36)**: Place stimulator probe between the jugular vein and carotid artery at 1-2 mA -- successful without direct vagal dissection in most cases
- **On the right side**: A pattern of high vagal positive stimulation proximally and absent/low vagal stimulation distally is diagnostic for right NRLN (Randolph Ch 36)
- **Audio-only systems are problematic**: They do not offer information on waveform morphology, threshold, amplitude, or latency; definitive LOS determination and artifact differentiation may be impossible (Randolph Ch 36)

### Signal Parameters

| Parameter | Normal Value |
|---|---|
| Amplitude | >100 microvolts (typically 500-2000 microvolts); amplitude >250 microvolts highly predictive of functioning RLN (Caragacianu et al., Randolph Ch 36) |
| Latency (RLN stimulation) | 3-4 ms |
| Latency (vagal stimulation) | 6-8 ms (left vagal latency significantly longer than right due to longer vagal/RLN pathway) (Randolph Ch 36) |
| Threshold | <0.8 mA (RLN maximally depolarises at 0.8 mA; 1 mA is used for routine suprathreshold stimulation, 2 mA for mapping) (Randolph Ch 36) |

### Anesthesia Considerations (Randolph Ch 36)

- **Neuromuscular blockade must be avoided** during the monitoring phase -- any residual blockade will affect EMG activity
- Three approaches: (1) No muscle relaxant for entire case, (2) Succinylcholine or small dose of short-acting non-depolarising agent at induction with recovery before surgical stimulation, (3) Single induction dose of rocuronium or atracurium with spontaneous recovery
- The larynx recovers from neuromuscular blockade faster than systemic skeletal muscle (shorter response time)
- Inhalational anaesthesia must be sufficiently deep to prevent spontaneous vocal cord activity, which would confound monitoring

### Loss of Signal (LOS) -- Definition and Management

**Definition of LOS (INMSG, Randolph Ch 36)**: Three conditions must be present for true LOS:
1. Satisfactory baseline EMG (V1 amplitude >500 microvolts)
2. No or low response (amplitude <250 microvolts) with stimulation at 1-2 mA in a dry field
3. Absence of laryngeal twitch and/or glottis twitch on ipsilateral vagal stimulation

**LOS Troubleshooting Algorithm (Randolph Ch 36)**:

1. **Assess laryngeal twitch** while stimulating the vagus (palpate posterior cricoid plate):
   - **Laryngeal twitch present** = Recording side problem (stimulation side and nerve are functional) -- reposition ETT as the most common cause is tube malposition (rotation, depth, or size error in 3.8-23% of monitored patients)
   - **Laryngeal twitch absent** = Stimulation side problem or true neural injury -- confirm stimulation current 1-2 mA, dry field, check current return on monitor, test probe on strap/SCM muscle; check neuromuscular blockade
   - If strap/SCM responds but contralateral vagus is negative = check connections and consider neuromuscular blockade
   - If contralateral vagus is positive = consider ipsilateral neural injury

2. **If equipment OK -- localise the lesion by retrograde nerve mapping**:
   - Start stimulating at the laryngeal entry point and progress proximally along the entire nerve
   - **Type 1 LOS (segmental injury)**: Signal present below the injury site but absent above it -- identifies the specific neuropraxic segment; review for traction, compression, clamping, or thermal injury at that point
   - **Type 2 LOS (global injury)**: No signal anywhere along the RLN and vagus; implies an intralaryngeal focus of dysfunction (Randolph Ch 36)

3. **Intraoperative management after confirmed LOS**:
   - Check if a clip or suture is compressing the nerve -- if identified and corrected, damage may be reversible (Randolph Ch 36)
   - If nerve is visually intact = likely neuropraxia (traction injury) = observe
   - If nerve is transected = immediate repair (primary anastomosis or nerve graft)
   - **Critical decision**: If V2 shows LOS on the first side during planned bilateral thyroidectomy, give 20 minutes for recovery. If no recovery, **STOP** and do not proceed to the contralateral side (Randolph Ch 36, INMSG 2018). Goretzki et al. showed that when the second side was operated on after first-side LOS, 19% of patients developed bilateral VCP (Randolph Ch 36)

### Green-Yellow-Red-Black EMG Model (INMSG 2018, Randolph Ch 36)

| EMG Stage | Definition | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Green** | Stable normative EMG (amplitude >50% of baseline, latency <10% of baseline) | No risk of VCP |
| **Yellow** | Amplitude decrease >50% AND latency increase >10% ("combined event") | Impending neuropraxia -- often reversible if surgical manoeuvre is modified |
| **Red** | EMG amplitude drops to <100 microvolts | Evolving neuropraxia -- increased risk of VCP vs yellow stage |
| **Black** | Final prognostic EMG value (obtained up to 20 min after adverse EMG); amplitude <250 microvolts or <50% of baseline | High risk of postoperative VCP -- staged surgery strongly recommended |

### Intermittent vs Continuous IONM

| Feature | Intermittent IONM (I-IONM) | Continuous IONM (C-IONM) |
|---|---|---|
| Stimulation | Surgeon-initiated, intermittent | Automatic, continuous vagal stimulation (1 impulse/sec) via implanted vagal electrode (APS electrode) |
| Detection | After-the-fact (detects injury post-hoc) | Real-time (detects impending injury before permanent damage) |
| Equipment | Standard NIM tube + stimulator probe | Additional vagal electrode (APS -- automatic periodic stimulation) |
| Response time | Delayed -- injury detected at next stimulation | Immediate -- EMG change alerts surgeon in real time |
| Baseline requirement | V1 >500 microvolts | V1 >500 microvolts (essential -- below this, artefacts may be misconstrued and ROC analysis yields amplitude threshold of 259 microvolts for VCP prediction) (Randolph Ch 36) |
| Evidence | Extensive | Growing (Schneider 2013) |
| Cost | Standard | Higher |

### C-IONM Combined EMG Changes (Schneider 2013, Randolph Ch 36)

C-IONM can detect impending nerve injury through progressive EMG changes -- the "combined electromyographic event":

| Warning Sign | Significance |
|---|---|
| Amplitude decrease >50% from baseline | Early warning -- reduce traction, pause dissection |
| Latency increase >10% | Nerve stress -- release tension on nerve |
| Combined amplitude decrease + latency increase | Imminent injury -- immediate action required (the clinically important "combined event") |
| Complete loss of signal | Nerve injury has occurred |

C-IONM enables the surgeon to (1) identify impending nerve injury as it unfolds, (2) release distressed nerves by reversing causative surgical manoeuvres, and (3) verify functional nerve recovery after intraoperative LOS (Randolph Ch 36).

### Evidence: Does IONM Prevent RLN Injury?

| Finding | Evidence |
|---|---|
| IONM does NOT reduce permanent RLN injury rates | Multiple RCTs and meta-analyses; an adequately powered study would require 9 million patients per arm for benign goiter and 40,000 per arm for malignancy (Dralle, Randolph Ch 36) |
| IONM may reduce bilateral VCP | By preventing contralateral dissection when LOS on first side (strongest benefit); Goretzki showed complete avoidance of bilateral VCP with LOS-guided staging |
| IONM reduces temporary VCP rates | Acknowledged by AAOHNS guidelines (Randolph Ch 36) |
| IONM aids in nerve identification | Particularly useful in reoperations, advanced cancer, and distorted anatomy; reduces RLN identification time |
| IONM predicts postoperative function | V2 signal intact: negative predictive value 92-100% for normal postoperative vocal cord function; amplitude >250 microvolts is highly predictive of a functioning RLN (Randolph Ch 36) |
| IONM positive predictive value | Lower and variable -- directly related to accurate LOS definition and equipment troubleshooting (Randolph Ch 36) |
| IONM is a teaching tool | Helps trainees identify the nerve more reliably; learning curve mastered in 50-100 cases (Randolph Ch 36) |
| Organisational support | AAOHNS, ATA (2015 Guidelines), AHNS consensus statements on recurrent cancer, central neck dissection, and invasive thyroid carcinoma all recognise IONM as an important adjunct (Randolph Ch 36) |

### Dralle et al. (2004) -- Key Findings

- 16,448 thyroid operations, 29,998 nerves at risk
- **Visual identification** is the gold standard (reduces RLN palsy vs no identification)
- IONM added to visual identification showed a trend toward reduced paralysis but was not statistically significant
- Reoperative surgery had the highest nerve injury rates (8-12% vs 1-3% for primary surgery)
- Extent of resection (bilateral > unilateral) was an independent risk factor
- Risk factors for permanent paresis in descending order of odds ratio (Randolph Ch 36): carcinoma recurrence (6.66), goiter recurrence (4.67), carcinoma first operation (2.04), hemithyroidectomy vs subtotal resection (1.76), no nerve identification vs identification (1.41), hospital experience (1.34), surgeon experience (1.23)

### Staged Thyroidectomy Rationale (Randolph Ch 36, INMSG 2018)

- Bilateral VCP results in tracheostomy in approximately 30% of patients
- If LOS occurs on the first side of a planned bilateral thyroidectomy, postponing contralateral surgery is "possibly the utmost extension of neural prognostication function of IONM" (Randolph Ch 36)
- Al-Qurayshi et al. showed that IONM with LOS incorporated into surgical strategy and leading to staged surgery was the most cost-effective algorithm for all rates of contralateral nerve paralysis (1-17%) (Randolph Ch 36)
- **Preoperative informed consent** should include discussion of the possibility of staging based on intraoperative monitoring findings
- **Operate on the dominant (cancer) side first** so the more critical side is completed before any LOS may occur
- **Timing of completion surgery**: (1) Laryngeal recovery is the primary determinant -- laryngoscopy at 2 weeks to 2 months, then every 4 weeks; (2) Completion is safest <3 days or >3 months after primary surgery; (3) No oncological disadvantage if completion is within 6 months for most differentiated thyroid cancers (Randolph Ch 36)

## Mechanisms of RLN Injury (Randolph Ch 42)

### Sunderland Classification of Nerve Injury

| Degree | Histological Level | Description | Recovery |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | Myelin | Interruption of impulse conduction without anatomic disruption (compression/ischaemia) | Full recovery expected (neuropraxia) |
| 2nd | Axon | Endoneurial tube intact, axon loses continuity | Good recovery -- intact endoneurial tubules guide regenerating axons to original targets |
| 3rd | Endoneurium | Intrafascicular structure disorganised | Variable recovery -- axons may reinnervate wrong target (synkinesis); scarring may block regeneration |
| 4th | Perineurium | Fascicles disrupted | Poor recovery -- greater disorganisation and scarring |
| 5th | Epineurium | Complete nerve transection | No spontaneous functional recovery -- no conduit for regeneration |

- Mixed lesions (heterogeneous injury within the same nerve) are common in thyroidectomy injuries (Randolph Ch 42)

### Injury Mechanisms During Thyroid Surgery (Randolph Ch 36, 42)

| Mechanism | Frequency (Descending Order) | Permanent VCP Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Traction (especially at LOB during lobar retraction) | Most common | 1.4% |
| Thermal (energy devices) | | 28% |
| Compression | | 0% |
| Clamping | | 50% |
| Ligature entrapment | | 0% |
| Suction-related | | 0% |
| Transection | Least common | 100% |

(Data from Dionigi et al., cited in Randolph Ch 36)

- **Thermal injury**: Critical temperature for RLN injury is approximately 60 degrees C. Ultrasonic, bipolar, and integrated energy devices cause LOS when fired within 1 mm of the nerve but not at 2 mm distance (Randolph Ch 42)
- **Traction injury**: In pigs, LOS documented at traction power of 2.83 MPa; all animals recovered within 7 days without structural nerve damage. Signal loss with traction is usually recovered unless amplitude drops >50% (Randolph Ch 42)
- **Increased latency is the first sign of injury** before amplitude loss occurs (Randolph Ch 42)
- The RLN tolerates appropriate surgical dissection -- nerves requiring extensive dissection of >5 cm in goiter do not have higher paralysis rates than those requiring less dissection (Chiang et al., Randolph Ch 36)

### Synkinesis and Reinnervation Biology (Randolph Ch 42)

- After RLN transection, Wallerian degeneration begins within hours distally; proximal stump degenerates back to the first node of Ranvier
- Regenerating axons grow at 1-3 mm/day, guided by Schwann cells that secrete neurotrophins (NGF, BDNF, NT4/5)
- **Synkinesis**: Misdirected regenerating axons reinnervate wrong targets, causing opposing and inappropriate muscle contractions with no net movement -- "aberrant and poorly functioning reinnervation, rather than denervation, is the most common laryngeal problem after RLN injury" (Crumley, Randolph Ch 42)
- **Favourable synkinesis**: Maintains vocal fold in a good position with preserved muscle bulk
- **Unfavourable synkinesis**: Paradoxical adduction with inspiration, spasms disrupting speech
- Preferential reinnervation of adductor muscles with poor/no reinnervation of PCA is a key explanation for vocal fold immobility despite reinnervation (similar to flexion contracture of a limb) (Randolph Ch 42)
- The reinnervation window closes by approximately 2 years when muscle denervation atrophy reaches the terminal phase (Randolph Ch 42)
- The RLN has a strong propensity for regeneration -- even transected and ligated nerves show some degree of regeneration, though normal motion never returns (Randolph Ch 42)

## RLN Surgical Dissection Tips (Randolph Ch 36)

1. **"Don't see, don't cut" rule**: During thyroidectomy, never cut any band of tissue in the distribution of the RLN that is not transparent without neural stimulation
2. **Nerve wins over parathyroid**: If the nerve and a parathyroid compete for surgical attention, prioritise the nerve -- the parathyroid can be autotransplanted
3. **Confine pretracheal dissection** to the anterior face of the trachea -- avoid extending dissection or cautery laterally where the RLN can be drawn up and injured
4. **Vagal stimulation first and last** is a must (V1 and V2)
5. **Watch the nerve as you retract**: In the final stages at the LOB, lobar retraction can convey tension to the nerve through posterior leaflets of the LOB; a feeding artery may bowstring the nerve
6. **Ventral delivery is risky** until you see the nerve at the LOB -- precipitous ventral delivery may stretch the nerve
7. **Keep the nerve under direct visual control** at all stages, especially at the LOB
8. **If the distal nerve looks thin**, go back down and redissect it to exclude branching
9. **Beware the large posterior branch misleading you** -- the anterior (motor) branch may be smaller but is typically the critical branch
10. **After surgery is complete**: Avoid imprudent suction, gauze, or peanuts near the nerve -- fingertips are better than instruments
11. **Bipolar cautery near the nerve**: Use fine jeweller's-tipped cautery, applied transiently
12. **Anterior cricoid arch** is a superb midline landmark -- the RLN is at risk until it dives under the inferior constrictor at the lower edge of the lateral cricoid

## RLN Reinnervation

### Incidence and Timing

- Spontaneous reinnervation after RLN injury is unpredictable
- Synkinesis (aberrant reinnervation with simultaneous adductor and abductor contraction) occurs in ~40% of recovered nerves -- may cause paradoxical vocal cord movement
- For the first 3 months after RLN section, laryngeal musculature atrophies; from 3 to 9 months, evidence of ipsilateral laryngeal reinnervation appears by EMG (Randolph Ch 36)

### Ansa Cervicalis-to-RLN Anastomosis

**Technique**:
1. Identify the ansa cervicalis (typically the superior root from C1 descending on the carotid sheath)
2. Mobilise an adequate length of the ansa cervicalis nerve
3. Perform microsurgical end-to-end anastomosis between the ansa cervicalis and the distal RLN stump
4. Use 9-0 or 10-0 nylon sutures under microscopic magnification
5. Tension-free anastomosis with epineural suturing

**Rationale**:
- The ansa cervicalis provides tonic input to the thyroarytenoid muscle
- This maintains vocal fold tone and bulk, preventing atrophy
- Does NOT restore vocal fold movement (phonation is achieved through medialisation effect of reinnervated adductor muscles providing tone)

**Indications**:
- Known RLN transection during surgery (immediate repair if primary anastomosis not possible)
- Planned RLN sacrifice for cancer resection
- Selected cases of delayed RLN paralysis with poor recovery

**Outcomes**:
- Voice improvement in 80-90% of patients
- Better voice quality than static medialisation procedures alone in some studies
- Takes 4-6 months for reinnervation to occur
- No restoration of vocal fold mobility (provides bulk and tone only)

### Primary RLN Repair

- **Indication**: Clean RLN transection identified intraoperatively
- **Technique**: Tension-free end-to-end microsurgical anastomosis using 9-0 nylon
- **Timing**: Immediate (at the time of transection) gives best results
- **Outcome**: Partial return of vocal fold tone; synkinesis common; normal motion never returns even with precise realignment and repair (Randolph Ch 42)
- Even after transection, every nerve regenerates to some degree even if ligated -- but this results in dysfunctional motion from synkinesis rather than useful recovery (Randolph Ch 42)

### Free Nerve Grafting

- **Indication**: RLN segment loss where primary repair is not possible (gap >5 mm)
- **Graft sources**: Great auricular nerve, sural nerve, ansa cervicalis
- **Technique**: Interposition graft with microsurgical anastomosis at both ends

## Bilateral Vocal Cord Palsy (BVCP) Management

### Presentation

- **Acute BVCP**: Airway compromise immediately post-extubation (stridor, dyspnoea)
- **Both cords fixed in midline (paramedian position)**: Good voice but severely compromised airway
- Over time, reinnervation tends to shift both vocal folds toward the midline, worsening airway (Randolph Ch 42)
- **Emergency**: Requires immediate airway management

### Management Algorithm

**Acute phase**:
1. **Re-intubation**: Immediate if possible
2. **Tracheostomy**: If intubation fails or for medium-term airway management
3. **Observation**: Wait 6-12 months for possible spontaneous recovery before definitive intervention

**Definitive procedures** (if no recovery after 6-12 months):

| Procedure | Description | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Posterior cordotomy | Transverse laser incision of the posterior vocal cord | Enlarges glottic airway; some voice deterioration |
| Medial arytenoidectomy | Partial or total removal of one arytenoid cartilage (laser or open) | Good airway improvement; moderate voice deterioration |
| Posterior cordotomy + partial arytenoidectomy | Combined procedure for optimal airway | Best airway improvement; voice compromise |
| Suture lateralisation | Suture-based lateral fixation of one vocal cord | Reversible; useful as a temporising measure |
| Reinnervation (bilateral) | Bilateral ansa cervicalis-RLN anastomosis | Experimental; aims to restore tone to both cords |

### Trade-off

- Airway and voice are inversely related in BVCP management
- Procedures that improve airway (lateralisation) worsen voice and vice versa
- The goal is to achieve a satisfactory balance: adequate airway without tracheostomy while maintaining serviceable voice

## Pre- and Postoperative Management of RLN Injury

### Preoperative Assessment

1. **Laryngoscopy (VLS)**: Mandatory before any thyroid surgery to document baseline vocal cord function (Randolph Ch 36)
   - Flexible nasolaryngoscopy in clinic
   - Document mobility of both vocal cords
   - Particularly important in revision surgery, advanced thyroid cancer, and patients with hoarseness
   - **Rationale (Randolph Ch 36)**: (1) VCP may present preoperatively without voice complaints; (2) preoperative VCP suggests invasive malignancy and informs planning; (3) VCP may exist even in benign disease (approximately 0.7%); (4) management of the invaded RLN is affected by knowledge of preoperative function; (5) postoperative VCP may be wrongly attributed without preoperative documentation; (6) Scandinavian Quality Register found that RLN paralysis rates double when all patients receive postoperative laryngeal examination
2. **Voice assessment**: Baseline GRBAS or VHI-10 score (Randolph Ch 43)
3. **CT/MRI**: Evaluate tumour relationship to RLN in thyroid cancer cases; also identify subtle laryngeal signs of VCP (PCA atrophy, TA atrophy, anteromedial arytenoid deviation) (Randolph Ch 36)

### Preoperative Counselling

- Inform patient of RLN injury risk (1-3% permanent for primary surgery; true rate may be higher -- Lo found surgeons recognised intraoperative injury in only 1% of cases while injury had actually occurred in 7% [Randolph Ch 36])
- Discuss implications: hoarseness, voice changes, aspiration risk
- Discuss IONM implementation and the possibility of staging bilateral surgery based on monitoring findings (Randolph Ch 36)
- Document informed consent

### Postoperative Assessment

| Timing | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Day 0-1 | Clinical assessment of voice quality; cough effectiveness |
| Day 1-7 | Flexible laryngoscopy if voice abnormal |
| 6 weeks | Repeat laryngoscopy for all patients with abnormal voice |
| 3 months | If no recovery -- early medialization consideration |
| 6-12 months | Definitive assessment for permanent procedures |

### Management of Unilateral VCP

**Early phase (0-6 months)**:
1. **Voice therapy**: Speech-language pathologist -- compensatory strategies, push exercises; Heuer et al. found 68% of female and 64% of male patients with UVFP did not require surgical intervention after voice therapy (Randolph Ch 43)
2. **Observation**: Most neuropraxic injuries recover within 6-12 months
3. **Injection medialization** (temporary): Hyaluronic acid, carboxymethylcellulose, calcium hydroxyapatite, or collagen injection into the paralysed vocal fold for immediate voice improvement (lasts 2-6 months, bridges recovery period)
   - Five main office-based approaches: transoral, transthyrohyoid, transthyroid cartilage, transcricothyroid, transnasal endoscopic (Randolph Ch 43)
   - Early injection is safe and useful -- not necessary to wait for nerve recovery when voice, swallowing, or aspiration is significantly affected (Randolph Ch 43)
4. **LEMG (Randolph Ch 43)**: Useful for prognosis when VCP persists >4 weeks but <6 months; active voluntary motor unit potential recruitment and polyphasic motor unit potentials predict recovery

**Late phase (>6-12 months, no recovery)**:
1. **Medialization thyroplasty (Type 1)**: Silastic or Gore-Tex implant via external window in thyroid cartilage -- permanent medialization (popularised by Isshiki; Randolph Ch 43)
2. **Arytenoid adduction**: Rotates the arytenoid medially, particularly useful for posterior glottic gap
3. **Combined medialization + arytenoid adduction**: For large posterior gaps
4. **RLN reinnervation** (ansa cervicalis-to-RLN): Maintains vocal fold tone long-term

### Aspiration Management

- Unilateral VCP: aspiration risk increased (especially liquids) -- thicken fluids, chin-tuck swallow
- FEES or videofluoroscopy to objectively assess swallowing safety
- Injection medialization improves both voice and swallowing

## Key Points for Exam

1. The RLN is most vulnerable at the **ligament of Berry** where it enters the larynx -- Berlin found the RLN courses through the LOB itself in 30% of cases (Randolph Ch 36)
2. Right RLN loops around the subclavian artery; left RLN loops around the aortic arch
3. RLN-ITA relationship has 6 variations -- the ITA is NOT a reliable sole landmark; Reed found 28 different patterns with bilateral symmetry in only 17% (Randolph Ch 36)
4. **Tubercle of Zuckerkandl** "points to the nerve" at the LOB; RLN is deep to TOZ in 93%, ventral (high risk) in 7% (Randolph Ch 36)
5. **Superior parathyroid** is posterior to RLN; **inferior parathyroid** is anterior to RLN
6. Non-recurrent laryngeal nerve: 0.5-1% right side (associated with arteria lusoria), <0.04% left; IONM algorithm (proximal positive/distal negative vagal stimulation) reliably identifies NRLN before dissection (Randolph Ch 36)
7. **Cernea classification** (EBSLN): Type 2b (below the superior pole) is at highest risk; prevalence rises from 14% in small goiters to 54% in large goiters (Randolph Ch 35)
8. IONM uses the **L1-V1-R1-R2-V2-L2 algorithm** (Randolph 2011) -- L1 and L2 are pre/postoperative laryngoscopy, not just vagal stimulation
9. Loss of signal on V2 during planned bilateral thyroidectomy: give 20 minutes for recovery; if no recovery, **STOP -- do not proceed to contralateral side** (INMSG 2018, Randolph Ch 36)
10. **Green-Yellow-Red-Black EMG model**: Yellow = combined event (amplitude >50% decrease + latency >10% increase) = reversible if manoeuvre modified; Black = final prognostic value <250 microvolts = stage surgery (INMSG 2018)
11. IONM has NOT proven to reduce permanent RLN injury rates but prevents bilateral VCP, reduces temporary VCP, and aids identification
12. **Most common mechanism of injury** is traction at the LOB; thermal and clamping injuries carry the highest permanent VCP rates (Randolph Ch 36, 42)
13. Continuous IONM detects impending injury in real time (amplitude decrease + latency increase = combined event)
14. Ansa cervicalis-to-RLN anastomosis provides vocal fold tone but NOT movement; normal motion never returns after RLN transection even with repair (Randolph Ch 42)
15. BVCP management: tracheostomy then wait 6-12 months then posterior cordotomy or arytenoidectomy
16. Preoperative VLS is mandatory before all thyroid surgeries -- the Scandinavian Quality Register showed RLN paralysis rates double when all patients receive postoperative laryngeal examination (Randolph Ch 36)
17. Staged thyroidectomy timing: safest <3 days or >3 months after primary; no oncological disadvantage if completion within 6 months for differentiated thyroid cancer (Randolph Ch 36)

> [!tip] RLN Surgical Dissection Tips (Randolph Ch 36)
> - **"Don't see, don't cut"**: Never divide non-transparent tissue in the RLN distribution without neural stimulation
> - **Vagal stimulation first (V1) and last (V2)**: Only accept negative stimulation as true negative after a true positive has been identified
> - **Fingertips over instruments**: After completing surgery, avoid imprudent suction, gauze, or peanuts near the nerve
> - **Three approaches to RLN**: Lateral (routine), Inferior (revision/goiter), Superior (substernal goiter/NRLN/failed other approaches) -- the superior approach targets the most constant point of the RLN in the neck
