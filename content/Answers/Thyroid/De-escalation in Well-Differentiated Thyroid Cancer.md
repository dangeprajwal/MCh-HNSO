## The Paradigm Shift in Thyroid Cancer Management

### Historical Context

Historically, differentiated thyroid cancer (DTC) management was aggressive:
- **Total thyroidectomy** for virtually all tumours >1 cm
- **High-dose RAI** (100-150 mCi) for all patients after total thyroidectomy
- **TSH suppression** (<0.1 mU/L) for all patients
- **Routine completion thyroidectomy** after incidental finding on lobectomy

### Modern De-escalation Principles

The 2015 ATA guidelines (Haugen et al.) codified a de-escalation approach based on accumulating evidence:

| Historical Approach | De-escalated Approach | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Total thyroidectomy for all DTC >1 cm | Lobectomy sufficient for T1-T2 N0 without adverse features | Adam 2014; ATA 2015 |
| Immediate surgery for all PTMC | Active surveillance for select PTMC | Ito 2003, 2014 |
| High-dose RAI (100-150 mCi) for all | Low-dose RAI (30 mCi) or no RAI for low-risk | HiLo 2012; ESTIMABL1 2012 |
| TSH suppression <0.1 for all | Risk-adapted TSH targets | ATA 2015 |
| Routine bilateral CND | Therapeutic CND only (selective prophylactic) | ATA 2015 |

## Lobectomy vs Total Thyroidectomy

### ATA 2015 Guideline Recommendations

| Tumour Characteristics | Recommended Surgery | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| PTMC (<1 cm), unifocal, no ETE, N0 | Lobectomy (or active surveillance) | Strong |
| 1-4 cm, unifocal, intrathyroidal, N0 | Lobectomy sufficient (TT also acceptable) | Strong |
| >4 cm | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Any size with gross ETE (T4) | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Bilateral disease / multifocal | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Clinical N1 disease | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Prior radiation to head/neck | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Planned RAI (for adjuvant therapy) | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |

### Evidence for De-escalation of Surgical Extent

#### Adam et al. (2014) — NCDB and SEER Analysis

- **Design**: Retrospective analysis of 61,775 patients with PTC (1998-2006)
- **Comparison**: Total thyroidectomy (n=54,926) vs lobectomy (n=6,849)
- **Key findings**:
  - No survival difference for tumours 1.0-4.0 cm after multivariable adjustment
  - 10-year OS: TT 93.9% vs lobectomy 94.5% (NS)
  - No difference when stratified by tumour size (1-2 cm and 2.1-4 cm)
  - Even in tumours 2.1-4 cm without adverse features, lobectomy had similar survival
- **Limitations**: Retrospective, no data on recurrence, selection bias (lobectomy patients may have been lower risk)

#### Other Supporting Evidence

| Study | Year | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Mendelsohn 2010 (SEER) | 2010 | No survival difference for PTC 1-4 cm treated with lobectomy vs TT |
| Bilimoria 2007 (NCDB) | 2007 | TT showed improved recurrence/survival for tumours >1 cm (contradicted later studies) |
| Nixon 2012 (MSKCC) | 2012 | Lobectomy for low-risk PTC 1-4 cm: 2% recurrence at 10 years |
| Haigh 2005 (SEER) | 2005 | No survival benefit for TT in PTC 1-2 cm |

### Advantages and Disadvantages of Lobectomy

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|
| Avoids bilateral RLN risk | Cannot administer RAI (if needed later) |
| Lower hypocalcemia risk (no parathyroid risk) | Requires completion thyroidectomy if high-risk pathology found |
| No lifelong thyroid hormone dependence (50-70%) | Thyroglobulin less reliable as tumour marker |
| Shorter operative time | Contralateral lobe recurrence risk (1-5%) |
| Lower complication rate overall | Cannot perform radioiodine whole-body scan |

### Factors Favouring Total Thyroidectomy (Despite De-escalation Trend)

1. Planned postoperative RAI therapy
2. Bilateral/multifocal disease
3. Family history of DTC
4. History of radiation exposure
5. Clinically apparent lymph node metastasis (N1)
6. Gross extrathyroidal extension
7. Aggressive histological variants (tall cell, columnar, hobnail)
8. BRAF V600E + TERT co-mutation (if known preoperatively)
9. Patient preference for simplified follow-up (no contralateral concern)

## Active Surveillance for Papillary Thyroid Microcarcinoma

### Background

- Papillary thyroid microcarcinoma (PTMC): PTC <=10 mm
- Prevalence at autopsy: 5-35% (most are indolent and never clinically significant)
- Overdiagnosis and overtreatment have been major concerns
- Most PTMCs have an indolent course with minimal progression risk

### The Kuma Hospital Experience (Ito/Miyauchi)

#### Protocol

- **Institution**: Kuma Hospital, Kobe, Japan (largest single-institution thyroid practice)
- **Study period**: Ongoing since 1993 (landmark publications 2003, 2010, 2014)
- **Patients**: Those with low-risk PTMC who chose observation over immediate surgery

#### Eligibility Criteria for Active Surveillance

| Include | Exclude |
|---|---|
| Cytologically confirmed PTC <=10 mm | Tumour adjacent to trachea (risk of invasion) |
| Intrathyroidal | Tumour adjacent to RLN (risk of nerve invasion) |
| No clinical LN metastasis (N0) | Clinical N1 disease |
| No distant metastasis | Aggressive cytology (tall cell features) |
| Patient willing to comply with follow-up | Patient preference for surgery |
| No prior radiation exposure | PTMC with rapid growth on prior imaging |

#### Surveillance Protocol

| Interval | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Every 6 months (first 2 years) | Neck ultrasound + clinical examination |
| Annually (after 2 years) | Neck ultrasound + clinical examination |
| Triggers for intervention | Size increase >=3 mm, new lymph node metastasis, patient preference |

#### Key Outcomes (Ito 2014)

- **1,235 patients** observed for a median of 75 months
- **Tumour enlargement (>=3 mm)**: 8% at 10 years (low and slow progression)
- **New lymph node metastasis**: 3.8% at 10 years
- **Distant metastasis during observation**: 0%
- **Progression more common in patients <40 years** (patient age is significantly related to progression)
- **Disease-specific mortality**: 0%
- **Rescue surgery after progression**: 100% successful with excellent outcomes

#### Key Findings

1. The vast majority of PTMCs do NOT progress during observation
2. Progression is more common in younger patients (<40 years)
3. Delayed surgery after disease progression does NOT worsen outcomes
4. Active surveillance avoids complications of surgery in 90%+ of patients
5. Disease-specific mortality is zero in all published AS series

### ATA 2015 Position on Active Surveillance

The 2015 ATA guidelines include active surveillance as an acceptable management option for:
- **Very low-risk PTMC** (unifocal, intrathyroidal, N0, not near trachea/RLN)
- **Patients who are high surgical risk** (comorbidities)
- **Patient preference** (after informed shared decision-making)

### MSKCC Active Surveillance Programme

- American validation of the Kuma Hospital approach
- Tuttle et al.: 291 patients, median follow-up 25 months
- Tumour growth >=3 mm in 3.8%
- New nodal metastasis: 1%
- Demonstrates feasibility in Western populations

## Low-Dose vs High-Dose Radioiodine Ablation

### Background

Radioiodine (I-131) is administered after total thyroidectomy for DTC to:
1. **Ablate remnant thyroid tissue** (improve thyroglobulin monitoring)
2. **Treat occult micrometastatic disease** (adjuvant therapy)
3. **Enable postoperative diagnostic whole-body scan**

Historically, high doses (100-150 mCi / 3.7-5.5 GBq) were used. The HiLo and ESTIMABL1 trials challenged this practice.

### HiLo Trial (Mallick 2012)

- **Design**: Multicentre, randomised, non-inferiority, 2x2 factorial design (UK)
- **Patients**: 438 patients with low-risk DTC after total thyroidectomy
- **Comparison**:
  - **Dose**: Low-dose (1.1 GBq / 30 mCi) vs high-dose (3.7 GBq / 100 mCi)
  - **Preparation**: Recombinant human TSH (rhTSH) vs thyroid hormone withdrawal (THW)
- **Primary endpoint**: Successful remnant ablation at 6-8 months (stimulated Tg <2 ng/mL and negative US)
- **Results**:

| Arm | Ablation Success Rate |
|---|---|
| Low-dose + rhTSH | 85.0% |
| Low-dose + THW | 87.1% |
| High-dose + rhTSH | 90.2% |
| High-dose + THW | 92.1% |
| Non-inferiority for low-dose | Confirmed (difference within margin) |
| Non-inferiority for rhTSH | Confirmed |

- **Conclusion**: Low-dose RAI with rhTSH is non-inferior to high-dose RAI with thyroid hormone withdrawal for remnant ablation in low-risk DTC

### ESTIMABL1 Trial (Schlumberger 2012)

- **Design**: Multicentre, randomised, non-inferiority, 2x2 factorial design (France)
- **Patients**: 752 patients with low-risk DTC
- **Comparison**: Identical to HiLo (low vs high dose; rhTSH vs THW)
- **Primary endpoint**: Ablation success at 8 months (stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL and negative neck US and negative WBS)
- **Results**:

| Arm | Ablation Success |
|---|---|
| Low-dose (1.1 GBq) | 92% |
| High-dose (3.7 GBq) | 94% |
| Non-inferiority for low-dose | Confirmed |

- **Long-term follow-up (5 years)**: No difference in recurrence between low and high dose (published in Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2018)

### Implications for Clinical Practice

| Patient Risk | RAI Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Low risk (T1-T2 N0, no adverse features) | No RAI or low-dose (30 mCi) ablation |
| Low-intermediate risk (T1-T2 N1a, minor adverse features) | Low-dose ablation (30 mCi) |
| Intermediate risk (T3, N1a, aggressive histology) | Low or standard dose (30-100 mCi), case-by-case |
| High risk (T4, N1b, incomplete resection, distant mets) | High-dose therapeutic RAI (100-200 mCi) |

### Avoiding RAI in Low-Risk Patients

The 2015 ATA guidelines recommend that RAI can be avoided entirely in:
- **ATA low-risk DTC**: T1-T2 N0 M0, no vascular invasion, no aggressive histology, complete resection
- **Classic papillary, follicular variant, encapsulated follicular thyroid cancer with minor capsular invasion**
- **Expected excellent response**: Postoperative unstimulated Tg <1 ng/mL

## ATA Risk Stratification (2015)

### Risk Categories for Structural Disease Recurrence

| Risk Level | Features | Estimated Recurrence |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Intrathyroidal DTC; <=5 micrometastatic N1 (<0.2 cm); R0; no vascular invasion; no aggressive histology; clinical N0 or <=5 LN micro | 1-5% |
| Intermediate | Minor ETE; vascular invasion; aggressive histology; clinical N1 or >5 LN involved; RAI-avid metastatic foci on post-therapy WBS | 5-20% |
| High | Gross ETE (T4); incomplete resection (R1/R2); distant metastasis; postoperative Tg suggestive of distant mets; N1 with any LN >=3 cm | 20-50% |

### Dynamic Risk Stratification (Response to Therapy)

| Response | Criteria | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Excellent | Tg <0.2 ng/mL (suppressed) or <1 ng/mL (stimulated); negative imaging | Reduce intensity of follow-up; loosen TSH suppression |
| Indeterminate | Tg 0.2-1 ng/mL (suppressed) or 1-10 ng/mL (stimulated); nonspecific imaging | Continue surveillance |
| Biochemical incomplete | Tg >1 ng/mL (suppressed) or >10 ng/mL (stimulated); negative imaging | Consider additional RAI or observation |
| Structural incomplete | Structural disease (imaging positive) | Directed therapy (surgery, RAI, external beam RT, systemic) |

## TSH Suppression — Risk-Adapted Targets

| ATA Risk Category | Initial TSH Target | After Excellent Response |
|---|---|---|
| Low risk | 0.5-2.0 mU/L | 0.5-2.0 mU/L |
| Intermediate risk | 0.1-0.5 mU/L | 0.5-2.0 mU/L |
| High risk (with disease) | <0.1 mU/L | 0.1-0.5 mU/L |
| Structural persistent disease | <0.1 mU/L | Maintain <0.1 mU/L |

## Ongoing Controversies

### Unanswered Questions

1. **Optimal threshold for lobectomy**: Is 4 cm the right cutoff, or can lobectomy be extended to larger tumours?
2. **Active surveillance in Western populations**: Long-term data still accumulating outside Japan
3. **Molecular markers in surgical decision-making**: Should preoperative BRAF/TERT status influence surgical extent?
4. **Omission of RAI in intermediate-risk patients**: IoN trial and other ongoing studies
5. **Completion thyroidectomy after lobectomy**: How aggressive should the threshold be for re-operation?
6. **Age and sex as factors in de-escalation**: Younger patients may benefit from different approaches

### The IoN Trial (Currently Enrolling)

- Randomised trial of ablation versus no ablation in low-risk DTC
- Aims to definitively determine if RAI can be omitted entirely
- Primary endpoint: recurrence-free survival

## Key Points for Exam

1. **ATA 2015 guidelines** represent a paradigm shift toward de-escalation in low-risk DTC
2. **Lobectomy is sufficient** for unifocal T1-T2 N0 PTC without adverse features (Adam 2014)
3. **Active surveillance** is an acceptable option for low-risk PTMC (Ito/Kuma Hospital protocol)
4. AS criteria: PTMC, intrathyroidal, N0, not near trachea/RLN, patient willing to comply
5. 8% tumour enlargement and 3.8% new LN at 10 years during AS — low progression rate
6. **HiLo trial**: Low-dose RAI (30 mCi) + rhTSH is non-inferior to high-dose RAI (100 mCi) + THW
7. **ESTIMABL1**: Independently confirmed HiLo findings in French population
8. RAI can be **avoided entirely** in ATA low-risk patients with excellent response to surgery
9. Dynamic risk stratification using thyroglobulin response guides ongoing management
10. TSH suppression targets are now risk-adapted (not <0.1 for all patients)
