## The Paradigm Shift in Thyroid Cancer Management

### Historical Context

Historically, differentiated thyroid cancer (DTC) management was aggressive:
- **Total thyroidectomy** for virtually all tumours >1 cm
- **High-dose RAI** (100-150 mCi) for all patients after total thyroidectomy
- **TSH suppression** (<0.1 mU/L) for all patients
- **Routine completion thyroidectomy** after incidental finding on lobectomy

### The Overdiagnosis Problem (Randolph Ch 20)

The detection of papillary microcarcinoma has reached epidemic proportions, accounting for over 40% of thyroid cancers excised at some centres. Autopsy studies demonstrate a prevalence of 5-36% (up to 36% in Finland, where the smallest forms were considered "a normal finding"). In the United States, autopsy prevalence of approximately 6% translates to an estimated 18 million individuals harbouring PTMC, of whom only 4.3% have been clinically detected. Despite a threefold increase in thyroid cancer incidence since 1975 (from 4.85 to 15.03 per 100,000), mortality has remained constant at 0.5 per 100,000 -- underscoring that the rising incidence is driven primarily by overdiagnosis, not a true increase in lethal disease. The South Korean screening experience (inexpensive thyroid ultrasound offered with mammography) resulted in a 14-fold increase in thyroid cancer incidence, prompting a national reckoning about screening-driven overdiagnosis.

### Modern De-escalation Principles

The 2015 ATA guidelines (Haugen et al.) codified a de-escalation approach based on accumulating evidence:

| Historical Approach | De-escalated Approach | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Total thyroidectomy for all DTC >1 cm | Lobectomy sufficient for T1-T2 N0 without adverse features | Adam 2014; ATA 2015 |
| Immediate surgery for all PTMC | Active surveillance for select PTMC | Ito 2003, 2014; Miyauchi (Kuma Hospital) |
| High-dose RAI (100-150 mCi) for all | Low-dose RAI (30 mCi) or no RAI for low-risk | HiLo 2012; ESTIMABL1 2012 |
| TSH suppression <0.1 for all | Risk-adapted TSH targets | ATA 2015 |
| Routine bilateral CND | Therapeutic CND only (selective prophylactic) | ATA 2015 |
| FNA all suspicious subcentimetric nodules | No FNA for subcentimetric nodules (even if suspicious) | ATA 2015 |

## Lobectomy vs Total Thyroidectomy

### ATA 2015 Guideline Recommendations

| Tumour Characteristics | Recommended Surgery | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| PTMC (<1 cm), unifocal, no ETE, N0 | Lobectomy (or active surveillance) | Strong |
| 1-4 cm, unifocal, intrathyroidal, N0 | Lobectomy sufficient (TT also acceptable) | Strong |
| >4 cm | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Any size with gross ETE (T4) | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Bilateral disease / multifocal | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Clinical N1 disease | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Prior radiation to head/neck | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Planned RAI (for adjuvant therapy) | Total thyroidectomy | Strong |

### Evidence for De-escalation of Surgical Extent

#### Adam et al. (2014) — NCDB and SEER Analysis

- **Design**: Retrospective analysis of 61,775 patients with PTC (1998-2006)
- **Comparison**: Total thyroidectomy (n=54,926) vs lobectomy (n=6,849)
- **Key findings**:
  - No survival difference for tumours 1.0-4.0 cm after multivariable adjustment
  - 10-year OS: TT 93.9% vs lobectomy 94.5% (NS)
  - No difference when stratified by tumour size (1-2 cm and 2.1-4 cm)
  - Even in tumours 2.1-4 cm without adverse features, lobectomy had similar survival
- **Limitations**: Retrospective, no data on recurrence, selection bias (lobectomy patients may have been lower risk)

#### Other Supporting Evidence

| Study | Year | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Mendelsohn 2010 (SEER) | 2010 | No survival difference for PTC 1-4 cm treated with lobectomy vs TT |
| Bilimoria 2007 (NCDB) | 2007 | TT showed improved recurrence/survival for tumours >1 cm (contradicted later studies) |
| Nixon 2012 (MSKCC) | 2012 | Lobectomy for low-risk PTC 1-4 cm: 2% recurrence at 10 years |
| Haigh 2005 (SEER) | 2005 | No survival benefit for TT in PTC 1-2 cm |
| Lin 2009 (SEER) | 2009 | 99.9% 15-year disease-specific survival for PTMC regardless of lobectomy or TT |

#### Lobectomy vs Total Thyroidectomy in PTMC (Randolph Ch 20)

Specific data on surgical extent in papillary microcarcinoma from Randolph:
- **NTCTCSG registry** (710 patients with PTMC): 6.2% overall recurrence rate. In patients with **less than near-total thyroidectomy**, recurrence was 18% in multifocal disease vs 4% in unifocal (p < 0.01). However, in near-total/total thyroidectomy patients, recurrence was 6% regardless of multifocality
- **Samsung Medical Center** (8,676 patients): Lobectomy had higher contralateral recurrence (HR 0.398 for TT, p < 0.001), but no difference in ipsilateral or nodal recurrence; the authors concluded lobectomy is reasonable initial surgery with completion thyroidectomy reserved for contralateral recurrence
- **SEER Database** (1988-2005): 99.9% 15-year disease-specific survival for PTMC whether treated with lobectomy or total thyroidectomy
- **ATA guideline recommendation**: Lobectomy is recommended for apparent unifocal intrathyroidal PTMC; completion thyroidectomy reserved for contralateral recurrence

### Advantages and Disadvantages of Lobectomy

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|
| Avoids bilateral RLN risk | Cannot administer RAI (if needed later) |
| Lower hypocalcemia risk (no parathyroid risk) | Requires completion thyroidectomy if high-risk pathology found |
| No lifelong thyroid hormone dependence (50-70%) | Thyroglobulin less reliable as tumour marker |
| Shorter operative time | Contralateral lobe recurrence risk (1-5%) |
| Lower complication rate overall | Cannot perform radioiodine whole-body scan |

### Factors Favouring Total Thyroidectomy (Despite De-escalation Trend)

1. Planned postoperative RAI therapy
2. Bilateral/multifocal disease
3. Family history of DTC
4. History of radiation exposure
5. Clinically apparent lymph node metastasis (N1)
6. Gross extrathyroidal extension
7. Aggressive histological variants (tall cell, columnar, hobnail)
8. BRAF V600E + TERT co-mutation (if known preoperatively)
9. Patient preference for simplified follow-up (no contralateral concern)

### Completion Thyroidectomy After Lobectomy for PTMC (Randolph Ch 20)

Based on current guidelines, it is unlikely that a patient who had a lobectomy for PTMC would require immediate completion thyroidectomy. Indications for completion thyroidectomy in PTMC are rare and include:
- High-grade histology on final pathology
- Gross extrathyroidal extension
- Extensive nodal involvement (>5 nodes, >2 mm in size)
- Distant metastases
- Contralateral recurrence on surveillance

## Active Surveillance for Papillary Thyroid Microcarcinoma

### Background

- Papillary thyroid microcarcinoma (PTMC): PTC <=10 mm
- Prevalence at autopsy: 5-36% (most are indolent and never clinically significant)
- Overdiagnosis and overtreatment have been major concerns
- Most PTMCs have an indolent course with minimal progression risk
- Despite 20-40% multifocality and 17-43% nodal involvement at surgery, overall mortality is well under 1% and 15-year disease-specific survival is 99.9% (Randolph Ch 20)

### The Scientific Rationale (Randolph Ch 21 — Miyauchi/Tuttle)

Miyauchi from Kuma Hospital hypothesised more than 20 years ago that most PMCs would remain small and never develop into clinically significant disease. He further posited that immediate surgery for all PMCs may be associated with more harm than good, as the few low-risk papillary thyroid cancers destined to become clinically significant would progress slowly and be very effectively treated at the time of documented disease progression. These hypotheses provided the scientific rationale for an observational clinical trial that began at Kuma Hospital in 1993 and subsequently at the Cancer Institute Hospital in Tokyo in 1995.

**Cost-effectiveness**: The calculated total cost of immediate surgery with postoperative management for 10 years (including reoperation for recurrence) was **4.1 times** the total cost of active surveillance for 10 years (including conversion surgery) -- establishing active surveillance as a cost-effective alternative.

### The Kuma Hospital Experience (Ito/Miyauchi)

#### Eligibility Criteria for Active Surveillance (Clinical Framework — Brito/Ito/Miyauchi/Tuttle)

A three-domain clinical framework classifies patients as **ideal**, **appropriate**, or **inappropriate** for active surveillance:

**Ideal for AS**:
- Older patients with solitary PTMC within an otherwise normal thyroid gland
- Tumour well within the lobe, surrounded by >=2 mm of normal thyroid tissue
- Motivated to avoid surgery; supportive family and referring physicians
- Followed by experienced multidisciplinary team with high-quality neck ultrasonography

**Appropriate for AS** (majority of AS patients fall here):
- Nonspecific benign findings elsewhere in thyroid
- Ill-defined tumour margins (making serial measurement harder)
- FDG-avid PMCs or multifocal PMCs
- Primary tumour 1-1.5 cm (MSKCC criteria)
- Women with childbearing potential
- Strong family history of thyroid cancer (if patient motivated for observation)
- Capsule involvement on lateral/anterior aspects (minor ETE not adjacent to RLN/trachea)

**Inappropriate for AS** (contraindications):

| Contraindication | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Tumour adjacent to trachea with obtuse or near-right angle | Risk of tracheal invasion |
| Tumour >=7 mm immediately adjacent to RLN without rim of normal thyroid tissue | Risk of nerve invasion |
| Clinical N1 disease (suspicious lymph nodes on US) | Metastatic disease |
| Vocal cord paralysis | Evidence of RLN invasion |
| Distant metastasis | Advanced disease |
| Aggressive/high-grade cytology | Biologically aggressive |
| Patient unwilling to comply with follow-up | Safety concern |
| Patient preference for surgery | Autonomy |

**Key anatomic detail (Randolph Ch 21)**: None of the PMCs <7 mm in maximal diameter demonstrated invasion into the RLN or trachea regardless of location within the thyroid. Tumours attaching the trachea at an acute angle did not show tracheal invasion (appropriate for AS), whereas those with obtuse or near-right angle were at risk (inappropriate).

#### Surveillance Protocol

| Interval | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Every 6-12 months (first 2 years) | Neck ultrasound + clinical examination |
| Annually or less frequently (after 2 years) | Neck ultrasound + clinical examination (frequency adjusted by tumour location, growth rate, other findings) |
| Thyroid function tests | Yearly; maintain TSH <3 mIU/mL (levothyroxine if needed) |
| Repeat FNA | Not routinely planned during observation |
| Triggers for intervention | Size increase >=3 mm (or 100% volume increase), new lymph node metastasis, invasion of RLN/trachea/strap muscles, patient preference |

#### Key Outcomes — Multi-Centre Active Surveillance Cohorts (Randolph Ch 21, Table 21.1)

| Cohort | n (Tumour Size) | Median F/U | Size Increase >=3 mm | Stable Size | Size Decrease >=3 mm | Volume Increase >=50% | LN Metastasis |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ito (Kuma Hospital) | 1,235 (<1 cm) | 5 yrs | 5% (5 yr), 8% (10 yr) | 92-95% | — | — | 2% (5 yr), 4% (10 yr) |
| Sugitani (Tokyo) | 415 (<1 cm) | 6.5 yrs | 6% | 91% | 3% | — | 1% |
| Tuttle (MSKCC) | 291 (<1.5 cm) | 2 yrs | 4% | 92% | 4% | 12% | 0% |
| Kwon (Korea) | 192 (<1 cm) | 2.5 yrs | 2% | 95% | 3% | 14% | 0.5% |

- **Tumour shrinkage**: Decrease >3 mm in 3-4% of patients; volume decrease >50% in 7-17%. Possible causes include FNA biopsy-related damage, natural history, or immune response
- **Distant metastasis during observation**: 0% across all cohorts
- **Disease-specific mortality**: 0%
- **Rescue surgery after progression**: 100% successful with excellent outcomes

#### Age-Related Disease Progression (Randolph Ch 20 and Ch 21)

Progression is significantly more common in younger patients:
- Patients **<40 years**: 5.9% tumour growth and 5.3% lymphadenopathy
- Patients **>=60 years**: 2.2% tumour growth and 0.4% lymphadenopathy (p < 0.001)

**Lifetime probability of disease progression** (Miyauchi et al., estimated to age 85):

| Age at Diagnosis | Lifetime Progression Risk |
|---|---|
| 20s | 48.6% (60% to age 85) |
| 30s | 25.3% (37%) |
| 40s | 20.9% (27%) |
| 50s | 10.3% (15%) |
| 60s | 8.2% (10%) |
| 70s | 3.5% |

This indicates that more than half of patients diagnosed in their 20s may eventually require surgery, about 75% of patients in their 30s will not require surgery, and the vast majority of older patients will never need thyroid surgery in their lifetime.

#### Tumour Growth Kinetics (Randolph Ch 21)

MSKCC data showed that tumour growth demonstrated classic **log-linear exponential growth patterns**, with rates of change that varied between tumours but were remarkably constant for an individual PMC. Tumour volume doubling time is a key parameter:
- Doubling time **>2 years**: surgery may be deferred even with documented size increase
- Doubling time **<1 year**: more concerning, supports intervention

#### TSH and Progression During Active Surveillance

- Sustained TSH elevations >2.5 mIU/mL are associated with PTMC disease progression (Korean data)
- Kuma Hospital: none of 50 patients treated with levothyroxine to achieve low-normal TSH demonstrated disease progression
- **Recommendation**: Maintain TSH in the low-normal reference range during AS; avoid TSH above 2.5 mIU/mL

#### Pregnancy and Active Surveillance (Randolph Ch 21)

- Initial small case series suggested frequent enlargement during pregnancy
- Larger follow-up study of **51 pregnancies**: only 8% had enlargement during pregnancy; after delivery, 2 underwent surgery and 2 had disease stabilisation and continued observation
- PMCs are more likely to grow in younger patients, so growth during pregnancy may reflect underlying tumour kinetics rather than a specific pregnancy effect
- **Women with childbearing potential are considered appropriate candidates for AS**, provided they understand the potential for pregnancy-associated tumour growth

#### Molecular Markers and Active Surveillance (Randolph Ch 20 and Ch 21)

- **BRAF V600E**: Present in >50% of PMCs but **not predictive** of tumour progression or identification of nodal metastases during active surveillance
- **TERT promoter mutations**: Not found in any PMCs that went to surgery after a period of observation
- In the absence of traditional high-risk features, BRAF V600E alone should **not** be considered an indication for aggressive treatment
- Currently, molecular profiling cannot accurately identify the few tumours destined to progress and therefore is **not used** in patient selection for AS

#### Indications to Transition from Active Surveillance to Surgery (Randolph Ch 21, Box 21.1)

1. **Increase in primary tumour size**:
   - >=3 mm increase in maximal diameter, and/or
   - >=100% increase in tumour volume
2. **Identification of metastatic disease** (new lymph node metastases)
3. **Direct invasion** into surrounding structures (RLN, trachea, strap muscles)
4. **Patient preference**

**Nuances**:
- Surgery **can be considered** with a confirmed 50% volume increase based on: proximity to RLN/trachea, patient preference, or primary tumour >1 cm
- Surgery **may be deferred** even with documented size increase if: maximum diameter <13 mm (Kuma) or <15 mm (MSKCC), and/or tumour volume doubling time >2 years
- A confirmatory ultrasound 3-4 months after suspected progression is recommended before proceeding to surgery

#### Alternatives to Surgery After Active Surveillance (Randolph Ch 21)

Several groups are exploring experimental alternatives: alcohol ablation, radiofrequency ablation, and laser therapy. These may have a role if the goal is only to destroy the primary lesion. However, these procedures destroy the primary tumour marker of disease progression while potentially leaving lymph node metastases unaddressed. These remain investigational.

### ATA 2015 Position on Active Surveillance

The 2015 ATA guidelines include active surveillance as an acceptable management option for:
- **Very low-risk PTMC** (unifocal, intrathyroidal, N0, not near trachea/RLN)
- **Patients who are high surgical risk** (comorbidities)
- **Patient preference** (after informed shared decision-making)

The ATA guidelines also endorse observation without cytologic confirmation for subcentimetric thyroid nodules with highly suspicious ultrasonographic characteristics, and recommend against routine FNA biopsy of subcentimetric nodules even when suspicious features are present.

### MSKCC Active Surveillance Programme (Randolph Ch 21)

- American validation of the Kuma Hospital approach
- Tuttle et al.: 291 patients (tumours up to **1.5 cm**, broader than original Kuma <1 cm criterion), median follow-up 25 months
- Tumour growth >=3 mm in 3.8% (12.1% at 5 years)
- Volume increase >=50% in 12%; volume decrease >=50% in 7%
- New nodal metastasis: 0% (none in the MSKCC cohort)
- Demonstrates feasibility in Western populations
- MSKCC routinely offers AS for properly selected patients with tumours up to 1.5 cm; less commonly, AS offered for tumours up to 2 cm if concurrent major medical comorbidities make surgery very high risk

### Immediate Surgery vs Active Surveillance: Complication Comparison (Randolph Ch 21)

The incidence of unfavourable events was significantly higher in immediate surgery than in AS even when performed by experienced endocrine surgeons at Kuma Hospital:
- Immediate surgery risks: temporary and permanent vocal cord paralysis, temporary and permanent hypoparathyroidism, lifelong levothyroxine requirement
- Active surveillance avoids these complications in >90% of patients who never progress

## Central Neck Dissection in PTMC (Randolph Ch 20)

### Prophylactic CND — Evidence Against Routine Use

- In one study (Wada et al.), 61% of patients undergoing prophylactic CND had positive central nodes, yet recurrence was only 0.4% (comparable to 0.7% in patients with no CND who had incidental PTMC)
- Male sex and age <40 are independent risk factors for large-volume central node metastases
- Meta-analysis of 19 studies (8,345 patients): positive central nodes correlated with male sex, age <45, size >5 mm, multifocality, and ETE, but **presence of positive central nodes did not correlate with recurrence**
- Risk of hypoparathyroidism was threefold higher with CND
- The majority of malignant deposits in central nodes appear to have **minimal biologic significance**

### Recommended Strategy (Randolph Ch 20)

Because recurrences in PTMC are rarely life-threatening, a better strategy is to avoid initial prophylactic CND and refer the few patients with central compartment recurrence to a high-volume thyroid surgeon. Redo central compartment surgery by experienced surgeons shows no difference in long-term complications compared with initial dissection.

## Low-Dose vs High-Dose Radioiodine Ablation

### Background

Radioiodine (I-131) is administered after total thyroidectomy for DTC to:
1. **Ablate remnant thyroid tissue** (improve thyroglobulin monitoring)
2. **Treat occult micrometastatic disease** (adjuvant therapy)
3. **Enable postoperative diagnostic whole-body scan**

Historically, high doses (100-150 mCi / 3.7-5.5 GBq) were used. The HiLo and ESTIMABL1 trials challenged this practice.

### HiLo Trial (Mallick 2012)

- **Design**: Multicentre, randomised, non-inferiority, 2x2 factorial design (UK)
- **Patients**: 438 patients with low-risk DTC after total thyroidectomy
- **Comparison**:
  - **Dose**: Low-dose (1.1 GBq / 30 mCi) vs high-dose (3.7 GBq / 100 mCi)
  - **Preparation**: Recombinant human TSH (rhTSH) vs thyroid hormone withdrawal (THW)
- **Primary endpoint**: Successful remnant ablation at 6-8 months (stimulated Tg <2 ng/mL and negative US)
- **Results**:

| Arm | Ablation Success Rate |
|---|---|
| Low-dose + rhTSH | 85.0% |
| Low-dose + THW | 87.1% |
| High-dose + rhTSH | 90.2% |
| High-dose + THW | 92.1% |
| Non-inferiority for low-dose | Confirmed (difference within margin) |
| Non-inferiority for rhTSH | Confirmed |

- **Conclusion**: Low-dose RAI with rhTSH is non-inferior to high-dose RAI with thyroid hormone withdrawal for remnant ablation in low-risk DTC

### ESTIMABL1 Trial (Schlumberger 2012)

- **Design**: Multicentre, randomised, non-inferiority, 2x2 factorial design (France)
- **Patients**: 752 patients with low-risk DTC
- **Comparison**: Identical to HiLo (low vs high dose; rhTSH vs THW)
- **Primary endpoint**: Ablation success at 8 months (stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL and negative neck US and negative WBS)
- **Results**:

| Arm | Ablation Success |
|---|---|
| Low-dose (1.1 GBq) | 92% |
| High-dose (3.7 GBq) | 94% |
| Non-inferiority for low-dose | Confirmed |

- **Long-term follow-up (5 years)**: No difference in recurrence between low and high dose (published in Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2018)

### Radioiodine in Papillary Microcarcinoma (Randolph Ch 20)

Multiple lines of evidence demonstrate RAI has no benefit in PTMC:
- **NTCTCSG study**: No reduction in recurrence with adjunctive RAI, even in multifocal PTMC or node-positive PTMC
- **Mayo Clinic**: In node-negative PTMC, recurrence was 0% with RAI and 0.6% without RAI (no difference)
- **Meta-analysis** (Hu et al. 2016): No benefit of adjunctive RAI for PTMC
- **SEER Database**: 99.9% 15-year disease-specific survival regardless of RAI use
- **Ito/Kuma Hospital approach**: Central node dissection without radioiodine achieves 2% recurrence in T1 patients (2,638 patients, mean 91-month follow-up) -- arguing that adequate surgical staging can substitute for RAI

### Implications for Clinical Practice

| Patient Risk | RAI Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Low risk (T1-T2 N0, no adverse features) | No RAI or low-dose (30 mCi) ablation |
| Low-intermediate risk (T1-T2 N1a, minor adverse features) | Low-dose ablation (30 mCi) |
| Intermediate risk (T3, N1a, aggressive histology) | Low or standard dose (30-100 mCi), case-by-case |
| High risk (T4, N1b, incomplete resection, distant mets) | High-dose therapeutic RAI (100-200 mCi) |

### Avoiding RAI in Low-Risk Patients

The 2015 ATA guidelines recommend that RAI can be avoided entirely in:
- **ATA low-risk DTC**: T1-T2 N0 M0, no vascular invasion, no aggressive histology, complete resection
- **Classic papillary, follicular variant, encapsulated follicular thyroid cancer with minor capsular invasion**
- **Expected excellent response**: Postoperative unstimulated Tg <1 ng/mL

Specific indications for RAI in PTMC are limited to: gross extrathyroidal invasion, extensive nodal disease (>5 nodes >2 mm), distant metastases, and possibly high-grade histology.

## ATA Risk Stratification (2015)

### Risk Categories for Structural Disease Recurrence (Randolph Ch 24, Box 24.1)

| Risk Level | Features | Estimated Recurrence |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Intrathyroidal DTC; <=5 micrometastatic N1 (<0.2 cm); R0; no vascular invasion; no aggressive histology; clinical N0 or <=5 LN micro; intrathyroidal encapsulated follicular variant PTC; intrathyroidal FTC with capsular invasion and no/minimal (<4 foci) vascular invasion; intrathyroidal PTMC (unifocal or multifocal), including BRAF V600E mutated | 1-5% |
| Intermediate | Microscopic ETE into perithyroidal soft tissues; RAI-avid metastatic foci in neck on first post-therapy WBS; aggressive histology (tall cell, hobnail, columnar cell); vascular invasion; clinical N1 or >5 pathologic N1 with all nodes <3 cm; multifocal PTMC with ETE and BRAF V600E mutated | 5-20% |
| High | Gross ETE (macroscopic invasion); incomplete resection (R1/R2); distant metastasis; postoperative Tg suggestive of distant mets; N1 with any LN >=3 cm; FTC with extensive vascular invasion (>4 foci) | 20-50% |

### Dynamic Risk Stratification — Response to Therapy (Randolph Ch 24)

The key insight from Randolph Ch 24 is that **initial staging systems are static and do not change over time**, yet clinical risk estimates clearly change depending on response to therapy and tumour biology. The ATA Response to Therapy system addresses this critical limitation.

| Response | Criteria | Clinical Outcomes | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Excellent | Negative imaging AND suppressed Tg <0.2 ng/mL or stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL | 1-4% recurrence; <1% disease-specific death | Reduce intensity/frequency of follow-up; loosen TSH suppression |
| Biochemical incomplete | Negative imaging AND suppressed Tg >=1 ng/mL or stimulated Tg >=10 ng/mL or rising anti-Tg Ab | >=30% spontaneously evolve to NED; 20% achieve NED after additional therapy; 20% develop structural disease; <1% death | Continued observation with TSH suppression if Tg stable/declining; rising Tg prompts additional investigation |
| Structural incomplete | Structural or functional evidence of disease with any Tg level | 50-85% persistent disease despite therapy; up to 11% death (locoregional) and 50% death (distant mets) | Additional treatments or observation depending on size, location, growth rate, RAI/FDG avidity |
| Indeterminate | Nonspecific imaging findings; faint thyroid bed uptake on RAI; nonstimulated Tg detectable but <1 ng/mL; stimulated Tg detectable but <10 ng/mL | 15-20% will have structural disease on follow-up; remainder stable or resolve; <1% death | Continued observation with serial imaging and Tg monitoring |

### Impact of Response to Therapy on Initial Risk Estimates (Randolph Ch 24)

The response to therapy powerfully modifies initial risk:

| Initial ATA Risk | Initial Risk of Persistent/Recurrent Disease | After Excellent Response | After Incomplete Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low | 3% | **2%** | 13% |
| Intermediate | 18% | **2%** | 41% |
| High | 66% | **14%** | 79% |

This has major clinical implications:
- **Intermediate-risk patients with excellent response** (the largest group, ~50% of patients) can be reclassified and followed as low-risk with dramatically reduced surveillance intensity
- **Low-risk patients with incomplete response** (uncommon) require intensified follow-up beyond what initial staging would suggest
- Age further stratifies response: younger patients (<55) have significantly fewer structural incomplete responses and better disease-specific survival than older patients, especially in the high-risk group

### AJCC 8th Edition Changes (Randolph Ch 24)

Key changes from 7th to 8th edition AJCC/TNM:
1. **Age cutoff raised** from 45 to 55 years
2. **Microscopic ETE removed** from T3 definition -- only gross ETE remains significant
3. **Nodal positivity deemphasised** -- no longer automatically upgrades to Stage III/IV
4. Net effect: significant downstaging that more accurately reflects low risk of death

However, AJCC staging remains poor at predicting risk of **recurrence** (rates of persistent disease were similar between Stages II, III, and IV), which is why the ATA Risk Stratification system and dynamic response assessment are essential complements.

## TSH Suppression — Risk-Adapted Targets

| ATA Risk Category | Initial TSH Target | After Excellent Response |
|---|---|---|
| Low risk | 0.5-2.0 mU/L | 0.5-2.0 mU/L |
| Intermediate risk | 0.1-0.5 mU/L | 0.5-2.0 mU/L |
| High risk (with disease) | <0.1 mU/L | 0.1-0.5 mU/L |
| Structural persistent disease | <0.1 mU/L | Maintain <0.1 mU/L |

TSH suppressive therapy is associated with atrial fibrillation and reduced bone density, supporting the move toward less aggressive suppression in low-risk patients with excellent response to therapy.

## Ongoing Controversies

### Unanswered Questions

1. **Optimal threshold for lobectomy**: Is 4 cm the right cutoff, or can lobectomy be extended to larger tumours?
2. **Active surveillance in Western populations**: Long-term data still accumulating outside Japan
3. **Molecular markers in surgical decision-making**: Should preoperative BRAF/TERT status influence surgical extent?
4. **Omission of RAI in intermediate-risk patients**: IoN trial and other ongoing studies
5. **Completion thyroidectomy after lobectomy**: How aggressive should the threshold be for re-operation?
6. **Age and sex as factors in de-escalation**: Younger patients may benefit from different approaches
7. **Defining tumour growth criteria that mandate surgical intervention during AS** (Randolph Ch 21)
8. **Optimal TSH level during active surveillance observation** (Randolph Ch 21)
9. **Role of molecular testing in predicting disease progression during AS** (Randolph Ch 21)
10. **Psychological effects of observational management** on patients (Randolph Ch 21)
11. **Role of alternative interventions** (alcohol ablation, laser therapy, radiofrequency ablation) for PTMC (Randolph Ch 21)

### The IoN Trial (Currently Enrolling)

- Randomised trial of ablation versus no ablation in low-risk DTC
- Aims to definitively determine if RAI can be omitted entirely
- Primary endpoint: recurrence-free survival

## Key Points for Exam

1. **ATA 2015 guidelines** represent a paradigm shift toward de-escalation in low-risk DTC
2. **Lobectomy is sufficient** for unifocal T1-T2 N0 PTC without adverse features (Adam 2014)
3. **Active surveillance** is an acceptable option for low-risk PTMC (Ito/Kuma Hospital protocol, endorsed by ATA 2015 and Japanese guidelines since 2011)
4. AS criteria: PTMC, intrathyroidal, N0, not near trachea/RLN (tumours <7 mm never invaded RLN/trachea regardless of location), patient willing to comply
5. Three-domain clinical framework (tumour/US characteristics, patient characteristics, medical team characteristics) classifies patients as ideal, appropriate, or inappropriate for AS
6. 8% tumour enlargement and 3.8% new LN at 10 years during AS — low progression rate; 0% distant metastases; 0% disease-specific mortality
7. **Lifetime progression risk is age-dependent**: 48.6% in 20s, 10.3% in 50s, 3.5% in 70s (Miyauchi et al.)
8. AS is **4.1 times less costly** than immediate surgery with 10-year management (Kuma Hospital data)
9. **MSKCC expanded AS criteria** to tumours up to 1.5 cm with excellent outcomes in Western populations
10. **HiLo trial**: Low-dose RAI (30 mCi) + rhTSH is non-inferior to high-dose RAI (100 mCi) + THW
11. **ESTIMABL1**: Independently confirmed HiLo findings in French population
12. RAI can be **avoided entirely** in ATA low-risk patients with excellent response to surgery; RAI has no proven benefit in PTMC (meta-analysis, SEER, NTCTCSG)
13. **Dynamic risk stratification** using response to therapy powerfully modifies initial risk estimates — intermediate-risk patients with excellent response have only 2% risk of recurrence
14. TSH suppression targets are now risk-adapted (not <0.1 for all patients); TSH suppressive therapy risks include atrial fibrillation and osteoporosis
15. **BRAF V600E alone is not an indication** for aggressive treatment in PTMC and is not predictive of progression during AS
