(2006) conducted an international randomised controlled study demonstrating that rhTSH-aided remnant ablation achieved comparable ablation success to THW preparation, establishing the foundation for approval of rhTSH for ablation therapy. (2015) conducted a meta-analysis of 16 studies (2811 patients) comparing rhTSH versus THW for RAI ablation and found equivalent ablation success with significantly better quality of life, fewer side effects, and lower radiation exposure with rhTSH.

## Principles of Radioiodine Therapy

### Biological Basis

Radioiodine (I-131) therapy exploits the unique ability of thyroid follicular cells to concentrate iodine through the sodium/iodide symporter (NIS):

1. **Sodium/Iodide Symporter (NIS)** -- an intrinsic transmembrane glycoprotein located on the basolateral membrane of thyroid follicular cells
2. NIS actively transports iodide against its electrochemical gradient with a sodium:iodide stoichiometry of 2:1
3. NIS expression is upregulated by TSH -- hence the need for TSH elevation before RAI therapy
4. Differentiated thyroid cancer cells retain NIS expression, though at reduced levels compared to normal thyroid tissue -- Randolph (Ch 48) emphasises that tumours of follicular cell origin are less likely to concentrate RAI due to lower membrane expression of the transporter
5. Poorly differentiated and anaplastic thyroid cancers typically lose NIS expression and are RAI-refractory
6. **Histology-dependent RAI avidity** -- different histological subtypes of DTC vary in their ability to express the membrane transporter, with some tumours (e.g., tall cell variant, insular component) unable to concentrate enough RAI for an effective treatment response (Randolph Ch 48)

### Physics of I-131

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Half-life | 8.02 days |
| Principal beta emission | 606 keV (mean), 0.4 mm tissue penetration |
| Principal gamma emission | 364 keV (allows imaging) |
| Mode of cell kill | Beta radiation -- DNA double-strand breaks |
| Effective tissue penetration | 0.5--2.0 mm (beta particles); Randolph notes average penetration of 2--5 mm in tumoral tissues |
| Typical absorbed dose to remnant | 300 Gy (at ablation doses) |
| Size limitation | RAI therapy is not expected to destroy persistent disease exceeding 1 cm in the longest diameter due to limited tissue penetrance (Randolph Ch 48) |

**I-123 vs I-131** (Randolph Ch 48): I-123 emits predominantly gamma rays, which provide good image resolution but low tissue absorption/concentration, making I-123 suitable primarily for diagnostic imaging. I-131 emits beta rays that cause extensive DNA damage -- tumour cells lack efficient double-strand DNA repair mechanisms and are more susceptible to ionising radiation effects than normal tissues, leading to apoptosis via p53-pathway activation.

### Goals of RAI After Thyroidectomy

RAI therapy serves three distinct purposes, with different dose ranges for each (Randolph Ch 48, Table 48.1):

1. **Remnant ablation** -- Destruction of residual normal thyroid tissue remaining after total thyroidectomy to facilitate Tg surveillance and WBS interpretation. The patient is implicitly considered already cured; RAI is given to improve staging and facilitate follow-up
2. **Adjuvant therapy** -- Treatment of suspected but unproven microscopic residual disease to reduce recurrence risk. Many of these patients are already cured by surgery, but others may harbour subclinical deposits; the goal is to decrease recurrence, improve disease-specific survival, and achieve cure
3. **Treatment of known disease** -- Therapy for known residual, recurrent, or metastatic DTC. All patients have persistent disease detectable by imaging or elevated Tg; the goal is to achieve cure or stable disease and improve progression-free and disease-specific survival

## Indications for RAI Based on ATA Risk Stratification

### ATA Risk-Adapted Approach (Randolph Ch 48)

Randolph (Ch 48) describes the evolution of RAI use: before 2009, almost all DTC patients received RAI therapy except those with isolated papillary microcarcinoma. The 2009 ATA guidelines introduced the risk-adaptive approach, and the 2015 revision further expanded the low-risk category, thus increasing the proportion of patients for whom RAI is not recommended. The decision to use RAI is based on: (1) risk for recurrence and mortality, (2) postoperative disease status, (3) likelihood of clinical benefit, (4) likelihood of adverse effects, (5) need for staging, (6) need for sensitive follow-up, (7) multidisciplinary team philosophy, and (8) patient wishes and values.

### ATA Low-Risk Patients

- **RAI is NOT routinely recommended**
- Criteria: intrathyroidal PTC, <4 cm, no aggressive histology, no vascular invasion, cN0 or <=5 microscopic N1 (<0.2 cm each), no distant metastases
- Also includes encapsulated intrathyroidal follicular variant PTC and intrathyroidal FTC without significant vascular invasion -- recurrence risk <2% (Randolph Ch 48)
- May consider RAI remnant ablation to facilitate follow-up (Tg monitoring)
- If given, **low-dose (30 mCi / 1.1 GBq)** is sufficient (ESTIMABL1, HiLo)
- Multifocal PTMC without other adverse features has an estimated recurrence risk of ~5% -- RAI decision is more nuanced in this subgroup (Randolph Ch 48)

### ATA Intermediate-Risk Patients

- **RAI is generally recommended** -- ATA recommendation 51D states RAI adjuvant therapy "should be considered," though based on low-quality evidence (Randolph Ch 48)
- Criteria: aggressive histology, minor ETE, vascular invasion, >5 involved nodes or any node >3 cm, N1 with all nodes <3 cm
- Dose: **30--150 mCi (1.1--5.5 GBq)** depending on specific risk features
- rhTSH preparation is acceptable for remnant ablation
- The decision is most difficult in intermediate-risk patients: co-existence of multiple individually borderline features in one patient may justify RAI administration (Randolph Ch 48)

### ATA High-Risk Patients

- **RAI is recommended** -- strong recommendation with moderate-quality evidence for decreased recurrence and improved survival (Randolph Ch 48)
- Criteria: gross ETE (T4), incomplete tumour resection, distant metastases, high postoperative Tg suggestive of distant metastases
- Dose: **100--200 mCi (3.7--7.4 GBq)**
- THW preparation may be preferred for treatment of known structural disease (higher TSH levels achieved)
- Randolph (Ch 47) specifically recommends THW over rhTSH for patients with structurally persistent/metastatic disease

### Postoperative Disease Assessment (Randolph Ch 48)

- The ATA 2015 guidelines (Recommendation 50) state that postoperative disease status "should be considered in deciding whether additional treatment may be needed"
- Postoperative Tg should be measured at least 4 weeks after surgery to allow levels to reach nadir
- No consensus on the optimal Tg cutoff or the state in which it should be measured (stimulated vs on thyroid hormone therapy)
- Postoperative neck ultrasound and diagnostic WBS provide additional staging information
- Each centre should develop its own acceptable protocol for postoperative disease assessment (Randolph Ch 48)

## RAI Dose Selection

### Dosing Strategies: Empiric vs Dosimetric (Randolph)

Two fundamental approaches to RAI dose selection:

**Empiric (fixed-dose) approach:**
- Administered activity is chosen based on the clinical indication (ablation, adjuvant, or treatment) and clinicopathological features
- Advantages: simplicity, reproducibility, widely available
- Randolph (Ch 48) provides specific activity recommendations based on indication and postoperative Tg level (see Table below)

**Dosimetric approach:**
- Calculates the maximum safe dose based on blood/marrow dosimetry
- Blood/marrow dose limit: <=200 cGy (2 Gy) to blood
- Lung retention limit: <=80 mCi (3 GBq) at 48 hours (to prevent pulmonary fibrosis)
- Administered activities can exceed 200 mCi with dosimetric guidance
- Randolph (Ch 47): retrospective data showed no difference in efficacy between a standardised dose (3.7 GBq / 100 mCi) and a personalised dose based on dosimetry for distant metastases
- Randolph (Ch 48) notes that dosimetry-based approaches achieved higher lifetime cumulative RAI activities with comparable clinical responses but a higher risk for serious adverse effects (salivary gland cancer, lung cancer, myelodysplastic syndromes)

### Randolph Activity Recommendations by Indication (Ch 48, Table 48.2)

| Clinical Indication | RAI Activity | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Primary tumour >4 cm | 30--75 mCi (normal postop Tg); 100--150 mCi (elevated postop Tg) | May facilitate staging/follow-up |
| Gross extrathyroidal extension | 100--150 mCi | Likely decreases recurrence and mortality |
| Distant metastases | 150--200 mCi | Likely decreases recurrence and mortality |
| Large-volume cervical LN metastases | 75--100 mCi (normal postop Tg); 100--150 mCi (elevated postop Tg) | Conflicting data on recurrence reduction |
| Vascular invasion (PTC/FTC/HCC) | 30--75 mCi (normal postop Tg); 100--150 mCi (elevated postop Tg) | Risk of distant metastases |
| Poorly differentiated thyroid cancer | 30--75 mCi (normal postop Tg); 100--150 mCi (elevated postop Tg) | Inadequate data on recurrence effect |
| Elevated postop Tg, no structural disease | 75--100 mCi | May facilitate staging/follow-up |

**Note on postop Tg thresholds** (Randolph Ch 48): Normal postoperative Tg is defined as nonstimulated Tg <5 ng/mL or stimulated Tg <10 ng/mL with negative anti-Tg antibodies.

### Remnant Ablation (30 mCi / 1.1 GBq)

- Goal: destroy postsurgical thyroid remnant
- Indicated in low-risk and selected intermediate-risk patients
- Supported by ESTIMABL1 and HiLo: non-inferior to 100 mCi for ablation success (ablation rate ~90%)
- Randolph (Ch 48): 30 mCi is sufficient to ablate a thyroid remnant and eliminate low-volume nodal metastasis
- Advantages: shorter isolation, fewer side effects, lower lifetime cumulative dose
- Risk for adverse effects becomes significant when activities exceed 70 mCi (Randolph Ch 48)

### Adjuvant RAI (100--150 mCi / 3.7--5.5 GBq)

- Goal: treat possible microscopic residual disease
- Indicated in intermediate-risk and selected high-risk patients
- No true consensus on appropriate adjuvant therapy activity, which can range between 30--150 mCi (Randolph Ch 48)
- Evidence for improved recurrence-free survival in intermediate-risk patients
- Standard dose historically; now questioned in low-risk patients post-ESTIMABL1/HiLo
- Randolph (Ch 48): In younger patients (<=45 years) with N1b disease, clinical outcomes did not differ based on activity (100 mCi vs 150 mCi vs 200 mCi). In older patients, structurally incomplete response was lower with 150--250 mCi compared to 100 mCi (p = 0.03)

### Therapeutic RAI for Metastatic Disease (150--200 mCi / 5.5--7.4 GBq)

- Goal: treat known structural disease (cervical recurrence, lung metastases, bone metastases)
- Can be administered empirically (fixed dose) or based on dosimetry
- Randolph (Ch 48): in recent years, the preference has been to avoid activities exceeding 200 mCi outside of clinical trials, based on evidence of non-inferiority of lower empiric activities compared to dosimetry-based higher activities
- Randolph (Ch 47): treatment can be repeated every 6 months for 2 years, then yearly, up to a cumulative dose of 22.2 GBq (600 mCi); higher cumulative doses may be warranted in very select cases
- **Dosimetric approach** -- calculates the maximum safe dose based on:
  - Blood/marrow dose limit: <=200 cGy (2 Gy) to blood
  - Lung retention limit: <=80 mCi (3 GBq) at 48 hours (to prevent pulmonary fibrosis)
  - Administered activities can exceed 200 mCi with dosimetric guidance

### Treatment of Lung Metastases (Randolph Ch 47)

- RAI therapy leads to remission (normal imaging, absence of residual I-131 uptake) in 45% of patients with RAI-avid lung tumours
- Almost all complete responses are attained with cumulative activities <=22 GBq (600 mCi), with few recurrences (7%) and few tumour-related deaths (3%) after remission
- 10-year survival: 92% in metastatic patients with I-131 uptake who achieved complete remission vs 29% in those with persistent imaging abnormalities vs 10% in those without any initial I-131 uptake
- Favourable response factors: age <40 years, small metastases (<1 cm), high RAI uptake (remission >90%)
- Poor response: older patients with metastases >1 cm (remission <15%)

### RAI-Refractory Disease (Randolph Ch 47)

Defined as:
1. I-131 uptake is absent in all target lesions (initially or during treatment)
2. Uptake is present in some lesions but not in others
3. Progression within 12 months after I-131 treatment
4. Some centres also consider persistent disease after cumulative activity of 600 mCi to be RAI-refractory

## Preparation for RAI Therapy

### Thyroid Hormone Withdrawal (THW)

- **Standard protocol:** Discontinue levothyroxine (T4) for 3--4 weeks, or switch to liothyronine (T3) for 2 weeks then withdraw T3 for 2 weeks
- **Target TSH:** >30 mIU/L (ideally >50 mIU/L)
- Randolph (Ch 47): THW for 4 weeks, with or without temporary substitution with liothyronine (LT3), which should also be withdrawn for at least 2 weeks before I-131 administration
- Produces hypothyroid symptoms: fatigue, weight gain, constipation, cold intolerance, depression, cognitive impairment
- Higher TSH levels may provide superior stimulation of NIS expression in tumour cells
- **Preferred for treatment of known structural/metastatic disease** -- Randolph (Ch 47, Box 47.3) explicitly recommends THW over rhTSH injections in patients with metastatic disease

### Recombinant Human TSH (rhTSH / Thyrogen)

- **Mechanism:** Thyrotropin alfa is a recombinant human TSH glycoprotein produced in Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells. It binds to TSH receptors on thyroid follicular cells and differentiated thyroid cancer cells, stimulating NIS expression, iodine uptake, and thyroglobulin synthesis
- **Protocol:** 0.9 mg IM injection on two consecutive days (Day 1 and Day 2); RAI administered on Day 3; diagnostic scan on Day 5
- Randolph (Ch 47): 1 injection per day on the 2 days preceding I-131 administration
- **TSH levels achieved:** Peak TSH typically 100--200 mIU/L at 24 hours after second injection
- Patient remains on levothyroxine throughout -- **avoids hypothyroidism**
- **Duration of TSH stimulation is shorter with rhTSH** -- less risk of promoting cancer growth (Randolph Ch 48)

### Comparison: THW vs rhTSH

| Parameter | THW | rhTSH |
|---|---|---|
| TSH level achieved | 50--100+ mIU/L | 100--200 mIU/L |
| Duration of TSH elevation | Weeks | Days |
| Hypothyroid symptoms | Yes (significant) | No |
| Time off work | 2--4 weeks | Minimal |
| Quality of life | Significantly impaired | Maintained |
| Radiation exposure to body | Higher (slower iodine clearance) | Lower (faster renal clearance) |
| Ablation success rate | ~90% | ~90% (non-inferior) |
| Stimulated Tg sensitivity | Comparable | Comparable |
| Risk of cancer growth promotion | Higher (prolonged TSH elevation) | Lower (brief TSH exposure) |

Randolph (Ch 48) states: For ablation, rhTSH was found equivalent to THW with non-inferiority in ablation efficacy, no difference in long-term outcomes, and better short-term quality of life. For adjuvant therapy, no RCTs have compared clinical response and outcomes by preparation method -- rhTSH is used and considered an adequate alternative to THW, but based on low-quality evidence. For treatment of metastatic disease, no data exists comparing clinical outcomes between rhTSH and THW -- rhTSH is not FDA-approved for this indication but can be used in special situations (severe comorbidities precluding prolonged hypothyroidism).

### Approved Indications for rhTSH (Thyrogen)

1. **Diagnostic whole body scanning** with I-131 in follow-up of DTC patients
2. **Stimulated thyroglobulin testing** in DTC patients on thyroid hormone suppression
3. **Remnant ablation** with RAI in low-risk and intermediate-risk DTC patients (approved following ESTIMABL1/HiLo data)
4. **NOT currently approved** for treatment of known residual or metastatic disease -- though some centres use it off-label when THW is medically contraindicated

### Low-Iodine Diet (LID)

- **Duration:** 1--2 weeks before RAI administration
- **Rationale:** Reduces the total body iodine pool, increasing the percentage uptake of I-131 by thyroid tissue/cancer cells
- **Target urinary iodine:** <100 mcg/L (ideally <50 mcg/L)
- **Iodine contamination avoidance** -- Randolph (Ch 48) recommends avoidance of high iodine load a month before RAI therapy in addition to the low-iodine diet
- **Foods to avoid:** iodised salt, dairy products, seafood, eggs, soy products, processed foods with iodine-containing additives, iodine-based contrast agents (requires 4--8 week washout)
- **Foods allowed:** fresh meat, fresh fruits and vegetables, rice, pasta, non-iodised salt

## Whole Body Scan (WBS)

### Diagnostic WBS

- Performed with low-dose I-131 (1--5 mCi) or I-123 (1--2 mCi)
- **Timing:** 48--72 hours after tracer administration
- Identifies functioning thyroid tissue and metastases
- **Stunning effect:** Pre-therapy diagnostic I-131 may reduce subsequent therapeutic uptake by damaging the NIS-expressing cells or downregulating iodine trapping -- use I-123 or very low-dose I-131 to minimise this effect. The stunning phenomenon is a key reason some centres avoid pre-therapy diagnostic WBS with I-131 altogether, proceeding directly to therapeutic administration

### Post-therapy WBS

- Performed 3--10 days after therapeutic RAI administration
- Randolph (Ch 47) specifies SPECT-CT coupled with whole-body scintiscan, performed 3--5 days after I-131 administration
- **More sensitive than pre-therapy scan** -- detects additional foci in 10--15% of patients
- Provides information on distribution of disease and adequacy of ablation
- Identifies previously unsuspected metastases in approximately 10--25% of patients
- Randolph (Ch 47, Box 47.3): a total body scan performed several days after radioiodine administration is **mandatory** and may guide further treatment in case of residual disease

## Adverse Effects of RAI Therapy

### Acute Effects (Days to Weeks)

| Effect | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Sialadenitis** | 10--30% | Sialogogues (sour candy/lemon juice), hydration, gentle massage; amifostine for prevention |
| **Xerostomia** | 10--20% (transient); 5--10% (permanent) | Pilocarpine, artificial saliva, adequate hydration |
| **Nausea/vomiting** | 10--30% | Antiemetics (ondansetron), adequate hydration |
| **Neck pain/tenderness** | 5--20% | NSAIDs, corticosteroids if severe radiation thyroiditis |
| **Taste alteration (dysgeusia)** | 10--30% | Usually transient (weeks to months) |
| **Parotid swelling** | 5--15% | Ice packs, sialogogues, hydration |
| **Gastric radiation exposure** | Variable | Randolph (Ch 47): may cause transient nausea/vomiting |

### Sialadenitis -- Detailed (Randolph Ch 47)

RAI is concentrated in salivary glands and may cause dose-related acute and late effects on salivary function:
- **Acute sialadenitis** -- swelling, pain, distorted or absent taste; parotid glands most often affected (uni- or bilaterally); symptoms resolve spontaneously in days to weeks
- **Chronic sialadenitis** -- affects parotid glands bilaterally; manifests with pain, swelling, and xerostomia due to salivary duct stenosis; generally resolves within 1 year with atrophy of salivary gland parenchyma. Chronic sialadenitis is **not related** to the occurrence of acute sialadenitis (Randolph Ch 47)
- Xerostomia can lead to increased risk of **dental caries**

### Subacute Effects (Weeks to Months)

- **Bone marrow suppression** -- transient leucopenia and thrombocytopenia; rarely clinically significant at standard doses. Randolph (Ch 47): mild pancytopenia may occur after repeated I-131 therapy, especially in patients with bone metastases also treated with external radiotherapy
- **Lacrimal gland dysfunction and nasolacrimal duct stenosis** -- Randolph (Ch 47): ocular dryness from lacrimal gland dysfunction and epiphora from lacrimal duct stenosis; notably, this has **not been shown to be dose-related**; may require dacryocystorhinostomy (DCR)
- **Transient amenorrhoea/oligospermia** -- gonadal effects are usually temporary
- **Tumour swelling** -- Randolph (Ch 47): tumours in the brain, spinal cord, and paratracheal region may swell in response to TSH stimulation or after I-131 therapy, causing compressive symptoms; should be prevented by corticosteroid therapy at high doses for a few days. The use of rhTSH does not eliminate and may even increase the possibility of rapid swelling of metastatic lesions
- **Colon and bladder irradiation** -- Randolph (Ch 47): colon irradiation should be mitigated by laxative treatment; bladder and gonadal irradiation reduced by abundant hydration and frequent urination

### Late Effects (Months to Years)

| Effect | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Secondary malignancy** | Small absolute increase | Leukaemia risk increases with cumulative dose >600 mCi; solid tumour risk (breast, bladder, salivary gland, colorectal) |
| **Permanent xerostomia** | 5--10% at ablation doses | Higher with repeated treatments |
| **Pulmonary fibrosis** | Rare | Risk with diffuse lung metastases receiving high-dose RAI (>80 mCi retention at 48 h) |
| **Gonadal effects** | Variable | Males: transient oligospermia (recovery 12--18 months); permanent azoospermia with cumulative dose >500 mCi. Females: temporary amenorrhoea; recommend delaying pregnancy for 6--12 months |
| **Chronic sialadenitis** | 5--15% | More common with repeated treatments |

### Gonadal Effects -- Detailed (Randolph Ch 47)

- **Females:** Temporary infertility and temporary increase in miscarriages reported after RAI. Pregnancy should be avoided for 6--12 months after I-131 administration. Premature menopause (average 1 year earlier) may occur
- **Males:** I-131 may result in decreased sperm count. Procreation should be avoided for 3--6 months after I-131 administration. Cumulative activities >14.8 GBq (>400 mCi) are correlated with risk of permanent male infertility -- sperm banking should be proposed for patients likely to receive repeated doses

### Secondary Malignancy Risk (Randolph Ch 47)

- Relative risk of second primary malignancies (leukaemia, breast, colon, kidney, salivary, bone/soft tissue tumours) reported as 1.19 in a meta-analysis of two multicentre studies
- Risk is dose-related and higher with cumulative doses of 500--600 mCi
- No direct evidence that a single administration of 30--100 mCi carries a significant risk for developing a second primary cancer

### Lifetime Cumulative Dose Considerations

- **No absolute upper limit**, but risks increase with cumulative dose
- Leukaemia risk increases significantly above 600 mCi (22 GBq) cumulative activity
- Randolph (Ch 47): almost all complete responses for lung metastases attained with cumulative activities <=22 GBq -- this represents a practical upper boundary for most patients
- Blood dosimetry should be performed when cumulative doses exceed 600 mCi or when high-dose therapy is planned for patients with extensive lung metastases
- Each treatment decision should weigh the incremental benefit against cumulative risk

## Radiation Safety

### Patient Isolation

- Inpatient isolation required for activities >30 mCi in most jurisdictions (varies by country)
- **Discharge criteria:** Measured radiation exposure rate <5 mR/h at 1 metre, OR calculated dose to others <500 mrem (5 mSv)
- Typical isolation: 1--3 days depending on dose and renal function

### Precautions After Discharge

- Maintain distance from pregnant women and children for 1--2 weeks
- Avoid prolonged close contact (<1 metre) for duration based on administered activity
- Use separate bathroom facilities where possible
- Flush toilet twice after use
- Use separate eating utensils for 48--72 hours
- Sleep in separate bed for 1--2 weeks

### Special Considerations

- **Pregnancy:** Absolute contraindication to RAI; pregnancy test required within 72 hours before administration. Randolph (Ch 47): RAI administration is contraindicated in cases of pregnancy or lactation and should be postponed
- **Breastfeeding:** Must be discontinued at least 6 weeks before RAI; cannot be resumed after I-131 therapy for the current child
- **Fertility preservation:** Sperm banking should be offered to males before cumulative dose exceeds 400 mCi (Randolph Ch 47: cumulative activities >14.8 GBq / 400 mCi correlated with permanent male infertility); egg preservation should be discussed with females of reproductive age

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **ESTIMABL1 and HiLo** are the two landmark 2012 NEJM trials: both showed 30 mCi is non-inferior to 100 mCi for remnant ablation in low-risk DTC
2. **rhTSH** achieves higher peak TSH (~100--200 mIU/L) than THW (~50--100 mIU/L) but for a shorter duration
3. The **NIS** (sodium/iodide symporter) is the molecular basis of RAI therapy -- loss of NIS expression defines RAI-refractory disease
4. **Low-iodine diet** for 1--2 weeks enhances RAI uptake by depleting the total body iodine pool
5. **Post-therapy WBS is more sensitive** than diagnostic WBS -- identifies additional disease in 10--25% of patients; Randolph (Ch 47) states it is **mandatory**
6. **Sialadenitis** is the most common acute side effect -- sialogogues (sour candy) are the key preventive measure; chronic sialadenitis is NOT related to prior acute sialadenitis (Randolph Ch 47)
7. **Leukaemia risk** increases significantly above 600 mCi cumulative activity -- this is the most important long-term complication to remember; overall relative risk of second primary malignancy is 1.19 (Randolph Ch 47)
8. **Dosimetric approach** is mandatory for diffuse lung metastases to prevent pulmonary fibrosis (48-hour lung retention must be <80 mCi); Randolph notes dosimetry-based approaches do not show better efficacy than standard fixed dosing for distant metastases
9. **Pregnancy is an absolute contraindication** -- always obtain a pregnancy test before RAI administration
10. **RAI is not expected to destroy disease >1 cm** due to limited tissue penetrance of beta particles (Randolph Ch 48) -- bulky nodal disease should be surgically resected before RAI
11. **Stunning phenomenon** -- pre-therapy diagnostic I-131 may reduce subsequent therapeutic uptake; use I-123 or very low-dose I-131 to avoid this
12. **Lacrimal duct stenosis** is NOT dose-related (Randolph Ch 47) -- unlike sialadenitis, which is dose-related
13. **Age matters for RAI activity selection** -- In older patients with N1b disease, higher RAI activities (150--250 mCi) are associated with lower structurally incomplete response rates compared to 100 mCi; this difference was not seen in younger patients (Randolph Ch 48)
