## Overview of Thyroidectomy Complications

### Classification by Timing

| Timing | Complications |
|---|---|
| Intraoperative | Haemorrhage, RLN injury, EBSLN injury, parathyroid devascularisation/removal, tracheal injury, oesophageal injury |
| Early (0-48h) | Neck haematoma, airway compromise, symptomatic hypocalcemia, wound infection, seroma |
| Late (>48h) | Permanent hypoparathyroidism, permanent RLN palsy, hypothyroidism, keloid/scar, chyle leak (thoracic duct injury), Horner syndrome |

### Core Surgical Principles for Complication Prevention

Randolph (Ch 44) emphasises that endocrine neck surgery mandates adherence to core principles: absolute hemostasis, distinct identification and preservation of relevant surrounding tissue, clear illumination and visualization of the operative field, and staying in the correct dissection plane. The importance of teamwork in preventing complications cannot be overemphasised. The modern endocrine surgeon should utilise ancillary technology (intraoperative nerve monitoring, immunofluorescence for parathyroid viability assessment) to provide actionable information at the point of care.

### Incidence of Major Complications

| Complication | Transient Rate | Permanent Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Hypocalcemia | 19-38% (Randolph Ch 31) | 0-3% (meta-analysis); up to 8% in community setting (ACS survey) |
| RLN palsy (unilateral) | 3-8% | 0.3-3% |
| RLN palsy (bilateral) | <1% | <0.5% |
| Post-operative haematoma | 0.3-4.4% (Randolph Ch 44) | — |
| Wound infection | 0.5-3% (Randolph Ch 44) | — |
| EBSLN injury | 0-25% (often unrecognised) | — |
| Horner syndrome | ~0.2% (Randolph Ch 44) | — |
| Thoracic duct injury | 0.6-1.4% (central dissection); 4.4-8.3% (lateral dissection) | — |
| Seroma | Up to 7% (Randolph Ch 44) | — |

### Quality Metrics and Mortality

Randolph (Ch 44) notes the in-hospital (30-day) mortality of thyroidectomy is approximately 0.6%. Reexploration rates for haemorrhage in large single-centre series (>1000 patients) range from 0.3% to 1.2%, with mortality related to postoperative haemorrhage at 0.01% overall and 0.6% among those requiring reoperation for bleeding. Permanent hypoparathyroidism is associated with a twofold increased risk of death (Scandinavian Endocrine Surgery Registry data). The risk of hypoparathyroidism is vitally linked to surgeon experience — one experienced surgeon noted hypoparathyroidism in only 3.2% of patients overall but 25% in his first 25 total thyroidectomy cases (Randolph Ch 31).

## Post-Thyroidectomy Hypocalcemia

### Pathophysiology

The parathyroid glands are the primary regulators of calcium homeostasis via PTH secretion. Hypocalcemia after thyroidectomy results from:

1. **Inadvertent parathyroidectomy**: Removal of one or more glands within the thyroid specimen (incidence: 5-20% of thyroidectomies when specimens are carefully examined). Randolph (Ch 44) stresses that after thyroidectomy, a thorough examination of the surgical specimen should be undertaken to identify any parathyroid glands inadvertently removed
2. **Parathyroid devascularisation**: Disruption of the end-arterial blood supply (branches from inferior thyroid artery for inferior glands, and superior thyroid artery/ITA for superior glands). Randolph (Ch 44) notes that collateral blood supply also arises from the thyroid ima and other small neighbouring vessels
3. **Stunning**: Transient parathyroid suppression from surgical manipulation, even with intact blood supply
4. **Hungry bone syndrome**: Rapid calcium uptake into bone after correction of thyrotoxicosis (particularly in Graves disease). Randolph (Ch 44) specifically highlights pediatric hyperparathyroidism patients who uniformly develop hungry bone syndrome due to high bone turnover

### Risk Factors for Hypocalcemia

| Risk Factor | Evidence Level |
|---|---|
| Female sex | Meta-analysis (Edafe 2014); Thomusch (Randolph Ch 31) |
| Graves disease / thyrotoxicosis | Prospective multicentre (Thomusch 2000); Randolph Ch 44 — increased vascularity and firm texture create challenges in preserving parathyroid blood supply; inherent hypermetabolic state exacerbates postoperative hypoparathyroidism |
| Hashimoto thyroiditis | Randolph Ch 44 — "woody" texture of thyroid gland compromises parathyroid dissection |
| Central neck dissection (level VI/VII) | Multiple RCTs; Randolph Ch 44 — shared blood supply with parathyroids |
| Bilateral thyroid surgery (vs lobectomy) | Strong |
| Inadvertent parathyroidectomy (any gland) | Strong |
| <3 parathyroid glands preserved in situ | Lorente-Poch 2015 |
| Parathyroid autotransplantation (as marker of gland compromise) | Moderate; Randolph Ch 31 notes autotransplantation associated with high temporary hypoparathyroidism rates but low permanent rates |
| Surgeon inexperience / low volume | Bergenfelz 2008; Randolph Ch 31 — "vitally linked to the experience of the surgeon" |
| Substernal goiter | Moderate; Randolph Ch 31 |
| Reoperation / redo thyroidectomy | Strong; Randolph Ch 44 — reoperative cases at increased risk |
| Autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto) | Moderate; Randolph Ch 31 |
| Prior gastric bypass | Randolph Ch 31 — listed as risk factor for permanent hypoparathyroidism |
| Simultaneous thyroidectomy and parathyroidectomy | Randolph Ch 31 |
| Familial cases (MEN 1) and secondary/tertiary hyperparathyroidism | Randolph Ch 44 — all require multigland excision, increasing risk |

### Preoperative Prevention Strategy (Randolph Ch 44)

For patients identified as high risk preoperatively (Graves disease, MEN 1, secondary/tertiary hyperparathyroidism, reoperative cases, pediatric patients), Randolph recommends commencing oral calcium supplementation with calcitriol (Rocaltrol) **5 to 7 days before surgery**. This proactive approach minimises the occurrence and clinical severity of postoperative hypocalcemia.

### Prediction of Post-thyroidectomy Hypocalcemia

#### PTH Measurement Protocol

| Timing of PTH | Threshold | Clinical Utility |
|---|---|---|
| Intraoperative (pre-closure) | <10 pg/mL | High sensitivity for predicting hypocalcemia |
| 1-hour postoperative | <15 pg/mL | Rapid risk stratification |
| 4-6 hour postoperative | <10-15 pg/mL | Most widely validated time point |
| PTH decline >75% from baseline | — | High sensitivity for permanent hypoparathyroidism |

Randolph (Ch 44) specifies that rapid intraoperative PTH levels less than 15 pg/mL correlate with a higher incidence of postoperative hypoparathyroidism and can assist with intraoperative decision making.

**Key thresholds**:
- PTH >15 pg/mL at 4-6 hours: low risk of symptomatic hypocalcemia — consider same-day discharge
- PTH 5-15 pg/mL: moderate risk — start oral calcium + calcitriol, close monitoring
- PTH <5 pg/mL or undetectable: high risk — aggressive supplementation, possible IV calcium, extended observation

#### ICG Fluorescence for Parathyroid Viability (Randolph Ch 31, Ch 44)

Indocyanine green (ICG) fluorescence angiography is a relatively new technique that can assess parathyroid gland viability intraoperatively. A randomised controlled trial demonstrated that ICG imaging can reliably predict postoperative gland function and obviate the need for laboratory testing and oral supplementation (Vidal Fortuny et al., 2018). This technology is especially helpful in determining parathyroid remnant viability during subtotal parathyroidectomy for multigland disease (MEN 1, secondary/tertiary hyperparathyroidism).

#### Parathyroid Autofluorescence (Randolph Ch 31)

Parathyroid tissue exhibits a distinct optical characteristic: when excited in near-infrared light (~785 nm), it reemits at ~820 nm with a signal 2 to 10 times stronger than surrounding tissues (thyroid, fat, muscle). This autofluorescence-based detection is more accurate than visual identification alone and is associated with better postoperative outcomes. Limitations include: variable intensity between patients, weaker signal in diseased glands, false positives from colloid nodules, brown fat, and metastatic lymph nodes.

**Key distinction**: Autofluorescence properties are maintained even after parathyroid removal, so autofluorescence identifies tissue but does not assess function — ICG angiography is needed for vascularity/function assessment.

#### Corrected Calcium Monitoring

- Measure corrected calcium (adjusted for albumin) every 6-8 hours for 48 hours after total thyroidectomy
- Symptomatic hypocalcemia typically manifests 24-72 hours postoperatively
- Nadir typically at 48-72 hours

### Clinical Features of Hypocalcemia

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Perioral numbness/tingling | Earliest symptom, often within 24-48h |
| Acral paraesthesia | Fingers, toes, circumoral |
| Chvostek sign | Tapping facial nerve at parotid — ipsilateral facial muscle twitch; **positive in up to 20% of normocalcemic individuals** (Randolph Ch 44) |
| Trousseau sign | Inflating BP cuff above systolic for 3 min — carpal spasm (more specific) |
| Carpopedal spasm | Tetanic contraction of hand and foot muscles |
| Tetany | Patients describe the inability to move as "nothing short of terrifying," leading to anxiety and in some cases PTSD (Randolph Ch 44) |
| Laryngospasm | Life-threatening; can mimic bilateral VCP |
| QT prolongation | ECG monitoring essential; risk of arrhythmia; can progress to heart failure |
| Seizures | Severe hypocalcemia (<6.0 mg/dL) |
| Bronchospasm | Randolph Ch 44 — recognised manifestation of neuromuscular excitability |
| Altered mental status | Severe cases (Randolph Ch 44) |

### Management of Hypocalcemia

Randolph (Ch 44) states the goal of treatment is to maintain a **low-normal** calcium level, thereby controlling symptoms while stimulating residual gland function.

#### Elemental Calcium Content by Preparation (Randolph Ch 44, Table 44.1)

| Preparation | % Elemental Calcium |
|---|---|
| Calcium carbonate | 40% |
| Calcium citrate | 21% |
| Calcium lactate | 13% |
| Calcium gluconate | 9% |
| Calcium glubionate | 6.6% |

#### Mild Asymptomatic Hypocalcemia (Ca 7.5-8.5 mg/dL)

| Medication | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Oral calcium supplementation | 2-10 g daily in divided doses (Randolph Ch 44) | Different preparations provide different elemental calcium — see table above |
| Calcitriol (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D) | 0.25-0.5 mcg BD | Rapid onset (1-2 days), no renal activation needed; improves calcium absorption |
| Cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3) | 1000-2000 IU/day | Adjunctive; slower onset |

#### Symptomatic or Severe Hypocalcemia (Ca <7.0 mg/dL or symptomatic)

| Treatment | Protocol |
|---|---|
| IV calcium gluconate (10%) | 10 mL (93 mg elemental Ca) over 10 min, then infusion 1-2 mg/kg/hr |
| IV calcium chloride | May be used when a **central venous catheter** is present (Randolph Ch 44) — vesicant risk precludes peripheral use |
| Cardiac monitoring | Continuous ECG (QT interval monitoring) |
| IV magnesium | Check and correct (hypomagnesemia causes PTH resistance) |
| Oral calcium + calcitriol | Start simultaneously and titrate up |

#### Weaning Protocol

- Once stable on oral supplementation, reduce calcium by 500 mg/week
- Reduce calcitriol by 0.25 mcg every 1-2 weeks
- Target: normocalcemia on minimal supplementation
- **Permanent hypoparathyroidism** defined as: need for calcium/vitamin D supplementation beyond 6-12 months

### Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Transient hypocalcemia | Calcium normalises within 6 months without supplementation |
| Permanent hypoparathyroidism | Undetectable intact PTH level 6 months after surgery with ongoing calcium supplementation required (Randolph Ch 44); ATA position statement: intact PTH below lower limit of laboratory standard (~12 pg/mL) with resultant hypocalcemia persisting >6 months (Randolph Ch 31) |
| Chronic hypoparathyroidism | PTH-dependent hypocalcemia persisting >12 months |

## Parathyroid Autotransplantation

### Indications

1. Parathyroid gland inadvertently removed during thyroidectomy (identified in specimen)
2. Parathyroid gland identified with compromised blood supply (devascularised, discoloured, non-viable in situ) — Randolph (Ch 44) notes this is recognised by a gland that becomes dusky or even black during the course of surgery
3. Central neck dissection where parathyroid glands cannot be preserved in situ
4. Planned parathyroidectomy for secondary/tertiary hyperparathyroidism

### Surgical Technique for Parathyroid Preservation (Randolph Ch 44)

**Dissection approach**: Many surgeons commence gland dissection with a **top-down approach**, gently sweeping the parathyroid glands from the thyroid gland in a posterolateral direction. Every precaution should be taken to avoid dissection near the pedicle of the parathyroid gland. Suction should be used judiciously, as parathyroid glands can easily be aspirated into the suction tubing (Randolph Ch 44).

**Intraoperative assessment (Randolph Ch 31)**: Parathyroid colour changes associated with devascularisation may not always be reliable. Characteristic blackened colour change may be associated with venous disruption, but arterial interruption may be unassociated with this colour change yet still result in significant parathyroid dysfunction. This is where ICG angiography and autofluorescence provide complementary assessment.

### Autotransplantation Technique

1. **Identification**: Confirm tissue is parathyroid by visual inspection (tan/brown, bean-shaped, soft consistency) and/or frozen section biopsy
2. **Frozen section**: A small fragment (~1 mm) sent for histological confirmation before transplantation
3. **Preparation**:
   - Mince the parathyroid gland into 1 mm x 1 mm x 1 mm fragments using a scalpel on a sterile surface (Randolph Ch 44: "sectioning the gland with a sharp scalpel into 1-mm pieces")
   - Place fragments in cold saline until ready for implantation
4. **Implantation site**:
   - **Strap musculature or sternocleidomastoid muscle (SCM)**: Most common site — create a pocket in the muscle belly using blunt dissection (Randolph Ch 44)
   - **Alternative technique (Randolph Ch 44)**: Suspending the tissue in saline and injecting it with a syringe and needle into the muscle
   - **Brachioradialis muscle of non-dominant forearm**: Preferred for secondary/tertiary hyperparathyroidism (allows monitoring and re-exploration)
5. **Placement**: Distribute 10-20 fragments into the muscle pocket, spacing them for optimal vascularisation
6. **Closure**: Close the muscle pocket with non-absorbable suture or clip (to mark the site)
7. **Documentation**: Record number of glands transplanted, location, and frozen section result

### Evidence: In Situ Preservation vs Autotransplantation

**Lorente-Poch et al. (2015)** — key findings:
- Analysed 678 consecutive total thyroidectomies
- The number of parathyroid glands preserved in situ (not autotransplanted) was the strongest predictor of permanent hypoparathyroidism
- Patients with 4 glands preserved in situ: 0% permanent hypoparathyroidism
- Patients with 3 glands in situ: 2.6%
- Patients with 2 glands in situ: 6.4%
- Patients with 1 gland in situ: 16.4%
- **Critical conclusion**: Autotransplantation does not compensate for loss of in situ preserved glands; in situ preservation is paramount

Randolph (Ch 31) notes that while parathyroid autotransplantation has been associated with high rates of temporary hypoparathyroidism, rates of permanent hypoparathyroidism are very low. These manoeuvres have been shown to decrease rates of permanent hypoparathyroidism (Randolph Ch 44).

### Tailoring Contralateral Surgery Based on Parathyroid Status (Randolph Ch 31)

The extent of thyroidectomy should be tailored not only to the patient's risk group but also to the progress of the specific surgery. If dissection of the first side has revealed two parathyroids of good colour with good vascular pedicles and an RLN that has been identified and stimulates well electrically, contralateral surgery can be safely contemplated. **If the first side has not gone well, then elective contralateral lobe resection should be deferred, at least on that day.**

### Confirmation of Graft Function

- PTH measurement from the graft site (forearm) vs peripheral vein: ratio >1.5 suggests graft function
- Graft takes 4-8 weeks to establish blood supply and resume PTH secretion
- Success rate of autotransplantation: 60-90% (but variable and often insufficient to prevent permanent hypoparathyroidism)

## Post-operative Haematoma

### Incidence and Significance

- Incidence: less than 2% in developed countries, but some studies show incidence up to 4.39% (Randolph Ch 44, Liu meta-analysis)
- **Most feared early complication**: potential airway emergency
- 50% of haematomas occur within 6 hours of surgery; majority within first 24 hours (Randolph Ch 44)
- Rare delayed haematomas (24-72h) associated with coughing, straining, uncontrolled hypertension
- Reexploration rates for haemorrhage: 0.3-1.2% in large single-centre series; mortality related to postoperative haemorrhage: 0.01% overall, 0.6% for patients requiring reoperation (Randolph Ch 44)
- Trend toward outpatient thyroidectomy increases the stakes, as postthyroidectomy haemorrhage can threaten life in discharged patients (Randolph Ch 44)

### Classification (Randolph Ch 44)

Haematomas are classified as **superficial** or **deep** depending on their relationship to the strap muscles. Deep haematomas cause respiratory distress by venous congestion and laryngopharyngeal oedema. Randolph (Ch 44) advocates that strap muscles be reapproximated **loosely or incompletely** to allow egress of any accumulating deep neck haematoma.

### Pathophysiology of Airway Compromise

The mechanism is **NOT** direct tracheal compression but rather:
1. Haematoma causes venous congestion and lymphatic obstruction
2. Resulting laryngeal and pharyngeal oedema
3. Progressive airway narrowing
4. Stridor — respiratory failure
5. **Time is critical**: the longer the haematoma is present, the greater the resultant airway oedema and the more difficult intubation becomes (Randolph Ch 44)

### Risk Factors

| Factor | Comment |
|---|---|
| Older age | Randolph Ch 44 (Liu meta-analysis) |
| Male sex | Higher risk (Randolph Ch 44) |
| Graves disease (hypervascularity) | Higher bleeding risk (Randolph Ch 44) |
| Anticoagulant/antiplatelet/antithrombotic therapy | Withhold when safe preoperatively; Randolph Ch 44 — include herbal supplements |
| Bilateral operation | Randolph Ch 44 |
| Neck dissection | Randolph Ch 44 |
| Previous thyroid surgery | Randolph Ch 44 |
| Obesity | Suzuki et al., cited in Randolph Ch 44 |
| Blood transfusion on day of surgery | Suzuki et al., cited in Randolph Ch 44 |
| Uncontrolled hypertension | Aggressive BP control perioperatively |
| Valsalva manoeuvre during extubation | Smooth extubation technique critical |
| Substernal extension | Increased vascularity |
| Native or acquired coagulopathy | Randolph Ch 44 — detailed bleeding history including familial bleeding mandatory |

### Management Algorithm

**Signs and symptoms** (Randolph Ch 44): neck swelling, pain, oozing from the suture line, ecchymosis, dysphagia/odynophagia, stridor, respiratory distress.

**Suspected haematoma with respiratory distress**:

1. **Immediate bedside action** (do NOT wait for operating theatre):
   - Open wound at bedside (remove clips/sutures)
   - Evacuate haematoma digitally
   - Pack wound
   - Provides immediate airway relief
2. **Secure airway**: Intubation may be difficult depending on the degree of laryngopharyngeal oedema. Emergent tracheostomy may be necessary — tracheotomy access is usually straightforward because of the ease of accessing the exposed trachea (Randolph Ch 44)
3. **Return to operating theatre**: Formal exploration, haemostasis, irrigation, re-closure with drain
4. **Post-operative**: ICU monitoring, consider delayed extubation if significant oedema

**Key teaching point**: Every thyroidectomy patient with a wound haematoma and respiratory distress requires immediate wound opening at the bedside — this is a surgical emergency where minutes matter. A protocol for the management of this complication should be written and widely available to all members of staff responsible for postoperative care (Randolph Ch 44). Regular examination of the neck (palpation and visualisation) should be performed postoperatively at regular intervals.

### Prevention

- Meticulous haemostasis during surgery (bipolar cautery, energy devices, clips). Randolph Ch 44: any bleeding near the RLN should be treated with judicious use of bipolar electrocautery; fibrin sealants, cellulose-based hemostatic agents, or microfibrillar collagen may be useful adjuncts when electrocautery is unsafe
- Controlled blood pressure perioperatively (target SBP <140 mmHg)
- Valsalva manoeuvre before wound closure (check for bleeding at elevated venous pressure); Trendelenburg position can also be used (Randolph Ch 44)
- Smooth extubation (avoid coughing, straining). Randolph Ch 44: prophylactic dexamethasone before induction reduces postoperative nausea and vomiting in thyroidectomies
- **Drains** (Randolph Ch 44): Multiple studies show haematoma rates are equivalent in patients with and without wound drainage; drains neither prevent postoperative bleeding nor facilitate early diagnosis. Use should be determined case-by-case and a drain should not be relied upon to prevent haematoma formation
- **Cervical pressure dressings** (e.g. Queen Anne dressing): Randolph Ch 44 — these are cumbersome, may delay recognition of a haematoma, do not prevent haematoma formation, and are **not recommended**
- Patient education: avoid straining, heavy lifting for 2 weeks

## Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve Injury

### Brief Overview

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Transient RLN palsy | 3-8%; recovery within 6-12 months |
| Permanent RLN palsy | 0.3-3% |
| Mechanism | Traction, thermal injury, transection, ischaemia (vasa nervorum damage) |

| Unilateral injury | Hoarseness, breathy voice, aspiration risk |
| Bilateral injury | Airway compromise, stridor, may require tracheostomy |

## External Branch of Superior Laryngeal Nerve (EBSLN) Injury

### Clinical Significance

- Supplies the cricothyroid muscle (tensor of the vocal fold)
- Injury causes loss of vocal pitch range and projection (difficulty with high-pitched sounds)
- Often unrecognised ("the forgotten nerve of thyroid surgery")
- Cernea classification guides risk assessment

### Cernea Classification

| Type | Description | Risk of Injury |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | EBSLN crosses >1 cm above the superior pole | Low |
| 2a | EBSLN crosses <1 cm above the superior pole | Moderate |
| 2b | EBSLN crosses below the plane of the superior pole | High (most vulnerable) |

### Prevention

- Identify and preserve the EBSLN during superior pole dissection
- Ligate superior thyroid vessels individually, close to the thyroid capsule (not en masse)
- IONM can identify EBSLN with cricothyroid muscle twitch monitoring

## Hypothyroidism

- Expected after total thyroidectomy (100% require replacement)
- After partial/less-than-total thyroidectomy: clinically apparent hypothyroidism arises in **11-50%** of patients (Randolph Ch 44), with 1 in 5 developing some form of hypothyroidism and 1 in 25 developing clinical hypothyroidism (Verloop meta-analysis, cited in Randolph)
- Most important preoperative predictors: TSH in the high-normal range and positive anti-TPO status (Randolph Ch 44)
- Begin levothyroxine replacement on postoperative day 1 after total thyroidectomy
- TSH target depends on risk stratification (DTC vs benign disease)
- Randolph (Ch 44) notes that hypothyroidism after total thyroidectomy is a **consequence, not a complication**; the need for hormone therapy should be an important consideration when deciding the extent of surgery

## Thyrotoxic Storm (Randolph Ch 44)

### Overview

Thyrotoxic storm is a rare but life-threatening complication precipitated by surgery, trauma, infection, or iodine exposure (iodinated contrast, amiodarone). The physiology involves sudden dissociation of thyroid hormone from binding proteins, with an increase in free thyroid hormone producing multi-system effects.

### Clinical Features

Fever, tachycardia, cardiac arrhythmias; in extreme cases: cardiovascular collapse, hepatic failure, altered mentation (confusion, agitation, anxiety), and death.

### Prevention (Randolph Ch 44)

- Antithyroid medications administered before surgery
- Beta-blockade to control systemic cardiac effects
- For poorly controlled thyrotoxicosis requiring urgent thyroidectomy: **iopanoic acid** (500 mg bid), **dexamethasone** (1 mg bid), propylthiouracil or methimazole, and beta-blockade preoperatively

### Treatment

| Intervention | Mechanism / Dose |
|---|---|
| Beta-blockade (Propranolol) | 4-10 mg/kg — reduces sympathetic activity and blocks T4 to T3 conversion |
| Hydrocortisone | 100-300 mg — reduces fever, iodine uptake, TSH, and inhibits T4 to T3 conversion |
| Thioamides (Methimazole/PTU) | Block thyroid hormone synthesis; PTU also blocks peripheral T4 to T3 conversion |
| Sodium iodide | 1.0-2.5 g — reduces thyroid hormone synthesis and release |
| Cooling | Cooling blankets and acetaminophen (avoid aspirin — displaces T4 from binding proteins) |

If recognised intraoperatively: cease manipulation of the thyroid gland and terminate the operation.

## Other Complications

### Seroma

- Incidence: up to 7% (Randolph Ch 44)
- Wound drainage does not significantly decrease seroma incidence (Randolph Ch 44)
- Risk factors: older age, increased BMI, decreased postoperative ionised calcium (Randolph Ch 44)
- Fibrin sealants appear to be a useful tool to reduce seroma formation (Randolph Ch 44)
- Management: observation, serial needle aspirations (using strict sterile technique to avoid wound contamination), or placement of a suction drain
- Conservative management leads to gradual resolution but can be a source of anxiety and slight discomfort for the patient

### Wound Infection

- Incidence: 0.5-3% (clean surgical wound; Randolph Ch 44)
- **Perioperative antibiotics have not been shown to reduce wound infection incidence and are not routinely used** (Randolph Ch 44)
- Two principal specific risk factors (Randolph Ch 44): **use of drains** and **concomitant lymph node dissection** — both are independent risk factors
- Infection associated with modified lymph node dissection occurs in 13-20% of patients (Randolph Ch 44)
- Consideration should be given to antibiotic prophylaxis in patients undergoing lymph node surgery with drain placement (Randolph Ch 44)
- Management: cellulitis — oral antibiotics; infected seroma — drainage and culture-directed IV antibiotics
- **Deep infections should raise suspicion of aerodigestive tract injury** — at incision and drainage, the oesophagus and trachea should be explored (Randolph Ch 44)

### Horner Syndrome

- Incidence: ~0.2% (Randolph Ch 44)
- Results from injury along the cervical sympathetic chain, most at risk during nodal dissection
- Features: partial ptosis, enophthalmos, miosis, anhidrosis (ipsilateral); occasionally ipsilateral facial flushing due to capillary vasodilation (Randolph Ch 44)
- If secondary to haematoma/seroma, it may be **reversible by prompt drainage** (Randolph Ch 44)
- Permanent injury may arise from division, ischaemia, or stretching from lateral retraction
- Diagnosis confirmed with **apraclonidine** applied to both eyes — an increased mydriatic effect is observed on the affected side (Randolph Ch 44)
- Corrective surgery may be indicated if ptosis is severe
- Refer to ophthalmology when suspected

### Thoracic Duct Injury (Chyle Leak)

- Risk: primarily with left-sided surgery involving lymph node dissection. The thoracic duct typically drains into the venous system within 1 cm of the confluence of the left internal jugular and subclavian veins, but wide variability makes it prone to unrecognised injury (Randolph Ch 44)
- Incidence: 0.6-1.4% for central node dissection; 4.4-8.3% for lateral dissection (Randolph Ch 44)
- Presentation: milky-white drainage from wound/drain; without drains, presents later as fluctuant swelling under the wound
- Chyle leak impairs wound healing, can lead to laryngeal oedema, and prolongs hospital stays. Loss of chyle (fat, protein, electrolytes, lymphocytes) can cause severe nutritional, metabolic, and immunologic disorders (Randolph Ch 44)
- Management:
  - **Conservative** (cure rate 58-100%; Randolph Ch 44): medium-chain triglyceride diet (absorbed directly into bloodstream, bypassing thoracic duct); may take up to 3 weeks to resolve. Alternative: NPO with total parenteral nutrition
  - **Somatostatin analogue (octreotide)**: decreases splanchnic blood flow, reduces chyle formation — a promising adjunctive therapy (Randolph Ch 44)
  - **Surgical** (high-volume fistula >700 mL/day, or failed conservative management): thoracic duct ligation via transcervical approach. Oversewing the duct is favoured; other options include clips, glues, and muscular flaps. If these fail, thoracoscopic duct ligation or interventional embolisation of the thoracic duct can be considered (Randolph Ch 44)

### Aerodigestive Tract Injury

- Very rare; inadvertent injury is "almost unheard of" in thyroidectomy (Randolph Ch 44)
- **Oesophageal injury**: recognised intraoperatively — close with inverting absorbable sutures, copious irrigation, suction drain. Unrecognised injury may present with neck pain, dysphagia, subcutaneous emphysema, fever, leukocytosis, or sepsis. Diagnosis confirmed by Gastrografin swallow (Randolph Ch 44). Management: conservative (drainage + antibiotics) or surgical repair. NPO for several days
- **Tracheal injury**: close primarily with absorbable sutures; drain and antibiotics. When primary closure not possible — tracheotomy tube or segmental tracheal resection. Strap muscles can buttress the closure. Delayed presentation may occur with subcutaneous emphysema from platelet plug detachment on mucosal surface of trachea (Randolph Ch 44)

### Tracheomalacia (Randolph Ch 44)

- Incidence: 0-10%, related to long-standing goiter with significant retrosternal extension
- Occurs when cartilaginous trachea becomes weak from chronic pressure and narrows with negative inspiratory force of inhalation
- Recognised intraoperatively as a soft, collapsible trachea on palpation
- In majority of cases, no clinical significance
- Management when anticipated: keep intubated overnight, deflate ETT cuff next morning — if good air leak, extubate. Repeat airway obstruction necessitates tracheostomy or longer intubation (ETT serves as stent while paratracheal fibrosis supports airway). Alternatives: tracheopexy, miniplate fixation, mesh splinting, or tracheal resection
- Important to rule out bilateral vocal cord paralysis as cause of airway obstruction

### Difficult Intubation (Randolph Ch 44)

- Incidence of difficult intubation: 1.8% in general surgical population; rises to **4-11% for goiter surgery**
- Risk factors: neck thickness, reduced mouth opening, older age, malignancy, large goiter (>40 g), reduced thyromental distance (<6.5 cm)
- Interestingly, radiologic signs of tracheal compression are **not** associated with difficult intubation (Randolph Ch 44)
- Mask ventilation is not typically impaired despite potential for difficult intubation
- Surgeon should be present at time of intubation; fiberoptic intubation considered when routine intubation is challenging

### Hypertrophic Scar and Keloid (Randolph Ch 44)

- Strategies to minimise: place incision in natural skin crease (higher skin crease allows inferior pole mobilisation through the incision); avoid energy-device or traction-related skin edge burns
- Postoperative: timely suture removal, Steri-strips, wound care products, avoid sun exposure, breast support in women to avoid wound tension
- Management of hypertrophic scar/keloid: intralesional triamcinolone acetonide; if this fails, scar revision (noting keloid revision can lead to worse outcome). Laser therapy for thyroidectomy scars has been studied and found efficacious (Randolph Ch 44)

## Key Points for Exam

1. **Hypocalcemia** is the most common complication of total thyroidectomy — transient 19-38%, permanent 0-3% (up to 8% in community setting per ACS survey; Randolph Ch 31)
2. PTH measured at 4-6 hours postoperatively is the best predictor of hypocalcemia; intraoperative rapid PTH <15 pg/mL correlates with higher postoperative hypoparathyroidism (Randolph Ch 44)
3. PTH <10 pg/mL or decline >75% predicts significant hypocalcemia
4. **In situ preservation** of parathyroid glands is more important than autotransplantation (Lorente-Poch 2015); however, autotransplantation decreases permanent hypoparathyroidism rates (Randolph Ch 44)
5. Parathyroid autotransplantation technique: mince into 1mm fragments, implant into strap muscle/SCM pocket or inject as saline suspension (Randolph Ch 44), confirm by frozen section
6. **Preoperative calcium/calcitriol supplementation 5-7 days before surgery** for high-risk patients (Randolph Ch 44)
7. ICG fluorescence angiography and parathyroid autofluorescence are emerging tools for intraoperative parathyroid identification and viability assessment (Randolph Ch 31)
8. **Post-operative haematoma** is a surgical emergency — open wound at bedside first. Haematoma causes airway compromise via laryngopharyngeal oedema (not direct tracheal compression)
9. Drains do not prevent haematoma; cervical pressure dressings (Queen Anne) are not recommended (Randolph Ch 44)
10. Chvostek sign (less specific; positive in 20% of normocalcemic individuals) and Trousseau sign (more specific) are bedside tests for hypocalcemia
11. IV calcium gluconate (not chloride via peripheral line) is the treatment for symptomatic hypocalcemia; calcium chloride may be used via central line (Randolph Ch 44)
12. Permanent hypoparathyroidism: undetectable PTH at 6 months with ongoing supplementation requirement (Randolph Ch 44); associated with twofold increased mortality risk
13. Surgeon experience is vitally linked to hypoparathyroidism risk — volume-outcome relationship is clearly established (Randolph Ch 31)
14. Thyrotoxic storm prevention: antithyroid drugs + beta-blockade + iopanoic acid + dexamethasone for poorly controlled thyrotoxicosis (Randolph Ch 44)
15. If first side dissection goes poorly (parathyroid or RLN compromise), defer elective contralateral surgery (Randolph Ch 31)
