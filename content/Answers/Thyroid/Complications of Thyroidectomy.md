## Overview of Thyroidectomy Complications

### Classification by Timing

| Timing | Complications |
|---|---|
| Intraoperative | Haemorrhage, RLN injury, EBSLN injury, parathyroid devascularisation/removal, tracheal injury, oesophageal injury |
| Early (0-48h) | Neck haematoma, airway compromise, symptomatic hypocalcemia, wound infection, seroma |
| Late (>48h) | Permanent hypoparathyroidism, permanent RLN palsy, hypothyroidism, keloid/scar, chyle leak (thoracic duct injury), Horner syndrome |

### Incidence of Major Complications

| Complication | Transient Rate | Permanent Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Hypocalcemia | 20-30% | 1-4% |
| RLN palsy (unilateral) | 3-8% | 0.3-3% |
| RLN palsy (bilateral) | <1% | <0.5% |
| Post-operative haematoma | 1-2% | — |
| Wound infection | 1-2% | — |
| EBSLN injury | 0-25% (often unrecognised) | — |
| Horner syndrome | <1% | — |
| Thoracic duct injury | <1% (left side) | — |

## Post-Thyroidectomy Hypocalcemia

### Pathophysiology

The parathyroid glands are the primary regulators of calcium homeostasis via PTH secretion. Hypocalcemia after thyroidectomy results from:

1. **Inadvertent parathyroidectomy**: Removal of one or more glands within the thyroid specimen (incidence: 5-20% of thyroidectomies when specimens are carefully examined)
2. **Parathyroid devascularisation**: Disruption of the end-arterial blood supply (branches from inferior thyroid artery for inferior glands, and superior thyroid artery/ITA for superior glands)
3. **Stunning**: Transient parathyroid suppression from surgical manipulation, even with intact blood supply
4. **Hungry bone syndrome**: Rapid calcium uptake into bone after correction of thyrotoxicosis (particularly in Graves disease)

### Risk Factors for Hypocalcemia

| Risk Factor | Evidence Level |
|---|---|
| Female sex | Meta-analysis (Edafe 2014) |
| Graves disease / thyrotoxicosis | Prospective multicentre (Thomusch 2000) |
| Central neck dissection (level VI) | Multiple RCTs |
| Bilateral thyroid surgery (vs lobectomy) | Strong |
| Inadvertent parathyroidectomy (any gland) | Strong |
| <3 parathyroid glands preserved in situ | Lorente-Poch 2015 |
| Parathyroid autotransplantation (as marker of gland compromise) | Moderate |
| Surgeon inexperience / low volume | Bergenfelz 2008 |
| Substernal goiter | Moderate |
| Reoperation / redo thyroidectomy | Strong |
| Autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto) | Moderate |

### Prediction of Post-thyroidectomy Hypocalcemia

#### PTH Measurement Protocol

| Timing of PTH | Threshold | Clinical Utility |
|---|---|---|
| Intraoperative (pre-closure) | <10 pg/mL | High sensitivity for predicting hypocalcemia |
| 1-hour postoperative | <15 pg/mL | Rapid risk stratification |
| 4-6 hour postoperative | <10-15 pg/mL | Most widely validated time point |
| PTH decline >75% from baseline | — | High sensitivity for permanent hypoparathyroidism |

**Key thresholds**:
- PTH >15 pg/mL at 4-6 hours: low risk of symptomatic hypocalcemia → consider same-day discharge
- PTH 5-15 pg/mL: moderate risk → start oral calcium + calcitriol, close monitoring
- PTH <5 pg/mL or undetectable: high risk → aggressive supplementation, possible IV calcium, extended observation

#### Corrected Calcium Monitoring

- Measure corrected calcium (adjusted for albumin) every 6-8 hours for 48 hours after total thyroidectomy
- Symptomatic hypocalcemia typically manifests 24-72 hours postoperatively
- Nadir typically at 48-72 hours

### Clinical Features of Hypocalcemia

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Perioral numbness/tingling | Earliest symptom, often within 24-48h |
| Acral paraesthesia | Fingers, toes, circumoral |
| Chvostek sign | Tapping facial nerve at parotid → ipsilateral facial muscle twitch |
| Trousseau sign | Inflating BP cuff above systolic for 3 min → carpal spasm (more specific) |
| Carpopedal spasm | Tetanic contraction of hand and foot muscles |
| Laryngospasm | Life-threatening; can mimic bilateral VCP |
| QT prolongation | ECG monitoring essential; risk of arrhythmia |
| Seizures | Severe hypocalcemia (<6.0 mg/dL) |

### Management of Hypocalcemia

#### Mild Asymptomatic Hypocalcemia (Ca 7.5-8.5 mg/dL)

| Medication | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Oral calcium carbonate | 1-3 g elemental calcium/day (divided TDS) | Take with food for absorption |
| Calcitriol (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D) | 0.25-0.5 mcg BD | Rapid onset (1-2 days), no renal activation needed |
| Cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3) | 1000-2000 IU/day | Adjunctive; slower onset |

#### Symptomatic or Severe Hypocalcemia (Ca <7.0 mg/dL or symptomatic)

| Treatment | Protocol |
|---|---|
| IV calcium gluconate (10%) | 10 mL (93 mg elemental Ca) over 10 min, then infusion 1-2 mg/kg/hr |
| Cardiac monitoring | Continuous ECG (QT interval monitoring) |
| IV magnesium | Check and correct (hypomagnesemia causes PTH resistance) |
| Oral calcium + calcitriol | Start simultaneously and titrate up |
| Avoid | IV calcium chloride via peripheral line (vesicant risk) |

#### Weaning Protocol

- Once stable on oral supplementation, reduce calcium by 500 mg/week
- Reduce calcitriol by 0.25 mcg every 1-2 weeks
- Target: normocalcemia on minimal supplementation
- **Permanent hypoparathyroidism** defined as: need for calcium/vitamin D supplementation beyond 6-12 months

### Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Transient hypocalcemia | Calcium normalises within 6 months without supplementation |
| Permanent hypoparathyroidism | Need for supplementation >6-12 months with low/absent PTH |
| Chronic hypoparathyroidism | PTH-dependent hypocalcemia persisting >12 months |

## Parathyroid Autotransplantation

### Indications

1. Parathyroid gland inadvertently removed during thyroidectomy (identified in specimen)
2. Parathyroid gland identified with compromised blood supply (devascularised, discoloured, non-viable in situ)
3. Central neck dissection where parathyroid glands cannot be preserved in situ
4. Planned parathyroidectomy for secondary/tertiary hyperparathyroidism

### Technique

1. **Identification**: Confirm tissue is parathyroid by visual inspection (tan/brown, bean-shaped, soft consistency) and/or frozen section biopsy
2. **Frozen section**: A small fragment (~1 mm) sent for histological confirmation before transplantation
3. **Preparation**:
   - Mince the parathyroid gland into 1 mm x 1 mm x 1 mm fragments using a scalpel on a sterile surface
   - Place fragments in cold saline until ready for implantation
4. **Implantation site**:
   - **Sternocleidomastoid muscle (SCM)**: Most common site — create a pocket in the muscle belly using blunt dissection
   - **Brachioradialis muscle of non-dominant forearm**: Preferred for secondary/tertiary hyperparathyroidism (allows monitoring and re-exploration)
5. **Placement**: Distribute 10-20 fragments into the muscle pocket, spacing them for optimal vascularisation
6. **Closure**: Close the muscle pocket with non-absorbable suture or clip (to mark the site)
7. **Documentation**: Record number of glands transplanted, location, and frozen section result

### Evidence: In Situ Preservation vs Autotransplantation

**Lorente-Poch et al. (2015)** — key findings:
- Analysed 678 consecutive total thyroidectomies
- The number of parathyroid glands preserved in situ (not autotransplanted) was the strongest predictor of permanent hypoparathyroidism
- Patients with 4 glands preserved in situ: 0% permanent hypoparathyroidism
- Patients with 3 glands in situ: 2.6%
- Patients with 2 glands in situ: 6.4%
- Patients with 1 gland in situ: 16.4%
- **Critical conclusion**: Autotransplantation does not compensate for loss of in situ preserved glands; in situ preservation is paramount

### Confirmation of Graft Function

- PTH measurement from the graft site (forearm) vs peripheral vein: ratio >1.5 suggests graft function
- Graft takes 4-8 weeks to establish blood supply and resume PTH secretion
- Success rate of autotransplantation: 60-90% (but variable and often insufficient to prevent permanent hypoparathyroidism)

## Post-operative Haematoma

### Incidence and Significance

- Incidence: 1-2% after thyroidectomy
- **Most feared early complication**: potential airway emergency
- 50% of haematomas occur within 6 hours of surgery; 80% within 24 hours
- Rare delayed haematomas (24-72h) associated with coughing, straining, uncontrolled hypertension

### Pathophysiology of Airway Compromise

The mechanism is **NOT** direct tracheal compression but rather:
1. Haematoma causes venous congestion and lymphatic obstruction
2. Resulting laryngeal and pharyngeal oedema
3. Progressive airway narrowing
4. Stridor → respiratory failure

### Risk Factors

| Factor | Comment |
|---|---|
| Anticoagulant/antiplatelet therapy | Withhold when safe preoperatively |
| Uncontrolled hypertension | Aggressive BP control perioperatively |
| Graves disease (hypervascularity) | Higher bleeding risk |
| Valsalva manoeuvre during extubation | Smooth extubation technique critical |
| Male sex | Higher risk |
| Substernal extension | Increased vascularity |

### Management Algorithm

**Suspected haematoma with respiratory distress**:

1. **Immediate bedside action** (do NOT wait for operating theatre):
   - Open wound at bedside (remove clips/sutures)
   - Evacuate haematoma digitally
   - Pack wound
   - Provides immediate airway relief
2. **Secure airway**: Intubation (may be difficult due to oedema) or emergency surgical airway
3. **Return to operating theatre**: Formal exploration, haemostasis, irrigation, re-closure with drain
4. **Post-operative**: ICU monitoring, consider delayed extubation if significant oedema

**Key teaching point**: Every thyroidectomy patient with a wound haematoma and respiratory distress requires immediate wound opening at the bedside — this is a surgical emergency where minutes matter.

### Prevention

- Meticulous haemostasis during surgery (bipolar cautery, energy devices, clips)
- Controlled blood pressure perioperatively (target SBP <140 mmHg)
- Valsalva manoeuvre before wound closure (check for bleeding at elevated venous pressure)
- Smooth extubation (avoid coughing, straining)
- Drain placement (controversial — may not prevent haematoma but provides early warning)
- Patient education: avoid straining, heavy lifting for 2 weeks

## Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve Injury

*For detailed anatomy, IONM, and management of RLN injury, see [[Answers/Thyroid/RLN Anatomy, IONM, and Nerve Injury|RLN Anatomy, IONM, and Nerve Injury]].*

### Brief Overview

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Transient RLN palsy | 3-8%; recovery within 6-12 months |
| Permanent RLN palsy | 0.3-3% |
| Mechanism | Traction, thermal injury, transection, ischaemia (vasa nervorum damage) |
| Prevention | Visual identification (gold standard), IONM (adjunct) |
| Unilateral injury | Hoarseness, breathy voice, aspiration risk |
| Bilateral injury | Airway compromise, stridor, may require tracheostomy |

## External Branch of Superior Laryngeal Nerve (EBSLN) Injury

### Clinical Significance

- Supplies the cricothyroid muscle (tensor of the vocal fold)
- Injury causes loss of vocal pitch range and projection (difficulty with high-pitched sounds)
- Often unrecognised ("the forgotten nerve of thyroid surgery")
- Cernea classification guides risk assessment

### Cernea Classification

| Type | Description | Risk of Injury |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | EBSLN crosses >1 cm above the superior pole | Low |
| 2a | EBSLN crosses <1 cm above the superior pole | Moderate |
| 2b | EBSLN crosses below the plane of the superior pole | High (most vulnerable) |

### Prevention

- Identify and preserve the EBSLN during superior pole dissection
- Ligate superior thyroid vessels individually, close to the thyroid capsule (not en masse)
- IONM can identify EBSLN with cricothyroid muscle twitch monitoring

## Other Complications

### Seroma

- Incidence: 2-5%
- Usually resolves spontaneously within 2-4 weeks
- Aspiration if large, symptomatic, or cosmetically concerning
- Prevention: careful haemostasis, drain placement (debated)

### Wound Infection

- Incidence: 1-2% (clean surgical wound)
- Risk factors: diabetes, obesity, immunosuppression, haematoma
- Management: antibiotics, drainage if collection present

### Horner Syndrome

- Rare (<1%); results from injury to the cervical sympathetic chain
- Features: miosis, ptosis, anhydrosis (ipsilateral)
- Usually occurs with extensive lateral dissection or redo surgery
- Most cases transient; permanent injury rare

### Thoracic Duct Injury

- Risk: primarily with left-sided surgery involving level IV dissection
- Presentation: milky drainage from wound/drain (chyle leak)
- Management:
  - Low-output (<500 mL/day): conservative (NPO or medium-chain triglyceride diet, pressure dressing)
  - High-output (>500 mL/day) or persistent (>5-7 days): surgical exploration and ligation

### Hypothyroidism

- Expected after total thyroidectomy (100% require replacement)
- After lobectomy: 20-30% eventually require supplementation
- Begin levothyroxine replacement on postoperative day 1 after total thyroidectomy
- TSH target depends on risk stratification (DTC vs benign disease)

### Injury to Trachea and Oesophagus

- Very rare; associated with advanced cancer surgery or revision thyroidectomy
- Tracheal injury: usually recognised intraoperatively; primary repair
- Oesophageal injury: intraoperative recognition → primary repair + drain; delayed recognition → high morbidity

## Key Points for Exam

1. **Hypocalcemia** is the most common complication of total thyroidectomy (transient 20-30%, permanent 1-4%)
2. PTH measured at 4-6 hours postoperatively is the best predictor of hypocalcemia
3. PTH <10 pg/mL or decline >75% predicts significant hypocalcemia
4. **In situ preservation** of parathyroid glands is more important than autotransplantation (Lorente-Poch 2015)
5. Parathyroid autotransplantation technique: mince into 1mm fragments, implant into SCM pocket, confirm by frozen section
6. **Post-operative haematoma** is a surgical emergency — open wound at bedside first
7. Haematoma causes airway compromise via laryngeal oedema (not direct tracheal compression)
8. Chvostek sign (less specific) and Trousseau sign (more specific) are bedside tests for hypocalcemia
9. IV calcium gluconate (not chloride via peripheral line) is the treatment for symptomatic hypocalcemia
10. Permanent hypoparathyroidism is defined as need for supplementation beyond 6-12 months
