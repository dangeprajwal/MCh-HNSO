## RET Proto-oncogene: Structure and Function

### Gene Structure

- **Location:** Chromosome 10q11.2
- **Gene size:** ~55 kb with 21 exons
- **Protein:** Transmembrane receptor tyrosine kinase
- The name "RET" derives from "**RE**arranged during **T**ransfection" -- the gene was originally discovered as a rearranged oncogene in a transfection assay (Takahashi 1985)

### Protein Structure

The RET protein is a single-pass transmembrane receptor tyrosine kinase with the following domains:

| Domain | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Extracellular domain** | N-terminal | Contains 4 cadherin-like repeats and a cysteine-rich region; binds GDNF family ligands via GFRalpha co-receptors |
| **Transmembrane domain** | Single-pass | Anchors receptor in cell membrane |
| **Intracellular domain** | C-terminal | Contains two tyrosine kinase subdomains; initiates downstream signalling |

### Normal Function

- RET is the receptor for **glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) family** ligands: GDNF, neurturin, artemin, persephin
- These ligands first bind to a **GFRalpha co-receptor** (GFRalpha1--4), and the ligand-co-receptor complex then activates RET by inducing dimerisation and autophosphorylation
- RET signalling is essential for:
  - Development of the enteric nervous system (Hirschsprung disease results from RET loss-of-function)
  - Development of the kidney and urinary tract
  - Differentiation and survival of neural crest-derived cells (C-cells, adrenal medulla, sympathetic ganglia)
  - Spermatogenesis

### Downstream Signalling Pathways

Upon activation, RET triggers multiple signalling cascades:
1. **RAS-MAPK pathway** -- cell proliferation and differentiation
2. **PI3K-AKT pathway** -- cell survival and anti-apoptosis
3. **JAK-STAT pathway** -- gene transcription
4. **PLCgamma pathway** -- calcium signalling
5. **Src kinase pathway** -- cell migration and adhesion

## Gain-of-Function Mutations in Thyroid Cancer

### RET/PTC Rearrangements in Papillary Thyroid Cancer

- Chromosomal rearrangements that fuse the intracellular kinase domain of RET to the 5' portion of various partner genes
- Result in constitutive (ligand-independent) RET kinase activation
- Found in 10--30% of sporadic PTC (higher in radiation-associated PTC)

| Rearrangement | Fusion Partner | Frequency | Association |
|---|---|---|---|
| **RET/PTC1** | CCDC6 (H4) | Most common (~60--70% of rearrangements) | Sporadic PTC, classical variant |
| **RET/PTC2** | PRKAR1A | Rare | Sporadic PTC |
| **RET/PTC3** | NCOA4 (ELE1) | Second most common (~20--30%) | Radiation-associated PTC, solid variant |

- RET/PTC rearrangements are more common in:
  - Younger patients
  - Radiation-associated PTC (especially post-Chernobyl)
  - Papillary microcarcinomas
- Generally associated with favourable prognosis (more differentiated tumours)

### RET Point Mutations in Medullary Thyroid Cancer

Unlike PTC (where RET rearrangements dominate), MTC is driven by **point mutations** in the RET gene that cause constitutive kinase activation:

#### Hereditary MTC (MEN2 Syndromes)

- **Germline** RET mutations found in virtually 100% of MEN2 families
- Autosomal dominant inheritance with high penetrance
- Mutation type determines clinical syndrome (MEN2A, MEN2B, FMTC)

#### Sporadic MTC

- **Somatic** RET mutations found in 40--65% of sporadic MTC
- M918T is the most common somatic mutation (~80% of somatic mutations)
- Somatic RET mutations are independently associated with worse prognosis (Elisei 2008)

## Genotype-Phenotype Correlation

### Clinical Syndromes

| Syndrome | RET Mutations | Components | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MEN2A** | Cys634 (most common), Cys609, 611, 618, 620 | MTC (95%), phaeochromocytoma (50%), PHPT (20--30%) | 55% of MEN2 |
| **MEN2B** | M918T (95%), A883F (5%) | MTC (100%), phaeochromocytoma (50%), mucosal neuromas (100%), marfanoid habitus, intestinal ganglioneuromatosis | 5--10% of MEN2 |
| **FMTC** | Various (609, 611, 618, 620, 768, 790, 791, 804, 891) | MTC only (no phaeochromocytoma or PHPT) | 35--40% of MEN2 |

### ATA Risk Categories for RET Mutations (Wells 2015)

The ATA 2015 MTC guidelines classify RET mutations into three risk levels based on aggressiveness of MTC development:

#### Highest Risk (ATA-HST)

| Codon | Mutation | Syndrome | MTC Behaviour |
|---|---|---|---|
| **918** | M918T | MEN2B | Most aggressive -- MTC may develop in first year of life; metastases by age 2--3 |
| | | | Prophylactic thyroidectomy within **first 6 months of life** (ideally by age 1) |

#### High Risk (ATA-H)

| Codon | Mutation | Syndrome | MTC Behaviour |
|---|---|---|---|
| **634** | C634R/Y/W | MEN2A | Aggressive -- MTC by age 5--10 in most carriers |
| **883** | A883F | MEN2B variant | Aggressive -- similar to C634 |
| | | | Prophylactic thyroidectomy by **age 5** |

#### Moderate Risk (ATA-MOD)

| Codon | Mutation | Syndrome | MTC Behaviour |
|---|---|---|---|
| **609, 611, 618, 620** | Various Cys mutations | MEN2A/FMTC | Later onset MTC; lower penetrance |
| **768** | E768D | FMTC | Indolent |
| **790** | L790F | FMTC | Indolent |
| **791** | Y791F | FMTC | Indolent |
| **804** | V804L/M | FMTC | Indolent |
| **891** | S891A | FMTC | Indolent |
| | | | Prophylactic thyroidectomy may be deferred to **age 5--10** if calcitonin remains normal and neck US is negative |

## Prophylactic Thyroidectomy: Timing and Technique

### Rationale

- Hereditary MTC is inevitable in RET mutation carriers -- it is not a question of "if" but "when"
- The window between C-cell hyperplasia (precursor) and invasive MTC (with potential for nodal metastases) provides an opportunity for curative surgical intervention
- Machens et al. (2003) demonstrated the age-related genotype-specific progression pattern that guides timing

### Recommended Timing by Risk Level

| ATA Risk Category | Recommended Timing | Calcitonin Monitoring | Evidence Base |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Highest (M918T)** | Within first 6 months of life, no later than 1 year | Not used to delay surgery | MTC may be present at birth; metastases by age 2--3 |
| **High (C634, A883F)** | By age 5 years | Annually from early childhood; surgery if calcitonin rises | MTC rare before age 5 but common by age 10 |
| **Moderate** | Consider by age 5--10, OR defer based on calcitonin | Every 6--12 months from age 3--5; surgery if calcitonin rises above normal | MTC typically develops in second or third decade |

### Preoperative Workup

**Critical:** Before any thyroidectomy in MEN2, **phaeochromocytoma must be excluded first**:

1. **Biochemical screening:**
   - 24-hour urine catecholamines and metanephrines, OR
   - Plasma-free metanephrines (preferred -- higher sensitivity)
   - If elevated, adrenalectomy must precede thyroidectomy to prevent hypertensive crisis

2. **Imaging (if biochemical screen positive):**
   - CT or MRI of adrenal glands
   - MIBG scintigraphy if needed

3. **Calcium and PTH:**
   - Screen for primary hyperparathyroidism (MEN2A)

4. **Calcitonin level:**
   - Basal calcitonin; stimulated calcitonin (pentagastrin or calcium) if available
   - Elevated calcitonin suggests MTC has already developed (may indicate need for more extensive surgery including CCND)

5. **Neck ultrasonography:**
   - Evaluate for thyroid nodules and cervical lymphadenopathy

### Extent of Surgery

| Scenario | Recommended Procedure |
|---|---|
| **Prophylactic thyroidectomy (normal calcitonin, no nodules)** | Total thyroidectomy alone (no CCND) |
| **Elevated calcitonin <40 pg/mL, no imaging abnormalities** | Total thyroidectomy + prophylactic bilateral CCND |
| **Elevated calcitonin >=40 pg/mL** | Total thyroidectomy + bilateral CCND + consider lateral neck dissection if N1b |
| **Known MTC on imaging/biopsy** | Total thyroidectomy + bilateral CCND + lateral neck dissection if N1b |

### Surgical Considerations in Young Children

- Surgery should be performed by a **high-volume thyroid surgeon** experienced in paediatric thyroidectomy
- Parathyroid preservation is paramount -- autotransplantation of at least one gland is recommended
- The recurrent laryngeal nerve is more vulnerable in small children (thinner, closer to surgical field)
- Intraoperative nerve monitoring (IONM) should be used when available

## RET Testing Methodology

### Genetic Testing

- **DNA sequencing** of RET exons 10, 11, 13, 14, 15, and 16 is standard
- Next-generation sequencing (NGS) panels increasingly used, allowing detection of rare/novel mutations
- **Indications for germline RET testing:**
  - All patients with apparent sporadic MTC (to exclude hereditary disease)
  - All first-degree relatives of known RET mutation carriers
  - All MEN2 syndrome patients
  - Patients with bilateral phaeochromocytoma
  - Patients with C-cell hyperplasia

### Somatic RET Testing

- Performed on tumour tissue (FFPE or fresh frozen)
- Identifies somatic RET point mutations (for prognostication and targeted therapy)
- Identifies RET fusions in PTC and other tumours (for targeted therapy eligibility)

### Cascade Screening

- Once a RET mutation is identified in an index case, all first-degree relatives must be tested
- **At-risk children** should be tested as early as possible (ideally in the first year of life for highest-risk mutations)
- Negative test result (no mutation identified in a family with known mutation) eliminates the need for prophylactic thyroidectomy and ongoing surveillance
- RET-negative family members can be reassured and discharged from screening

## Genetic Counselling

Key elements of genetic counselling for RET mutation carriers:

1. **Autosomal dominant inheritance** with ~100% penetrance for MTC in most mutations
2. **50% risk** of transmitting the mutation to each offspring
3. **Variable expressivity** -- even within the same family, the age of MTC onset and involvement of other organs may vary
4. **De novo mutations** account for ~5--10% of MEN2A and ~50% of MEN2B cases
5. **Prenatal/preimplantation genetic testing** is available and should be discussed
6. **Psychosocial support** -- prophylactic thyroidectomy in young children requires extensive counselling of parents regarding the rationale, timing, risks, and lifelong implications

## RET-Directed Targeted Therapy

### Background

For patients with advanced, unresectable, or metastatic MTC (or RAI-refractory RET-fusion DTC), conventional chemotherapy has limited efficacy. The development of selective RET inhibitors has revolutionised treatment.

### Selpercatinib (LOXO-292 / Retevmo)

- **Mechanism:** Highly selective, ATP-competitive small-molecule inhibitor of RET kinase
- **Selectivity:** Designed to inhibit RET with minimal activity against VEGFR2 and other kinases (reducing off-target toxicity)
- **LIBRETTO-001 Trial (Wirth 2020):** Phase 1--2 trial

| Population | ORR | Median DOR | Key Results |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Previously treated MTC** (n=55) | 69% | Not reached at median 14.1 months | 1-year PFS 82% |
| **Treatment-naive MTC** (n=88) | 73% | Not reached | Responses rapid and durable |
| **RET-fusion thyroid cancer** (n=19) | 79% | 18.4 months | Previously RAI-refractory |

- **Adverse effects:** Hypertension (21%), increased AST/ALT (12%), diarrhoea, dry mouth, fatigue
- **Approved:** FDA approved (May 2020) for advanced RET-mutant MTC and RET-fusion thyroid cancer in adults and children >=12 years

### Pralsetinib (BLU-667 / Gavreto)

- **Mechanism:** Selective RET kinase inhibitor similar to selpercatinib
- **ARROW Trial:** Phase 1--2 trial

| Population | ORR | Key Results |
|---|---|---|
| **Previously treated MTC** | 60% | Durable responses |
| **Treatment-naive MTC** | 71% | Responses regardless of specific RET mutation |
| **RET-fusion thyroid cancer** | 89% | High response rate in RAI-refractory disease |

- **Adverse effects:** Hypertension, neutropenia, anaemia, increased AST/ALT, constipation
- **Approved:** FDA approved (December 2020) for advanced RET-mutant MTC and RET-fusion thyroid cancer

### Comparison with Non-selective Kinase Inhibitors

| Feature | Selpercatinib/Pralsetinib | Vandetanib/Cabozantinib |
|---|---|---|
| RET selectivity | Highly selective | Multi-kinase (VEGFR, EGFR, RET) |
| Response rate in MTC | 60--73% | 30--45% |
| Off-target toxicity | Lower | Higher (diarrhoea, skin rash, QTc prolongation, hypertension, hand-foot syndrome) |
| Quality of life | Better tolerated | More side effects |
| RET V804 gatekeeper mutation | Active | Resistant |
| Approval status | Approved for RET-altered | Approved for all advanced MTC |

### Resistance Mechanisms

- **Acquired RET mutations:** Solvent front mutations (G810R/S/C), gatekeeper mutations (V804M/L to selpercatinib but not pralsetinib, and vice versa)
- **Bypass pathway activation:** RAS-MAPK, PI3K-AKT amplification
- **Sequential therapy:** Switching between selpercatinib and pralsetinib may overcome some resistance mutations
- Next-generation RET inhibitors are in development to address resistance

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **RET** stands for "Rearranged during Transfection" -- a transmembrane receptor tyrosine kinase on chromosome 10q11.2
2. **Gain-of-function** mutations cause MTC and MEN2; **loss-of-function** mutations cause Hirschsprung disease -- this bidirectional pathology is a favourite examination topic
3. **M918T** (MEN2B) is the most aggressive mutation -- prophylactic thyroidectomy within 6 months of life
4. **C634** (MEN2A) is the most common hereditary MTC mutation -- prophylactic thyroidectomy by age 5
5. **Always exclude phaeochromocytoma before thyroidectomy** in MEN2 -- failure to do so risks intraoperative hypertensive crisis and death
6. **All patients with apparently sporadic MTC** should undergo germline RET testing -- 5--10% will have occult hereditary disease
7. **LIBRETTO-001** established selpercatinib as the first selective RET inhibitor with practice-changing efficacy (ORR 69--73%)
8. **Calcitonin monitoring** can guide timing of prophylactic thyroidectomy in ATA-MOD patients -- rising calcitonin triggers surgery
9. **RET/PTC rearrangements** are found in PTC (not MTC) and are associated with younger patients and radiation-associated thyroid cancer
10. **Cascade screening** of first-degree relatives is mandatory once a germline RET mutation is identified -- this is standard of care
