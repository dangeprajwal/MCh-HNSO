## Introduction

Medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) arises from the parafollicular C-cells of the thyroid gland, which are derived from the neural crest via the ultimobranchial body. C-cells produce calcitonin, a polypeptide hormone that serves as the primary tumour marker for MTC. MTC accounts for approximately 1-2% of all new thyroid cancers (Randolph Ch26), though historically quoted as 3-5% before the surge in papillary thyroid cancer incidence. It is responsible for a disproportionate share of thyroid cancer-related deaths (up to 13%). It occurs in sporadic form (75%) and hereditary form (25%), the latter as part of MEN2A, MEN2B, or familial MTC (now classified as a MEN2A variant per ATA 2015). Randolph emphasises that MTC is classified as thyroid cancer only because of its location; in truth, it is a neuroendocrine tumour, fundamentally different from follicular cell-derived cancers. Unlike differentiated thyroid cancer (papillary and follicular), MTC does not concentrate radioiodine and is not TSH-dependent, making it fundamentally different in its management.

## Pathology and Origin

### C-Cell Biology

- **Origin:** Neural crest cells migrate to the ultimobranchial body and then to the thyroid gland, predominantly in the upper and middle poles of each thyroid lobe (Randolph Ch26)
- **Function:** Produce calcitonin (involved in calcium homeostasis), and also produce CEA, chromogranin A, somatostatin, ACTH, VIP, serotonin, calcitonin gene-related peptide, histaminase, neuron-specific enolase, substance P, gastrin-related peptide (Randolph Ch26)
- **Distribution:** C-cells are concentrated at the junction of the upper one-third and lower two-thirds of each thyroid lobe — this explains why MTC typically arises in this location. In non-cancer specimens, C-cells normally exist in clusters of 6-8 cells at the periphery of thyroid follicles and constitute only 1% of thyroid cells (Randolph Ch26)

### Histopathological Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| **Gross appearance** | Well-demarcated, firm, grey-white, gritty tumour (Randolph Ch26) |
| Cell pattern | Uniform round, polygonal, or spindle-shaped cells with finely granular eosinophilic cytoplasm and central nuclei; sheets, nests with peripheral palisading in a vascular stroma (Randolph Ch26) |
| **Histologic variants** | Classic (48.9%), amyloid-rich (38.3%), insular, trabecular, epithelial (Randolph Ch26) |
| Stroma | Amyloid deposition (Congo red positive, apple-green birefringence under polarised light) — present in 50-80% of cases. Amyloid arises from calcitonin/procalcitonin, a different precursor than other amyloid-rich tumours (Randolph Ch26) |
| Immunohistochemistry | Calcitonin (+), chromogranin A (+), synaptophysin (+), CEA (+), TTF-1 (+), CK7/CK18 (+) |
| **Negative markers** | Thyroglobulin NEGATIVE — critical distinction from DTC |
| C-cell hyperplasia | Precursor lesion in hereditary MTC; >50 C-cells per low-power field or clusters of >6 C-cells. Dralle (Randolph Ch27) describes a stochastic sequence from C-cell hyperplasia to MTC requiring acquisition of somatic mutations ("second hits") in RET or other genes |
| Grading | Low-grade vs high-grade based on mitotic rate and Ki-67 (ATA 2015 introduced grading) |

### Sporadic vs Hereditary MTC

| Feature | Sporadic (75%) | Hereditary (25%) |
|---|---|---|
| Bilateral/Multifocal | 32% (Randolph Ch26) | >90% (Randolph Ch26); 65% multifocal (Dralle, Ch27) |
| Associated C-cell hyperplasia | Rare | Always present — precursor to malignant transformation |
| Age at diagnosis | 4th-6th decade (Randolph Ch26) | Variable (depends on mutation — infancy to adulthood) |
| Somatic mutations | RET M918T (40-60%; 60-80% of those with somatic RET), RAS (15-25%) | Germline RET mutations (95%) |
| Family history | Absent (but 1-7% harbour occult germline RET mutations — Randolph Ch26) | Present (but >90% de novo in MEN2B — Dralle, Ch27) |
| Behaviour | Tends to be more aggressive with more frequent nodal mets (Randolph Ch26) | MEN2A better than sporadic stage-for-stage; MEN2B worst |
| **Somatic M918T impact** | More advanced stage, higher recurrence, poorer 10-20 year survival (Randolph Ch26) | Strongest germline mutation (ATA HST category) |

### C-Cell Hyperplasia to MTC: The Malignant Progression Model (Randolph Ch27 — Dralle)

The transition from C-cell hyperplasia to invasive MTC follows a genotype-dependent, age-related sequence:
- **Highest-risk (ATA HST — codon 918, MEN2B):** MTC may be present at birth; window of surgical cure closes by age 4 years
- **High-risk (ATA H — codon 634, classic MEN2A):** MTC may develop as early as age 1 year
- **Moderate-risk (ATA MOD — codons 609, 611, 618, 620):** MTC as early as age 5 years
- **Low-to-moderate risk (ATA MOD — codons 768, 790, 804, 891):** MTC as early as age 10 years
- Annual primary tumour growth: 0.4-0.5 mm/year in node-negative carriers; 1.2 mm (MOD mutations) and 2.6 mm (H mutations) in node-positive carriers (Randolph Ch27)

## Diagnostic Workup

### Serum Calcitonin

Calcitonin is the single most important tumour marker in MTC. Randolph (Ch26) notes that improvements in accuracy of measuring basal calcitonin levels have made stimulated testing largely unnecessary in current practice:

| Test | Details |
|---|---|
| Basal calcitonin | Normal <10 pg/mL (Randolph reference range); >100 pg/mL highly suggestive of MTC |
| **Neonatal/infant calcitonin** | Mean 9.81 pg/mL (range 2-49) in 1st year of life; 4.56 pg/mL (range 2-15) in 2nd year — higher than adults, important when evaluating gene carriers (Dralle, Ch27) |
| Pre-operative level | Correlates with tumour burden and nodal metastasis. 70% of patients with palpable MTC also have cervical mets; 10% have distant mets (Randolph Ch26) |
| **Calcitonin 20-50 pg/mL** | Some literature suggests ipsilateral lateral neck dissection at this threshold (Randolph Ch26, ATA Task Force) |
| Calcitonin >200 pg/mL | High probability of contralateral central and bilateral lateral nodal metastases; contralateral neck dissection should be considered (Randolph Ch26/27) |
| Calcitonin >500 pg/mL | Likely distant metastases — triggers full metastatic workup including liver MRI, bone scan, axial MRI (Randolph Ch26) |
| **Calcitonin >1000 pg/mL** | Reoperation unlikely to achieve biochemical cure if no localisable disease (Randolph Ch27) |
| **Calcitonin >10,000 pg/mL** | Biochemical cure not achievable even with bilateral compartment-oriented surgery (Dralle, Ch27) |
| Stimulated calcitonin | Pentagastrin (0.5 mcg/kg IV) or calcium gluconate (2 mg/kg over 1 min) stimulation — calcitonin >100 pg/mL after stimulation is diagnostic. Pentagastrin causes nausea, diaphoresis, agitation; now largely replaced by improved basal assays (Randolph Ch26) |
| False positives | Chronic kidney disease, proton pump inhibitors, other neuroendocrine tumours, reactive (non-neoplastic) C-cell hyperplasia, small cell lung cancer |

### CEA (Carcinoembryonic Antigen)

- Produced by MTC cells; has a longer half-life than calcitonin and is less specific (Randolph Ch26)
- Rapidly rising CEA with stable or falling calcitonin suggests tumour dedifferentiation — poorer prognosis
- **CEA doubling time** should be measured concurrently with calcitonin doubling time; both should be tracked at least every 6 months with a minimum of 4 data points over 2 years for reliable estimation (Randolph Ch26, ATA 2015)
- Used in combination with calcitonin for follow-up

### RET Mutation Testing

- **Mandatory in ALL MTC patients** regardless of family history or apparent sporadic presentation
- Identifies hereditary disease in 1-7% of apparently sporadic cases (Randolph Ch26); Dralle cites 6.5-9.5% (Randolph Ch27)
- Determines need for cascade family screening — starting with first-degree relatives
- Guides therapeutic decisions (RET-selective inhibitors)
- **Somatic RET testing** of tumour tissue: growing evidence of clinical utility for predicting TKI responsiveness (Randolph Ch26). Somatic RET mutations/rearrangements found in 40-50% of sporadic MTC; of these, 60-80% are M918T. Not all cells within a tumour may harbour the mutation (tumour heterogeneity)
- **Genotype-phenotype correlation (Randolph Ch27):** RET codon determines MEN2 phenotype — codon 634 is the classic "MEN2A codon" (ATA H); codon 918 is the MEN2B codon (ATA HST); codons 609/611/618/620 are moderate risk (ATA MOD)

### Imaging Workup

| Modality | Role | Details |
|---|---|---|
| Neck ultrasound | Primary assessment | Thyroid mass, bilateral assessment, cervical lymph node mapping. Calcitonin screening is superior to ultrasonography in predicting MTC (Randolph Ch27) |
| CT neck/chest | Staging | Assess central and lateral compartments, mediastinal nodes, pulmonary mets |
| **Liver imaging** | Hepatic metastases | Three-phase contrast-enhanced multidetector liver CT or contrast-enhanced MRI (Randolph Ch26 specifies both options); MTC has predilection for liver; micrometastases often missed on standard CT |
| Bone scintigraphy + axial MRI | Bone metastases | MRI of pelvis and axial skeleton recommended in addition to bone scan (Randolph Ch26) |
| FDG-PET/CT | Advanced/recurrent | Less sensitive in detecting MTC metastases; should NOT be used during initial workup (Randolph Ch26). Useful when calcitonin >500 pg/mL |
| **18F-DOPA PET/CT** | Superior for MTC | Higher sensitivity than FDG-PET for MTC detection, especially for occult recurrence. Better than conventional imaging for detecting recurrent metastatic MTC (Randolph Ch27) |

**When to obtain full metastatic imaging (Randolph Ch26):** Patients with extensive neck disease, signs/symptoms of regional or distant metastases, or serum calcitonin >500 pg/mL should have CT neck/chest, liver CT or MRI, bone scintigraphy, and axial MRI.

## Staging — AJCC 8th Edition

### T Classification (MTC-specific)

| Stage | Criteria |
|---|---|
| T1 | <=2 cm, limited to the thyroid (T1a <=1 cm; T1b >1-2 cm — Randolph Ch26) |
| T2 | >2-4 cm, limited to the thyroid |
| T3 | >4 cm, limited to the thyroid, or with extrathyroidal extension |
| T4a | Moderately advanced — invades subcutaneous soft tissue, larynx, trachea, oesophagus, recurrent laryngeal nerve |
| T4b | Very advanced — invades prevertebral fascia, encases carotid artery, mediastinal vessels |

### N Classification

| Stage | Criteria |
|---|---|
| N0 | No regional lymph node metastasis (N0a: cytologically/histologically confirmed benign nodes; N0b: no radiologic/clinical evidence — Randolph Ch26) |
| N1a | Level VI or VII (pretracheal, paratracheal, prelaryngeal/Delphian, upper mediastinal) nodes |
| N1b | Unilateral, bilateral, or contralateral lateral neck (levels I-V) or retropharyngeal or superior mediastinal nodes |

### Nodal Metastasis Rate by T Stage (Randolph Ch26)

| T Stage | Central compartment mets | Lateral compartment mets |
|---|---|---|
| T1 | 14% | 11% |
| T4 | 86% | 93% |

### Stage Grouping for MTC

| Stage | T | N | M |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | T1 | N0 | M0 |
| II | T2-T3 | N0 | M0 |
| III | T1-T3 | N1a | M0 |
| IVA | T4a or N1b | Any N/Any T | M0 |
| IVB | T4b | Any N | M0 |
| IVC | Any T | Any N | M1 |

### Prognostic Factors

Key findings from the GETC study (Modigliani 1998) and survival data from Randolph Ch26:

| Factor | Prognostic Impact |
|---|---|
| Age at diagnosis | <40 years: better prognosis |
| Sex | Female sex: slightly better prognosis |
| Stage at presentation | Most important prognostic factor |
| **10-year survival by stage** | Stage I: 100%, Stage II: 93%, Stage III: 71%, Stage IV: 21% (Randolph Ch26) |
| **Overall 10-year survival** | 69-89% across series (Randolph Ch26) |
| Completeness of initial surgery | Complete tumour resection: strongest modifiable prognostic factor |
| Post-operative calcitonin | Undetectable = biochemical cure; 97.7% 10-year survival (Randolph Ch26) |
| Calcitonin doubling time | <6 months: 5-yr 25%, 10-yr 8%; 6-24 months: 5-yr 92%, 10-yr 37%; >24 months: all alive at end of study (up to 29 years follow-up) — Randolph Ch26 |
| CEA level and doubling time | Rising CEA with dedifferentiation: poor prognosis |
| **Somatic M918T mutation** | More advanced stage at diagnosis, higher recurrence, poorer long-term (10-20 yr) survival (Randolph Ch26) |
| Hereditary vs sporadic | No difference in biochemical cure and survival after adjusting for extent of disease (Dralle, Ch27); MEN2B worst overall |
| **Lymph node metastases** | Indicative of progressive disease; portend worse prognosis for sporadic and hereditary MTC alike (Randolph Ch27) |

### AJCC 8th Edition Recommended Parameters to Record (Randolph Ch26)

- Number of involved lymph nodes and size of involved nodes with measurement of metastatic focus
- Completeness of resection
- Pre-operative and post-operative calcitonin and CEA
- Genetic mutation analysis

## Surgical Management

### Standard Surgery for MTC

**Total thyroidectomy is mandatory** — even for apparently unilateral sporadic MTC — because (Randolph Ch26/27):
- High rate of bilateral C-cell hyperplasia (occult contralateral foci)
- Multifocality in 32% of sporadic (Randolph Ch26) and >90% of hereditary cases
- Facilitates calcitonin monitoring postoperatively (no residual C-cells)
- Every single parafollicular C-cell has the potential for malignant progression; only total thyroidectomy reliably eliminates this risk (Dralle, Ch27)
- MTC cells do not take up radioiodine; hormone suppression is ineffective — surgery is the only curative treatment

**Completion thyroidectomy** (if hemithyroidectomy was performed without preoperative MTC diagnosis): recommended per ATA 2015/Randolph for patients with germline RET mutation, elevated postoperative calcitonin, residual MTC on imaging, C-cell hyperplasia, multicentric disease, positive margins, or extrathyroidal extension (Randolph Ch26).

### Neck Dissection — Extent (Incorporating ATA 2015 per Randolph Ch26/27)

| Scenario | Minimum Surgery | Additional |
|---|---|---|
| Clinically node-negative, no distant mets on US | TT + central compartment (level VI) dissection | Ipsilateral lateral ND (II-V) "may be considered" (ATA 2015) |
| **Calcitonin 20-50 pg/mL, clinical N0** | TT + CCND | Consider ipsilateral lateral neck dissection (Randolph Ch26) |
| Calcitonin >200 pg/mL, ipsilateral lateral N+ but contralateral N0 | TT + bilateral CCND + ipsilateral lateral ND | Contralateral lateral ND should be performed (Randolph Ch26/27) |
| Calcitonin >500 or clinically N+ bilateral | TT + bilateral CCND + bilateral lateral ND | Full metastatic workup before surgery |
| Hereditary MTC (MEN2A/2B) | TT + bilateral CCND (minimum) | Lateral ND based on calcitonin/imaging; most clinically apparent hereditary MTC patients harbour bilateral nodal mets requiring bilateral compartment-oriented microdissection (Dralle, Ch27) |
| **Hereditary MTC — asymptomatic gene carrier with normal basal calcitonin** | TT alone (no lymph node dissection needed) | Lymph node mets found only with increased basal calcitonin typically >30 pg/mL; carriers with normal calcitonin may safely forego LND (Dralle, Ch27) |

### Rationale for Bilateral Central Compartment Neck Dissection

- MTC metastasises early and predictably to central compartment nodes
- Occult central node metastases present in 50-75% of palpable MTC; even T1 tumours have 14% central compartment mets (Randolph Ch26)
- Central compartment reoperative surgery carries significantly higher morbidity (recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, hypoparathyroidism)
- Therefore, comprehensive initial surgery is critical
- Compartment-directed therapy is stressed — avoidance of limited "berry-picking" procedures (Randolph Ch26, ATA 2015)

### Palliative Intent Surgery for Advanced MTC (Randolph Ch26)

When patients present with advanced MTC:
- Surgery may be palliative — minimise complications and prevent morbidity of locoregional progression
- In metastatic disease, hemithyroidectomy may be considered to prevent local invasion while avoiding contralateral RLN injury and hypoparathyroidism
- In locally advanced disease invading trachea/larynx/oesophagus: consider life expectancy, focus on preserving speech, swallowing, parathyroid, and shoulder function
- However, Randolph Ch26 authors believe there is still a role for radical extirpative surgery (laryngectomy, oesophagectomy, laryngopharyngectomy) in locally advanced MTC without extensive distant metastatic disease

## Tisell's Microdissection Technique

### Concept

Tisell (from Gothenburg, Sweden) pioneered a compartment-oriented microdissection technique specifically for MTC neck dissection. The technique emphasises systematic removal of all fibrofatty tissue from anatomically defined compartments, without necessarily removing non-lymphatic structures. Dralle (Randolph Ch27) describes this as "compartment-oriented microdissection" — the standard approach for hereditary MTC in the German/Austrian surgical tradition, used to remove tumour deposits from the vicinity of vital neck structures.

### Principles

1. **Compartment-oriented dissection** — not berry-picking of individual nodes
2. **Systematic removal of fibrofatty tissue** from defined anatomical boundaries
3. **Preservation of vital structures** — RLN, parathyroid glands (with autotransplantation if devascularised)
4. **Bilateral central dissection as standard** — because of bilateral C-cell disease in hereditary and high bilaterality in sporadic MTC
5. **In situ preservation of normal parathyroids** — standard practice in hereditary MTC surgery; autografting only if completely devascularised (Dralle, Ch27)

### Central Compartment Microdissection (Level VI)

| Boundary | Landmark |
|---|---|
| Superior | Hyoid bone |
| Inferior | Innominate artery (right) / thoracic inlet |
| Lateral (bilateral) | Carotid sheaths |
| Anterior | Strap muscles |
| Posterior | Prevertebral fascia |

Steps:
1. Identify and preserve both recurrent laryngeal nerves throughout their course — use of intraoperative neural integrity monitor (IONM) recommended, especially in reoperative cases (Randolph Ch26)
2. Identify all four parathyroid glands
3. Remove all fibrofatty tissue between the carotid sheaths, from hyoid to innominate artery
4. Include pretracheal, paratracheal, and prelaryngeal (Delphian) nodes
5. Remove thymus horn and upper mediastinal tissue (level VII)
6. Devascularised parathyroids: autotransplant into sternocleidomastoid or forearm. Normal parathyroids should be preserved in situ whenever feasible (Dralle, Ch27)

### Lateral Compartment Microdissection (Levels II-V)

| Boundary | Landmark |
|---|---|
| Superior | Skull base / posterior belly of digastric |
| Inferior | Clavicle |
| Anterior | Strap muscles / sternohyoid |
| Posterior | Anterior border of trapezius |
| Medial | Carotid artery |
| Lateral | Posterior triangle |

Steps:
1. Systematic removal of fibrofatty tissue from levels IIA, IIB, III, IV, VA, VB
2. Preserve spinal accessory nerve, internal jugular vein (if not involved), phrenic nerve, brachial plexus
3. Include nodes along the recurrent laryngeal nerve chain
4. Remove tissue from the transverse cervical and supraclavicular regions

### Advantages of Tisell's Technique

- Higher biochemical cure rates than selective node-picking
- Reproducible and systematic
- Allows identification of occult micrometastases within fibrofatty tissue
- Compartment clearance reduces risk of reoperative surgery
- Can aid in preventing tumour-related complications and supporting systemic targeted therapies (Dralle, Ch27)
- Bilateral compartment-oriented surgery can achieve biochemical cure in at least 50% of patients with pretherapeutic basal calcitonin <=1000 pg/mL (Dralle, Ch27)

### Complications of Compartment-Oriented Surgery (Randolph Ch27 — Dralle)

From a large series of 167 children undergoing prophylactic thyroidectomy:
- Transient hypoparathyroidism: more frequent in older children (32% oldest group vs 3% youngest)
- Transient RLN palsy: occurred only in children who had central node dissection (all resolved within 6 months)
- Without CND: transient hypoparathyroidism 4%, permanent 0% (age <=3 years)
- With CND: permanent hypoparathyroidism 0% in experienced hands (149 children, mean age 6 years)
- Postoperative calcitonin normalisation achieved in 99.1% (114/115 children with raised preoperative values)

## Adjuvant Treatment

### External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT)

| Indication | Details |
|---|---|
| Positive margins (R1/R2) | If re-excision not feasible |
| Extensive extrathyroidal extension | T4a/T4b disease |
| Extensive nodal disease | Multiple positive nodes, extranodal extension |
| Rising calcitonin without structural disease | Controversial — consider empiric neck/mediastinal EBRT |
| Dose | 60-66 Gy in 30-33 fractions; gross residual disease 70 Gy or higher (Randolph Ch26); IMRT preferred, especially adjacent to spinal cord |
| Field | Thyroid bed, bilateral central and lateral neck, upper mediastinum |
| **SEER data (Randolph Ch26)** | No overall survival benefit with adjuvant EBRT in node-positive MTC; but locoregional control is a valid endpoint |
| **Caveat** | EBRT causes radiation-induced scarring/fibrosis making subsequent surgery significantly riskier — ensure patient is not a candidate for reoperation before initiating (Randolph Ch26) |

**In one cohort of 34 high-risk patients (Randolph Ch26):** 5-year locoregional relapse-free survival was 87%, disease-specific and overall survival 62% and 56%, respectively.

### No Role for RAI

- MTC arises from C-cells, NOT follicular cells
- C-cells do not express the sodium-iodide symporter (NIS) — confirmed by Dralle (Randolph Ch27): "C-cells do not express the sodium/iodine symporter and therefore do not concentrate radioiodine, rendering radioiodine ablation futile for treatment of MTC"
- RAI has NO role in MTC management

### No Role for TSH Suppression

- C-cells do not express TSH receptors (unlike follicular-derived DTC)
- TSH suppression does not benefit MTC patients
- Thyroid hormone replacement to maintain euthyroid state (not suppressive doses)

## Follow-Up: Hypercalcitoninemia

### Post-Operative Calcitonin Assessment

Calcitonin levels drop slowly after surgery (Randolph Ch26). ATA 2015 guidelines (per Randolph) recommend measuring serum calcitonin and CEA at 3 months postoperatively:

| Calcitonin Status | Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Undetectable (<2 pg/mL) | Biochemical cure — 97.7% 10-year survival (Randolph Ch26) | If normal: repeat every 6 months for 1 year, then annually |
| Detectable but <150 pg/mL | Residual microscopic disease or circulating half-life | Physical exam + neck US; repeat every 6 months with calcitonin/CEA |
| >150 pg/mL, stable | Persistent disease, likely nodal or micrometastatic | Neck US, chest CT, liver CT or MRI, bone scan, axial MRI (Randolph Ch26) |
| >150 pg/mL, rising | Progressive disease | Intensive imaging and consider intervention |
| **Borderline elevated, stable** | Many patients do well clinically for years; follow-up should not be overly aggressive (Randolph Ch26) | Surveillance with calcitonin/CEA doubling time monitoring |

### Calcitonin Doubling Time (CDT)

The most important prognostic variable in follow-up. Randolph Ch26 cites the following survival data:

| CDT | 5-Year Survival | 10-Year Survival | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| <6 months | 25% | 8% | Active treatment — consider systemic therapy |
| 6-24 months | 92% | 37% | Close surveillance, consider local interventions |
| >24 months | All alive | All alive (up to 29 yr follow-up) | Observation with annual imaging |

**ATA/Randolph recommendations for CDT measurement:**
- Measure calcitonin AND CEA at least every 6 months to determine doubling times
- Reliable estimates require minimum 4 data points over minimum 2 years
- However, doubling times <6 months can be reliably estimated within first 12 months after surgery
- ATA provides a free online calculator for doubling times (Randolph Ch26)

### CEA Doubling Time (Randolph Ch26)

- Should be measured in parallel with calcitonin doubling time
- CEA doubling time has independent prognostic value
- Discordance pattern: rising CEA with stable/falling calcitonin indicates dedifferentiation — worst prognosis
- Systemic therapy should NOT be given to patients with increasing calcitonin and CEA but no evidence of metastatic disease on imaging (Randolph Ch26)
- Even with low-volume metastatic disease on imaging, systemic therapy should be withheld if disease is stable and calcitonin/CEA doubling times are >2 years (Randolph Ch26)

### Imaging for Persistent/Recurrent Hypercalcitoninemia

| Calcitonin Level | Recommended Imaging |
|---|---|
| <150 pg/mL | Neck US every 6-12 months |
| 150-500 pg/mL | Neck US, CT neck/chest, liver MRI |
| >500 pg/mL | CT neck/chest/abdomen, bone scan, axial MRI, consider 18F-DOPA PET/CT |
| Rising with negative conventional imaging | 18F-DOPA PET/CT (most sensitive — Randolph Ch27), or 68Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT |

### Revision Surgery for Persistent Disease

- Compartment-oriented revision neck dissection if localisable structural disease
- Higher morbidity than primary surgery (scarring, distorted anatomy; risks include thoracic duct leak, RLN injury, hypoparathyroidism — Randolph Ch26)
- **Finding the RLN in revision surgery:** aided by IONM and by finding the nerve in previously undissected regions (Randolph Ch26)
- Success rate for biochemical cure after reoperation: approximately one-third of patients achieve calcitonin normalisation (Randolph Ch26)
- **Dralle (Randolph Ch27) thresholds for curative reoperation:** biochemical cure possible if <=5 lymph node metastases at initial operation AND pre-reoperation calcitonin <=1000 pg/mL. Above these thresholds, strategy shifts to locoregional tumour control
- Patient selection is critical — surgery unlikely to benefit if calcitonin >1000 pg/mL and no localisable disease
- Patients with elevated calcitonin but no radiographic disease should NOT undergo elective exploration — minimal chance of biochemical remission (Randolph Ch26)
- **"Pacemaker of disease" concept (Dralle, Ch27):** In carriers with gross recurrent disease, eradicate the locally progressive disease as the "pacemaker" from the neck, mediastinum, or distant site if reasonably feasible

## Management of Metastatic MTC

### Sites of Distant Metastasis

| Site | Frequency | Features |
|---|---|---|
| Lungs | 33% | Miliary nodules, often bilateral; solitary lung mets considered for surgical resection; smaller peripheral mets may be treated with RF ablation (Randolph Ch26) |
| Liver | 25-45% | Often micrometastatic, best detected by three-phase contrast-enhanced CT or MRI; hemoembolisation if multiple small tumours involve <1/3 of liver (Randolph Ch26) |
| Bone | 15-20% | Osteoblastic and osteolytic, axial skeleton; fractures/impending fractures treated with surgery, thermoablation (RF or cryo), EBRT, or cement injection (Randolph Ch26) |
| Brain | Rare | Late manifestation; isolated brain mets: surgical resection or stereotactic radiosurgery; multiple: whole brain EBRT (Randolph Ch26) |
| **Cutaneous** | Uncommon | Surgical resection for isolated; EBRT or ethanol injection for multiple cutaneous mets (Randolph Ch26) |

### Management of Diarrhea and Secretory Complications (Randolph Ch26)

- Diarrhea in advanced MTC due to hypersecretory state from calcitonin and enhanced GI motility; more common with hepatic mets
- Avoid alcohol and high-fibre foods; symptomatic relief with loperamide
- Somatostatin analogue therapy; chemoembolisation for liver mets with diarrhea
- **Ectopic ACTH / Cushing syndrome:** usually a marker of very advanced disease; poor prognosis; treatment: medical therapy or bilateral adrenalectomy (Randolph Ch26)

### Systemic Therapy

#### When NOT to Start Systemic Therapy (Randolph Ch26)

- Rising calcitonin/CEA with no evidence of metastatic disease on imaging
- Low-volume metastatic disease that is stable on imaging with calcitonin/CEA doubling times >2 years
- Therapy reserved for significant tumour burden with symptomatic or progressive disease per RECIST criteria

#### First-Generation MKIs

| Agent | Trial | PFS (drug vs placebo) | ORR | Key Targets | Notes (Randolph Ch26) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vandetanib | ZETA (Wells 2012) | 30.5 vs 19.3 months | 45% | RET, VEGFR2, EGFR | Duration of response 22 months; responders included RET-mutant and wild-type; FDA/EMA approved 2011-2012 |
| Cabozantinib | EXAM (Elisei 2013) | 11.2 vs 4.0 months | 28% | MET, VEGFR2, RET | 16% discontinued for toxicity; 79% required dose reduction; subgroup with RET M918T had significant OS benefit (Randolph Ch26) |

**Unresolved issues (Randolph Ch26):** Optimal dosing, toxicity management algorithms, resistance development (almost all patients develop resistance), timing of initiation, when to stop in stable/mixed response, limited long-term toxicity and OS data. Patients on these drugs are at significant risk for hypothyroidism.

#### RET-Selective Inhibitors (Second-Generation)

| Agent | Trial | ORR (previously treated) | ORR (treatment-naive) | Key Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Selpercatinib | LIBRETTO-001 (Wirth 2020) | 69% | 73% | Highly selective; fewer off-target toxicities |
| Pralsetinib | ARROW | 60% | 71% | Similar selectivity; once daily dosing |

#### Treatment Sequencing

1. **Indolent, asymptomatic metastatic disease:** Active surveillance with CDT monitoring; do NOT start systemic therapy (Randolph Ch26)
2. **Progressive, symptomatic, or high tumour burden:** Initiate systemic therapy
3. **RET-mutant MTC (germline or somatic):** Selpercatinib or pralsetinib preferred (emerging as first-line)
4. **RET wild-type or prior RET-inhibitor progression:** Vandetanib or cabozantinib
5. **Subsequent lines:** Switch MKI class; consider clinical trials
6. **RAS-mutant MTC:** MEK inhibitor-based regimens under investigation
7. **Cytotoxic chemotherapy:** Doxorubicin + 5-FU or dacarbazine — only in select settings, not first-line given TKI effectiveness (Randolph Ch26)

### Palliative Interventions

- EBRT for painful bone metastases or airway compromise; spinal cord compression requires urgent steroids + surgical decompression (Randolph Ch26)
- Hepatic arterial embolisation/chemoembolisation for symptomatic liver metastases
- Bone-modifying agents (bisphosphonates, denosumab) for skeletal metastases
- Peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT) for somatostatin receptor-positive disease

## Special Situations

### Calcitonin Screening for Thyroid Nodules

- Routine measurement of serum calcitonin in patients with thyroid nodules is performed in European centres
- MTC present in only 0.3-1.4% of patients with thyroid nodules (Randolph Ch26)
- Detects unsuspected MTC — FNAC has false-negative rate of 30-50% for MTC
- **FNA washout calcitonin** and IHC staining for calcitonin, chromogranin, CEA (with absence of thyroglobulin) recommended for inconclusive FNA suggestive of MTC (Randolph Ch26)
- Cost-effectiveness debated; ATA 2015 (per Randolph) suggests physicians decide on a case-by-case basis
- If calcitonin >100 pg/mL with thyroid nodule: high suspicion for MTC

### Sporadic MTC Microcarcinoma (SmMTC — Randolph Ch26)

- There may be greater consensus for less aggressive lateral neck management in SmMTC
- Some surgeons/endocrinologists believe high-quality US showing no suspicious nodes negates the need for elective lateral neck dissection in this setting

### MEN2B Special Considerations (Randolph Ch27 — Dralle)

- >90% harbour de novo germline RET mutations — family screening rarely possible
- MTC present in virtually every MEN2B patient; frequently advanced at diagnosis
- Window of surgical cure: only before age 4 years; most patients diagnosed after this are not curable
- **Early clues in infancy:** "tearless crying" (reduced tear production), pseudo-Hirschsprung's disease (intestinal ganglioneuromatosis with chronic constipation), failure to thrive, suckling weakness
- **Non-endocrine stigmata:** oral mucosal neuromas (lips, tongue, buccal mucosa), thickened corneal nerves (visible on slit-lamp), entropium, marfanoid habitus, hyperflexible joints, pes cavus, scoliosis
- **Thickened recurrent laryngeal nerves** are a recognized finding in MEN2B (Randolph Ch27)
- Truly "prophylactic" surgery is exceptional due to very early disease onset
- Compartment-oriented surgery should be performed as early in life as possible but may also improve survival in late-onset patients (Dralle, Ch27)

### MEN2A and Synchronous PCC/HPT (Randolph Ch27)

- **PCC present in 30-50% of carriers;** synchronous with MTC in 60% (codon 918) and 35% (codon 634)
- **Surgical sequence:** Adrenalectomy first (with alpha-blockade), then thyroidectomy — either one-stage (limited neck disease) or two-stage (advanced neck disease)
- Subtotal "tissue-sparing" adrenalectomy preferred to minimise steroid dependency
- **HPT in MEN2A:** ~20-30% prevalence (codon 634); usually mild; remove only grossly enlarged parathyroids (limited parathyroidectomy), NOT subtotal/total parathyroidectomy with autografting (carries 6% risk of permanent hypoparathyroidism — Dralle, Ch27)

### MTC and Ectopic ACTH (Cushing Syndrome)

- MTC can produce ACTH, causing ectopic Cushing syndrome
- Occurs in 2-5% of advanced MTC — usually marker of very advanced disease (Randolph Ch26)
- Poor prognostic sign — indicates dedifferentiation
- Management: treat MTC + bilateral adrenalectomy or medical adrenal blockade (ketoconazole, metyrapone)

### MTC in Pregnancy

- Defer surgery to second trimester if possible
- Screen for and manage phaeochromocytoma first
- Calcitonin crosses placenta minimally — does not harm foetus
- RET testing of offspring at birth if hereditary

## Exam-Relevant Key Points

> [!tip] High-Yield Points
> - MTC arises from parafollicular C-cells (neural crest origin) and produces calcitonin — thyroglobulin is NEGATIVE
> - RAI has NO role in MTC management — C-cells lack NIS (Randolph: "radioiodine ablation is futile for MTC")
> - TSH suppression is NOT indicated — C-cells lack TSH receptors
> - Total thyroidectomy + bilateral CCND is the minimum surgery for MTC
> - Calcitonin >200 pg/mL: consider bilateral lateral neck dissection; >500 pg/mL: likely distant mets; >1000 pg/mL: reoperation unlikely to cure; >10,000 pg/mL: biochemical cure impossible even with bilateral compartment surgery (Dralle)
> - Calcitonin doubling time is the single most important prognostic factor in follow-up: <6 months = 25% 5-yr survival, 8% 10-yr; >24 months = all alive up to 29 years (Randolph)
> - CEA doubling time should be measured in parallel; discordant rising CEA with falling calcitonin indicates dedifferentiation
> - Tisell's microdissection: compartment-oriented systematic removal of fibrofatty tissue from defined anatomical boundaries
> - Amyloid deposition in stroma is characteristic (Congo red positive with apple-green birefringence); arises from calcitonin/procalcitonin
> - RET mutation testing is mandatory in ALL MTC patients — 1-7% (up to 9.5% per Dralle) of "sporadic" cases are hereditary
> - Selpercatinib/pralsetinib (RET-selective inhibitors) represent precision oncology for RET-mutant MTC
> - Cabozantinib subgroup analysis: RET M918T mutation confers significant OS benefit (Randolph)
> - Systemic therapy should NOT be started for rising markers without structural disease or for stable low-volume mets with CDT >2 years
> - DOPA-PET/CT is the most sensitive imaging modality for occult MTC recurrence
> - The GETC study (Modigliani 1998) established that completeness of initial surgery is the strongest modifiable prognostic factor
> - 10-year survival by stage: I=100%, II=93%, III=71%, IV=21% (Randolph)
> - MEN2B: >90% de novo mutations; early clues = tearless crying, pseudo-Hirschsprung's; surgical cure window closes by age 4
> - Dralle's "pacemaker of disease" concept: eradicate locally progressive disease as the driver of recurrence
> - Reoperation: biochemical cure possible if <=5 initial nodal mets AND pre-reop calcitonin <=1000 pg/mL (Dralle)
