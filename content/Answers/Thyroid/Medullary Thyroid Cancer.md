## Introduction

Medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) arises from the parafollicular C-cells of the thyroid gland, which are derived from the neural crest via the ultimobranchial body. C-cells produce calcitonin, a polypeptide hormone that serves as the primary tumour marker for MTC. MTC accounts for approximately 3-5% of all thyroid cancers but is responsible for a disproportionate share of thyroid cancer-related deaths (up to 13%). It occurs in sporadic form (75%) and hereditary form (25%), the latter as part of MEN2A, MEN2B, or familial MTC (now classified as a MEN2A variant). Unlike differentiated thyroid cancer (papillary and follicular), MTC does not concentrate radioiodine and is not TSH-dependent, making it fundamentally different in its management.

## Pathology and Origin

### C-Cell Biology

- **Origin:** Neural crest cells migrate to the ultimobranchial body and then to the thyroid gland, predominantly in the upper two-thirds of the lateral lobes
- **Function:** Produce calcitonin (involved in calcium homeostasis), and also produce CEA, chromogranin A, somatostatin, ACTH, VIP, and serotonin
- **Distribution:** C-cells are concentrated at the junction of the upper one-third and lower two-thirds of each thyroid lobe — this explains why MTC typically arises in this location

### Histopathological Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell pattern | Sheets, nests, trabeculae of polygonal to spindle cells |
| Stroma | Amyloid deposition (Congo red positive, apple-green birefringence under polarised light) — present in 50-80% of cases |
| Immunohistochemistry | Calcitonin (+), chromogranin A (+), synaptophysin (+), CEA (+), TTF-1 (+) |
| **Negative markers** | Thyroglobulin NEGATIVE — critical distinction from DTC |
| C-cell hyperplasia | Precursor lesion in hereditary MTC; >50 C-cells per low-power field or clusters of >6 C-cells |
| Grading | Low-grade vs high-grade based on mitotic rate and Ki-67 (ATA 2015 introduced grading) |

### Sporadic vs Hereditary MTC

| Feature | Sporadic (75%) | Hereditary (25%) |
|---|---|---|
| Bilateral | 15-20% | >80% (near universal) |
| Multifocal | 15-20% | >80% |
| Associated C-cell hyperplasia | Rare | Always present |
| Age at diagnosis | 40-60 years | Variable (depends on mutation — infancy to adulthood) |
| Somatic mutations | RET M918T (40-60%), RAS (15-25%) | Germline RET mutations (95%) |
| Family history | Absent | Present (but 50% de novo in MEN2B) |
| Prognosis | Depends on stage | Better for MEN2A; worst for MEN2B |

## Diagnostic Workup

### Serum Calcitonin

Calcitonin is the single most important tumour marker in MTC:

| Test | Details |
|---|---|
| Basal calcitonin | Normal <10 pg/mL; >100 pg/mL highly suggestive of MTC |
| Pre-operative level | Correlates with tumour burden and nodal metastasis |
| Calcitonin >200 pg/mL | High probability of contralateral central and lateral nodal metastases |
| Calcitonin >500 pg/mL | Likely distant metastases |
| Stimulated calcitonin | Pentagastrin (0.5 mcg/kg IV) or calcium gluconate (2 mg/kg over 1 min) stimulation — calcitonin >100 pg/mL after stimulation is diagnostic |
| False positives | Chronic kidney disease, proton pump inhibitors, other neuroendocrine tumours, C-cell hyperplasia, small cell lung cancer |

### CEA (Carcinoembryonic Antigen)

- Produced by MTC cells; correlates with tumour dedifferentiation
- Rapidly rising CEA with stable or falling calcitonin suggests tumour dedifferentiation — poorer prognosis
- Used in combination with calcitonin for follow-up

### RET Mutation Testing

- **Mandatory in ALL MTC patients** regardless of family history or apparent sporadic presentation
- Identifies hereditary disease in 6-7% of apparently sporadic cases
- Determines need for cascade family screening
- Guides therapeutic decisions (RET-selective inhibitors)
- Somatic RET testing of tumour tissue also relevant for TKI selection

### Imaging Workup

| Modality | Role | Details |
|---|---|---|
| Neck ultrasound | Primary assessment | Thyroid mass, bilateral assessment, cervical lymph node mapping |
| CT neck/chest | Staging | Assess central and lateral compartments, mediastinal nodes, pulmonary mets |
| Liver MRI | Hepatic metastases | MTC has predilection for liver; micrometastases often missed on CT |
| Bone scintigraphy | Bone metastases | MTC is one of the bone-avid thyroid cancers |
| FDG-PET/CT | Advanced/recurrent | Useful when calcitonin >500 pg/mL; sensitivity increases with higher calcitonin |
| DOPA-PET/CT | Superior for MTC | Higher sensitivity than FDG-PET for MTC detection, especially for occult recurrence |

## Staging — AJCC 8th Edition

### T Classification (MTC-specific)

| Stage | Criteria |
|---|---|
| T1 | <=2 cm, limited to the thyroid |
| T2 | >2-4 cm, limited to the thyroid |
| T3a | >4 cm, limited to the thyroid |
| T3b | Any size with gross extrathyroidal extension into strap muscles |
| T4a | Moderately advanced — invades subcutaneous tissue, larynx, trachea, oesophagus, recurrent laryngeal nerve |
| T4b | Very advanced — invades prevertebral fascia, encases carotid artery, mediastinal vessels |

### N Classification

| Stage | Criteria |
|---|---|
| N0 | No regional lymph node metastasis |
| N1a | Level VI or VII (central compartment) nodes |
| N1b | Unilateral, bilateral, or contralateral lateral neck (levels I-V) or retropharyngeal nodes |

### Stage Grouping for MTC

| Stage | T | N | M |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | T1 | N0 | M0 |
| II | T2-T3 | N0 | M0 |
| III | T1-T3 | N1a | M0 |
| IVA | T4a or N1b | Any N/Any T | M0 |
| IVB | T4b | Any N | M0 |
| IVC | Any T | Any N | M1 |

### Prognostic Factors (Modigliani 1998 — GETC Study)

Key findings from the 899-patient GETC study:

| Factor | Prognostic Impact |
|---|---|
| Age at diagnosis | <40 years: better prognosis |
| Sex | Female sex: slightly better prognosis |
| Stage at presentation | Most important prognostic factor |
| Completeness of initial surgery | Complete tumour resection: strongest modifiable prognostic factor |
| Post-operative calcitonin | Undetectable = biochemical cure (60-90% 10-year survival) |
| Calcitonin doubling time | <6 months: 5-year survival 25%; >24 months: 5-year survival 95% |
| CEA level and doubling time | Rising CEA with dedifferentiation: poor prognosis |
| Hereditary vs sporadic | MEN2A better than sporadic; MEN2B worst |

## Surgical Management

### Standard Surgery for MTC

**Total thyroidectomy is mandatory** — even for apparently unilateral sporadic MTC — because:
- High rate of bilateral C-cell hyperplasia (occult contralateral foci)
- Multifocality in 20% of sporadic and >80% of hereditary cases
- Facilitates calcitonin monitoring postoperatively (no residual C-cells)

### Neck Dissection — Extent

| Scenario | Minimum Surgery | Additional |
|---|---|---|
| Clinically node-negative, calcitonin <200 | TT + ipsilateral CCND | Consider bilateral CCND |
| Calcitonin 200-500, clinical N0 | TT + bilateral CCND | Consider ipsilateral lateral ND (IIA-V) |
| Calcitonin >500 or clinically N+ | TT + bilateral CCND + bilateral lateral ND | — |
| Hereditary MTC | TT + bilateral CCND (minimum) | Lateral ND based on calcitonin/imaging |

### Rationale for Bilateral Central Compartment Neck Dissection

- MTC metastasises early and predictably to central compartment nodes
- Occult central node metastases present in 50-75% of palpable MTC
- Central compartment reoperative surgery carries significantly higher morbidity (recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, hypoparathyroidism)
- Therefore, comprehensive initial surgery is critical

## Tisell's Microdissection Technique

### Concept

Tisell (from Gothenburg, Sweden) pioneered a compartment-oriented microdissection technique specifically for MTC neck dissection. The technique emphasises systematic removal of all fibrofatty tissue from anatomically defined compartments, without necessarily removing non-lymphatic structures.

### Principles

1. **Compartment-oriented dissection** — not berry-picking of individual nodes
2. **Systematic removal of fibrofatty tissue** from defined anatomical boundaries
3. **Preservation of vital structures** — RLN, parathyroid glands (with autotransplantation if devascularised)
4. **Bilateral central dissection as standard** — because of bilateral C-cell disease in hereditary and high bilaterality in sporadic MTC

### Central Compartment Microdissection (Level VI)

| Boundary | Landmark |
|---|---|
| Superior | Hyoid bone |
| Inferior | Innominate artery (right) / thoracic inlet |
| Lateral (bilateral) | Carotid sheaths |
| Anterior | Strap muscles |
| Posterior | Prevertebral fascia |

Steps:
1. Identify and preserve both recurrent laryngeal nerves throughout their course
2. Identify all four parathyroid glands
3. Remove all fibrofatty tissue between the carotid sheaths, from hyoid to innominate artery
4. Include pretracheal, paratracheal, and prelaryngeal (Delphian) nodes
5. Remove thymus horn and upper mediastinal tissue (level VII)
6. Devascularised parathyroids: autotransplant into sternocleidomastoid or forearm

### Lateral Compartment Microdissection (Levels II-V)

| Boundary | Landmark |
|---|---|
| Superior | Skull base / posterior belly of digastric |
| Inferior | Clavicle |
| Anterior | Strap muscles / sternohyoid |
| Posterior | Anterior border of trapezius |
| Medial | Carotid artery |
| Lateral | Posterior triangle |

Steps:
1. Systematic removal of fibrofatty tissue from levels IIA, IIB, III, IV, VA, VB
2. Preserve spinal accessory nerve, internal jugular vein (if not involved), phrenic nerve, brachial plexus
3. Include nodes along the recurrent laryngeal nerve chain
4. Remove tissue from the transverse cervical and supraclavicular regions

### Advantages of Tisell's Technique

- Higher biochemical cure rates than selective node-picking
- Reproducible and systematic
- Allows identification of occult micrometastases within fibrofatty tissue
- Compartment clearance reduces risk of reoperative surgery

## Adjuvant Treatment

### External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT)

| Indication | Details |
|---|---|
| Positive margins (R1/R2) | If re-excision not feasible |
| Extensive extrathyroidal extension | T4a/T4b disease |
| Extensive nodal disease | Multiple positive nodes, extranodal extension |
| Rising calcitonin without structural disease | Controversial — consider empiric neck/mediastinal EBRT |
| Dose | 60-66 Gy in 30-33 fractions; IMRT preferred |
| Field | Thyroid bed, bilateral central and lateral neck, upper mediastinum |

### No Role for RAI

- MTC arises from C-cells, NOT follicular cells
- C-cells do not express the sodium-iodide symporter (NIS)
- MTC does not concentrate radioiodine
- RAI has NO role in MTC management

### No Role for TSH Suppression

- C-cells do not express TSH receptors (unlike follicular-derived DTC)
- TSH suppression does not benefit MTC patients
- Thyroid hormone replacement to maintain euthyroid state (not suppressive doses)

## Follow-Up: Hypercalcitoninemia

### Post-Operative Calcitonin Assessment

| Calcitonin Status | Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Undetectable (<2 pg/mL) | Biochemical cure | Routine surveillance every 6-12 months |
| Detectable but <150 pg/mL | Residual microscopic disease or circulating half-life | Observe; repeat at 3 months; trend over time |
| >150 pg/mL, stable | Persistent disease, likely nodal or micrometastatic | Localisation imaging; observation if stable |
| >150 pg/mL, rising | Progressive disease | Intensive imaging and consider intervention |

### Calcitonin Doubling Time (CDT)

The most important prognostic variable in follow-up:

| CDT | Prognosis | Management |
|---|---|---|
| <6 months | Aggressive; 5-year survival ~25% | Active treatment — consider systemic therapy |
| 6-24 months | Intermediate | Close surveillance, consider local interventions |
| >24 months | Indolent; 5-year survival ~95% | Observation with annual imaging |

### Imaging for Persistent/Recurrent Hypercalcitoninemia

| Calcitonin Level | Recommended Imaging |
|---|---|
| <150 pg/mL | Neck US every 6-12 months |
| 150-500 pg/mL | Neck US, CT neck/chest, liver MRI |
| >500 pg/mL | CT neck/chest/abdomen, bone scan, consider DOPA-PET/CT |
| Rising with negative conventional imaging | 18F-DOPA PET/CT (most sensitive), or 68Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT |

### Revision Surgery for Persistent Disease

- Compartment-oriented revision neck dissection if localisable structural disease
- Higher morbidity than primary surgery (scarring, distorted anatomy)
- Success rate for biochemical cure after reoperation: 20-30%
- Patient selection is critical — surgery unlikely to benefit if calcitonin >1000 pg/mL and no localisable disease

## Management of Metastatic MTC

### Sites of Distant Metastasis

| Site | Frequency | Features |
|---|---|---|
| Lungs | 33% | Miliary nodules, often bilateral |
| Liver | 25-45% | Often micrometastatic, best detected by MRI |
| Bone | 15-20% | Osteoblastic and osteolytic, axial skeleton |
| Brain | Rare | Late manifestation |

### Systemic Therapy

#### First-Generation MKIs

| Agent | Trial | PFS (drug vs placebo) | ORR | Key Targets |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vandetanib | ZETA (Wells 2012) | 30.5 vs 19.3 months | 45% | RET, VEGFR2, EGFR |
| Cabozantinib | EXAM (Elisei 2013) | 11.2 vs 4.0 months | 28% | MET, VEGFR2, RET |

#### RET-Selective Inhibitors (Second-Generation)

| Agent | Trial | ORR (previously treated) | ORR (treatment-naive) | Key Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Selpercatinib | LIBRETTO-001 (Wirth 2020) | 69% | 73% | Highly selective; fewer off-target toxicities |
| Pralsetinib | ARROW | 60% | 71% | Similar selectivity; once daily dosing |

#### Treatment Sequencing

1. **Indolent, asymptomatic metastatic disease:** Active surveillance with CDT monitoring
2. **Progressive, symptomatic, or high tumour burden:** Initiate systemic therapy
3. **RET-mutant MTC (germline or somatic):** Selpercatinib or pralsetinib preferred (emerging as first-line)
4. **RET wild-type or prior RET-inhibitor progression:** Vandetanib or cabozantinib
5. **Subsequent lines:** Switch MKI class; consider clinical trials
6. **RAS-mutant MTC:** MEK inhibitor-based regimens under investigation

### Palliative Interventions

- EBRT for painful bone metastases or airway compromise
- Hepatic arterial embolisation for symptomatic liver metastases
- Bone-modifying agents (bisphosphonates, denosumab) for skeletal metastases
- Peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT) for somatostatin receptor-positive disease

## Special Situations

### Calcitonin Screening for Thyroid Nodules

- Routine measurement of serum calcitonin in patients with thyroid nodules is performed in European centres
- Detects unsuspected MTC — FNAC has false-negative rate of 30-50% for MTC
- Cost-effectiveness debated; not universally recommended by ATA
- If calcitonin >100 pg/mL with thyroid nodule: high suspicion for MTC

### MTC and Ectopic ACTH (Cushing Syndrome)

- MTC can produce ACTH, causing ectopic Cushing syndrome
- Occurs in 2-5% of advanced MTC
- Poor prognostic sign — indicates dedifferentiation
- Management: treat MTC + adrenalectomy or medical adrenal blockade (ketoconazole, metyrapone)

### MTC in Pregnancy

- Defer surgery to second trimester if possible
- Screen for and manage phaeochromocytoma first
- Calcitonin crosses placenta minimally — does not harm foetus
- RET testing of offspring at birth if hereditary
