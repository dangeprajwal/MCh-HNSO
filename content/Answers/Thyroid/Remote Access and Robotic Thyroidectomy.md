### Classification of Approaches

**By access route**:

| Category | Approach | Scar Location |
|---|---|---|
| Cervical (minimal access) | MIVAT | Small (2-3 cm) cervical incision |
| Remote access — axillary | Gasless transaxillary (robotic/endoscopic) | Axillary fold |
| Remote access — chest/breast | Anterior chest, bilateral breast, BABA | Areolae + bilateral axillae |
| Remote access — postauricular | Facelift / retroauricular | Behind ear |
| Natural orifice | TOETVA, TORT | Oral vestibule (no visible scar) |

## Minimally Invasive Video-Assisted Thyroidectomy (MIVAT)

### Technique (Miccoli)

- **Incision**: 1.5-2.5 cm central cervical incision above the sternal notch
- **Approach**: Subplatysmal dissection, midline strap muscle retraction
- **Instruments**: 5 mm 30-degree endoscope, conventional thyroid instruments (spatula dissector, small retractors)
- **Gasless**: Maintained by external retraction
- **Key features**: Direct cervical access, 2D magnified visualisation, no CO2 insufflation

### Indications

- Thyroid nodules <3.5 cm
- Thyroid volume <30 mL
- No previous neck surgery
- Low-risk WDTC (T1-T2, N0)
- Graves disease (small gland)

### Outcomes

- Comparable oncologic outcomes to conventional thyroidectomy
- Cosmetic advantage (smaller scar)
- Shorter operative time than remote-access approaches
- Lower cost than robotic approaches

## Robotic Transaxillary Thyroidectomy

### Technique (Kang/Chung — Yonsei)

**Patient positioning and setup**:
1. Supine position, arm on operative side extended and fixed above head
2. Pillow under ipsilateral shoulder, neck slightly extended and rotated contralaterally
3. Incision: 5-6 cm in the axillary fold (hidden in natural crease)

**Dissection**:
1. Subplatysmal flap raised from axilla to thyroid bed using electrocautery and lighted retractors
2. External retractor (Chung retractor) maintains working space (gasless technique)
3. Sternal and clavicular heads of SCM separated
4. Strap muscles retracted medially to expose thyroid lobe

**Robotic docking**:
- da Vinci system docked from contralateral side
- 3-4 robotic arms: 30-degree endoscope, ProGrasp forceps, Maryland dissector, harmonic shears
- Surgeon operates at console

**Key surgical steps**:
1. Identify and preserve RLN (using IONM when available)
2. Identify and preserve parathyroid glands
3. Ligate superior and inferior thyroid vessels
4. Dissect thyroid from trachea at ligament of Berry
5. Deliver specimen through axillary incision

### Advantages of Transaxillary Approach

- No visible neck scar
- Excellent cosmetic outcomes (highest patient satisfaction in cosmesis-focused studies)
- Magnified 3D view
- Wristed instruments (7 degrees of freedom)
- Tremor filtration

### Limitations

- Unilateral approach (contralateral lobectomy requires separate axillary incision or conversion)
- Long operative field (increased instrument collision risk)
- Risk of brachial plexus injury from arm positioning
- Longer operative time and higher cost

## BABA (Bilateral Axillo-Breast Approach) Robotic Thyroidectomy

### Technique (Lee KE)

**Port placement** (4 ports):
1. Two bilateral circumareolar ports (12 mm)
2. Two bilateral axillary ports (8 mm)
3. Working space created by subplatysmal flap elevation with CO2 insufflation or external retraction

**Advantages over transaxillary**:
- Midline, symmetric working space — bilateral thyroid access
- Both lobes accessible through the same setup (ideal for total thyroidectomy)
- Central neck dissection feasible
- Better ergonomics for bilateral procedures

**Disadvantages**:
- Four incision sites (though all hidden)
- CO2 insufflation may cause subcutaneous emphysema
- Risk of anterior chest wall complications (flap necrosis, haematoma)
- Longer operative time

### Comparative Outcomes: BABA vs Transaxillary

| Parameter | BABA | Transaxillary |
|---|---|---|
| Bilateral access | Excellent | Limited |
| Central neck dissection | Feasible | Possible but difficult |
| Cosmesis | 4 hidden scars | 1 hidden scar |
| Operative time | Longer | Shorter |
| Brachial plexus risk | Lower | Higher |
| Learning curve | Longer | Shorter |

## Transoral Endoscopic Thyroidectomy Vestibular Approach (TOETVA)

### Technique (Anuwong)

**Port placement**:
1. Central 10 mm port: labial frenulum incision (camera)
2. Two lateral 5 mm ports: bilateral oral vestibule at the level of the canines (working instruments)
3. All incisions within the oral vestibule, completely hidden

**Dissection**:
1. CO2 insufflation (6-8 mmHg) through the central port to create subplatysmal working space
2. Subcutaneous dissection extends inferiorly over the chin, between the mental nerves
3. Subplatysmal flap raised to expose strap muscles
4. Midline raphe incision, strap muscles retracted laterally
5. Standard thyroidectomy performed with endoscopic instruments or robotic arms

**Advantages**:
- Truly scarless procedure (no visible external incision)
- Midline approach — bilateral access
- No arm positioning issues
- Growing evidence base

**Limitations**:
- Mental nerve injury risk (1-5%) — causes lower lip numbness
- Infection risk (oral flora, though low with antibiotic prophylaxis)
- Limited working space for large goiters
- CO2 insufflation complications (subcutaneous emphysema, mediastinal emphysema)
- Learning curve (50-60 cases to achieve proficiency)
- Not suitable for cancer requiring lateral neck dissection

### Contraindications for TOETVA

| Absolute | Relative |
|---|---|
| Thyroid cancer requiring lateral neck dissection | Substernal extension |
| Prior neck radiation | Thyroiditis (dense adhesions) |
| Oral/dental infection | Large goiter (>6 cm) |
| Coagulopathy | Previous neck surgery |
| Tracheal invasion | Obesity (BMI >35) |

## Postauricular / Facelift Approach

### Technique (Terris)

- **Incision**: Postauricular crease extending into occipital hairline
- **Dissection**: Subplatysmal flap from mastoid tip to thyroid bed
- **Advantages**: Scar completely hidden behind ear; unilateral or bilateral approach
- **Limitations**: Limited access for large tumours; long working distance; risk to great auricular nerve and marginal mandibular nerve

## Indications and Contraindications for Remote-Access/Robotic Thyroidectomy

### Indications

| Indication | Details |
|---|---|
| Benign thyroid nodules | <5 cm, symptomatic or cosmetically concerning |
| Low-risk WDTC | T1-T2 (<4 cm), N0 or limited N1a |
| Graves disease | Small to moderate gland size |
| Cosmetic preference | Strong patient preference for no neck scar |
| Completion thyroidectomy | After prior lobectomy (approach-dependent) |

### Absolute Contraindications

1. **Advanced thyroid cancer**: T4 disease, tracheal/oesophageal invasion
2. **Bulky lateral neck disease**: N1b requiring comprehensive lateral neck dissection
3. **Large goiter** (>5-6 cm, approach-dependent)
4. **Prior neck surgery** (extensive scarring)
5. **Prior neck radiation** (fibrosis, altered anatomy)

### Relative Contraindications

1. Thyroiditis (Hashimoto — dense adhesions)
2. Substernal goiter extension
3. BMI >35 (depending on approach)
4. Retrosternal/posterior mediastinal extension
5. Coagulopathy

## Comparative Outcomes

### Robotic vs Conventional Open Thyroidectomy

| Outcome | Robotic | Conventional Open |
|---|---|---|
| Operative time | Longer (120-180 min) | Shorter (60-120 min) |
| Blood loss | Similar or slightly more | Similar |
| RLN palsy (transient) | 2-5% | 3-8% |
| RLN palsy (permanent) | 0-2% | 0.3-3% |
| Hypocalcemia (transient) | 15-25% | 20-30% |
| Hypocalcemia (permanent) | 0-3% | 1-4% |
| Haematoma | 0.5-2% | 1-2% |
| Hospital stay | Similar (1-2 days) | Similar (1-2 days) |
| Cosmetic satisfaction | Significantly higher | Standard |
| Cost | Higher (robot + instruments) | Lower |
| Pain (early postoperative) | Variable (chest/axillary pain) | Neck pain |
| Oncologic adequacy | Comparable (similar margin rates) | Standard |

## The da Vinci Surgical System in Thyroidectomy

### Components

1. **Surgeon console**: 3D high-definition binocular vision, master controllers, foot pedals
2. **Patient-side cart**: 3-4 robotic arms (camera + 2-3 instruments)
3. **Vision system**: 8-10x magnification, 3D stereoscopic view
4. **EndoWrist instruments**: Maryland dissector, ProGrasp forceps, harmonic curved shears, monopolar cautery

### Advantages of Robotic Platform

- 7 degrees of freedom (wristed instruments)
- 3D magnified visualisation
- Tremor filtration
- Motion scaling
- Ergonomic surgeon posture (reduced fatigue)
- Intuitive instrument control

### Disadvantages

- High capital cost (USD 1.5-2.5 million for system)
- Instrument cost (USD 1,500-2,500 per case)
- Bulky equipment requiring dedicated OR space
- No tactile/haptic feedback
- System maintenance costs
- Dependence on trained surgical team

## Learning Curve

### Definition and Evidence

The learning curve for robotic thyroidectomy varies by approach:

| Approach | Cases to Competency | Cases to Proficiency |
|---|---|---|
| Transaxillary | 35-50 cases | 50-80 cases |
| BABA | 40-50 cases | 60-80 cases |
| TOETVA | 50-60 cases | 80-100 cases |
| MIVAT | 20-30 cases | 40-50 cases |

### Prerequisites for Starting a Robotic Thyroid Programme

1. High-volume thyroid surgery practice (>50 cases/year conventional)
2. Dedicated robotic surgical team (surgeon, assistant, scrub nurse, anaesthetist)
3. Robotic surgery training (didactic + simulation + proctored cases)
4. Cadaveric workshops for specific approach
5. Proctored initial cases (10-20) by experienced robotic thyroid surgeon
6. Structured outcomes tracking and quality assurance

## Current Evidence and Guidelines

### ATA (2015) Guidelines

- Remote-access thyroidectomy not specifically recommended or discouraged
- States that surgical approach should be at the surgeon's discretion based on expertise
- Emphasises that oncologic principles should not be compromised

### Korean Thyroid Association

- Robotic thyroidectomy endorsed as an option in experienced centres
- Largest body of evidence (predominantly from South Korea)

### Current Consensus

1. Robotic/endoscopic thyroidectomy achieves **comparable oncologic outcomes** to conventional surgery in appropriately selected patients
2. **Cosmetic outcomes** are superior with remote-access approaches
3. **Cost** remains the principal barrier to wider adoption
4. **Case selection** is paramount — appropriate patient and tumour characteristics
5. **Learning curve** is significant and requires structured training
6. **Not appropriate** for advanced thyroid cancers requiring extensive resection or lateral neck dissection

## Key Points for Exam

1. MIVAT (Miccoli) was the first minimally invasive thyroid approach — small cervical incision, no robot needed
2. The gasless transaxillary robotic approach (Kang/Chung, Yonsei) is the most widely studied remote-access technique
3. BABA uses 4 ports (bilateral circumareolar + bilateral axillary) — ideal for total thyroidectomy
4. TOETVA (Anuwong) achieves truly scarless thyroidectomy through oral vestibular incisions
5. Key contraindications: large goiter (>5-6 cm), advanced cancer (T4), prior neck radiation, lateral neck disease
6. Robotic thyroidectomy has longer operative times and higher costs but comparable complication rates
7. Learning curve is 35-60 cases depending on approach
8. Mental nerve injury (lower lip numbness) is unique to TOETVA (1-5%)
9. Brachial plexus injury risk is unique to transaxillary approach (arm positioning)
10. No evidence that robotic approach improves oncologic outcomes — benefit is purely cosmetic
