Per Randolph Ch 32, the surgical robot overcame the two key limitations of endoscopic remote-access thyroidectomy: two-dimensional visualisation and rigid instruments. The robot system provided three-dimensional visualisation and multi-articulated endoscopic arms ideal for the limited workspace of the neck.

### Classification of Approaches

**By access route**:

| Category | Approach | Scar Location |
|---|---|---|
| Cervical (minimal access) | MIVAT | Small (1.5-2 cm) cervical incision |
| Remote access — axillary | Gasless transaxillary (robotic/endoscopic) | Axillary fold |
| Remote access — chest/breast | Anterior chest, bilateral breast, BABA | Areolae + bilateral axillae |
| Remote access — postauricular | Facelift / retroauricular | Behind ear |
| Natural orifice | TOETVA, TORT | Oral vestibule (no visible scar) |

### ATA Statement on Remote-Access Thyroid Surgery (2016)

Per Randolph Ch 32, the proliferation of multiple remote-access techniques prompted the ATA to issue a formal statement in 2016 acknowledging that remote-access thyroid surgery has a role in selected circumstances when performed by surgeons with high-volume thyroid surgery practices. The statement recommended adherence to strict selection criteria to ensure safe outcomes. At publication, the four most commonly utilised approaches in the United States were: endoscopic breast approach, BABA (endoscopic and robotic), transaxillary approach (endoscopic and robotic), and facelift/retroauricular approach (endoscopic and robotic).

## Minimally Invasive Video-Assisted Thyroidectomy (MIVAT)

### Technique (Miccoli / Bellantone — Randolph Ch 34)

- **Incision**: 1.5-2 cm central cervical incision just below (1 cm) the cricoid cartilage, placed within an existing skin crease for optimal cosmesis (Randolph Ch 34)
- **Approach**: Subplatysmal dissection, midline strap muscle retraction using two small Farabeuf retractors
- **Instruments**: 5 mm 30-degree endoscope, dedicated small (2-3 mm diameter) reusable spatula instruments and spatula-shaped aspirator, Harmonic Focus Plus ultrasonic device (Randolph Ch 34)
- **Gasless**: Completely gasless procedure — maintained by external retraction with Farabeuf retractors (initially CO2 was used but was abandoned early; Randolph Ch 34)
- **Key features**: Direct cervical access, 2-3x endoscopic magnification, no CO2 insufflation, reproduces all steps of conventional thyroidectomy
- **Surgical team**: Requires surgeon plus two assistants (one handling the endoscope) — the need for three surgeons has been considered a limitation (Randolph Ch 34)
- **Anaesthesia**: Can be performed under general anaesthesia or locoregional cervical block (superficial modified deep cervical block), the latter being particularly useful in patients with relative contraindications to GA such as pregnant patients with PTC (Randolph Ch 34)

**Operative steps per Randolph Ch 34**:
1. Upper and lower subplatysmal flaps prepared; cervical linea alba opened
2. Thyroid lobe separated from strap muscles; endoscope and spatula instruments introduced through the single incision
3. Complete freeing of thyroid from strap muscles to expose prevertebral fascia posteriorly, common carotid artery laterally, tracheo-oesophageal groove medially
4. Thyroid retracted downward — superior peduncle vessels dissected and selectively clipped/cut or divided with ultrasonic device; external branch of SLN identified during this phase
5. Thyroid retracted medially and upward — RLN and parathyroid glands identified using endoscopic magnification
6. RLN bluntly dissected under endoscopic vision to its laryngeal entry point
7. Thyroid lobe extracted through the skin incision; final dissection from trachea completed under combined endoscopic and direct vision
8. Dissection close to RLN and parathyroids done by non-thermal vessel division (clips or ligature) rather than energy device

### Indications (Randolph Ch 34 — Expanded Criteria)

- Thyroid nodules <35 mm (initially strict, now expanded with experience)
- Thyroid volume <30 mL
- Low-risk and intermediate-risk differentiated thyroid carcinoma (PTC)
- Completion thyroidectomy after prior contralateral video-assisted lobectomy (Randolph Ch 34)
- Selected patients with Graves disease — feasible with results comparable to open surgery (Randolph Ch 34)
- Prophylactic thyroidectomy and central compartment lymphadenectomy in RET mutation carriers for familial MTC (Randolph Ch 34)
- Contraindication: preoperative diagnosis of PTC with lymph-node involvement (Randolph Ch 34)

### Outcomes (Bellantone Personal Series — Randolph Ch 34)

In a 10-year series of **1363 MIVAT procedures** (Randolph Ch 34):
- Conversion to conventional surgery: **7 cases** (0.5%)
- Central neck node dissection performed in 126 patients through the same access
- Pathology: benign 986, PTC 368, C-cell hyperplasia 1, medullary microcarcinoma 1
- Transient RLN palsy: 27 (2.0%); permanent RLN palsy: 1 (0.07%)
- Transient hypocalcaemia: 230 (16.9%); permanent hypoparathyroidism: 10 (0.7%)
- Postoperative haematoma: 4 (0.3%); wound infection: 5 (0.4%)

### MIVAT: Evidence-Based Advantages (Randolph Ch 34)

Per Randolph Ch 34, multiple RCTs (evidence level IIB) have confirmed:
- Comparable complication rates to conventional thyroidectomy
- Less postoperative pain (validated by objective pain assessment)
- Better cosmetic results and higher patient satisfaction
- Reduced incidence and severity of early voice and swallowing symptoms
- Cost of MIVAT appears equal to that of open thyroidectomy
- Safe and effective for small papillary thyroid carcinomas with equivalent oncological effectiveness

### Video-Assisted Neck Dissection (Randolph Ch 34)

- **VA-CCD (video-assisted central compartment dissection)**: Feasible when unexpected suspicious/enlarged lymph nodes found during MIVAT; outcomes equivalent to conventional open operation (Randolph Ch 34)
- **VALNED (video-assisted functional lateral neck dissection)**: Preliminary experience — eligible for low-risk PTC with lateral neck nodal metastases <2 cm without great vessel involvement; mean 25 nodes per side removed (Randolph Ch 34)

## Robotic Transaxillary Thyroidectomy

### Technique (Kang/Chung — Yonsei; detailed per Randolph Ch 32)

**Patient positioning and setup (Randolph Ch 32)**:
1. Supine position, general anaesthesia with EMG endotracheal tube for IONM
2. Neck slightly extended with shoulder roll
3. Ipsilateral arm placed cephalad and flexed above head into the **modified Ikeda arm position** (Randolph Ch 32)
4. Contralateral arm padded and tucked
5. **Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEP)** can monitor median and ulnar nerve signals to prevent brachial plexus injury (Randolph Ch 32)
6. Intraoperative ultrasound performed after positioning (Randolph Ch 32)

**Incision marking (Randolph Ch 32)**:
- Inferior point: transverse line drawn from sternal notch laterally to axilla
- Superior point: 60-degree oblique line from thyrohyoid membrane to axilla
- Points connected with a longitudinal mark (~5 cm / 2 inches) along the border of the pectoralis major muscle

**Dissection (Randolph Ch 32)**:
1. Subcutaneous dissection to expose lateral border of pectoralis major; flap created in subplatysmal plane superficial to pectoralis fascia
2. Lighted breast retractors facilitate flap creation
3. Clavicle identified and followed medially to sternal notch; ipsilateral SCM identified
4. **Avascular plane between sternal and clavicular heads of SCM** developed — a key anatomical landmark (Randolph Ch 32)
5. Omohyoid fibres divided to expose superior pole of thyroid
6. Self-retaining **Chung retractor** (or modified thyroidectomy retractor) mounted contralateral side to lift strap muscles anteriorly

**Robotic docking (Randolph Ch 32)**:
- da Vinci system brought from contralateral side
- Dual-channel camera in **30-degree down** configuration docked centrally
- **4 robotic arms**: (1) endoscope, (2) vessel sealer on surgeon's dominant side, (3) Maryland dissector contralateral, (4) ProGrasp on upper edge of incision
- Assistant inserts laparoscopic suction/irrigation through axillary incision — also retracts clavicular SCM head or trachea

**Key surgical steps (Randolph Ch 32)**:
1. Vagus nerve stimulated in carotid sheath; for bilateral/neck dissection, electrode placed on vagus for **continuous nerve monitoring** (Randolph Ch 32)
2. Upper pole retracted medially and inferiorly; superior thyroid vessels dissected and divided close to thyroid capsule to avoid EBSLN injury
3. Superior and inferior parathyroid glands identified and preserved
4. RLN identified in tracheo-oesophageal groove and traced to laryngeal entry point; functional integrity confirmed with nerve monitor
5. Inferior pedicle dissected; thyroid dissected from trachea; isthmus divided; specimen extracted through axillary incision
6. **For total thyroidectomy**: contralateral lobe dissected subcapsularly from trachea through same incision; contralateral RLN identified, stimulated, and traced (Randolph Ch 32)
7. **Central lymph node dissection**: nodes dissected en-bloc circumferentially from nerve, removed en-bloc with thymus and thyroid (Randolph Ch 32)
8. Final stimulation of RLN and vagus nerve; drain placed; axillary incision closed in two layers

### Advantages of Transaxillary Approach

- No visible neck scar
- Excellent cosmetic outcomes (highest patient satisfaction in cosmesis-focused studies)
- Magnified 3D view
- Wristed instruments (7 degrees of freedom)
- Tremor filtration
- Feasible in patients with BMI >40 kg/m2 when performed by experienced surgeons (Randolph Ch 32 — Kandil/Tulane series)

### Limitations

- Unilateral approach (contralateral lobectomy requires subcapsular dissection from same side, or separate axillary incision, or conversion)
- Long operative field (increased instrument collision risk)
- Risk of brachial plexus injury from arm positioning (mitigated by SSEP monitoring; Randolph Ch 32)
- Longer operative time and higher cost
- Contraindicated in rotator cuff pathology or cervical spine stenosis (Randolph Ch 32)

## BABA (Bilateral Axillo-Breast Approach) Robotic Thyroidectomy

### Technique (Lee KE)

**Port placement** (4 ports):
1. Two bilateral circumareolar ports (12 mm)
2. Two bilateral axillary ports (8 mm)
3. Working space created by subplatysmal flap elevation with CO2 insufflation or external retraction

**Advantages over transaxillary**:
- Midline, symmetric working space — bilateral thyroid access
- Both lobes accessible through the same setup (ideal for total thyroidectomy)
- Central neck dissection feasible
- Better ergonomics for bilateral procedures

**Disadvantages**:
- Four incision sites (though all hidden)
- CO2 insufflation may cause subcutaneous emphysema
- Risk of anterior chest wall complications (flap necrosis, haematoma)
- Longer operative time
- Extensive dissection required compared with transaxillary or transoral approaches (Randolph Ch 33)

### Comparative Outcomes: BABA vs Transaxillary

| Parameter | BABA | Transaxillary |
|---|---|---|
| Bilateral access | Excellent | Limited |
| Central neck dissection | Feasible | Yes — en-bloc with thymus (Randolph Ch 32) |
| Cosmesis | 4 hidden scars | 1 hidden scar |
| Operative time | Longer | Shorter |
| Brachial plexus risk | Lower | Higher (0.2-2.2%; Randolph Ch 32) |
| Learning curve | Longer | Shorter (25-40 cases; Randolph Ch 32) |
| Angle of dissection | Unfamiliar to some surgeons | Unfamiliar to some surgeons |

## Transoral Endoscopic Thyroidectomy Vestibular Approach (TOETVA)

### History and Rationale (Randolph Ch 33)

Per Randolph Ch 33 (Richmon, Anuwong, Gooi, Russell), the transoral approach offers several key advantages over other remote-access techniques:
- **Shortest dissection route** to the thyroid of all remote-access methods — reduces operative time and postoperative pain
- **Midline approach** affords equal exposure to both sides of the central neck
- **Superior-to-inferior angle of dissection** is a vantage point familiar to high-volume thyroid surgeons who routinely find the RLN at its insertion
- **No cutaneous incision** — the only approach that is truly "scarless"
- **Same-day discharge** more common compared with other remote-access approaches (Randolph Ch 33)

Oral mucosal healing occurs rapidly and incisions are usually not visible within weeks after surgery (Randolph Ch 32).

### Technique (Anuwong; detailed per Randolph Ch 32 and Ch 33)

**Anaesthesia and positioning (Randolph Ch 33)**:
- General anaesthesia with **nasotracheal intubation** (preferred; orotracheal possible but may limit instrument movement)
- Nerve-integrity monitoring endotracheal tube recommended for IONM
- Supine position with slightly more neck extension than conventional open thyroidectomy
- Patient prepped and draped from nose to areolae (in case of conversion or axillary specimen retrieval)
- Surgeon stands at head of bed; endoscopic tower at foot of bed
- Preoperative antibiotics given; oral cavity cleaned with normal saline and povidone-iodine

**Port placement (Randolph Ch 32 and Ch 33)**:
1. Central 10 mm port: 2 cm horizontal incision at gingivobuccal sulcus above the labial frenulum (camera)
2. Two lateral 5 mm ports: **stab incisions at upper lateral edge of lower lip, just medial to the commissure** (NOT at canine level — this is the Anuwong modification that avoids mental nerve injury; Randolph Ch 33)
3. All incisions infiltrated with saline-epinephrine or local anaesthetic
4. For men, lateral ports placed slightly more medially (Randolph Ch 33)

**Hydrodissection and working space creation (Randolph Ch 33)**:
1. Central incision bluntly dissected over midline mandible into submental subplatysmal plane (mentalis muscle fascicles divided; care to avoid skin penetration)
2. Veress needle hydrodissection: 500 mL saline with 1 mg epinephrine infiltrated in subplatysmal plane from chin to sternal notch in fanning fashion between SCM muscles (Randolph Ch 33)
3. Blunt dilator (Hegar/Pratt) passed through central incision in subplatysmal plane
4. 10 mm cannula placed through central incision, tip advanced just beyond mandible
5. CO2 insufflation at **6 mmHg**, flow 15-25 L/min (Randolph Ch 32 and Ch 33)
6. Suspension suture may be placed through skin at cannula tip to elevate flap
7. 5 mm cannulas advanced through lateral ports to same level as central trocar

**Working space dissection (Randolph Ch 33)**:
- Fibrous attachments taken down from cannula tips to sternal notch using monopolar hook and energy device under endoscopic vision
- Boundaries: clavicle inferiorly, medial borders of both SCMs laterally
- Vent opened on one or both lateral cannulae for smoke evacuation

**Thyroid dissection (Randolph Ch 33)**:
1. Midline raphe of strap muscles identified and divided with cautery or advanced energy device (Harmonic/LigaSure)
2. Strap muscles elevated off target lobe until carotid visualised
3. Transcutaneous suture through strap muscles for retraction — increases working space
4. Pyramidal lobe elevated; avascular pretracheal fascia identified; **isthmus divided superior to inferior**
5. Cricothyroid (Joll's) space opened; superior pole retracted contralaterally
6. **EBSLN predictably found** during this manoeuvre (Randolph Ch 33)
7. Superior thyroid vessels divided with energy device
8. Superior parathyroid identified and lateralised with blood supply preserved
9. **RLN found at its insertion site and dissected caudally** — may be unfamiliar to some surgeons (Randolph Ch 33)
10. IONM stimulator invaluable during RLN identification; no tissue divided without assurance nerve is protected
11. Berry's ligament divided; inferior vascular pedicle divided
12. Contralateral side repeated for total thyroidectomy; central neck dissection feasible on one or both sides

**Specimen extraction (Randolph Ch 33)**:
- Endoscopic bag advanced through central cannula; specimen placed in bag and extracted through central vestibular incision
- Specimens <4 cm can usually be teased through intact; for larger specimens, serial Hegar dilation usually sufficient for nodules <6 cm
- Strap muscle raphe may be reapproximated with barbed suture; oral incisions closed with 4-0 absorbable suture
- **Jaw-bra** or compression dressing placed for 24 hours to obliterate dead space (Randolph Ch 33)
- No drain required; liquid diet same day; perioperative antibiotics for 24 hours

### TOTS: Robotic vs Endoscopic Approach (Randolph Ch 33)

Per Randolph Ch 33 (Box 33.1):

| Feature | TOETVA (Endoscopic) | TORT (Robotic) |
|---|---|---|
| Equipment cost | Less expensive, more accessible | More expensive, limited access |
| Optics | 2D view | 3D view (better) |
| Instrumentation | Non-wristed laparoscopic instruments | Wristed, articulated, tremor-free |
| Instrument control | 2 instruments | 3-4 instruments precisely controlled |
| Learning curve | Longer (standard laparoscopic skills) | Quicker (familiar to OTO-HNS surgeons) |
| Additional ports | No | May use 4th arm via axillary port for retraction |
| Administration | Fewer credentialing burdens | More admin/credentialing required |
| Adoption | >50 centres in 13 countries | Primarily few centres in South Korea |

### Indications for TOETVA (Randolph Ch 32)

Per Randolph Ch 32, Table 32.2:
- Thyroid diameter no more than 10 cm
- Dominant nodule size no more than **6 cm** when benign or indeterminate (Bethesda II, III, IV)
- Dominant nodule no more than **2 cm** when Bethesda V (suspicious for malignancy) or confirmed WDTC
- Multinodular goitre
- In carefully selected patients: Graves disease, cytologically suspicious lesions, WDTC (within size limits)

**Patient selection recommendations for beginning surgeons (Randolph Ch 33)**:
- Maximal thyroid dimension 6 cm with maximal nodule 4 cm initially
- Avoid Bethesda V/VI in early cases
- Mid or lower pole nodules preferred (upper pole nodules increase difficulty)
- Nodule ipsilateral to surgeon's dominant hand is ideal
- Male patients not ideal initially (prominent chin and thyroid notch limit manoeuvrability)
- Presence of oral braces limits instrument space

### Contraindications for TOETVA (Randolph Ch 32, Table 32.2)

| Absolute | Relative |
|---|---|
| History of head and neck surgery (including mandibular surgery) | Severe chronic lymphocytic (Hashimoto) thyroiditis — increased friability |
| History of head/neck/upper mediastinum irradiation | Elevated BMI — greater difficulty with flap elevation |
| Preoperative RLN palsy | Graves disease — hypervascular, bleeding risk |
| Lymph node metastasis | Substernal extension |
| Extrathyroidal extension (tracheal or oesophageal invasion) | Previous neck surgery |
| Presence of oral abscesses | Obesity (BMI >35) |
| Evidence of substernal thyroidal extension | |
| Patients unfit for general anaesthesia | |
| Active clinical hyperthyroidism | |

### Outcomes (Anuwong Series — Randolph Ch 33)

In the **largest series of 425 TOETVA patients** (Randolph Ch 33):
- Conversion to open: **3 cases** (0.7%) — all due to excessive bleeding (2 with Graves disease, 1 with goitre >12 cm)
- Transient RLN palsy: **25 (5.9%)**; permanent RLN palsy: **0**
- Transient hypoparathyroidism: **46 (10.9%)**; permanent: **0**
- Transient mental nerve injury: **3** (all resolved)
- Haematoma: 1; seroma: 20
- Operative time significantly longer than open (hemi: 78.6 vs 64.2 min; total: 135.1 vs 103.3 min)
- Pain scores (VAS): significantly less with TOETVA than open thyroidectomy
- No significant differences in complications when matched with 216 open thyroidectomy patients

### TOETVA vs Other Remote Access Approaches (Randolph Ch 33)

Per Randolph Ch 33, compared with all other remote-access approaches, TOTS (transoral thyroid surgery) offers:
- **Broader indications** in a Western population
- **Faster discharge** (same-day discharge more common)
- **Lower pain scores**
- **Bilateral central neck access** without repositioning or additional incisions
- **Faster learning curve** (anecdotally 7-10 cases; defined as ~11 cases for laparoscopic-naive surgeons in North American series)
- **Best cosmetic outcome** — no cutaneous incision
- More than 50% of thyroid surgery patients may be candidates (Randolph Ch 33)

## Postauricular / Facelift Approach

### Technique (Terris; detailed per Randolph Ch 32)

**Patient positioning (Randolph Ch 32)**:
- Supine with arms tucked bilaterally
- Head rotated 20-30 degrees to contralateral side
- Hair at base of occipital hairline shaved 1 cm posteriorly along planned incision

**Incision (Randolph Ch 32)**:
- Inferior end at inferior extent of lobule in postauricular crease
- Carried superiorly and posteriorly into shaved occipital hairline in gentle curve
- Infiltrated with local anaesthetic

**Dissection (Randolph Ch 32)**:
1. Subplatysmal flap developed exposing SCM
2. Great auricular nerve identified; external jugular vein identified (may be divided if needed)
3. Dissection continues to anteromedial border of SCM to clavicle
4. Muscular triangle identified (SCM, omohyoid, sternohyoid)
5. Strap muscles retracted ventrally using Chung retractor or modified thyroidectomy retractor
6. Singer hook or army-navy retractor on Greenberg retractor retracts SCM laterally

**Robotic docking (Randolph Ch 32)**:
- Only 3 arms used (a limitation); camera in 30-degrees-down configuration
- Vessel sealer and Maryland grasper in arms 2 and 3
- When appropriate, excision of redundant skin before closure allows a simultaneous "facelift" procedure (Randolph Ch 32)

**Current trend (Randolph Ch 32)**: Per a US multi-institutional study (Russell et al. 2019), the robotic facelift approach is falling out of favour due to significantly longer operative time compared with transaxillary and transoral approaches, lack of access to contralateral thyroid lobe, and increased complications.

## Indications and Contraindications for Remote-Access/Robotic Thyroidectomy

### Patient Selection (Randolph Ch 32)

Per Randolph Ch 32, the "ideal" patient for robotic transaxillary and retroauricular approaches is a **young female with BMI <30 kg/m2**. Patients with a history of **keloid or hypertrophic scar formation** are particularly good candidates.

However, robotic transaxillary thyroidectomy can be safely performed in patients with **BMI above 40 kg/m2** by experienced surgeons (Kandil/Tulane series), and retroauricular approach can be done in obese patients with BMI <40 (Randolph Ch 32).

Per Randolph Ch 32, conservative patient selection is recommended during the learning curve of **25-40 cases**, starting with **partial thyroidectomy** before progressing to total thyroidectomy and neck dissection.

### Indications

| Indication | Details |
|---|---|
| Benign thyroid nodules | <5 cm, symptomatic or cosmetically concerning |
| Low-risk WDTC | T1-T2 (<4 cm), N0 or limited N1a |
| Graves disease | Small to moderate gland size (relative contraindication for some approaches) |
| Cosmetic preference | Strong patient preference for no neck scar; keloid/hypertrophic scar history |
| Completion thyroidectomy | After prior lobectomy (approach-dependent) |
| TOETVA: expanded criteria | Thyroid up to 10 cm, nodule up to 6 cm (benign), up to 2 cm (Bethesda V/WDTC) |

### Absolute Contraindications (Randolph Ch 32, Table 32.1)

**For transaxillary and retroauricular (Randolph Ch 32)**:
1. **Large substernal or retropharyngeal goiters**
2. **T3 or greater thyroid cancer** or any suspicious gross invasion
3. **Medullary thyroid cancer**

**For TOETVA (Randolph Ch 32, Table 32.2)**:
1. History of head and neck surgery (including mandibular surgery)
2. History of head/neck/upper mediastinum irradiation
3. Preoperative RLN palsy
4. Lymph node metastasis
5. Extrathyroidal extension (tracheal/oesophageal invasion)
6. Oral abscesses
7. Substernal thyroidal extension
8. Unfit for general anaesthesia; active clinical hyperthyroidism

### Relative Contraindications (Randolph Ch 32)

**For transaxillary/retroauricular (Randolph Ch 32, Table 32.1)**:
1. Nodules >5 cm
2. Large goiters with volumes >40 mL
3. Known T2 well-differentiated thyroid cancer (2-4 cm)
4. Graves disease with substernal extension
5. Obesity (BMI >40 kg/m2)
6. History of previous neck surgery or radiation
7. **Rotator cuff pathology or cervical spine stenosis** (specific positional contraindications; Randolph Ch 32)

**For TOETVA**: severe Hashimoto thyroiditis, elevated BMI

## Comparative Outcomes

### Robotic vs Conventional Open Thyroidectomy

Per Randolph Ch 32, several meta-analyses have found **no significant differences** between robotic and open thyroidectomy in terms of transient and permanent RLN injury, permanent hypoparathyroidism, or haematoma formation.

| Outcome | Robotic | Conventional Open |
|---|---|---|
| Operative time | Longer (120-180 min) | Shorter (60-120 min) |
| Blood loss | Similar or slightly more | Similar |
| RLN palsy (transient) | 2-5% | 3-8% |
| RLN palsy (permanent) | 0-2% | 0.3-3% |
| Hypocalcemia (transient) | 15-25% | 20-30% |
| Hypocalcemia (permanent) | 0-3% | 1-4% |
| Haematoma | 0.5-2% | 1-2% |
| Hospital stay | Similar (1-2 days) | Similar (1-2 days) |
| Cosmetic satisfaction | Significantly higher | Standard |
| Cost | Higher (robot + instruments) | Lower |
| Pain (early postoperative) | Variable (chest/axillary pain) | Neck pain |
| Oncologic adequacy | Comparable (similar margin rates) | Standard |

### Complications Specific to Each Access (Randolph Ch 32, Table 32.3)

| Approach | Specific Complications |
|---|---|
| Transaxillary | Brachial plexus neuropraxia (0.2-2.2%); rare oesophageal and tracheal injuries (early experience) |
| Retroauricular | Mouth corner deviation (marginal mandibular nerve); ear lobe numbness (great auricular nerve) |
| Transoral | Mental nerve injury; skin burns; temporary chin numbness; oral commissure tears |

Per Randolph Ch 32, brachial plexus neuropraxia in transaxillary surgery can be **prevented with SSEP monitoring** — in a Tulane series of 137 robotic transaxillary surgeries, 7 patients (5.1%) had significant intraoperative SSEP changes, but immediate arm repositioning led to prompt recovery with **zero postoperative brachial plexus injuries**.

### TOETVA Complications (Randolph Ch 32 — Shan and Liu Systematic Review)

In a review of 10 articles (211 cases; Randolph Ch 32):
- Temporary hypoparathyroidism: **7.1%**
- Temporary RLN injury: **4.3%**
- Mental nerve palsy: **4.3%** (in early series; reduced significantly with Anuwong's port placement modification)

### Comparison Across Approaches

| Parameter | MIVAT | Transaxillary Robot | BABA Robot | TOETVA | Facelift |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Scar visibility | Small neck (1.5-2 cm) | Axilla (hidden) | 4 sites (hidden) | None | Behind ear |
| Bilateral access | Yes | Limited (possible subcapsular) | Yes | Yes | Limited |
| Central ND | Yes (VA-CCD) | Yes (en-bloc) | Yes | Yes | Possible |
| Lateral ND | Yes (VALNED — preliminary) | No | No | No | No |
| Max tumour size | 3.5 cm | 4-5 cm | 4-5 cm | 6 cm (benign); 2 cm (WDTC) | 4 cm |
| Max thyroid diameter | — | — | — | 10 cm (Randolph Ch 32) | — |
| Operative time | Shortest | Moderate | Longest | Moderate | Longest (per US data) |
| Cost | Lowest (equal to open) | High | High | Moderate (laparoscopic instruments) | High |
| Learning curve | 20-30 cases | 25-40 cases (Randolph Ch 32) | 40-50 cases | ~11 cases (Randolph Ch 33) | 35-50 cases |
| Dissection extent | Minimal (cervical) | Extensive (axilla to neck) | Extensive (chest wall) | Shortest route | Extensive |
| Cutaneous incision | Yes (cervical) | Yes (axillary) | Yes (4 sites) | No | Yes (postauricular) |

## The da Vinci Surgical System in Thyroidectomy

### Components

1. **Surgeon console**: 3D high-definition binocular vision, master controllers, foot pedals
2. **Patient-side cart**: 3-4 robotic arms (camera + 2-3 instruments)
3. **Vision system**: 8-10x magnification, 3D stereoscopic view; dual-channel camera in 30-degree-down configuration (Randolph Ch 32)
4. **EndoWrist instruments**: Maryland dissector, ProGrasp forceps, harmonic curved shears, vessel sealer device, monopolar cautery

### Advantages of Robotic Platform

- 7 degrees of freedom (wristed instruments)
- 3D magnified visualisation
- Tremor filtration
- Motion scaling — allows for superb tissue visualisation and handling (Randolph Ch 33)
- Ergonomic surgeon posture (reduced fatigue)
- Intuitive instrument control
- Per Randolph Ch 32, the robot proved ideal for operating in the limited workspace of the neck, overcoming the two-dimensional visualisation and rigid-instrument limitations of endoscopic approaches

### Disadvantages

- High capital cost (USD 1.5-2.5 million for system)
- Instrument cost (USD 1,500-2,500 per case)
- Bulky equipment requiring dedicated OR space
- No tactile/haptic feedback
- System maintenance costs
- Dependence on trained surgical team
- Only 3 arms usable in facelift approach (Randolph Ch 32)

## Intraoperative Nerve Monitoring (IONM) in Remote-Access Surgery

Per Randolph Ch 32, IONM is specifically integrated into remote-access thyroidectomy:
- **EMG endotracheal tube** used for intubation in all robotic approaches (Randolph Ch 32)
- **Vagus nerve stimulation** performed in carotid sheath at start of dissection
- **Continuous nerve monitoring**: for bilateral dissection or neck dissection, an electrode may be placed on the vagus nerve (Randolph Ch 32)
- RLN integrity confirmed at identification and at procedure end (final stimulation of RLN and vagus nerve; Randolph Ch 32)
- In TOETVA, the **nerve-stimulating probe** is particularly invaluable given that the RLN is found at its insertion site and dissected caudally — a direction unfamiliar to some surgeons (Randolph Ch 33)
- The magnified, high-definition endoscopic view aids identification of terminal nerve branches during transoral dissection (Randolph Ch 33)

## Learning Curve

### Definition and Evidence

The learning curve for robotic thyroidectomy varies by approach:

| Approach | Cases to Competency | Cases to Proficiency |
|---|---|---|
| Transaxillary | 25-40 cases (Randolph Ch 32) | 50-80 cases |
| BABA | 40-50 cases | 60-80 cases |
| TOETVA | ~11 cases (Randolph Ch 33, North American data) | 30-50 cases |
| MIVAT | 20-30 cases | 40-50 cases |
| Facelift | 35-50 cases | 50-80 cases |

Per Randolph Ch 33, the TOETVA learning curve is notably shorter than other remote-access approaches, defined as approximately **11 cases** for a laparoscopic-naive surgeon in a North American population. The learning curves for BABA, RATS, and RFA are generally 35-50 cases each (Randolph Ch 33). Per Randolph Ch 32, the learning curve for transaxillary and retroauricular approaches is 25-40 cases, with conservative patient selection recommended during this period.

### Prerequisites for Starting a Robotic Thyroid Programme

Per Randolph Ch 32, requirements before considering remote-access approaches:
1. **High-volume thyroid surgery practice** (>50 cases/year conventional)
2. **Competence with necessary instrumentation**
3. **For robotic cases**: surgeons must be facile and credentialed in robotic surgery (Randolph Ch 32)
4. **Institutional support** — surgeon and team education, preparation (Randolph Ch 32)
5. Cadaveric workshops for specific approach
6. Proctored initial cases (10-20) by experienced robotic thyroid surgeon
7. **Structured outcomes recording** (Randolph Ch 32)
8. Begin with **partial thyroidectomy** before total thyroidectomy and neck dissection (Randolph Ch 32)

## Cost Considerations

| Factor | Robotic | Conventional |
|---|---|---|
| Capital cost | USD 1.5-2.5 million | Nil |
| Per-case disposables | USD 1,500-2,500 | USD 200-500 |
| Maintenance | USD 100-200K/year | Minimal |
| Operative time (cost) | 30-60% longer | Standard |
| Hospital stay | Similar | Similar |
| Return to work | Similar | Similar |
| **Total per-case cost** | **USD 3,000-5,000 more** | **Standard** |

Per Randolph Ch 33, the cost of robotic remote-access approaches decreases as operative times decrease with experience. TOETVA may have a cost advantage over robotic approaches as it uses standard reusable laparoscopic instruments (Randolph Ch 32). MIVAT cost appears equal to that of open thyroidectomy (Randolph Ch 34).

In fee-for-service systems (e.g., US), there is a financial disincentive for surgeons to perform longer-duration cases, especially during the learning curve; in systems where robotic approaches reimburse at higher rates, they are more common (Randolph Ch 33).

## Remote-Access Thyroidectomy: East vs West (Randolph Ch 33)

Per Randolph Ch 33, adoption of remote-access thyroidectomy has been much greater in Asia than in the Western world due to:
- **Body habitus**: Western patients have higher mean BMI — historically a contraindication
- **Tumour size**: larger in US series; nodules >3 cm traditionally contraindicated for remote access
- **Reimbursement differences**: financial incentives in some Asian systems vs disincentives in US fee-for-service
- However, evidence shows that ~20% of Western patients are self-conscious about cervical scar >1 year after surgery; >80% of respondents preferred to avoid cervical incision; and a majority would pay more and accept higher surgical risk to avoid a neck scar (Coorough survey, ~1000 US respondents; Randolph Ch 33)

## Current Evidence and Guidelines

### ATA (2015) Guidelines

- Remote-access thyroidectomy not specifically recommended or discouraged
- States that surgical approach should be at the surgeon's discretion based on expertise
- Emphasises that oncologic principles should not be compromised

### ATA Statement on Remote-Access Thyroid Surgery (2016) — per Randolph Ch 32

- Acknowledged that remote-access thyroid surgery has a role in selected circumstances
- Must be performed by surgeons with high-volume thyroid practices
- Adherence to strict selection criteria recommended for safe outcomes

### Korean Thyroid Association

- Robotic thyroidectomy endorsed as an option in experienced centres
- Largest body of evidence (predominantly from South Korea)

### Current Consensus

1. Robotic/endoscopic thyroidectomy achieves **comparable oncologic outcomes** to conventional surgery in appropriately selected patients
2. **Cosmetic outcomes** are superior with remote-access approaches
3. **Cost** remains the principal barrier to wider adoption
4. **Case selection** is paramount — appropriate patient and tumour characteristics
5. **Learning curve** is significant (25-50 cases for most approaches) and requires structured training; TOETVA has the shortest learning curve (~11 cases; Randolph Ch 33)
6. **Not appropriate** for advanced thyroid cancers requiring extensive resection or lateral neck dissection
7. **Transaxillary and transoral** are currently the most commonly performed remote-access procedures in the US, with the facelift approach falling out of favour (Randolph Ch 32)
8. TOETVA offers the broadest indications, shortest dissection route, fastest learning curve, and best cosmetic outcome of all remote-access approaches (Randolph Ch 33)

## Key Points for Exam

1. MIVAT (Miccoli/Bellantone) was the first minimally invasive thyroid approach — small cervical incision, completely gasless, no robot needed; 1363-case series showed 0.07% permanent RLN palsy (Randolph Ch 34)
2. The gasless transaxillary robotic approach (Kang/Chung, Yonsei) is the most widely studied remote-access technique; uses the avascular plane between sternal and clavicular heads of SCM (Randolph Ch 32)
3. BABA uses 4 ports (bilateral circumareolar + bilateral axillary) — ideal for total thyroidectomy
4. TOETVA (Anuwong) achieves truly scarless thyroidectomy through oral vestibular incisions; lateral ports placed at upper lateral lip edge near commissure (NOT canine level) to avoid mental nerve injury (Randolph Ch 33)
5. Key contraindications: For transaxillary — T3+ cancer, substernal goitre, medullary thyroid cancer, rotator cuff pathology; For TOETVA — prior head/neck surgery, irradiation, oral abscess, substernal extension (Randolph Ch 32)
6. Robotic thyroidectomy has longer operative times and higher costs but comparable complication rates — confirmed by multiple meta-analyses (Randolph Ch 32)
7. Learning curve: transaxillary 25-40 cases, BABA 40-50 cases, TOETVA ~11 cases (shortest), MIVAT 20-30 cases (Randolph Ch 32, 33, 34)
8. Mental nerve injury (lower lip numbness) is unique to TOETVA (reduced to <1% with Anuwong port modification; Randolph Ch 33)
9. Brachial plexus injury in transaxillary approach is preventable with SSEP monitoring (0.2-2.2% without, 0% with monitoring in Tulane series; Randolph Ch 32)
10. No evidence that robotic approach improves oncologic outcomes — benefit is purely cosmetic
11. IONM with EMG endotracheal tube is standard in robotic thyroidectomy; continuous vagal nerve monitoring possible for bilateral/neck dissection cases (Randolph Ch 32)
12. MIVAT can be performed under locoregional cervical block — advantageous in pregnant patients with PTC (Randolph Ch 34)
13. The ATA 2016 statement acknowledges remote-access thyroid surgery has a role when performed by high-volume surgeons with strict patient selection (Randolph Ch 32)
