## Hurthle Cell (Oncocytic) Neoplasm

### Definition and Histopathology

Hurthle cells (oncocytic cells) are large polygonal cells with abundant eosinophilic, granular cytoplasm due to densely packed mitochondria. In the WHO 2022 classification, the term "oncocytic" is preferred over "Hurthle cell" as the latter is technically a misnomer -- Max Askanazy first described these cells in 1898, while Hurthle himself actually described the interfollicular C-cells (parafollicular cells), not the oncocytic thyrocytes (Randolph Ch 25). Oncocytes are believed to develop as **metaplastic events resulting from stress**, including chronic inflammation, and are seen in both nonneoplastic conditions (Hashimoto thyroiditis, multinodular goiters) and neoplastic settings. Outside the thyroid, oncocytic cells are also found in the pituitary, salivary glands (e.g., Warthin tumour), kidney, and adrenal gland.

**Key Diagnostic Criteria:**
- The neoplasm must be composed of **>75% Hurthle/oncocytic cells** to qualify as a Hurthle cell neoplasm, although they typically involve 100% of the lesion (Randolph Ch 25)
- Cells demonstrate characteristic abundant eosinophilic granular cytoplasm on H&E staining due to **accumulation of mitochondria** (confirmed on electron microscopy)
- Large, variably pleomorphic nuclei with prominent, typically centrally located nucleoli
- Absence of papillary thyroid carcinoma nuclear features (to distinguish from oncocytic variant of PTC)
- Lymphocytes are frequently associated with Hurthle cell change, most commonly in Hashimoto thyroiditis

### WHO Classification Evolution

| Classification Era | Categorisation |
|---|---|
| WHO 3rd/4th Edition (2004) | Variant of follicular adenoma/carcinoma |
| WHO 2017 | Recognised as molecularly distinct entities |
| WHO 5th Edition (2022) | **Separate entity** -- oncocytic thyroid neoplasm |

In the 2022 WHO classification, oncocytic thyroid neoplasms are now a distinct category encompassing:
1. **Oncocytic adenoma** (formerly Hurthle cell adenoma)
2. **Oncocytic carcinoma** (formerly Hurthle cell carcinoma)
3. **Oncocytic carcinoma of uncertain malignant potential**

### Molecular Biology

Hurthle cell neoplasms have a **distinct molecular profile** compared to conventional follicular neoplasms. Two landmark whole-exome sequencing studies (Ganly et al.; Gopal et al.) have provided comprehensive genomic characterisation (Randolph Ch 25):

**Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) Mutations:**
- Disruptive somatic mtDNA mutations are highly prevalent in HCC -- a unique feature among thyroid cancers, as most other cancers do not harbour detrimental mtDNA mutations
- These inactivating mutations **nearly always occur in genes encoding subunits of complex I** (NADH dehydrogenase), which catalyses the first step in mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS)
- Loss of mitochondrial complex I represents a form of **altered tumour metabolism** that is a key genetic hallmark of HCC
- The mtDNA is a small, circular genome with maternal inheritance, coding for 13 proteins -- all participating in OXPHOS

**Chromosomal and Nuclear DNA Alterations:**
- **Widespread chromosomal losses (LOH)** -- the most prevalent nuclear DNA alteration, in some cases spanning the majority of the genome
- These striking LOH events produce **"near-haploid" DNA content**, sometimes accompanied by duplication of the remaining chromosome, resulting in **uniparental disomy** or even whole genome duplication
- Despite pervasive LOH, **chromosomes 5, 7, and 12 are rarely lost** and instead show frequent duplication, suggesting critical genes reside on these chromosomes
- LOH is the most frequently encountered event in metastatic HCC and **significantly correlates with worse outcomes** (progression-free and metastasis-free survival)
- Recurrent nuclear DNA mutations in: **DAXX, EIF1AX (11%), NF1 (9%), TP53, NRAS/HRAS/KRAS (15%), CDKN1A, ATM, PTEN**
- TERT promoter hotspot mutations in ~25% of HCCs
- RAS mutations (~15%) -- less frequent than in conventional FTC
- BRAF mutations uncommon (unless oncocytic variant of PTC); however, BRAF may be overexpressed via whole chromosome 7 duplication
- At least one receptor tyrosine kinase mutated in 20% of HCCs (including EGFR, ERBB2, PDGFR, MET, RET)
- Key pathways: **RTK/RAS/RAF/MAPK** and **PI3K/AKT/mTOR**

### Adenoma vs Carcinoma -- Distinguishing Features

| Feature | Hurthle Cell Adenoma | Hurthle Cell Carcinoma |
|---|---|---|
| Capsule | Intact, no invasion | **Capsular invasion** |
| Vascular invasion | Absent | **Present** |
| Growth pattern | Encapsulated | Invasive |
| Behaviour | Benign | Malignant -- metastatic potential |
| Overall proportion | ~67% of HCTs | ~33% of HCTs |

**Critical Point:** FNA cytology **CANNOT reliably distinguish** Hurthle cell adenoma from carcinoma because the diagnosis of malignancy depends on architectural features (capsular and vascular invasion) that cannot be assessed on cytology. The diagnostic variability in assessing invasion (subjective interpretation by pathologists, variable tumour submission patterns) is a major factor in the wide-ranging mortality rates reported across different series (Randolph Ch 25).

### FNA Cytology Limitations

- Bethesda System: Hurthle cell aspirates are classified under **Category IV** (Follicular Neoplasm/Suspicious for Follicular Neoplasm -- Hurthle cell type)
- Malignancy risk in Bethesda IV (Hurthle cell): **20-30%** will have malignancy (Randolph Ch 25)
- FNA sensitivity for HCC detection is low (~24%) -- all oxyphil-predominant nodules should be referred for surgery
- Cytological features suggestive of malignancy (but not diagnostic): nuclear atypia and crowding, cellular dyshesion, increased nuclear:cytoplasm ratio, columnar rather than polygonal cells, increased mitotic rate
- In neoplastic lesions, Hurthle cells are **poorly cohesive** with generally absent colloid and rare lymphocytes (vs. abundant colloid and numerous lymphocytes in thyroiditis)
- **Molecular testing** (Afirma GSC, ThyroSeq v3) may assist in risk stratification but has lower specificity for Hurthle cell lesions due to unique molecular features

### Management of Hurthle Cell Neoplasms

**Diagnostic Strategy:**
1. **FNA** -- identifies Hurthle cell neoplasm but cannot determine benign vs malignant
2. **Lobectomy** -- standard diagnostic procedure for indeterminate Hurthle cell nodule
3. **Frozen section** -- unreliable for capsular/vascular invasion assessment; correctly diagnoses only ~19% of carcinomas, with 75% of results being indeterminate (Randolph Ch 25, Dahl et al.)
4. **Final histopathology** determines adenoma vs carcinoma

**Surgical Management:**
- **Hurthle cell adenoma** -- lobectomy is sufficient (curative)
- **Hurthle cell carcinoma:**
  - Minimally invasive (capsular invasion only) -- lobectomy may suffice for tumours <4 cm
  - Widely invasive or >4 cm -- **completion thyroidectomy** recommended
  - Rationale for completion thyroidectomy:
    - Facilitates RAI therapy (though HCC has decreased RAI avidity)
    - Enables thyroglobulin monitoring
    - Higher recurrence rates than conventional FTC
  - Extent of surgery as independent prognostic factor: the Royal Marsden 50-year series showed extent of surgery independently affected DSS (Randolph Ch 25)
- **Cervical lymph node metastases:** Unlike PTC, nodal involvement in HCC is associated with **poorer outcomes and increased mortality** -- treat aggressively with modified neck dissection (Level I involvement is uncommon and does not routinely need to be addressed)

**Radioactive Iodine (RAI) Considerations:**
- HCC has **decreased RAI avidity** compared to conventional DTC -- Lopez-Penabad et al. demonstrated only a **40% RAI uptake rate** in patients with known metastases
- However, a study of recurrent/persistent tumours showed an almost **70% uptake rate** (11/16 patients), suggesting RAI may be more effective than previously believed (Randolph Ch 25)
- Despite lower avidity, RAI should still be considered in:
  - Widely invasive HCC
  - Tumours >4 cm
  - Presence of distant metastases
  - Post-completion thyroidectomy
- Subgroup analysis showed patients receiving RAI for adjuvant ablation had better outcomes than those who did not

**Thyroid Hormone Suppression:**
- TSH target of **0.01-0.1 mU/L** in high-risk patients
- TSH target of **0.1-0.4 mU/L** in lower-risk patients to minimise cardiovascular and skeletal side effects

**Targeted Therapy (Emerging):**
- Phase II trials of sorafenib (multi-TKI) and everolimus (mTOR inhibitor) in widely invasive HCC have shown significant response rates, highlighting the importance of the RTK/RAS/RAF/MAPK and PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathways

### Prognosis

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| Widely invasive vs minimally invasive | Most important prognostic factor; MSKCC data: MI-HCC had no relapse or death vs. WI-HCC with 73% relapse and 55% mortality |
| Tumour size >4 cm | Worse prognosis; Sippel et al.: no malignancy <2 cm, 50% malignancy risk at >=4 cm, all tumours >6 cm malignant |
| Age >50 years | Adverse (approximately 10 years older than mean age for other DTC) |
| Vascular invasion | Key determinant of metastatic risk; associated with recurrence even in well-encapsulated HCC |
| LOH/chromosomal losses | Most frequently encountered event in metastatic HCC; correlates with worse PFS and MFS |
| RAI avidity | Avid tumours have better prognosis |
| Lymph node metastases | Unlike PTC, nodal involvement in HCC consistently predicts increased mortality |

- SEER database (555 non-metastatic HCCs): 5-year survival 85%, 10-year survival 71% (comparable to matched FTCs)
- Widely invasive HCC: 10-year DSS ~50-60%
- Mean interval to recurrence: ~5 years, but recurrences may occur >15 years after treatment -- lifelong surveillance required
- Common sites of distant metastasis: lung, bone (via haematogenous spread); distant metastasis rate 33-34%
- FDG-PET/CT is helpful in diagnosing metastatic disease, especially in tumours with low iodine avidity; intense FDG uptake is an indicator of poor prognosis

## Poorly Differentiated Thyroid Carcinoma (PDTC)

### Overview

PDTC occupies an intermediate position between well-differentiated thyroid carcinoma (DTC) and anaplastic thyroid carcinoma (ATC) in terms of morphology, biological behaviour, and prognosis. It accounts for approximately 2-5% of all thyroid carcinomas and demonstrates aggressive features with decreased differentiation markers.

### Diagnostic Criteria -- Turin Proposal (2007)

The Turin consensus criteria (Volante et al. 2007) established standardised diagnostic criteria for PDTC:

**Required Features (ALL must be present):**
1. **Solid, trabecular, or insular growth pattern** -- as the predominant architecture
2. **Follicular cell origin** -- confirmed by thyroid transcription factor-1 (TTF-1) and thyroglobulin positivity
3. **Absence of conventional PTC nuclear features** -- i.e., no ground-glass nuclei, nuclear grooves, pseudoinclusions

**Plus at least ONE of the following:**
- **Convoluted nuclei** (small, round, hyperchromatic nuclei)
- **Mitotic activity** >=3 mitoses per 10 high-power fields (HPF)
- **Tumour necrosis**

### Memorial Sloan Kettering (MSK) Criteria

The MSK criteria differ from Turin in that they:
- Use **mitotic rate** (>=5 per 10 HPF) and **tumour necrosis** as the primary defining features
- Do **NOT** require specific growth patterns (solid/trabecular/insular)
- Can be applied to tumours with follicular or papillary architecture that show high-grade features
- Are thus more inclusive -- capturing "high-grade" differentiated thyroid cancers

### WHO 2017/2022 Classification

The WHO incorporated both approaches:

| Criteria Set | Key Features | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| Turin criteria | Solid/trabecular/insular + necrosis or mitoses or convoluted nuclei | More restrictive, pattern-dependent |
| MSK criteria | Mitoses >=5/10 HPF and/or necrosis, regardless of growth pattern | More inclusive |
| WHO 2022 | Introduced **"differentiated high-grade thyroid carcinoma"** alongside PDTC | Unified approach |

The WHO 2022 classification introduced the concept of **"differentiated high-grade thyroid carcinoma" (DHGTC)** -- tumours that retain papillary or follicular architecture but show high mitotic rate (>=5/10 HPF) and/or necrosis. This essentially captures the MSK criteria tumours that do not meet Turin criteria.

### Clinical Features

- **Age:** Mean age at presentation 55-65 years
- **Gender:** Female predominance (2:1)
- **Presentation:** Rapidly growing thyroid mass, often with compressive symptoms
- **Extrathyroidal extension:** Common at diagnosis (~50%)
- **Lymph node metastases:** 30-50% at presentation
- **Distant metastases:** 25-40% at diagnosis (lung > bone)

### Molecular Profile

| Gene | Frequency | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| TP53 | 20-40% | Associated with de-differentiation |
| TERT promoter | 30-50% | Poor prognosis marker |
| RAS (NRAS, HRAS) | 25-35% | More common than in PTC |
| BRAF V600E | 10-20% | Therapeutic target |
| PIK3CA | 5-15% | PI3K/AKT pathway activation |
| EIF1AX | 5-10% | Often co-occurs with RAS |

**De-differentiation Model:**
DTC --> PDTC --> ATC follows a stepwise accumulation of mutations:
- DTC: BRAF or RAS driver mutation
- PDTC: + TERT promoter or TP53 mutation
- ATC: + TP53, PIK3CA, and/or additional pathway mutations

### Management

**Surgery:**
- **Total thyroidectomy** with **central neck dissection** is recommended
- Lateral neck dissection for clinically/radiologically positive lateral nodes
- Aggressive surgical resection improves outcomes
- R0 resection should be the goal when feasible

**Radioactive Iodine (RAI):**
- RAI avidity is variable but generally lower than DTC
- Post-operative RAI ablation/therapy is recommended
- Higher doses may be required (100-200 mCi)
- Response assessment with post-therapy scan and thyroglobulin levels

**External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT):**
- Consider for:
  - Incomplete surgical resection (R1/R2)
  - Gross extrathyroidal extension
  - Non-iodine-avid disease
  - Recurrent disease in the neck
- Dose: 60-66 Gy to tumour bed, 54-60 Gy to elective neck

**Systemic Therapy:**
- Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (lenvatinib, sorafenib) for progressive RAI-refractory disease
- BRAF-directed therapy (dabrafenib + trametinib) if BRAF V600E mutated
- Clinical trials should be considered

### Prognosis

- **5-year overall survival:** 60-75% (intermediate between DTC ~95% and ATC ~5%)
- **10-year overall survival:** 40-55%
- Median survival with distant metastases: 2-3 years
- Independent prognostic factors: age, tumour size, necrosis, mitotic count, distant metastases

## NIFTP -- Non-Invasive Follicular Thyroid Neoplasm with Papillary-Like Nuclear Features

### Historical Context and Reclassification

Prior to 2016, encapsulated follicular variant of papillary thyroid carcinoma (EFVPTC) was classified as a malignancy. In a landmark study, Nikiforov et al. (2016) demonstrated that the **noninvasive** subtype had an indolent behaviour with no adverse outcomes over 13 years of follow-up, leading to its reclassification as a non-malignant neoplasm. NIFTP was accepted by the WHO in 2017 and is defined as a **noninvasive neoplasm of thyroid follicular cells with a follicular growth pattern and nuclear features of papillary carcinoma that has a very low malignant potential** -- it is considered a **borderline premalignant lesion** rather than a benign lesion (Randolph Ch 23, Nikiforov & Ferris).

**Key Rationale for Reclassification:**
- 109 patients with noninvasive EFVPTC had **0% recurrence** and **0% metastasis** over 13 years median follow-up
- Invasive EFVPTC had 12% adverse events
- The "cancer" label caused unnecessary patient anxiety and overtreatment
- Removal of cancer diagnosis avoids lifelong surveillance, RAI therapy, and TSH suppression

**Epidemiology (Randolph Ch 23):**
- NIFTP likely comprises **10-20% of PTC incidence** observed before 2016 in North America and Europe
- Significantly lower in Asia (1-5% of papillary carcinomas), possibly reflecting ethnic differences or variation in histopathologic diagnostic thresholds
- Female:male ratio 3:1 to 4:1; most patients present in the 4th-6th decades

### Diagnostic Criteria for NIFTP

**ALL criteria must be met (Randolph Ch 23, Box 23.1):**

1. **Encapsulation or clear demarcation** -- thick, thin, or partial capsule, or well-circumscribed with clear demarcation from adjacent thyroid parenchyma
2. **Follicular growth pattern** -- predominantly follicular architecture; solid/trabecular/insular pattern must be <30%
3. **Nuclear features of papillary thyroid carcinoma** -- at least 2 of 3 nuclear feature categories must be present:
   - Nuclear size and shape changes (enlargement, overlapping, crowding, elongation)
   - Nuclear membrane irregularities (irregular contours, grooves, folds, pseudoinclusions)
   - Chromatin changes (clearing, margination, glassy nuclei)
4. **Nuclear score of 2-3** on the 3-point nuclear scoring system (each category scored 0 or 1, yielding a score of 0-3)

**Exclusion Criteria (the "NOs"):**
- **NO invasion** -- no capsular invasion, no vascular invasion, no lymphatic invasion (requires microscopic examination of the **entire tumour capsule interface**)
- **NO well-formed papillae** -- initially "<1% papillae" was proposed, but subsequent studies showed this led to misdiagnosis of classic PTC as NIFTP; the more stringent criterion of **no well-formed papillae** is now recommended (Randolph Ch 23). Single simple hyperplastic-type papillary infoldings without prominent PTC nuclear features are permissible.
- **NO psammoma bodies** (these provide evidence of preexisting papillary structures)
- **NO tumour necrosis**
- **NO high mitotic activity** (<3 per 10 HPF)
- **NO solid/trabecular/insular growth** >30% (excludes solid variant PTC and PDTC)
- **NO microscopic features of other PTC variants** (e.g., tall cell, cribriform-morular)

**Secondary Diagnostic Criteria (helpful but not required):**
- Lack of **BRAF V600E** mutation (by molecular assays or IHC)
- Lack of **RET/PTC, NTRK1/3, ALK fusions**
- Lack of high-risk mutations (**TERT, TP53**) -- these should not be found in NIFTP as it is a precancer lesion

### Molecular Profile of NIFTP

| Mutation | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| RAS mutations (NRAS > HRAS >> KRAS) | 30-60% | Most common; majority at codon 61 |
| PAX8-PPARG fusion | 5-10% | Characteristic of follicular pattern |
| BRAF K601E | Rare | Occasionally seen (unlike V600E which is excluded) |
| THADA fusions | Rare | Common with other follicular-patterned tumours |
| PTEN, DICER1 | Rare | Occasionally seen |
| EIF1AX | ~5% | May co-occur with RAS |
| BRAF V600E | 0% | By definition, excluded |
| TERT, TP53 | 0% | Should not be present (precancer lesion) |

NIFTP shares molecular alterations with other follicular-patterned thyroid tumours (follicular adenoma, FTC, FVPTC), and no single molecular marker is diagnostic. Some NIFTPs have gene expression profiles resembling follicular adenoma, while others resemble infiltrative FVPTC (Randolph Ch 23).

### Clinical Implications

**Impact on Bethesda System:**
- NIFTP reclassification reduced the malignancy risk for Bethesda categories III (AUS/FLUS), IV (FN/SFN), and **V (SFM)**
- The decrease in malignancy risk is **most significant (up to 50%) for Bethesda V (SFM)** (Randolph Ch 23)
- In an analysis of 6943 thyroid FNA specimens, 173 (2.5%) were diagnosed as NIFTP on resection; their preoperative FNA categories were: nondiagnostic 1%, benign 9%, AUS/FLUS 31%, FN/SFN 27%, SFM 24%, malignant 9% (Randolph Ch 23)
- Bethesda III malignancy risk decreased from ~10-30% to ~6-18%
- Bethesda IV malignancy risk decreased from ~25-40% to ~10-30%
- The **2023 Bethesda System update** incorporated NIFTP as a non-malignant entity

**Preoperative Features Suggestive of NIFTP (Randolph Ch 23, Box 23.2):**
- Ultrasound: low/intermediate suspicion nodule (iso/hypoechoic, oval-round, sharp regular margin, hypoechoic rim); no microcalcifications, no ETE, not taller-than-wide
- Cytology: Bethesda III, IV, or V with follicular/microfollicular/sheet-like pattern; no papillae, no psammoma bodies, no prominent pseudoinclusions or grooves
- Molecular: RAS mutation, THADA or PPARG fusion; no BRAF V600E, no RET/PTC fusion, no TERT promoter mutation

**Management:**
- **Lobectomy is sufficient** -- NIFTP is a borderline premalignant tumour; one of the intended consequences of reclassification is to limit surgical management to lobectomy in appropriately selected patients (Randolph Ch 23)
- **No completion thyroidectomy required** -- though total thyroidectomy remains valid if other clinical factors warrant it
- **No RAI therapy required** -- NIFTP is not cancer
- **No TSH suppression** -- standard thyroid hormone replacement; maintain TSH in normal range
- **No long-term cancer surveillance** -- reduced follow-up compared to low-risk DTC
  - Post-lobectomy thyroglobulin expected <30 ng/mL at 6 weeks
  - Post-total thyroidectomy thyroglobulin expected <5 ng/mL at 6 weeks
  - Neck ultrasound every 1-2 years for the first decade if significant thyroid tissue remains
  - Thyroglobulin trend over 3+ data points more valuable than single measurements
- **No cancer diagnosis** -- removes psychological burden and insurance implications

### Diagnostic Pitfalls

1. **Diagnosis requires complete histological examination** -- NIFTP is a histological diagnosis, NOT a cytological diagnosis
2. **Entire capsule must be examined** -- to rule out invasion; if capsule was not fully sampled, NIFTP diagnosis cannot be established with certainty (Randolph Ch 23)
3. **Cannot be diagnosed on FNA or frozen section** -- only on final permanent sections; NIFTP cannot be reliably distinguished from invasive FVPTC on cytology as both share microfollicular pattern and PTC nuclear features
4. **Interobserver variability** in nuclear feature assessment -- ~20% discordance rate among pathologists; the 3-point nuclear score has moderate reproducibility
5. **Must exclude BRAF V600E** if molecular testing available -- BRAF-positive tumours should be diagnosed as classic PTC
6. **Papillae exclusion criterion** -- well-formed papillae must be absent; if occasional papillary structures are seen, finding BRAF V600E or RET/PTC fusion strongly supports classic PTC diagnosis instead
7. **Metastases at diagnosis** exclude NIFTP -- the presence of lymph node or distant metastases signifies malignant behaviour and is evidence for lymphovascular invasion

### Outcomes

- **Recurrence rate:** <1% when strict criteria applied (confirmed in 352 cases from the literature and 5 subsequent large retrospective series totalling 285 patients with mean follow-up 5.7-11.2 years; Randolph Ch 23)
- **Metastasis rate:** 0% with stringent diagnostic criteria
- **Cancer-specific mortality:** 0%
- Incomplete excision may lead to local recurrence
- If invasion is missed (should have been diagnosed as invasive EFVPTC), the tumour is prone to haematogenous spread to bones and lungs; such tumours are frequently positive for RAS and TERT mutations
- Most important differential: invasive EFVPTC, which retains cancer classification and requires more aggressive management

## Key Examination Points

### Comparative Table -- Special Thyroid Variants

| Feature | Hurthle Cell Carcinoma | PDTC | NIFTP |
|---|---|---|---|
| Classification | Separate entity (WHO 2022) | Intermediate malignancy | Borderline premalignant neoplasm |
| Architecture | Follicular (>75% oncocytic) | Solid/trabecular/insular | Follicular |
| Nuclear features | No PTC nuclei | Convoluted nuclei | PTC nuclear features (score 2-3) |
| Key diagnostic | Capsular/vascular invasion | Turin: pattern + mitoses/necrosis | Encapsulated + no invasion + entire capsule examined |
| Key molecular | mtDNA complex I mutations, LOH/near-haploidy | TERT, TP53, RAS | RAS, PPARG fusions; NO BRAF V600E/TERT/TP53 |
| RAI avidity | Decreased (40-70%) | Variable, decreased | Not applicable |
| Surgery | Lobectomy then completion if cancer | Total thyroidectomy + CND | Lobectomy only |
| RAI therapy | If widely invasive/metastatic | Yes, post-operative | No |
| EBRT | For advanced/widely invasive disease | Consider if R1/R2/ETE | No |
| 10-year survival | MI: ~90%, WI: ~55% | 40-55% | ~100% (borderline, not cancer) |

### High-Yield Viva Points

1. Hurthle cell neoplasm requires **>75% oncocytic cells** for diagnosis; the misnomer derives from Hurthle describing C-cells, while Askanazy described the actual oncocyte (1898)
2. FNA **cannot** distinguish Hurthle cell adenoma from carcinoma -- lobectomy is diagnostic; frozen section correctly diagnoses only ~19% of carcinomas
3. HCC has **decreased RAI avidity** compared to conventional DTC (40% uptake with metastases; possibly ~70% in recurrent/persistent disease)
4. HCC harbours unique **mitochondrial DNA complex I mutations** (disrupting OXPHOS) and **widespread LOH** producing near-haploid genomes -- LOH correlates with worse outcomes
5. Turin PDTC criteria: **solid/trabecular/insular** pattern + necrosis OR >=3 mitoses OR convoluted nuclei
6. MSK criteria focus on **mitoses and necrosis** irrespective of growth pattern
7. PDTC represents the **de-differentiation pathway** from DTC to ATC (stepwise mutation accumulation)
8. NIFTP was previously classified as **encapsulated FVPTC** and reclassified in 2016; accepted by WHO in 2017 as a borderline premalignant lesion
9. NIFTP criteria: encapsulated, follicular growth, PTC nuclear score 2-3, **NO invasion, NO well-formed papillae, NO psammoma bodies, NO BRAF V600E/TERT/TP53**; entire capsule must be examined
10. NIFTP management: **lobectomy sufficient**, no RAI, no cancer label; the decrease in Bethesda malignancy risk is most significant (up to 50%) for **Bethesda V (SFM)**
11. WHO 2022 introduced **"differentiated high-grade thyroid carcinoma"** as a new entity
12. In HCC, unlike other DTC, **lymph node metastases predict poorer survival** -- treat aggressively with neck dissection
