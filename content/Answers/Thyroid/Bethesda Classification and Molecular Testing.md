## The Bethesda System for Reporting Thyroid Cytopathology

### Historical Context

Before the Bethesda System, thyroid FNA reporting lacked uniformity. In 2007, the National Cancer Institute hosted a multidisciplinary conference in Bethesda, Maryland, to develop a standardised reporting terminology. The resulting Bethesda System for Reporting Thyroid Cytopathology (TBSRTC) was first published in 2009 (Cibas & Ali) and has undergone two revisions: the 2017 second edition and the 2023 third edition. Per Randolph (Ch 11), the term "indeterminate" is specifically not advised for reporting thyroid FNA results because its meaning is not sufficiently specific and its use has been highly variable; instead, TBSRTC recommends that each report begin with one of six general diagnostic categories.

### Indications for Thyroid FNA

Per Randolph (Ch 11), a systematic approach to FNA indications is essential:

- **Palpable nodules**: Obtain serum TSH and thyroid ultrasound first. Patients with normal or elevated TSH proceed to US evaluation; those with depressed TSH require radionuclide scan correlated with sonographic findings. Functioning ("hot") nodules do not require FNA as the incidence of malignancy is exceedingly low
- **Size thresholds**: Nodules >1.0-1.5 cm with sonographic features warranting biopsy. FNA may be deferred for borderline nodules (1.0-1.5 cm) with features strongly associated with benign cytology
- **Sonographically suspicious features**: Microcalcifications, hypoechoic solid nodules, irregular/lobulated margins, intranodular vascularity, and nodal metastases or signs of extracapsular spread
- **FDG-PET incidentalomas**: 2-3% of all PET scans; carry approximately 35% risk of malignancy. Focal FDG-PET-avid thyroid nodules are much more likely to represent thyroid cancer than metastatic disease, even in patients with extrathyroidal malignancy — an indication for FNA. Diffuse uptake does not warrant FNA unless US detects a discrete nodule
- **Sestamibi incidentalomas**: Cancer risk 22-66%; all focal hot nodules on sestamibi confirmed by US should undergo FNA
- **CT/MRI incidentalomas**: Present in at least 16% of neck CT/MRI patients; require dedicated thyroid US evaluation

### FNA Technique: Key Points from Randolph

Per Randolph (Ch 11), technical details significantly affect FNA yield:

- **Needle gauge**: Smaller needles (25 and 27 G) are superior to larger ones
- **Dwell time**: Shorter dwell time is often superior to longer times, which introduce more blood
- **Technique**: Rapid oscillations within the nodule (2-5 per second) are better than slow excursions
- **Processing**: Both conventional smears (CS) and liquid-based cytology (LBC) are acceptable; some laboratories use a combination
- **Core-needle biopsy (CNB)**: Uses spring-activated 18-21 G needles producing tissue fragments; advantages include larger cell yields and decreased nondiagnostic rates, but disadvantages include higher complication rates (haemorrhage, local pain). Endorsed by some organisations only for repeated nondiagnostic yields
- **ROSE (Rapid On-Site Evaluation)**: When pathologists perform FNA with ROSE, adequacy rates are higher, though this requires significant resource investment

### Accuracy of Thyroid FNA

Per Randolph (Ch 11), in experienced hands the diagnostic accuracy of thyroid FNA for technically satisfactory specimens is greater than 95%, with positive predictive values of 89-98% and negative predictive values of 94-99%. False-negative and false-positive diagnoses average less than 5% and 1%, respectively. Sensitivities range from 43-98% and specificities from 72-100%, reflecting variability in operator skill and cytopathologist expertise. For cystic thyroid lesions, FNA has a notably low sensitivity (~40%) because cystic aspirates often yield only cyst contents.

### The Six Diagnostic Categories

| Category | Designation | Implied Malignancy Risk (2017) | Adjusted Risk (excl. NIFTP) | Recommended Management |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Nondiagnostic / Unsatisfactory | 5-10% | 5-10% | Repeat FNA with US guidance |
| II | Benign | 0-3% | 0-3% | Clinical and US follow-up |
| III | Atypia of Undetermined Significance (AUS) / Follicular Lesion of Undetermined Significance (FLUS) | 10-30% | 6-18% | Repeat FNA, molecular testing, or lobectomy |
| IV | Follicular Neoplasm / Suspicious for Follicular Neoplasm (FN/SFN) | 25-40% | 10-40% | Molecular testing or lobectomy |
| V | Suspicious for Malignancy | 50-75% | 45-60% | Lobectomy or total thyroidectomy |
| VI | Malignant | 97-99% | 94-96% | Total thyroidectomy (or lobectomy for select cases) |

Per Randolph (Ch 11, Table 11.1), when NIFTP is not considered carcinoma, the Bethesda VI risk adjusts to 94-96%, and "actual management may depend on other factors (e.g., clinical, sonographic) besides the FNA interpretation."

### Key Definitions and Adequacy Criteria

**Adequacy requirements for Bethesda II (Benign)**:
- Minimum 6 groups of well-visualised follicular cells
- Each group containing at least 10 cells
- Exceptions: colloid nodules with abundant colloid may be adequate with fewer cells; thyroiditis specimens with lymphocytes are adequate even without follicular cells

**Bethesda I (Nondiagnostic)**:
- Insufficient follicular cells
- Obscuring blood or artefact
- Cyst fluid only without adequate cellular component
- Recommendation: repeat FNA in 4-6 weeks (preferably US-guided)
- Per Randolph (Ch 11), a "nondiagnostic" result does not mean the FNA is benign — the aspiration must be repeated. Among nondiagnostic cases, cystic lesions are the most frequently encountered. When FNA of a cystic nodule with a solid component is repeatedly nonrepresentative, surgical excision should be considered because malignant lesions occur in 8-19% of such cases
- Per Randolph (Ch 11, Box 11.1), nondiagnostic subcategories include: cyst fluid only, virtually acellular specimen, and other (obscuring blood, clotting artefact, etc.)

### Bethesda II (Benign) — Specific Patterns from Randolph

Per Randolph (Ch 11), the benign category encompasses:
- **Consistent with a benign follicular nodule** (includes adenomatoid nodule, colloid nodule, etc.) — a colloid nodule bears a very low malignancy risk (<5%) and should be followed for at least 2 years with US
- **Consistent with lymphocytic (Hashimoto's) thyroiditis** in the proper clinical context — the key cytological feature is lymphoepithelial clusters in an inflammatory background; oxyphilic hyperplastic nodules in CLT are usually nonneoplastic with a malignancy risk similar to goiter
- **Consistent with granulomatous (subacute/De Quervain's) thyroiditis** — shows mature lymphocytes, plasma cells, and scattered giant cells in the context of a tender gland

### Bethesda III (AUS/FLUS) — The Grey Zone

Bethesda III is the most controversial category. Per Randolph (Ch 11), AUS/FLUS results are obtained in 1-22% of thyroid FNAs, though an effort should be made to limit its use to approximately 10% or fewer — higher rates likely represent overuse when other interpretations are more appropriate. Key features:

- **Cytological features**: Nuclear atypia falling short of suspicious for malignancy, or architectural atypia with microfollicular pattern but insufficient for Bethesda IV. Per Randolph (Ch 11), the category is reserved for specimens with "architectural and/or nuclear atypia that is not sufficient to be classified as suspicious for a follicular neoplasm, suspicious for malignancy, or malignant" but where "the atypia is more marked than can be ascribed confidently to benign changes"
- **Contributing factors**: Per Randolph (Ch 11), a common contributing factor is the compromised nature of the specimen (sparse cellularity, obscuring blood), but these conditions alone are not sufficient to place a specimen in the AUS category
- **True malignancy risk**: Per Randolph (Ch 11), only a minority of AUS/FLUS cases have surgical follow-up, and those resected represent a selected population with repeated AUS results or worrisome clinical/sonographic findings — in this selected population, up to 50% prove to have cancer, but this is undoubtedly an overestimate of the true risk (estimated at 10-30%, or 6-18% excluding NIFTP)
- **Repeat FNA outcome**: Per Randolph (Ch 11), only about 20% of nodules are repeatedly AUS/FLUS on repeat FNA
- **Subcategories** (2023 third edition):
  - Nuclear atypia (higher risk, closer to PTC)
  - Architectural atypia (follicular pattern)
  - Combined nuclear and architectural atypia
- **Management options**:
  1. Repeat FNA (resolves diagnosis in ~50% of cases)
  2. Molecular testing (Afirma GSC or ThyroSeq v3)
  3. Diagnostic lobectomy
- **Key decision factor**: TIRADS score integrated with Bethesda category

### Bethesda IV (FN/SFN)

- **Cytological pattern**: Predominantly microfollicular architecture with scant or absent colloid
- **Includes**: Hurthle cell (oncocytic) neoplasm — per Randolph (Ch 11), specify if Hurthle cell (oncocytic) type
- **Core issue**: Cannot distinguish follicular adenoma from follicular carcinoma on cytology (requires assessment of capsular/vascular invasion on histology)
- **Per Randolph (Ch 11)**: The most important cytological criterion distinguishing benign from potentially malignant is follicular architecture — specifically whether the lesion is composed predominantly of macrofollicles (benign) or microfollicles, trabeculae, and crowded groups (suspicious). Follicular carcinomas are virtually never composed of predominantly normal-sized or macrofollicles. Aspirates of microfollicles (small groups of 6-12 follicular cells with or without central colloid) or crowded trabeculae/3D groups are classified as "suspicious for a follicular neoplasm"
- **Hurthle cell neoplasms**: Per Randolph (Ch 11), a diagnosis of OCN (oncocytic cell neoplasm) correlates with a 15-40% risk of malignancy. Every lesion composed predominantly of Hurthle cells should be diagnosed as OCN, unless an inflammatory background or clinical/US picture of thyroiditis is detected. Oncocytic nodules in CLT should usually be regarded as nonneoplastic
- **NIFTP accommodation**: Per Randolph (Ch 11), the second edition of TBSRTC now includes aspirates of follicular-patterned lesions with mild to moderate nuclear atypia to accommodate NIFTP
- **Management**: Diagnostic lobectomy or molecular testing. ATA guidelines also recognise molecular testing as an acceptable method to triage patients for lobectomy versus clinical follow-up

### Bethesda V (Suspicious for Malignancy) — Subcategories from Randolph

Per Randolph (Ch 11, Box 11.1), the suspicious for malignancy category includes:
- Suspicious for papillary thyroid carcinoma
- Suspicious for medullary thyroid carcinoma
- Suspicious for metastatic carcinoma
- Suspicious for lymphoma

Per Randolph (Ch 12), Bethesda V carries a 45-60% risk of malignancy (excluding NIFTP). Molecular testing has limited applicability as a "rule out" for Bethesda V due to the higher cancer prevalence — a negative ThyroSeq v3 result still carries a 20% residual risk of malignancy.

### Bethesda VI (Malignant) — Specific Entities from Randolph

Per Randolph (Ch 11, Box 11.1), the malignant category includes:
- Papillary thyroid carcinoma (most common; over 90% correctly diagnosed as malignant or suspicious on FNA)
- Poorly differentiated carcinoma
- Medullary thyroid carcinoma (distinct immunoprofile: calcitonin+, CEA+, chromogranin+, TTF-1+, thyroglobulin-)
- Undifferentiated (anaplastic) carcinoma (often PAX-8+, p53+, but usually thyroglobulin- and TTF-1-)
- Squamous cell carcinoma
- Carcinoma with mixed features
- Metastatic carcinoma (0.1% of thyroid FNAs; most frequent: kidney, colorectal, lung, breast, melanoma)
- Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma

## Molecular Testing in Indeterminate Thyroid Nodules

### Rationale for Molecular Testing

The fundamental problem with Bethesda III/IV nodules:
- Malignancy rates of 10-40% mean that 60-90% of patients undergoing diagnostic surgery have benign disease
- Molecular testing aims to reduce unnecessary diagnostic surgeries
- Two paradigms: **rule-out** (high NPV) and **rule-in** (high PPV)

Per Randolph (Ch 12), up to 30% of FNA samples are classified as cytologically indeterminate. The malignancy risk for AUS/FLUS has been shown to vary widely across practice settings, with reported rates ranging from 6% to 40%. Traditionally, options were limited to repeat biopsy or diagnostic lobectomy, but molecular diagnostic tools have become widely available. The ATA guidelines include molecular testing as an acceptable adjunct and state that informed application is critical. Importantly, per Randolph (Ch 12), molecular testing has been validated specifically for cytologically indeterminate nodules — application to other Bethesda categories incurs excessive cost with little benefit.

### Repeat FNA Timing

Per Randolph (Ch 12), although conventional wisdom has led to the recommendation of waiting 8-12 weeks to avoid FNA-related atypia, one study showed that time between initial and repeat FNA was not a predictor of diagnostic yield or accuracy. Waiting 4-6 weeks or possibly less is likely sufficient, especially if the cytopathologist interpreting the specimen is aware of the timeline.

### Principles: "BRAF-like" vs "RAS-like" Tumours

Per Randolph (Ch 12), integrated genetics have articulated "BRAF-like" and "RAS-like" tumour profiles that shed light on the diversity of clinical behaviour among differentiated thyroid carcinomas:
- **BRAF V600E mutant tumours**: Typically locally invasive and behave like classical PTC
- **RAS mutant FVPTCs**: Tend to be encapsulated and behave more like follicular adenomas or carcinomas; should perhaps be managed and reclassified as follicular tumours
- **Hurthle cell carcinoma**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), data suggest it has a distinct genomic signature including chromosomal copy number alterations and novel mutations in mitochondrial DNA, supporting reclassification distinct from FTC

### Available Molecular Tests

Per Randolph (Ch 12, Table 12.1), the three commercially available molecular platforms with their validated performance data:

| Test | Company | Methodology | Cancer Prevalence | Sensitivity | Specificity | NPV | PPV |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Afirma GSC (2017) | Veracyte, Inc. | Microarray expression (1115 core genes); RNA-Seq for SNV/fusions | 24% | 91% | 68% | 96% | 47% |
| ThyroSeq v3 (2017) | UPMC / CBLPath | NGS of 112 genes (12,135 SNVs/indels, >120 fusions, 90 gene expression, 10 CNV regions) | 28% | 94% | 82% | 97% | 66% |
| ThyGeNEXT-ThyraMIR (2018) | Interpace Diagnostics | NGS of 10 genes (42 SNVs) + 28 fusions; reflex to 10-miRNA classifier | 32% | 89% | 85% | 94% | 74% |

### Afirma Gene Expression Classifier (GEC/GSC)

**Alexander et al. (2012) — NEJM Landmark Study**:
- Prospective multicentre validation (n=265 indeterminate nodules)
- 167-gene expression classifier trained on a panel of benign vs malignant thyroid tissue
- Per Randolph (Ch 12), the GEC was designed to achieve a high sensitivity and NPV of 95%, commensurate with the false-negative rate for benign cytology of 5% accepted by NCCN guidelines
- Samples first underwent a 25-gene screen for medullary thyroid carcinoma, parathyroid tissue, and metastatic tumours; negatives were submitted to the principal 142-gene classifier
- Results:
  - Bethesda III: NPV 95%, sensitivity 90%
  - Bethesda IV: NPV 94%, sensitivity 90%
  - Specificity: 52% (limitation — many benign nodules classified as "suspicious")
  - Per Randolph (Ch 12): Of seven false-negative results, six were due to insufficient sampling of follicular epithelium rather than a failure of the classifier algorithm itself
- **Clinical impact**: A "benign" Afirma result can avoid diagnostic surgery
- **Hurthle cell limitation**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), the GEC had problematic accuracy for Hurthle cell (oncocytic) nodules — only 19% of Hurthle cell adenomas received a GEC-benign result compared with 58% of follicular adenomas without Hurthle cell features. Specificity for Hurthle cell nodules was as low as 7.5% in one study

**Evolution to Afirma GSC**:
- Per Randolph (Ch 12), in 2017 Veracyte released the GSC using NGS technology and enhanced machine learning to evaluate expression of more than 10,000 genes, of which 1115 core genes drive the ensemble classification model (including 140 of the 142 original GEC genes)
- GSC incorporates RNA-Seq to detect point mutations, gene fusions, and genomic copy number alterations including loss of heterozygosity
- Samples are first screened for parathyroid tissue, MTC, BRAF V600E, and RET-PTC1/3 fusions — positives are reported as such without entering the main classifier
- **Hurthle cell improvement**: Dedicated Hurthle cell index and Hurthle cell neoplasm index classifiers were incorporated, using gene expression and mitochondrial transcript analysis. Specificity for Hurthle cells improved from 11.8% (GEC) to 58.8% (GSC)
- **Benign call rate (BCR)**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), the GSC achieved a higher BCR than the GEC (65.8% vs 47.9%, p<0.001), with a 45-66% relative reduction in indeterminate nodules progressing to surgery
- **Xpression Atlas (XA)**: For GSC-suspicious nodules, uses RNA-Seq to detect variants and fusions in >500 genes; does not assess TERT promoter mutations (noncoded DNA region)

### ThyroSeq v3

- **Next-generation sequencing panel**: 112 genes analysed for point mutations (12,135 SNVs and indels), gene fusions (>120), copy number alterations (10 CNV regions for FNA), and gene expression alterations (90 genes)
- **Dual utility**: Both rule-in (high PPV) and rule-out (high NPV)
- **Genomic Classifier (GC) score**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), a proprietary algorithmic analysis assigns each detected abnormality a value from 0 to 2 commensurate with its association with malignancy; values are summed. The test is reported positive if GC score ≥1.5, negative if <1.5
- **Validation**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), Steward et al. (JAMA Oncol 2018) — prospective blinded multicentre study of 286 FNA samples (Bethesda III-V). 10% of samples were inadequate (low total nucleic acids or insufficient thyroid cells). Of 257 evaluable samples, 152 (59%) tested negative and 105 (41%) tested positive. 147/152 negative nodules were benign on histology. All five false-negative results (four PTC, one minimally invasive FTC) fell into the ATA low-risk category. For Bethesda III/IV (n=247): sensitivity 94%, specificity 82%, NPV 97%, PPV 66%
- **NPV stability**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), the NPV remains ≥95% for disease prevalence up to 40% for Bethesda III and 60% for Bethesda IV. For Bethesda III/IV, the 3-5% cancer risk with a negative test is comparable to the cancer risk in Bethesda II nodules
- **Benign call rate**: 74.1% for Bethesda III/IV
- **Hurthle cell performance**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), all 10 Hurthle cell carcinomas were correctly identified in the validation study; in a separate analysis, 39/42 (93%) Hurthle cell malignancies were correctly identified, with sensitivity 93% and specificity 69%. ThyroSeq v3 may help determine the extent of surgery for Hurthle cell neoplasms
- **Sample flexibility**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), can be performed on dedicated FNA pass (in ThyroSeqPreserve, stable up to 12 months frozen), cell block slides (requires >300 cells on H&E review), or FFPE specimens

### ThyGenX/ThyraMIR

- **Two-step approach**:
  1. ThyGeNEXT: NGS of 10 genes (42 SNVs) and 28 gene fusions, plus markers for MTC and parathyroid tissue
  2. If ThyGeNEXT negative: ThyraMIR (10-miRNA classifier) applied — generates a binary "low risk" or "high risk" reading
- **Version history**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), evolved from miRInform (4 genes, 3 fusions) → ThyGenX (8 genes) → ThyGeNEXT (10 genes, 38 fusions)
- **Validation**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), Labourier et al. (J Clin Endocrinol Metab 2015) — 109 Bethesda III/IV nodules from 12 endocrinology centres with 32% malignancy prevalence; 61% of specimens were called benign with 6% residual risk of malignancy
- **Performance**: NPV 94%, PPV 74%, sensitivity 89%, specificity 85%
- **Practical advantage**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), specimens are stable at room temperature for up to 6 weeks without refrigeration; testing can also be performed on cytology slides

### Choosing a Molecular Test: Guidance from Randolph

Per Randolph (Ch 12), selection of a molecular test should consider:
1. **Local malignancy rates** for each Bethesda category — clinicians should calculate their own PPV/NPV as PPV and NPV are influenced by disease prevalence. At higher prevalence, PPV increases and NPV decreases
2. **Clinical context** of the patient, insurance coverage, and cost
3. **When molecular testing is less useful**: When nodule size, suspicious radiographic features, compressive symptoms, or lymph node findings warrant surgery regardless, or when patients have a strong upfront preference for surgery versus surveillance
4. **Cost-effectiveness**: Remains an area of active research; molecular testing predominantly saves costs by avoiding completion thyroidectomy, though long-term nodule surveillance costs may offset this

## BRAF V600E Mutation in Papillary Thyroid Cancer

### Molecular Biology

- **Gene**: BRAF (v-Raf murine sarcoma viral oncogene homologue B)
- **Location**: Chromosome 7q24
- **Mutation**: Thymine-to-adenine transversion at nucleotide 1799 → valine-to-glutamate substitution at codon 600 (V600E)
- **Pathway**: Constitutive activation of MAPK/ERK signalling cascade (RAS → RAF → MEK → ERK)
- **Mechanism**: Mimics phosphorylation of the activation segment, rendering the kinase constitutively active
- Per Randolph (Ch 12): BRAF V600E is the most prevalent alteration in PTC, followed by alterations in tyrosine kinase receptors such as RET and NTRK. BRAF V600E, TERT promoter, and P53 mutations have proven highly specific for malignancy, unlike RAS/PTEN alterations which may be found in follicular adenomas and other benign lesions

### Prevalence in Thyroid Cancer

| Histological Type | BRAF V600E Prevalence |
|---|---|
| Classical PTC | 40-45% |
| Tall cell variant PTC | 70-80% |
| Follicular variant PTC | 10-20% |
| Poorly differentiated thyroid cancer | 10-35% |
| Anaplastic thyroid cancer | 20-40% |
| Follicular thyroid cancer | Rare (<2%) |
| Medullary thyroid cancer | Absent |

### Prognostic Significance (Xing 2005)

BRAF V600E is independently associated with:
1. **Extrathyroidal extension** (OR 2.0-3.0)
2. **Lymph node metastasis** (OR 1.5-2.5)
3. **Advanced stage at presentation** (stages III/IV)
4. **Tumour recurrence** (HR 2.0-4.0)
5. **Loss of radioiodine avidity** (via downregulation of NIS — sodium-iodide symporter)
6. **RAI-refractory disease**
7. **Reduced disease-free survival**

Per Randolph (Ch 12), whereas both BRAF V600E and TERT promoter mutations are associated with higher recurrence rates individually, the combination carries a markedly higher rate of recurrence, suggesting oncogene cooperativity. Identification of these mutations in small cancers that might otherwise undergo active surveillance may change management.

### BRAF in Indeterminate Nodules

- Detection of BRAF V600E in a Bethesda III/IV nodule is essentially diagnostic of PTC (PPV >99%)
- Enables direct total thyroidectomy without diagnostic lobectomy
- However, BRAF mutation alone does not reliably exclude malignancy when absent (sensitivity ~45% for PTC)
- Per Randolph (Ch 12): RAS mutations are mutually exclusive of BRAF V600E and can be found in both FTC and FVPTC but almost never in classical PTC

### BRAF and Therapeutic Implications

- **Dabrafenib + trametinib**: FDA-approved combination for BRAF V600E-mutant anaplastic thyroid cancer (based on the ROAR basket trial). Per Randolph (Ch 12), this is the first approved genotype-specific therapy for thyroid cancer
- **Vemurafenib**: Studied in BRAF-mutant RAI-refractory differentiated thyroid cancer
- **Redifferentiation therapy**: BRAF/MEK inhibitors can restore NIS expression and RAI avidity in some patients
- **Other approved multikinase inhibitors**: Per Randolph (Ch 12), sorafenib and lenvatinib for DTC; vandetanib and cabozantinib for MTC

## Other Key Molecular Alterations

### RAS Mutations

- **Genes**: NRAS (codon 61 most common), HRAS (codon 61), KRAS (codons 12/13)
- **Prevalence**: 20-40% of follicular thyroid cancer, 10-20% of follicular variant PTC, 20-30% of NIFTP
- **Significance**: Associated with follicular-patterned neoplasms; less aggressive than BRAF
- **In indeterminate nodules**: RAS-positive nodules have ~85% malignancy rate but may include NIFTP (low-risk)
- Per Randolph (Ch 12): RAS mutations may be found in follicular adenomas and other benign lesions, creating challenges in using these signatures to differentiate benign from malignant specimens

### PAX8-PPARgamma Rearrangement

- **Prevalence**: 30-40% of follicular thyroid cancer, 5-10% of follicular variant PTC
- **Detection**: RT-PCR or FISH on FNA samples
- **Significance**: Nearly pathognomonic for follicular-patterned neoplasms
- Per Randolph (Ch 12): FTC is specifically associated with mutations in RAS, PTEN, and PIK3CA, and the PAX8-PPARgamma rearrangement

### RET/PTC Rearrangements

- **Types**: RET/PTC1 (most common, associated with radiation exposure), RET/PTC3
- **Prevalence**: 10-20% of PTC (higher in radiation-associated and paediatric PTC)
- **Clinical significance**: Generally associated with classical PTC with favourable prognosis
- Per Randolph (Ch 12): RET/PTC rearrangements tend to occur in indolent PTC tumours

### TERT Promoter Mutations

- **Mutations**: C228T and C250T in the telomerase reverse transcriptase promoter
- **Prevalence**: 7-22% of PTC, 14-36% of FTC, 29-73% of ATC
- **Significance**: When coexisting with BRAF V600E, synergistically confer the worst prognosis (highest recurrence and mortality rates)
- **BRAF + TERT**: The combination predicts aggressive behaviour more accurately than either mutation alone
- Per Randolph (Ch 12): Increased MAPK pathway output combined with TERT promoter, EIF1AX, DNA repair gene mutations (CHEK2, SWI/SNF complex), and P53 have all been implicated in de-differentiation and genomic instability, leading to loss of thyroglobulin expression and failure to respond to RAI

### De-differentiation Pathway (from Randolph Ch 12)

Per Randolph (Ch 12), the molecular alterations in thyroid cancer exist on a spectrum paralleling degree of cellular differentiation:
- **Well-differentiated (PTC/FTC)**: Relatively simple genome, predominantly MAPK pathway mutations
- **Poorly differentiated**: Accumulation of TERT, EIF1AX, and DNA repair gene mutations
- **Anaplastic (ATC)**: Most complex alterations with P53 mutations, genomic instability; clinical consequences include loss of thyroglobulin expression, RAI failure, and dismal prognosis

### NIFTP and Its Impact on Molecular Testing

Per Randolph (Ch 12), the reclassification of NIFTP from malignancy to neoplasm (2015) has important bearing on interpretation of molecular testing:
- NIFTP describes encapsulated FVPTC without capsular or vascular invasion, exhibiting indolent behaviour with excellent prognosis
- Cytologic specimens from NIFTP are frequently indeterminate (Bethesda III-V)
- NIFTP renders a "suspicious" or positive result on molecular tests, thereby lowering the PPV
- However, NIFTP is a histologic diagnosis requiring surgical excision, and its malignant potential if left untreated is unclear
- Grouping NIFTP with malignant nodules optimises test performance when results are reported in a binary fashion, because lobectomy directed by a positive result is in line with current therapeutic recommendations

## Clinical Decision Algorithm: Bethesda III with TIRADS 2

### Scenario

A patient presents with a thyroid nodule that is:
- **Cytology**: Bethesda III (AUS/FLUS)
- **Ultrasound**: ACR TIRADS 2 (not suspicious — likely benign features)

### Integrated Management

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Bethesda III malignancy risk | 10-30% |
| TIRADS 2 malignancy risk | <2% |
| Combined risk | Very low (estimated 2-5%) |
| Preferred approach | Repeat FNA in 3-6 months or molecular testing |

### Decision Pathway

1. **Repeat FNA** (first-line for Bethesda III with low-suspicion US):
   - If repeat FNA → Bethesda II: clinical follow-up (US in 12-24 months)
   - If repeat FNA → Bethesda III again: molecular testing or lobectomy (per Randolph Ch 11, only ~20% of nodules are repeatedly AUS/FLUS)
   - If repeat FNA → Bethesda IV-VI: proceed accordingly
2. **Molecular testing** (if available):
   - Afirma GSC "benign": follow clinically (NPV 96%)
   - ThyroSeq v3 negative: follow clinically (NPV 97%; per Randolph Ch 12, the residual 3-5% cancer risk is comparable to Bethesda II)
   - If positive: proceed to surgery based on specific mutation
3. **Diagnostic lobectomy** (if molecular testing unavailable or patient preference)

### Key Principle

The integration of TIRADS with Bethesda classification allows risk-adapted management. A TIRADS 2 nodule with Bethesda III carries a very low combined malignancy risk, supporting conservative follow-up or molecular testing over immediate surgery. Per Randolph (Ch 12), molecular testing is less useful when clinical factors such as nodule size, suspicious radiographic features, or compressive symptoms warrant surgery regardless.

## Illustrative Cases from Randolph

Per Randolph (Ch 12), two cases illustrate the clinical integration of molecular testing:

**Case 1**: 29-year-old woman with a 2.7 cm left thyroid nodule (ATA Intermediate Suspicion, TIRADS 4). FNA showed follicular lesion with Hurthle cell features (Bethesda III). Lymph node mapping US was negative. ThyroSeq v3 from cell block was positive. Lobectomy revealed FVPTC with capsular invasion but no angioinvasion (ATA low risk). Active surveillance initiated — demonstrating how a positive molecular result guided surgical decision-making for an indeterminate nodule.

**Case 2**: 53-year-old woman with 3.5 cm right thyroid nodule (Bethesda III) and 1.1 cm left nodule (PTC with BRAF V600E). Afirma GSC on the right nodule was "suspicious." Whereas a benign result might have led to left lobectomy only, the suspicious result led to total thyroidectomy. Right nodule proved to be NIFTP — illustrating how molecular testing informed the extent of surgery.

## Future Directions from Randolph

Per Randolph (Ch 12):
- **Prognostic molecular profiling**: Moving beyond diagnosis to prognosis, with gene panels or integrated measures of MAPK pathway output potentially serving as more robust biomarkers than individual mutations
- **Circulating tumour DNA (ctDNA)**: Circulating tumour cells and cell-free DNA have been detected in blood of patients with advanced thyroid cancers, raising the possibility of "liquid biopsy" for diagnosis and characterisation
- **Precision medicine**: Molecular testing will play a vital role in selecting patients for optimal therapies and identifying markers of treatment resistance

## Practical Summary for Exam

### Quick Reference: Bethesda Categories and Action

| Category | Risk | First-Line Action | Second-Line Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | 5-10% | Repeat FNA (US-guided) | — |
| II | 0-3% | Follow-up | Repeat FNA if growth |
| III | 10-30% | Repeat FNA / molecular testing | Lobectomy |
| IV | 25-40% | Molecular testing / lobectomy | — |
| V | 50-75% | Lobectomy (± frozen section) or TT | — |
| VI | 97-99% | Total thyroidectomy (or lobectomy for select low-risk) | — |

### High-Yield Points

1. The Bethesda System has 6 categories with progressively increasing malignancy risk
2. Bethesda III (AUS/FLUS) is the most common indeterminate category and has the widest range of malignancy risk (10-30%); per Randolph, limit AUS/FLUS to ≤10% of all FNAs
3. Molecular testing has two paradigms: **rule-out** (Afirma — high NPV) and **rule-in/rule-out** (ThyroSeq v3 — high NPV and PPV)
4. BRAF V600E in an indeterminate nodule is virtually diagnostic of PTC (PPV >99%)
5. BRAF V600E is associated with aggressive features: ETE, LNM, RAI-refractory disease, recurrence
6. BRAF + TERT promoter co-mutation predicts the worst prognosis (oncogene cooperativity per Randolph)
7. NIFTP reclassification (2016) lowered the implied malignancy risk for Bethesda III/IV; per Randolph, NIFTP renders positive molecular results and is best grouped with malignancy for test interpretation since it requires surgical excision
8. The 2023 third edition of Bethesda provides subcategories for AUS (nuclear vs architectural atypia)
9. Integration of TIRADS with Bethesda enables risk-adapted management
10. RAS mutations are associated with follicular-patterned neoplasms and may include NIFTP
11. Per Randolph (Ch 12): ThyroSeq v3 NPV remains ≥95% for disease prevalence up to 40% (Bethesda III) and 60% (Bethesda IV); a negative result carries only 3-5% cancer risk — comparable to Bethesda II
12. Per Randolph (Ch 12): Afirma GSC improved Hurthle cell specificity from 11.8% (GEC) to 58.8%, and BCR from 47.9% to 65.8% — resulting in 45-66% relative reduction in surgeries
13. Per Randolph (Ch 11): FDG-PET thyroid incidentalomas carry ~35% malignancy risk; sestamibi incidentalomas carry 22-66% risk — both require FNA
14. Per Randolph (Ch 12): Molecular testing is validated only for Bethesda III/IV; application to other categories incurs excessive cost with little benefit

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> - Per Randolph (Ch 11): A "nondiagnostic" FNA does not mean benign — it means repeat. For repeatedly nondiagnostic cystic nodules with solid components, surgical excision is warranted (8-19% malignancy rate)
> - Per Randolph (Ch 11): Smaller needles (25-27 G), shorter dwell time, and rapid oscillations (2-5/sec) optimise FNA yield
> - Per Randolph (Ch 12): The 7-gene panel (ThyroSeq v0) had high PPV but low NPV; progressive expansion to 112 genes (ThyroSeq v3) has achieved both high PPV and NPV — illustrating the evolution from "rule-in only" to dual-utility testing
> - Per Randolph (Ch 12): When applying molecular testing, know your local malignancy rates — PPV increases and NPV decreases with higher disease prevalence
> - Per Randolph (Ch 12): Dabrafenib + trametinib for BRAF V600E-mutant ATC is the first genotype-specific approved therapy for thyroid cancer
> - Per Randolph (Ch 11): Toxic ("hot") nodules on radionuclide scan do not require FNA — malignancy risk is exceedingly low. Detection of "fire-flare" hyperfunctioning thyrocytes on cytology supports follow-up over surgery
