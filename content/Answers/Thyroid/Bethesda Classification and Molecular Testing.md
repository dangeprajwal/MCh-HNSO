## The Bethesda System for Reporting Thyroid Cytopathology

### Historical Context

Before the Bethesda System, thyroid FNA reporting lacked uniformity. In 2007, the National Cancer Institute hosted a multidisciplinary conference in Bethesda, Maryland, to develop a standardised reporting terminology. The resulting Bethesda System for Reporting Thyroid Cytopathology (TBSRTC) was first published in 2009 (Cibas & Ali) and has undergone two revisions: the 2017 second edition and the 2023 third edition.

### The Six Diagnostic Categories

| Category | Designation | Implied Malignancy Risk (2017) | Adjusted Risk (excl. NIFTP) | Recommended Management |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Nondiagnostic / Unsatisfactory | 5-10% | 5-10% | Repeat FNA with US guidance |
| II | Benign | 0-3% | 0-3% | Clinical and US follow-up |
| III | Atypia of Undetermined Significance (AUS) / Follicular Lesion of Undetermined Significance (FLUS) | 10-30% | 6-18% | Repeat FNA, molecular testing, or lobectomy |
| IV | Follicular Neoplasm / Suspicious for Follicular Neoplasm (FN/SFN) | 25-40% | 10-40% | Molecular testing or lobectomy |
| V | Suspicious for Malignancy | 50-75% | 45-60% | Lobectomy or total thyroidectomy |
| VI | Malignant | 97-99% | 97-99% | Total thyroidectomy (or lobectomy for select cases) |

### Key Definitions and Adequacy Criteria

**Adequacy requirements for Bethesda II (Benign)**:
- Minimum 6 groups of well-visualised follicular cells
- Each group containing at least 10 cells
- Exceptions: colloid nodules with abundant colloid may be adequate with fewer cells; thyroiditis specimens with lymphocytes are adequate even without follicular cells

**Bethesda I (Nondiagnostic)**:
- Insufficient follicular cells
- Obscuring blood or artefact
- Cyst fluid only without adequate cellular component
- Recommendation: repeat FNA in 4-6 weeks (preferably US-guided)

### Bethesda III (AUS/FLUS) — The Grey Zone

Bethesda III is the most controversial category and accounts for approximately 5-15% of thyroid FNAs. Key features:

- **Cytological features**: Nuclear atypia falling short of suspicious for malignancy, or architectural atypia with microfollicular pattern but insufficient for Bethesda IV
- **Subcategories** (2023 third edition):
  - Nuclear atypia (higher risk, closer to PTC)
  - Architectural atypia (follicular pattern)
  - Combined nuclear and architectural atypia
- **Management options**:
  1. Repeat FNA (resolves diagnosis in ~50% of cases)
  2. Molecular testing (Afirma GSC or ThyroSeq v3)
  3. Diagnostic lobectomy
- **Key decision factor**: TIRADS score integrated with Bethesda category

### Bethesda IV (FN/SFN)

- **Cytological pattern**: Predominantly microfollicular architecture with scant or absent colloid
- **Includes**: Hurthle cell (oncocytic) neoplasm
- **Core issue**: Cannot distinguish follicular adenoma from follicular carcinoma on cytology (requires assessment of capsular/vascular invasion on histology)
- **Management**: Diagnostic lobectomy or molecular testing

## Molecular Testing in Indeterminate Thyroid Nodules

### Rationale for Molecular Testing

The fundamental problem with Bethesda III/IV nodules:
- Malignancy rates of 10-40% mean that 60-90% of patients undergoing diagnostic surgery have benign disease
- Molecular testing aims to reduce unnecessary diagnostic surgeries
- Two paradigms: **rule-out** (high NPV) and **rule-in** (high PPV)

### Available Molecular Tests

| Test | Type | Approach | NPV | PPV | Clinical Utility |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Afirma GEC (1st gen) | Gene expression | Rule-out | 94-95% | 37-47% | Benign result avoids surgery |
| Afirma GSC (2nd gen) | Genomic sequencing | Rule-out + improved specificity | 96% | 47-64% | Improved specificity over GEC |
| ThyroSeq v3 | NGS panel (112 genes) | Rule-in and rule-out | 97% | 66-82% | Identifies specific mutations; dual utility |
| ThyGenX/ThyraMIR | Mutation panel + miRNA | Rule-in complement | 94% | 74% | Two-step sequential testing |

### Afirma Gene Expression Classifier (GEC/GSC)

**Alexander et al. (2012) — NEJM Landmark Study**:
- Prospective multicentre validation (n=265 indeterminate nodules)
- 167-gene expression classifier trained on a panel of benign vs malignant thyroid tissue
- Results:
  - Bethesda III: NPV 95%, sensitivity 90%
  - Bethesda IV: NPV 94%, sensitivity 90%
  - Specificity: 52% (limitation — many benign nodules classified as "suspicious")
- **Clinical impact**: A "benign" Afirma result can avoid diagnostic surgery
- **Evolution**: Afirma GSC (Genomic Sequencing Classifier) replaced GEC in 2017 with improved specificity (68% vs 52%) while maintaining high NPV

### ThyroSeq v3

- **Next-generation sequencing panel**: 112 genes analysed for point mutations, gene fusions, copy number alterations, and gene expression
- **Dual utility**: Both rule-in (high PPV) and rule-out (high NPV)
- **Key advantage**: Identifies the specific mutation driving the nodule, enabling risk stratification
- **Validation**: Steward et al. — multicentre blinded prospective study (Nikiforov et al., 2018)
- **Molecular result categories**: Negative, Currently Positive (with specific mutation), Score-based risk

### ThyGenX/ThyraMIR

- **Two-step approach**:
  1. ThyGenX: tests for BRAF, RAS, RET/PTC, PAX8-PPARgamma mutations
  2. If ThyGenX negative: ThyraMIR (10-miRNA classifier) applied
- **Advantage**: Sequential testing reduces cost
- **Performance**: NPV 94%, PPV 74%

## BRAF V600E Mutation in Papillary Thyroid Cancer

### Molecular Biology

- **Gene**: BRAF (v-Raf murine sarcoma viral oncogene homologue B)
- **Location**: Chromosome 7q24
- **Mutation**: Thymine-to-adenine transversion at nucleotide 1799 → valine-to-glutamate substitution at codon 600 (V600E)
- **Pathway**: Constitutive activation of MAPK/ERK signalling cascade (RAS → RAF → MEK → ERK)
- **Mechanism**: Mimics phosphorylation of the activation segment, rendering the kinase constitutively active

### Prevalence in Thyroid Cancer

| Histological Type | BRAF V600E Prevalence |
|---|---|
| Classical PTC | 40-45% |
| Tall cell variant PTC | 70-80% |
| Follicular variant PTC | 10-20% |
| Poorly differentiated thyroid cancer | 10-35% |
| Anaplastic thyroid cancer | 20-40% |
| Follicular thyroid cancer | Rare (<2%) |
| Medullary thyroid cancer | Absent |

### Prognostic Significance (Xing 2005)

BRAF V600E is independently associated with:
1. **Extrathyroidal extension** (OR 2.0-3.0)
2. **Lymph node metastasis** (OR 1.5-2.5)
3. **Advanced stage at presentation** (stages III/IV)
4. **Tumour recurrence** (HR 2.0-4.0)
5. **Loss of radioiodine avidity** (via downregulation of NIS — sodium-iodide symporter)
6. **RAI-refractory disease**
7. **Reduced disease-free survival**

### BRAF in Indeterminate Nodules

- Detection of BRAF V600E in a Bethesda III/IV nodule is essentially diagnostic of PTC (PPV >99%)
- Enables direct total thyroidectomy without diagnostic lobectomy
- However, BRAF mutation alone does not reliably exclude malignancy when absent (sensitivity ~45% for PTC)

### BRAF and Therapeutic Implications

- **Dabrafenib + trametinib**: FDA-approved combination for BRAF V600E-mutant anaplastic thyroid cancer (based on the ROAR basket trial)
- **Vemurafenib**: Studied in BRAF-mutant RAI-refractory differentiated thyroid cancer
- **Redifferentiation therapy**: BRAF/MEK inhibitors can restore NIS expression and RAI avidity in some patients

## Other Key Molecular Alterations

### RAS Mutations

- **Genes**: NRAS (codon 61 most common), HRAS (codon 61), KRAS (codons 12/13)
- **Prevalence**: 20-40% of follicular thyroid cancer, 10-20% of follicular variant PTC, 20-30% of NIFTP
- **Significance**: Associated with follicular-patterned neoplasms; less aggressive than BRAF
- **In indeterminate nodules**: RAS-positive nodules have ~85% malignancy rate but may include NIFTP (low-risk)

### PAX8-PPARgamma Rearrangement

- **Prevalence**: 30-40% of follicular thyroid cancer, 5-10% of follicular variant PTC
- **Detection**: RT-PCR or FISH on FNA samples
- **Significance**: Nearly pathognomonic for follicular-patterned neoplasms

### RET/PTC Rearrangements

- **Types**: RET/PTC1 (most common, associated with radiation exposure), RET/PTC3
- **Prevalence**: 10-20% of PTC (higher in radiation-associated and paediatric PTC)
- **Clinical significance**: Generally associated with classical PTC with favourable prognosis

### TERT Promoter Mutations

- **Mutations**: C228T and C250T in the telomerase reverse transcriptase promoter
- **Prevalence**: 7-22% of PTC, 14-36% of FTC, 29-73% of ATC
- **Significance**: When coexisting with BRAF V600E, synergistically confer the worst prognosis (highest recurrence and mortality rates)
- **BRAF + TERT**: The combination predicts aggressive behaviour more accurately than either mutation alone

## Clinical Decision Algorithm: Bethesda III with TIRADS 2

### Scenario

A patient presents with a thyroid nodule that is:
- **Cytology**: Bethesda III (AUS/FLUS)
- **Ultrasound**: ACR TIRADS 2 (not suspicious — likely benign features)

### Integrated Management

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Bethesda III malignancy risk | 10-30% |
| TIRADS 2 malignancy risk | <2% |
| Combined risk | Very low (estimated 2-5%) |
| Preferred approach | Repeat FNA in 3-6 months or molecular testing |

### Decision Pathway

1. **Repeat FNA** (first-line for Bethesda III with low-suspicion US):
   - If repeat FNA → Bethesda II: clinical follow-up (US in 12-24 months)
   - If repeat FNA → Bethesda III again: molecular testing or lobectomy
   - If repeat FNA → Bethesda IV-VI: proceed accordingly
2. **Molecular testing** (if available):
   - Afirma GSC "benign": follow clinically (NPV 96%)
   - ThyroSeq v3 negative: follow clinically (NPV 97%)
   - If positive: proceed to surgery based on specific mutation
3. **Diagnostic lobectomy** (if molecular testing unavailable or patient preference)

### Key Principle

The integration of TIRADS with Bethesda classification allows risk-adapted management. A TIRADS 2 nodule with Bethesda III carries a very low combined malignancy risk, supporting conservative follow-up or molecular testing over immediate surgery.

## Practical Summary for Exam

### Quick Reference: Bethesda Categories and Action

| Category | Risk | First-Line Action | Second-Line Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | 5-10% | Repeat FNA (US-guided) | — |
| II | 0-3% | Follow-up | Repeat FNA if growth |
| III | 10-30% | Repeat FNA / molecular testing | Lobectomy |
| IV | 25-40% | Molecular testing / lobectomy | — |
| V | 50-75% | Lobectomy (± frozen section) or TT | — |
| VI | 97-99% | Total thyroidectomy (or lobectomy for select low-risk) | — |

### High-Yield Points

1. The Bethesda System has 6 categories with progressively increasing malignancy risk
2. Bethesda III (AUS/FLUS) is the most common indeterminate category and has the widest range of malignancy risk (10-30%)
3. Molecular testing has two paradigms: **rule-out** (Afirma — high NPV) and **rule-in/rule-out** (ThyroSeq v3 — high NPV and PPV)
4. BRAF V600E in an indeterminate nodule is virtually diagnostic of PTC (PPV >99%)
5. BRAF V600E is associated with aggressive features: ETE, LNM, RAI-refractory disease, recurrence
6. BRAF + TERT promoter co-mutation predicts the worst prognosis
7. NIFTP reclassification (2016) lowered the implied malignancy risk for Bethesda III/IV
8. The 2023 third edition of Bethesda provides subcategories for AUS (nuclear vs architectural atypia)
9. Integration of TIRADS with Bethesda enables risk-adapted management
10. RAS mutations are associated with follicular-patterned neoplasms and may include NIFTP
