## Rationale for Dynamic Risk Stratification

The fundamental limitation of all initial staging systems (AJCC/TNM, MACIS, AMES, AGES) is that they are **static** -- they classify patients at the time of initial presentation and do not change over time (Randolph, Ch24). In clinical practice, risk estimates clearly evolve depending on the response to therapy and biological behaviour of each tumour. A "low-risk" patient who develops a rapidly rising Tg or new pulmonary nodules would forever remain classified as "low risk" by initial staging, when in reality the patient is at increasing risk of progressive structural disease and death. Conversely, a "high-risk" patient who achieves an excellent response has dramatically better outcomes than the initial estimate would suggest.

The **ATA Response to Therapy system** addresses this by overlaying the patient's individual response onto the initial risk estimate, creating a **dynamic, ongoing risk assessment** that is updated at each follow-up visit (Randolph, Ch24). Importantly, the response to therapy assessment can be applied as early as **immediately after thyroidectomy** (e.g., using postoperative Tg) as well as after the traditional 2-year timepoint (Randolph, Ch24).

## ATA Initial Risk Stratification System

The ATA 2015 guidelines classify DTC patients into three risk categories at the time of initial treatment, based on clinicopathological features. This initial risk estimate guides the intensity of treatment and follow-up. A comprehensive initial risk stratification requires review of all available data obtained up to **4 months after initial diagnosis and treatment**, integrating preoperative findings (physical examination, vocal cord function, cross-sectional imaging), intraoperative findings (gross ETE, completeness of resection, nodal status), pathology findings (histology, vascular invasion, macroscopic ETE, molecular characterisation), laboratory findings (postoperative serum Tg), and functional/structural imaging (RAI scans, FDG-PET, neck US, CT scans) (Randolph, Ch24 -- Table 24.1).

### Low Risk of Recurrence (~5% recurrence)

Criteria (all must be met):
- Intrathyroidal PTC (with or without BRAF V600E mutation alone)
- Tumour <=4 cm
- No aggressive histological variant (no tall cell, hobnail, columnar, etc.)
- No vascular invasion
- No lymph node metastases, OR <=5 pathologic N1 micrometastases (<0.2 cm in largest dimension)
- No distant metastases
- Complete macroscopic resection (R0 or R1)
- No RAI uptake outside the thyroid bed on post-therapy WBS (if performed)
- Clinical N0 or pathologic N0, or <=5 micrometastatic N1
- Intrathyroidal, encapsulated follicular variant of PTC (Randolph, Ch24 -- Box 24.1)
- Intrathyroidal, well-differentiated FTC with capsular invasion and no or minimal (<4 foci) vascular invasion (Randolph, Ch24)
- Intrathyroidal papillary microcarcinoma, unifocal or multifocal, including BRAF V600E mutated (Randolph, Ch24)

**Persistent/recurrent disease rates** (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.2): 14--22% overall, but **80% of recurrence in this group is biochemical only** (elevated Tg without structural correlate).

### Intermediate Risk of Recurrence (5--20% recurrence)

Criteria (any one of the following):
- Microscopic invasion of tumour into the perithyroidal soft tissues (minor ETE)
- Aggressive histological variant (tall cell, hobnail, columnar cell, etc.)
- Papillary thyroid cancer with vascular invasion
- >5 involved lymph nodes, all <3 cm in largest dimension (Clinical N1 or >5 pathologic N1)
- Multifocal papillary microcarcinoma with ETE and BRAF V600E mutated (if known) (Randolph, Ch24 -- Box 24.1)
- RAI-avid metastatic foci in the neck on post-therapy WBS

**Persistent/recurrent disease rates** (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.2): 37--48% overall; 55% of recurrence has a structural correlate (compared with 20% in low-risk). This is the largest group (~50% of patients in Randolph's cohort), and the dynamic reclassification has its most dramatic impact here.

### High Risk of Recurrence (>20% recurrence)

Criteria (any one of the following):
- Macroscopic invasion of tumour into the perithyroidal soft tissues (gross ETE)
- Incomplete tumour resection (R2)
- Distant metastases (M1)
- Pathologic N1 with any metastatic lymph node >=3 cm in largest dimension
- Postoperative serum Tg suggestive of distant metastases
- Follicular thyroid cancer with extensive vascular invasion (>4 foci of vascular invasion) (Randolph, Ch24 -- Box 24.1)

**Persistent/recurrent disease rates** (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.2): 69--86% overall; 60--72% of recurrence is structurally identifiable disease. **Age further stratifies this group**: the proportion of structural incomplete responders is significantly smaller among younger (<55 years) patients compared with older patients (33% vs 53%, p = 0.002), and disease-specific survival is also significantly better for younger patients (74% vs 12%, p < 0.001) (Randolph, Ch24).

## Dynamic Risk Stratification: Response to Therapy

The pivotal innovation introduced by Tuttle (2010) and incorporated into the ATA 2015 guidelines is that the initial risk estimate is not fixed but rather is modified at each follow-up visit based on the patient's response to therapy. This "dynamic risk stratification" reclassifies patients into four response categories (Randolph, Ch24 -- Table 24.2).

### 1. Excellent Response

**Definition (after total thyroidectomy + RAI ablation):**
- Negative imaging (no structural or functional evidence of disease)
- Suppressed Tg <0.2 ng/mL or stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL
- Anti-Tg antibodies absent

**Definition (after total thyroidectomy WITHOUT RAI)** (Randolph, Ch47 -- Table 47.3):
- Negative imaging
- Suppressed Tg <0.2 ng/mL or stimulated Tg <2 ng/mL
- No thyroglobulin antibodies

**Definition (after thyroid lobectomy)** (Randolph, Ch47 -- Table 47.3):
- Negative imaging
- Tg <30 ng/mL
- No thyroglobulin antibodies

**Clinical Significance:**
- Risk of recurrence: 1--4%; disease-specific mortality <1% (Randolph, Ch24)
- Can de-escalate follow-up intensity and TSH suppression
- Approximately 85--90% of ATA low-risk patients achieve excellent response within 2 years
- 50--60% of ATA intermediate-risk patients reclassified to excellent response over time
- Even ATA high-risk patients who achieve excellent response see risk of persistent/recurrent structural disease decrease from 66% to **14%** (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.3)

### 2. Indeterminate Response

**Definition (after total thyroidectomy + RAI ablation):**
- Nonspecific findings on imaging (not clearly benign or malignant)
- Faint uptake in thyroid bed on RAI scanning
- Nonstimulated Tg detectable but <1 ng/mL, or stimulated Tg detectable but <10 ng/mL
- Anti-Tg antibodies stable or declining in absence of structural/functional disease

**Definition (after total thyroidectomy WITHOUT RAI)** (Randolph, Ch47 -- Table 47.3):
- Nonspecific findings on imaging
- Basal Tg 0.2--5 ng/mL or stimulated Tg 2--10 ng/mL
- Stable or declining Tg antibodies over time

**Definition (after thyroid lobectomy)** (Randolph, Ch47 -- Table 47.3):
- Nonspecific findings on imaging
- Stable or declining Tg antibodies over time

**Clinical Significance:**
- 15--20% will have structurally identifiable disease on follow-up; in the remainder, nonspecific changes remain stable or resolve (Randolph, Ch24)
- Disease-specific mortality <1%
- Most patients (80--90%) remain stable or reclassify to excellent response over time
- Requires continued surveillance but NOT automatic additional treatment
- Observation with serial Tg and imaging is appropriate

### 3. Biochemical Incomplete Response

**Definition (after total thyroidectomy + RAI ablation):**
- Negative imaging (no structural disease identified)
- Suppressed Tg >=1 ng/mL or stimulated Tg >=10 ng/mL
- Rising anti-Tg antibody levels

**Definition (after total thyroidectomy WITHOUT RAI)** (Randolph, Ch47 -- Table 47.3):
- Negative imaging
- Basal Tg >5 ng/mL or stimulated Tg >10 ng/mL
- Rising Tg levels over time with stable TSH, or rising thyroglobulin antibodies

**Definition (after thyroid lobectomy)** (Randolph, Ch47 -- Table 47.3):
- Negative imaging
- Basal Tg >30 ng/mL
- Rising Tg levels over time with stable TSH, or rising thyroglobulin antibodies

**Clinical Significance:**
- At least 30% spontaneously evolve to NED (Randolph, Ch24)
- ~20% achieve NED after additional therapy; ~20% develop structurally identifiable disease
- Disease-specific mortality <1% (Randolph, Ch24)
- Management: if associated with **stable or declining** Tg values -- continued observation with ongoing TSH suppression; **rising Tg or anti-Tg antibody** values should prompt additional investigations and potentially additional therapies (Randolph, Ch24 -- Table 24.2)
- Consider FDG-PET/CT if Tg elevated but no RAI-avid disease (possible loss of differentiation); sensitivity of FDG-PET/CT is 83% for non-RAI-avid DTC (Randolph, Ch47)
- FDG-PET/CT recommended when Tg >10 ng/mL; threshold should be lowered for poorly differentiated tumours that produce less Tg (Randolph, Ch47)

### 4. Structural Incomplete Response

**Definition:**
- Persistent or newly identified locoregional or distant metastases on imaging
- Any Tg level (typically elevated but can be low with anti-Tg antibodies)
- Structural disease confirmed by imaging (US, CT, MRI, PET/CT) with or without biopsy

**Clinical Significance:**
- 50--85% continue to have persistent disease despite additional therapy (Randolph, Ch24)
- Disease-specific death rates as high as **11% with locoregional metastases** and **50% with structural distant metastases** (Randolph, Ch24 -- Table 24.2)
- Management depends on size, location, rate of growth, RAI avidity, FDG avidity, and specific pathology of the structural lesions (Randolph, Ch24)
- Options: surgery, RAI (if avid), external beam RT, targeted therapy, observation (if stable/minimal disease)

## Thyroglobulin (Tg) as a Tumour Marker

### Principles

- Tg is produced exclusively by thyroid follicular cells (normal and malignant)
- After total thyroidectomy and RAI ablation, Tg should become undetectable
- Detectable or rising Tg indicates persistent/recurrent disease
- Tg has extremely high sensitivity and specificity when measured in the absence of anti-Tg antibodies
- Serum Tg is the **most sensitive means** of determining disease-free status for DTC (Randolph, Ch47)

### Unstimulated (Suppressed) Tg

- Measured while patient is on levothyroxine therapy
- Less sensitive than stimulated Tg
- Useful for serial monitoring -- trending is more important than absolute value
- Suppressed Tg <0.2 ng/mL: excellent response
- Suppressed Tg 0.2--1.0 ng/mL: indeterminate response
- Suppressed Tg >=1 ng/mL: biochemical incomplete response
- In low-risk patients, suppressed Tg is sufficient in most cases; rhTSH-stimulated Tg can be reserved for patients with Tg between 0.2 and 1 ng/mL (Randolph, Ch47)

### Stimulated Tg

- Measured after TSH stimulation (THW or rhTSH)
- More sensitive -- detects smaller volumes of residual disease
- Stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL: excellent response (98% chance of being disease-free)
- Stimulated Tg 1--10 ng/mL: indeterminate response
- Stimulated Tg >=10 ng/mL: biochemical incomplete response
- **Optimal negative predictive value for NED** is achieved by combining a stimulated Tg with negative imaging (usually neck US) -- this combination predicts NED at final follow-up in 97% of low-risk, 94% of intermediate-risk, but only 82% of high-risk patients (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.4)
- After total thyroidectomy **without RAI**: stimulated Tg <2 ng/mL defines excellent response (higher threshold due to residual normal thyroid remnant) (Randolph, Ch47 -- Table 47.3)
- After **lobectomy**: Tg <30 ng/mL defines excellent response; rising Tg and/or the appearance of initially absent anti-Tg antibodies should alert to possible recurrence (Randolph, Ch47)

### Anti-Thyroglobulin Antibodies (TgAb)

- Present in 20--25% of DTC patients (higher than general population ~10%)
- Interfere with Tg measurement -- cause falsely low Tg on immunometric assays
- **TgAb trend serves as a surrogate tumour marker:**
  - Declining TgAb: favourable -- suggests decreasing tumour antigen stimulation
  - Stable or rising TgAb: unfavourable -- suggests persistent/recurrent disease
- TgAb typically decline and disappear over 2--3 years in disease-free patients

## TSH Suppression Guidelines

TSH suppression is a cornerstone of DTC management, based on the principle that TSH stimulates growth of differentiated thyroid cancer cells. The degree of suppression is tailored to disease risk.

### Targets by Risk Category

| Risk / Response | TSH Target (mIU/L) | Duration | Evidence Level (Randolph) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High-risk** (active disease) | <0.1 | Indefinite (while disease present) | Moderate-quality evidence showing improved disease-specific survival (Randolph, Ch47) |
| **Intermediate-risk** (initial, before response assessment) | 0.1--0.5 | Until response to therapy evaluation | Weak recommendation, low-level evidence (Randolph, Ch47) |
| **Low-risk** (initial, before response assessment) | 0.1--0.5 | Until response to therapy evaluation | Weak recommendation, low-level evidence (Randolph, Ch47) |
| **Excellent response** (any initial risk) | 0.5--2.0 | After confirmed excellent response | TSH suppression will not decrease the already low recurrence rate but may cause adverse effects (Randolph, Ch47) |
| **Indeterminate response** | 0.1--0.5 | Until reclassification | |
| **Biochemical incomplete** | <0.1 | Until further assessment | |
| **Structural incomplete** | <0.1 | Indefinite | |
| **After lobectomy** (low-risk) | 0.5--2.0 | Ongoing | ~73% of lobectomy patients require levothyroxine supplementation to maintain TSH in range (Randolph, Ch47) |

Key point from Randolph Ch24 case example: once an intermediate-risk patient achieves an excellent response to therapy, TSH target should be relaxed to **0.5--2.0 mIU/L** -- the patient is followed as a "low-risk" patient based on the revised 2% risk estimate, not the initial 18% risk estimate.

### Risks of TSH Suppression

- **Cardiovascular:** Atrial fibrillation (3-fold increased risk with TSH <0.1), increased heart rate, left ventricular hypertrophy
- **Skeletal:** Accelerated bone loss, increased fracture risk (particularly postmenopausal women) -- osteoporosis (Randolph, Ch47)
- **Quality of life:** Anxiety, nervousness, tremor, heat intolerance, insomnia (Randolph, Ch47)
- De-escalation of TSH suppression in patients achieving excellent response reduces these iatrogenic risks

## Prognostic Scoring Systems

### MACIS Score (Mayo Clinic)

Developed for papillary thyroid cancer by Hay et al. (1993). Randolph Ch47 provides the formula:

- **M** = Distant **M**etastases (if present, add 3 points)
- **A** = **A**ge (if >=40 years: 0.08 x age; if <=39 years: 3.1)
- **C** = **C**ompleteness of resection (if incomplete, add 1 point)
- **I** = **I**nvasion (if locally invasive/T4, add 1 point)
- **S** = **S**ize (0.3 x tumour size in cm)

| MACIS Score | 20-year DSS |
|---|---|
| <6.0 | 99% |
| 6.0--6.99 | 89% |
| 7.0--7.99 | 56% |
| >=8.0 | 27% (Randolph, Ch47) |

~85% of patients with MACIS scores below 6 had no excess mortality over rates predicted for control subjects in the general population (Randolph, Ch47).

### AMES (Lahey Clinic)

- **A**ge (<40 men, <50 women = low risk)
- **M**etastases (distant metastases = high risk)
- **E**xtent (extrathyroidal extension = high risk)
- **S**ize (>5 cm = high risk)

### AJCC/TNM 8th Edition

Key changes from 7th to 8th edition (Randolph, Ch24):
- Age cutoff raised from 45 to **55 years**
- Microscopic ETE removed from T3 definition -- the 8th edition reemphasises gross ETE as the critical unfavourable prognostic factor but minimises the significance of minor extension beyond the thyroid capsule detected only on histologic examination
- T3a defined as tumour >=4 cm limited to thyroid
- T3b defined as gross ETE invading only strap muscles
- Nodal positivity alone no longer upgrades to Stage III or IV -- deemphasises importance of locoregional nodal disease
- Net effect: **downstaging** of a significant number of patients into lower stages that more accurately reflect their low risk of dying from thyroid cancer (Randolph, Ch24)

**Stage Grouping** (Randolph, Ch47 -- Box 47.2):
- Patients <55 years: Stage I (any T, any N, M0); Stage II (any T, any N, M1)
- Patients >=55 years: Stage I (T1--T2, N0, M0); Stage II (T1--T2 N1 M0, or T3 any N M0); Stage III (T4a, any N, M0); Stage IVA (T4b, any N, M0); Stage IVB (M1)

AJCC staging adequately predicts disease-specific mortality (0.5% for stage I, 7.5% for stage II, 20% for stage III, 67% for stage IV) but **fails to predict risk of persistent or recurrent disease** -- rates of persistent disease at 1 year are comparable between stages II, III, and IV (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.1).

### Prognostic Markers

| Factor | Favourable | Unfavourable |
|---|---|---|
| Age | <55 years | >=55 years |
| Tumour size | <2 cm | >4 cm |
| Extrathyroidal extension | None | Gross ETE (T4) |
| Nodal status | N0 | N1 (especially >5 nodes, any >=3 cm) |
| Distant metastases | M0 | M1 |
| Histological variant | Classical PTC, FVPTC | Tall cell, hobnail, columnar, solid |
| Vascular invasion | Absent | Present (especially >4 foci in FTC) |
| Completeness of resection | R0 | R1/R2 |
| Molecular markers | BRAF alone, RAS | BRAF + TERT promoter mutation |

Multivariate analyses identify **increasing patient age** and **extrathyroidal invasion** as independent prognostic factors in all studies. Presence of initial distant metastases and large tumour size are significant in most studies. Completeness of initial tumour resection is also a predictor of mortality. Nodal metastases, although relevant to future nodal recurrence, **do not independently influence cause-specific mortality** (Randolph, Ch47).

## Molecular Prognostic Markers

### BRAF V600E

- Present in 40--60% of PTC
- Associated with aggressive clinicopathological features (ETE, nodal metastases, recurrence)
- Alone, does not significantly worsen prognosis in low-risk patients
- **Combined with TERT promoter mutation** -- synergistic negative prognostic effect

### TERT Promoter Mutations (C228T, C250T)

- Present in 7--22% of DTC
- Strongly associated with recurrence, distant metastases, and disease-specific mortality
- The strongest independent molecular prognostic marker in DTC
- **BRAF + TERT coexistence** identifies the highest-risk subset of PTC

### RAS Mutations

- Present in 20--40% of follicular thyroid carcinoma and FVPTC
- Associated with follicular-patterned tumours
- Generally associated with intermediate prognosis

## Post-Thyroidectomy Follow-up Protocol

Follow-up should be tailored to the initial risk group and then to the response to therapy dynamic risk assessment, thus **avoiding oversurveillance** of patients with a very low risk of disease recurrence (Randolph, Ch47).

### First Year (3--12 months after initial therapy)

1. **Physical examination** -- neck palpation
2. **Serum Tg and TgAb** -- suppressed (on levothyroxine), every 6 months
3. **TSH level** -- to verify adequate suppression
4. **Neck ultrasonography** -- at 6--18 months post-RAI to determine response to therapy (Randolph, Ch47); suspicious lymph nodes should be assessed with FNAB + Tg in needle washout; FNAB indicated for suspicious central compartment nodes >=8 mm or lateral compartment nodes >=10 mm in smallest diameter (Randolph, Ch47)
5. **Stimulated Tg** (with rhTSH or THW) -- at 9--12 months if RAI was administered
6. **Post-therapy WBS** -- if RAI was administered, review for completeness of ablation

### Restaging at 1--2 Years (Dynamic Risk Reclassification)

This is the critical timepoint for formal **response to therapy assessment** (Randolph, Ch24):
- Integrate suppressed Tg, stimulated Tg, neck US, and (for high-risk patients) cross-sectional/functional imaging
- Reclassify into one of four response categories (excellent, indeterminate, biochemical incomplete, structural incomplete)
- Adjust TSH suppression targets and follow-up intensity based on reclassified risk
- Primary follow-up of low- and intermediate-risk patients can rely **heavily, if not almost exclusively**, on serum Tg and neck ultrasonography; additional imaging is probably only required if the response is judged to be indeterminate or incomplete (Randolph, Ch24)

### Years 1--5 (Follow-up Tailored to Response Category)

1. **Serum Tg and TgAb** -- every 6--12 months
2. **TSH** -- every 6--12 months
3. **Neck ultrasonography** -- frequency tailored to response (Randolph, Ch47):
   - Low-risk with excellent response: **not necessary before 3--5 years** after initial treatment
   - Intermediate-risk with excellent response: every 12--24 months
   - High-risk patients: every 6--12 months
4. **Stimulated Tg** -- not routinely repeated if initial response is excellent; may repeat if response is indeterminate or incomplete; additional stimulated Tg values will not be obtained in patients with excellent response (Randolph, Ch24 -- case example)
5. **Cross-sectional imaging** (CT, MRI, PET/CT) -- only if Tg rising or structural disease suspected

### Years 5--10 and Beyond

1. **Serum Tg and TgAb** -- annually
2. **TSH** -- annually
3. **Neck ultrasonography** -- every 2--3 years if excellent response
4. **De-escalation** -- patients with sustained excellent response can transition to annual clinical examination and suppressed Tg measurements with less frequent imaging; consistent with ATA guideline recommendation 63 (Randolph, Ch24)

### Prognosis and Recurrence Timing (Randolph, Ch47)

- Only 1--7% of patients have distant metastases at diagnosis
- ~15% of patients experience relapse, most commonly as cervical lymph nodes
- **80% of recurrences occur during the first 5 years** of follow-up
- <5% of DTC patients have a lethal outcome; among lethal cases, 20% of deaths occur in the first year and 80% within 10 years
- Disease-specific survival at 20 years: low-risk (T1T2N0) ~99%, intermediate-risk ~87%, high-risk ~57% (Randolph, Ch47)

### Management Based on Response Category

| Response | Action |
|---|---|
| **Excellent** | De-escalate TSH suppression (target 0.5--2.0 mIU/L), reduce imaging frequency, follow primarily with annual clinical examination and suppressed Tg (Randolph, Ch24); consider discharge to primary care at 5--10 years |
| **Indeterminate** | Continue observation with repeated imaging of nonspecific lesions and serum Tg monitoring (Randolph, Ch24 -- Table 24.2); nonspecific findings that become suspicious over time should be further evaluated with additional imaging or biopsy; most reclassify to excellent response |
| **Biochemical incomplete** | If Tg stable or declining -- continued observation with ongoing TSH suppression; if Tg or TgAb rising -- additional investigations and potentially additional therapies (Randolph, Ch24 -- Table 24.2); FDG-PET/CT when Tg >10 ng/mL (Randolph, Ch47); empiric RAI if Tg rising with no structural disease |
| **Structural incomplete** | Active intervention depending on size, location, rate of growth, RAI avidity, FDG avidity, and specific pathology (Randolph, Ch24 -- Table 24.2): surgery for resectable disease, RAI for iodine-avid disease, EBRT for unresectable disease, targeted therapy (lenvatinib, sorafenib) for progressive RAI-refractory disease (Randolph, Ch47) |

## Dynamic Reclassification Over Time

The key insight of dynamic risk stratification is that the initial risk estimate is continually updated:

### Reclassification Patterns (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.3; Tuttle 2010, Momesso 2016)

| Initial ATA Risk | Excellent Response at 2 yrs | Risk of Persistent/Recurrent Structural Disease |
|---|---|---|
| | | **At initial staging** --> **After excellent response** --> **After incomplete response** |
| Low | 86--91% | 3% --> 2% --> 13% |
| Intermediate | 57--63% | 18% --> 2% --> 41% |
| High | 14--16% | 66% --> 14% --> 79% |

- A patient initially classified as **intermediate-risk** who achieves an excellent response has a subsequent risk of only **2%** -- essentially equivalent to a low-risk patient; this patient should be followed as a "low-risk" patient with intensity tailored to the revised 2% risk estimate, not the initial 18% estimate (Randolph, Ch24)
- Conversely, a **low-risk** patient with an incomplete response sees the risk increase from 3% to **13%** (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.3)
- Even **high-risk** patients benefit from excellent response: risk decreases from 66% to **14%** (Randolph, Ch24)
- The dynamic reclassification has its **most dramatic practical impact** in the ~50% of patients initially classified as intermediate-risk (Randolph, Ch24)
- This reclassification allows appropriate de-escalation or escalation of treatment and follow-up intensity

## RAI-Refractory Disease (Randolph, Ch47)

RAI-refractory DTC is defined when:
1. RAI uptake is absent in all target lesions (initially or during treatment)
2. Uptake is present in some lesions but not in others
3. Progression within 12 months after RAI treatment
4. Persistent disease after cumulative activity of 600 mCi (some centres)

Management of RAI-refractory metastatic disease includes TSH suppression, focalised treatments (surgery, EBRT, radiofrequency ablation, vertebroplasty, cryotherapy), and systemic agents (lenvatinib, sorafenib). Patients with stable, asymptomatic disease need not be treated actively (Randolph, Ch47).

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **Dynamic risk stratification** reclassifies patients at each visit -- it is not a one-time assessment; the response to therapy is more prognostic than the initial risk category
2. **Four response categories:** excellent, indeterminate, biochemical incomplete, structural incomplete -- know the Tg cutoffs for each; note that cutoffs **differ** for patients treated with total thyroidectomy + RAI, total thyroidectomy without RAI, and lobectomy (Randolph, Ch47 -- Table 47.3)
3. **Stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL = excellent response** (after total thyroidectomy + RAI) -- this is the single most important number in thyroid cancer follow-up; after total thyroidectomy without RAI the threshold is <2 ng/mL; after lobectomy, Tg <30 ng/mL (Randolph, Ch47)
4. **Optimal negative predictive value** for NED requires both negative imaging AND undetectable stimulated Tg (97% in low-risk, 94% in intermediate-risk, 82% in high-risk) (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.4)
5. **TgAb serves as a surrogate tumour marker** when present -- declining is good, rising is concerning
6. **MACIS score** -- the most commonly tested prognostic score; <6 = excellent prognosis (99% 20-year DSS)
7. **Age 55** is the new cutoff in AJCC 8th edition (changed from 45 in 7th edition) -- frequently tested
8. **BRAF + TERT promoter mutation** is the most lethal molecular combination in PTC
9. **De-escalation** of TSH suppression and follow-up intensity is appropriate once excellent response is confirmed -- reduces cardiovascular and skeletal morbidity
10. **Durante 2013** showed recurrences concentrate in the first 5 years -- supports eventual discharge of excellent responders from oncologic surveillance; **80% of recurrences** occur in the first 5 years (Randolph, Ch47)
11. **AJCC staging predicts mortality but NOT recurrence** -- rates of persistent disease at 1 year are comparable between stages II, III, and IV; the ATA risk stratification system is needed to predict recurrence (Randolph, Ch24 -- Fig 24.1)
12. **Intermediate-risk patients** represent ~50% of the cohort and have the most to gain from dynamic reclassification: the ~20% initial risk of structural recurrence drops to 2% with excellent response (Randolph, Ch24)
