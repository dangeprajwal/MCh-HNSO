> [!cite] Landmark Articles

> Tuttle et al. (2010) introduced the concept of dynamic risk stratification in a retrospective study of 588 DTC patients, demonstrating that response to initial therapy during the first 2 years of follow-up can significantly refine the initial ATA risk estimates: 3% of low-risk, 21% of intermediate-risk, and 68% of high-risk patients had persistent structural disease, but these estimates could be modified based on response categories. Haugen et al. (2016) incorporated dynamic risk stratification into the ATA guidelines, formalising the four response categories (excellent, indeterminate, biochemical incomplete, structural incomplete). Momesso et al. (2016) validated dynamic risk stratification in 507 DTC patients treated without RAI, demonstrating that modified response-to-therapy definitions are applicable even in patients managed with lobectomy or total thyroidectomy without RAI. Vaisman et al. (2011) showed that subdividing the incomplete response category into biochemical incomplete and structural incomplete responses has significant prognostic implications, with structural incomplete response carrying a much worse outcome than biochemical incomplete response alone. Durante et al. (2013) examined 2010 PTC patients from 8 Italian centres and found that recurrences were concentrated in the first 5 years, with only 1.4% recurrence in patients without persistent disease at the end of the initial treatment period.

## ATA Initial Risk Stratification System

The ATA 2015 guidelines classify DTC patients into three risk categories at the time of initial treatment, based on clinicopathological features. This initial risk estimate guides the intensity of treatment and follow-up.

### Low Risk of Recurrence (~5% recurrence at 10 years)

Criteria (all must be met):
- Intrathyroidal PTC (with or without BRAF V600E mutation alone)
- Tumour <=4 cm
- No aggressive histological variant (no tall cell, hobnail, columnar, etc.)
- No vascular invasion
- No lymph node metastases, OR <=5 pathologic N1 micrometastases (<0.2 cm in largest dimension)
- No distant metastases
- Complete macroscopic resection (R0 or R1)
- No RAI uptake outside the thyroid bed on post-therapy WBS (if performed)
- Clinical N0 or pathologic N0, or <=5 micrometastatic N1

### Intermediate Risk of Recurrence (~20% recurrence at 10 years)

Criteria (any one of the following):
- Aggressive histological variant (tall cell, hobnail, columnar cell, etc.)
- Minor extrathyroidal extension (microscopic ETE into perithyroidal soft tissues)
- Vascular invasion
- >5 involved lymph nodes, all <3 cm in largest dimension
- Multifocal papillary microcarcinoma with minor ETE and BRAF V600E mutated (if known)
- RAI-avid metastatic foci in the neck on post-therapy WBS
- Clinical N1 or pathologic N1 with all metastatic lymph nodes <3 cm

### High Risk of Recurrence (~50--70% recurrence at 10 years)

Criteria (any one of the following):
- Gross extrathyroidal extension (T4a or T4b disease)
- Incomplete tumour resection (R2)
- Distant metastases (M1)
- Any lymph node >=3 cm in largest dimension
- Postoperative serum Tg suggestive of distant metastases
- Follicular thyroid cancer with extensive vascular invasion (>4 foci of vascular invasion)
- Pathologic N1 with any metastatic lymph node >=3 cm

## Dynamic Risk Stratification: Response to Therapy

The pivotal innovation introduced by Tuttle (2010) and incorporated into the ATA 2015 guidelines is that the initial risk estimate is not fixed but rather is modified at each follow-up visit based on the patient's response to therapy. This "dynamic risk stratification" reclassifies patients into four response categories.

### 1. Excellent Response

**Definition:**
- Negative imaging (no structural or functional evidence of disease)
- Suppressed Tg <0.2 ng/mL or stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL
- Anti-Tg antibodies absent or declining

**Clinical Significance:**
- Risk of recurrence: 1--4%
- Can de-escalate follow-up intensity and TSH suppression
- Approximately 85--90% of ATA low-risk patients achieve excellent response within 2 years
- 50--60% of ATA intermediate-risk patients reclassified to excellent response over time

### 2. Indeterminate Response

**Definition:**
- Nonspecific findings on imaging (not clearly benign or malignant)
- Suppressed Tg 0.2--1.0 ng/mL or stimulated Tg 1--10 ng/mL
- Anti-Tg antibodies stable or declining

**Clinical Significance:**
- Risk of structural disease: 15--20%
- Most patients (80--90%) remain stable or reclassify to excellent response over time
- Requires continued surveillance but NOT automatic additional treatment
- Observation with serial Tg and imaging is appropriate

### 3. Biochemical Incomplete Response

**Definition:**
- Negative imaging (no structural disease identified)
- Suppressed Tg >=1 ng/mL or stimulated Tg >=10 ng/mL
- Rising Tg or rising anti-Tg antibody levels

**Clinical Significance:**
- Risk of structural disease: ~30% will eventually develop structural disease
- 50--60% spontaneously become NED with declining Tg levels over time
- Management: observation with serial Tg and cross-sectional imaging
- Consider empiric RAI if rising Tg with no structural disease identified
- Additional imaging: FDG-PET/CT if Tg elevated but no RAI-avid disease (possible loss of differentiation)

### 4. Structural Incomplete Response

**Definition:**
- Persistent or newly identified locoregional or distant metastases on imaging
- Any Tg level (typically elevated but can be low with anti-Tg antibodies)
- Structural disease confirmed by imaging (US, CT, MRI, PET/CT) with or without biopsy

**Clinical Significance:**
- Risk of disease-specific mortality: 50--85% will have persistent disease; mortality varies with extent of disease
- Management depends on location, burden, and progression of disease
- Options: surgery, RAI (if avid), external beam RT, targeted therapy, observation (if stable/minimal disease)

## Thyroglobulin (Tg) as a Tumour Marker

### Principles

- Tg is produced exclusively by thyroid follicular cells (normal and malignant)
- After total thyroidectomy and RAI ablation, Tg should become undetectable
- Detectable or rising Tg indicates persistent/recurrent disease
- Tg has extremely high sensitivity and specificity when measured in the absence of anti-Tg antibodies

### Unstimulated (Suppressed) Tg

- Measured while patient is on levothyroxine therapy
- Less sensitive than stimulated Tg
- Useful for serial monitoring -- trending is more important than absolute value
- Suppressed Tg <0.2 ng/mL: excellent response
- Suppressed Tg 0.2--1.0 ng/mL: indeterminate response
- Suppressed Tg >=1 ng/mL: biochemical incomplete response

### Stimulated Tg

- Measured after TSH stimulation (THW or rhTSH)
- More sensitive -- detects smaller volumes of residual disease
- Stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL: excellent response (98% chance of being disease-free)
- Stimulated Tg 1--10 ng/mL: indeterminate response
- Stimulated Tg >=10 ng/mL: biochemical incomplete response

### Anti-Thyroglobulin Antibodies (TgAb)

- Present in 20--25% of DTC patients (higher than general population ~10%)
- Interfere with Tg measurement -- cause falsely low Tg on immunometric assays
- **TgAb trend serves as a surrogate tumour marker:**
  - Declining TgAb: favourable -- suggests decreasing tumour antigen stimulation
  - Stable or rising TgAb: unfavourable -- suggests persistent/recurrent disease
- TgAb typically decline and disappear over 2--3 years in disease-free patients

## TSH Suppression Guidelines

TSH suppression is a cornerstone of DTC management, based on the principle that TSH stimulates growth of differentiated thyroid cancer cells. The degree of suppression is tailored to disease risk.

### Targets by Risk Category

| Risk / Response | TSH Target (mIU/L) | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **High-risk** (active disease) | <0.1 | Indefinite (while disease present) |
| **Intermediate-risk** (initial) | 0.1--0.5 | First 5 years |
| **Low-risk** (initial) | 0.5--2.0 | First 1--2 years |
| **Excellent response** (any initial risk) | 0.5--2.0 | After confirmed excellent response |
| **Indeterminate response** | 0.1--0.5 | Until reclassification |
| **Biochemical incomplete** | <0.1 | Until further assessment |
| **Structural incomplete** | <0.1 | Indefinite |

### Risks of TSH Suppression

- **Cardiovascular:** Atrial fibrillation (3-fold increased risk with TSH <0.1), increased heart rate, left ventricular hypertrophy
- **Skeletal:** Accelerated bone loss, increased fracture risk (particularly postmenopausal women)
- **Quality of life:** Anxiety, tremor, heat intolerance, insomnia
- De-escalation of TSH suppression in patients achieving excellent response reduces these iatrogenic risks

## Prognostic Scoring Systems

### MACIS Score (Mayo Clinic)

Developed for papillary thyroid cancer by Hay et al. (1993):

- **M** = Distant **M**etastases (if present, add 3.1 points)
- **A** = **A**ge (if >=39 years: 0.08 x age; if <39 years: 3.1)
- **C** = **C**ompleteness of resection (if incomplete, add 1 point)
- **I** = **I**nvasion (if locally invasive/T4, add 1 point)
- **S** = **S**ize (0.3 x tumour size in cm)

| MACIS Score | 20-year DSS |
|---|---|
| <6.0 | 99% |
| 6.0--6.99 | 89% |
| 7.0--7.99 | 56% |
| >=8.0 | 24% |

### AMES (Lahey Clinic)

- **A**ge (<40 men, <50 women = low risk)
- **M**etastases (distant metastases = high risk)
- **E**xtent (extrathyroidal extension = high risk)
- **S**ize (>5 cm = high risk)

### AJCC/TNM 8th Edition

Key changes from 7th to 8th edition:
- Age cutoff raised from 45 to **55 years**
- Minor ETE (T3b old system) no longer upstages to T3 -- now only gross ETE matters
- T3a defined as tumour >4 cm limited to thyroid
- T3b defined as gross ETE invading only strap muscles
- Nodal staging simplified: N1a (level VI/VII) and N1b (lateral cervical or retropharyngeal)

### Prognostic Markers

| Factor | Favourable | Unfavourable |
|---|---|---|
| Age | <55 years | >=55 years |
| Tumour size | <2 cm | >4 cm |
| Extrathyroidal extension | None | Gross ETE (T4) |
| Nodal status | N0 | N1 (especially >5 nodes, any >=3 cm) |
| Distant metastases | M0 | M1 |
| Histological variant | Classical PTC, FVPTC | Tall cell, hobnail, columnar, solid |
| Vascular invasion | Absent | Present (especially >4 foci in FTC) |
| Completeness of resection | R0 | R1/R2 |
| Molecular markers | BRAF alone, RAS | BRAF + TERT promoter mutation |

## Molecular Prognostic Markers

### BRAF V600E

- Present in 40--60% of PTC
- Associated with aggressive clinicopathological features (ETE, nodal metastases, recurrence)
- Alone, does not significantly worsen prognosis in low-risk patients
- **Combined with TERT promoter mutation** -- synergistic negative prognostic effect

### TERT Promoter Mutations (C228T, C250T)

- Present in 7--22% of DTC
- Strongly associated with recurrence, distant metastases, and disease-specific mortality
- The strongest independent molecular prognostic marker in DTC
- **BRAF + TERT coexistence** identifies the highest-risk subset of PTC

### RAS Mutations

- Present in 20--40% of follicular thyroid carcinoma and FVPTC
- Associated with follicular-patterned tumours
- Generally associated with intermediate prognosis

## Post-Thyroidectomy Follow-up Protocol

### First Year (3--12 months after initial therapy)

1. **Physical examination** -- neck palpation
2. **Serum Tg and TgAb** -- suppressed (on levothyroxine), every 6 months
3. **TSH level** -- to verify adequate suppression
4. **Neck ultrasonography** -- at 6--12 months post-operatively
5. **Stimulated Tg** (with rhTSH or THW) -- at 9--12 months if RAI was administered
6. **Post-therapy WBS** -- if RAI was administered, review for completeness of ablation

### Years 1--5

1. **Serum Tg and TgAb** -- every 6--12 months
2. **TSH** -- every 6--12 months
3. **Neck ultrasonography** -- annually for first 3--5 years, then less frequently based on response
4. **Stimulated Tg** -- not routinely repeated if initial response is excellent; may repeat if response is indeterminate or incomplete
5. **Cross-sectional imaging** (CT, MRI, PET/CT) -- only if Tg rising or structural disease suspected

### Years 5--10 and Beyond

1. **Serum Tg and TgAb** -- annually
2. **TSH** -- annually
3. **Neck ultrasonography** -- every 2--3 years if excellent response
4. **De-escalation** -- patients with sustained excellent response can transition to annual thyroid function and Tg testing with less frequent imaging

### Management Based on Response Category

| Response | Action |
|---|---|
| **Excellent** | De-escalate TSH suppression, reduce imaging frequency, consider discharge to primary care at 5--10 years |
| **Indeterminate** | Continue observation with serial Tg and imaging every 6--12 months; most reclassify to excellent response |
| **Biochemical incomplete** | Serial Tg trending; cross-sectional imaging to seek structural disease; consider FDG-PET/CT; empiric RAI if Tg rising with no structural disease |
| **Structural incomplete** | Active intervention: surgery for resectable disease, RAI for iodine-avid disease, EBRT for unresectable disease, targeted therapy for progressive RAI-refractory disease |

## Dynamic Reclassification Over Time

The key insight of dynamic risk stratification is that the initial risk estimate is continually updated:

### Reclassification Patterns (Tuttle 2010, Momesso 2016)

| Initial ATA Risk | Excellent Response at 2 yrs | Structural Disease at Final Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Low | 86--91% | 2--3% |
| Intermediate | 57--63% | 8--22% |
| High | 14--16% | 50--68% |

- A patient initially classified as intermediate-risk who achieves an excellent response has a subsequent recurrence risk of only 1--2% -- essentially equivalent to a low-risk patient
- Conversely, a low-risk patient with a structural incomplete response has a much worse prognosis than predicted by initial staging alone
- This reclassification allows appropriate de-escalation or escalation of treatment and follow-up intensity

## Clinical Pearls for Examinations

1. **Dynamic risk stratification** reclassifies patients at each visit -- it is not a one-time assessment; the response to therapy is more prognostic than the initial risk category
2. **Four response categories:** excellent, indeterminate, biochemical incomplete, structural incomplete -- know the Tg cutoffs for each
3. **Stimulated Tg <1 ng/mL = excellent response** -- this is the single most important number in thyroid cancer follow-up
4. **TgAb serves as a surrogate tumour marker** when present -- declining is good, rising is concerning
5. **MACIS score** -- the most commonly tested prognostic score; <6 = excellent prognosis (99% 20-year DSS)
6. **Age 55** is the new cutoff in AJCC 8th edition (changed from 45 in 7th edition) -- frequently tested
7. **BRAF + TERT promoter mutation** is the most lethal molecular combination in PTC
8. **De-escalation** of TSH suppression and follow-up intensity is appropriate once excellent response is confirmed -- reduces cardiovascular and skeletal morbidity
9. **During 2013** showed recurrences concentrate in the first 5 years -- supports eventual discharge of excellent responders from oncologic surveillance
