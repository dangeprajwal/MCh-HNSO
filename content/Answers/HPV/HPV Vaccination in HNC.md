## Available HPV Vaccines

Three prophylactic HPV vaccines have been developed, all based on **virus-like particle (VLP)** technology:

| Vaccine | Brand | HPV Types Covered | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bivalent** | Cervarix (GSK) | 16, 18 | Licensed but limited market use; withdrawn in many countries |
| **Quadrivalent** | Gardasil 4 (Merck) | 6, 11, 16, 18 | Previously standard; largely superseded by Gardasil 9 |
| **9-valent** | **Gardasil 9 (Merck)** | **6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58** | **Currently recommended worldwide** |

### Gardasil 9 — Composition and Coverage
- Covers all 7 high-risk types in Gardasil 9: 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58
- Adds LR types 6 and 11 (recurrent respiratory papillomatosis prevention)
- Projected to prevent **~90% of HPV-attributable cervical cancers** (vs ~70% with bivalent/quadrivalent)
- Covers HPV-16 and HPV-18 which cause **>93% of HPV-associated OPC**
- Adjuvant: AAHS (amorphous aluminium hydroxyphosphate sulfate) — enhances immunogenicity

## Mechanism of Action — Virus-Like Particles (VLPs)

### VLP Technology
- VLPs are composed of the **L1 major capsid protein** of HPV, which self-assembles into empty icosahedral particles morphologically identical to native HPV virions
- VLPs contain **no viral DNA** — they are non-infectious and cannot cause HPV infection
- VLPs are highly immunogenic due to their repetitive surface structure, activating both B-cell and T-cell responses
- Production: recombinant expression in yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae* for Gardasil) or baculovirus/Sf9 insect cells (Cervarix)

### Immune Response
- VLP vaccination induces high-titre **virus-neutralising antibodies** (primarily targeting conformational epitopes on L1)
- Seropositivity: >99% of recipients after the complete series
- Antibody titres: **50–100× higher** than titres achieved by natural HPV infection
- Memory B-cells provide durable protection; antibody titres decline over time but are expected to persist ≥10 years (bridging studies ongoing)
- VLP vaccination is **strictly prophylactic**: antibodies prevent virion binding to basal epithelial cells, blocking initial infection

---

## Critical Distinction — Prophylactic vs Therapeutic

> **Prophylactic only.** HPV vaccines PREVENT new HPV infections in uninfected individuals. They do NOT:
> - Clear existing HPV infection
> - Treat established HPV-associated dysplasia or cancer
> - Have any therapeutic effect on HPV+ OPC

This distinction is high-yield for exam questions framed as "does vaccination help current patients?"

## Vaccination Recommendations

### WHO/ACIP/IAP Recommendations
| Population | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| **Routine schedule** | Age **11–12 years**, both males and females; 2-dose schedule if initiated before age 15 (0, 6–12 months) |
| **Catch-up (unvaccinated)** | Ages 13–26 years: 3-dose schedule (0, 1–2, 6 months) |
| **Shared decision (27–45 years)** | Limited benefit — most individuals already exposed; individual risk assessment |
| **Immunocompromised** | 3-dose series regardless of age at initiation |
| **Males** | Recommended since **2009 (ACIP)** for anal cancer, genital warts, and OPC prevention |

### Schedule Detail
- 2-dose (recommended age <15 at first dose): months 0 and 6–12
- 3-dose (age ≥15, or immunocompromised): months 0, 1–2, and 6

### Why Vaccinate Males?
- HPV+ OPC predominantly affects **males** (M:F ratio 3–4:1)
- Male vaccination provides direct protection (herd immunity too slow for OPC given long latency period of 25–30 years)
- Also prevents anal cancer (high risk in MSM), penile cancer, and genital warts
- ACIP formally extended recommendation to males ages 11–26 in 2009, with catch-up up to age 45 in 2019 (shared decision)

## Evidence for OPC Prevention

### Direct Evidence (Limited by Time Lag)
- HPV-related OPC develops **25–35 years after initial infection** — randomised trial data on OPC incidence as an endpoint are not yet available (vaccines were introduced 2006–2009)
- Surrogate endpoint: **oral HPV prevalence** in vaccinated cohorts

### Costa Rica HPV Vaccine Trial (Herrero 2013, JNCI)
- Randomised, double-blind, controlled trial (Cervarix vs hepatitis A vaccine control)
- **93.3% efficacy** against oral HPV-16/18 infection 4 years post-vaccination (95% CI 63–100%)
- First randomised evidence that HPV vaccination reduces oral HPV acquisition

### Population-Level Data
- Drolet et al. (Lancet 2019): population-level decreases in **anogenital warts** (67% reduction in girls 13–19 years) and CIN2+ (51% reduction) in countries with high-coverage vaccination programmes
- NHANES data (US): oral HPV-16 prevalence declined 37% in vaccinated vs unvaccinated young adults (2011–2014 vs 2015–2018)
- Mathematical modelling (Brouwer 2019): with 80% vaccination coverage, 56% reduction in HPV+ OPC predicted over 50–70 years

### Projected Impact on OPC
- HPV vaccination is projected to prevent **60–90% of HPV+ OPC** with sustained high-coverage programmes
- Time to measurable OPC incidence reduction: estimated 2030–2040 in countries with vaccination programmes since 2006–2009

## India-Specific Context

- **Licensed in India**: Cervarix (GSK) and Gardasil 4 (Merck) — Gardasil 9 licensed more recently
- **National Immunisation Programme (NIP)**: HPV vaccine inclusion debated; pilot programmes in select states (AP, Delhi, Punjab) initiated 2008–2010 but were suspended; relaunched 2023 with intent to include in Universal Immunisation Programme
- **Indian epidemiology**: HPV prevalence in Indian OPC lower than Western data (~30–40% of OPC HPV-positive) due to **tobacco predominance** in Indian HNC
- **Cost barrier**: primary challenge for nationwide rollout; government procurement pricing critical
- **Awareness gap**: male vaccination uptake very low in India; female vaccination limited to urban settings

---

## Limitations of Current Vaccines

1. **Prophylactic only** — no benefit after HPV exposure
2. Requires vaccination **before sexual debut** for maximum efficacy
3. **Cold-chain requirement** — 2–8°C; logistical challenge in LMICs
4. Males historically **underimmunised** — critical gap given OPC male predominance
5. Does not cover all HPV types causing sinonasal or oral cavity malignancies
6. Long latency of OPC means **decades** before vaccine impact on incidence is measurable
7. No validated **oral HPV screening test** to identify at-risk individuals in unvaccinated populations

> Gardasil 9 covers HPV types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, and 58 using virus-like particle technology — non-infectious L1 capsid particles that generate neutralising antibody titres 50–100× those of natural infection, preventing new HPV acquisition with >93% efficacy (Costa Rica Trial). > [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. Gardasil 9 covers 9 HPV types (6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58) — the 9-valent vaccine is currently recommended and supersedes bivalent and quadrivalent vaccines.
> 2. VLPs are non-infectious — they contain no HPV DNA; the L1 major capsid protein self-assembles into empty particles that mimic the virion structure.
> 3. Vaccination is PROPHYLACTIC ONLY — it has NO role in treating existing HPV infection or HPV-associated OPC.
> 4. Male vaccination was added to ACIP recommendations in 2009 — critical for OPC prevention given its 3:1 male predominance.
> 5. 2-dose schedule (0 and 6 months) is adequate when initiated before age 15; 3 doses required at age ≥15 or in immunocompromised individuals.
> 6. The Costa Rica HPV Vaccine Trial demonstrated 93.3% efficacy against oral HPV-16/18 acquisition — the first RCT evidence supporting OPC prevention.
> 7. India-specific: HPV accounts for only 30–40% of Indian OPC (tobacco predominance); Gardasil 9 is now licensed but NIP inclusion is recent.
> 8. Mathematical modelling predicts 60–90% reduction in HPV+ OPC with sustained high-coverage vaccination, but OPC incidence reduction will not be measurable until 2030–2040 due to the 25–35 year latency period.
