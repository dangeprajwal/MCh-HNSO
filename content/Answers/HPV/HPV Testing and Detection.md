## Clinical Context

HPV testing of oropharyngeal SCC is **mandatory** per AJCC 8th edition (2017) because HPV status:
1. Determines the staging system applied (separate p16+ OPC staging)
2. Drives prognosis (5-yr OS 75–85% vs 35–45% HPV-)
3. Is increasingly used to select patients for de-escalation trials
4. Guides adjuvant treatment planning post-TORS

Testing is applied to the **primary tumour** (biopsy or resection specimen) or to involved lymph nodes when primary is occult (T0 disease).

---

## Method 1 — p16 Immunohistochemistry (IHC)

### Principle
p16^INK4a (CDKN2A) is massively overexpressed as a compensatory response to pRb degradation by the HPV E7 oncoprotein (see [[Answers/HPV/HPV Biology and Carcinogenesis in HNC|HPV Biology and Carcinogenesis in HNC]]). p16 IHC is a **surrogate biomarker** for HPV E7 activity, not a direct test for HPV DNA.

### Protocol (CAP 2018)
- Antibody: **CINtec clone E6H4** (Ventana/Roche) — standard
- **Positive**: strong, diffuse **nuclear AND cytoplasmic** staining in **≥70%** of viable tumour cells
- Weak, focal, or cytoplasm-only staining = negative
- Applicable to FFPE sections (routine pathology material)

### Performance in OPC
| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Sensitivity (for HPV in OPC) | **94%** |
| Specificity (for HPV in OPC) | **83%** |
| Positive predictive value (OPC) | ~95% |
| Negative predictive value (OPC) | ~92% |

### Advantages
- Inexpensive, widely available, rapid (24–48 hours)
- Standard FFPE tissue — no special collection
- High sensitivity in OPC — few false negatives

### Limitations and Pitfalls
- **Site-specific validity**: valid as HPV surrogate ONLY in oropharynx
  - Oral cavity, larynx, hypopharynx: p16 overexpressed for non-HPV reasons (PIK3CA activation, CDKN2A promoter methylation reversal, pRb loss by mutation) → HIGH false-positive rate
- **~5–6% of p16-positive OPC are HPV-negative** by DNA testing (true false positives in OPC)
- **Discordant cases**: p16+/HPV DNA- are increasingly recognised; may represent p16 upregulation by non-HPV mechanisms — these patients likely have worse prognosis than true HPV+ OPC
- Does not identify specific HPV genotype
- Technical variability: staining protocol, antibody lot, interpretation thresholds

## Method 2 — HPV DNA In Situ Hybridisation (ISH)

### Principle
Detects HPV DNA directly within tumour cell nuclei in FFPE tissue sections using labelled DNA probes complementary to HPV sequences. Can use **broadspectrum probes** (cocktail covering HR-HPV types 16, 18, 31, 33, 35, 45, 51, 52, 56, 58, 66) or **type-specific** (HPV-16 only).

### Episomal vs Integrated Signal Pattern
- **Episomal HPV**: diffuse nuclear staining (scattered dot pattern)
- **Integrated HPV**: discrete nuclear dot signal ("punctate" pattern, indicating chromosomal integration)

### Performance
| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Sensitivity | ~90% |
| Specificity | ~98% |
| Best use | Confirmatory test after discordant or equivocal p16 IHC |

### Advantages
- High specificity — confirms true HPV presence in tissue
- Can assess integration status (episomal vs integrated)
- Available on standard FFPE

### Limitations
- Lower sensitivity than p16 IHC (may miss low-copy-number infections)
- More expensive and technically demanding than IHC
- Requires access to ISH platform

## Method 3 — HPV E6/E7 mRNA Detection

### Principle
Detection of **E6 and E7 mRNA transcripts** — this is the **most specific marker** of oncogenically active, transforming HPV. E6/E7 mRNA is only present in actively transformed cells (latent HPV DNA may be present without transcription). Detection of mRNA proves the virus is contributing to carcinogenesis.

### Available Assays
- **RNAscope ISH** (ACD Bio): chromogenic or fluorescent ISH for E6/E7 mRNA in FFPE sections; FDA-cleared; commercially available
- **E6/E7 mRNA RT-PCR**: quantitative; best on fresh/frozen tissue; research standard
- **NovaPlex HPV assay**: multiplex RT-PCR for multiple HPV types on FFPE or liquid-based cytology

### Performance
| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Sensitivity | ~95% |
| Specificity | ~99% |
| Best use | Definitive confirmation; non-OPC sites; p16+/DNA-discordant cases |

### Clinical Role
- **Gold standard for oncogenic activity** — if E6/E7 mRNA is negative, HPV is not driving carcinogenesis regardless of p16 or DNA status
- Increasingly used in non-OPC HPV+ tumours (sinonasal, oral cavity) where p16 IHC is unreliable
- Not routine in most centres due to cost and technical requirements

---

## Method 4 — HPV DNA PCR

### Principle
Amplification of HPV DNA from FFPE or fresh tissue using consensus (e.g., MY09/11 or GP5+/6+) or type-specific primers.

### Performance
- Sensitivity: very high (~98%) — may detect even trace contamination
- Specificity: dependent on primer design and controls
- Contamination risk: PCR contamination can produce false positives
- Does not differentiate episomal vs integrated DNA
- Quantitative PCR can estimate viral load (integrated HPV shows high copies per cell)

### Role
- Research applications; not routinely used in clinical pathology
- Hybrid capture 2 (HC2): signal amplification assay; validated for cervix; less validated in H&N tumours

## Method 5 — Circulating Tumour HPV DNA (ctHPV DNA)

### Principle
HPV-positive tumour cells shed **cell-free HPV DNA** (ctHPV DNA) into blood. This is detected in plasma or serum using highly sensitive methods. Different from tissue-based testing — this is a **liquid biopsy** approach.

### Detection Methods
- **Digital droplet PCR (ddPCR)**: partitions DNA into thousands of droplets; counts positive droplets; extreme sensitivity (can detect single molecules); most validated platform
- **Next-generation sequencing (NGS)**: broader detection; identifies integration sites; expensive
- **Real-time quantitative PCR**: less sensitive than ddPCR but widely available

### Clinical Applications

#### 1. Baseline Tumour Burden Marker
- ctHPV DNA levels correlate with **total tumour burden** at diagnosis
- Higher baseline levels associated with T-stage, nodal burden, and prognosis

#### 2. Treatment Response Monitoring (Mid-Treatment)
- Rapid decline of ctHPV DNA during RT/CRT = effective tumour killing = surrogate for complete response
- Chera BS et al. (Ann Oncol 2018): **post-treatment undetectable ctHPV DNA** predicts complete pathological response with 94% sensitivity

#### 3. Surveillance and Recurrence Detection
- **Rising ctHPV DNA after treatment completion** = tumour recurrence with ~90% sensitivity
- Detects recurrence **weeks to months before** clinical or radiological evidence
- May reduce or replace routine PET-CT surveillance in selected patients
- Ahn SM et al. (JAMA Oncol 2014): plasma ctHPV DNA detected recurrence before clinical detection in majority of relapsing patients

### Ongoing Trials Evaluating ctHPV DNA
- **SPIT trial**: saliva-based HPV DNA vs plasma for OPC surveillance
- **NRG HN005**: uses ctHPV DNA for de-escalation selection (clearance = go to de-escalated arm)
- **PATHOS**: exploring ctHPV DNA as adjunct to pathological risk stratification

### Limitations
- No standardised assay or threshold validated across centres
- Pre-analytical factors (sample handling, extraction method) affect sensitivity
- Not yet incorporated into routine clinical guidelines (CAP 2018 does not include ctHPV DNA)
- False positives from latent HPV in non-tumour tissue (very rare)
- Cost and availability outside research centres

## CAP 2018 Recommended Testing Algorithm

```
All oropharyngeal SCC (OPSCC)
         |
    p16 IHC (CINtec E6H4)
    /               \
p16 POSITIVE     p16 NEGATIVE
(≥70% cells,         |
nuclear+cyto)    HPV-NEGATIVE — no further testing
    |
Report as HPV-associated OPSCC
(sufficient for AJCC 8th Ed staging)
    |
[Optional confirmatory testing if clinically needed]
HPV DNA ISH or E6/E7 mRNA

For NON-OPC sites (oral cavity, larynx, sinonasal):
p16 IHC NOT valid as HPV surrogate
→ Use HPV DNA ISH or E6/E7 mRNA if HPV status clinically needed
```

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> p16 IHC (CINtec clone E6H4; positive = ≥70% nuclear+cytoplasmic staining) is the CAP 2018 first-line test for all oropharyngeal SCC, with 94% sensitivity and 83% specificity for HPV — sufficient for AJCC 8th edition staging; p16 is NOT a valid HPV surrogate outside the oropharynx. Confirmatory testing uses HPV DNA ISH (high specificity, ~98%) or E6/E7 mRNA (most specific, proves active oncogenic transcription). Plasma ctHPV DNA by ddPCR detects recurrence weeks before imaging (sensitivity ~90%) and is emerging as a liquid biopsy surveillance tool in de-escalation trials.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. p16 IHC threshold: ≥70% of tumour cells with BOTH nuclear AND cytoplasmic strong staining (using CINtec clone E6H4) — cytoplasmic-only staining is negative.
> 2. p16 IHC is a SURROGATE for HPV — it detects the consequence of E7-mediated pRb degradation, not HPV itself; ~5–6% of p16+ OPC are HPV DNA-negative.
> 3. E6/E7 mRNA is the most specific test — it confirms active HPV-driven carcinogenesis, not just latent viral presence.
> 4. In non-OPC sites (oral cavity, larynx, hypopharynx), p16 IHC has an unacceptably high false-positive rate and should NOT be used as HPV surrogate — use HPV DNA ISH or E6/E7 mRNA instead.
> 5. Integrated HPV shows a "punctate dot" pattern on ISH; episomal HPV shows diffuse nuclear signal — integration is associated with more aggressive behaviour.
> 6. ctHPV DNA (plasma ddPCR) can detect recurrence weeks to months before clinical/imaging evidence — rising ctHPV DNA post-treatment is highly predictive of disease relapse.
> 7. Post-treatment undetectable ctHPV DNA correlates with complete pathological response (Chera 2018: sensitivity 94% for complete response).
> 8. CAP 2018 guideline: p16 IHC alone is sufficient for AJCC 8th Ed staging of OPC — additional HPV DNA testing is confirmatory and optional in routine practice.
