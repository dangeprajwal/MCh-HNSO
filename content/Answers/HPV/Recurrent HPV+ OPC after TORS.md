## HPV+ OPC — Natural History After TORS

### Why HPV Status Matters for Recurrence
HPV-positive oropharyngeal SCC has a fundamentally different natural history to HPV-negative disease:
- **5-year OS**: 75–85% with standard CRT; 70–80% with TORS-based approach (comparable)
- **Pattern of recurrence**: distant metastasis (lung, bone, liver) predominates over locoregional recurrence — this is opposite to HPV-negative HNC
- **Locoregional recurrence rate after TORS ± adjuvant RT**: 10–15% at 2 years
- **Time to recurrence**: most recurrences occur within **2 years** of treatment
- **Late failures (>3 years)**: more common in HPV+ OPC than HPV- OPC; late distant metastases (especially lung) carry better prognosis than early locoregional failure
- Despite HPV+ better prognosis, recurrent/metastatic disease is still associated with median OS of 12–18 months (prior to immunotherapy era)

### Primary TORS Approach for OPC
- TORS for oropharynx: used for T1–T3 tonsil, soft palate, base of tongue tumours with adequate transoral access (Meyerhoff classification)
- Typically combined with bilateral or ipsilateral selective neck dissection (levels II–IV)
- Adjuvant therapy guided by pathological risk stratification (see [[Answers/HPV/De-escalation in HPV+ OPC|De-escalation in HPV+ OPC]]):
  - **Low risk** (clear margins, pN0–pN1 without ENE): RT 50 Gy (de-escalated)
  - **Intermediate risk** (close margins <3 mm, pN2 without ENE): RT 60 Gy
  - **High risk** (positive margins, ENE+): RT 66 Gy + concurrent cisplatin 100 mg/m² q3W
- ORATOR trial (Palma 2020, Lancet): TORS + neck dissection vs primary RT; similar 2-year OS and similar locoregional control; RT had better swallowing at 1 year; TORS had better quality of life measures at 2 years

## Surveillance After TORS

### Clinical Surveillance Protocol
- **History and physical examination** (including flexible nasopharyngolaryngoscopy):
  - Every 3 months for first 2 years
  - Every 6 months, years 3–5
  - Annually thereafter
- Aim: detect locoregional recurrence at an early, salvageable stage

### Imaging Surveillance
- **PET-CT at 12 weeks post-treatment completion** (if adjuvant CRT was given):
  - PET-negative neck → observation (PET-NECK study: PET-guided watchful waiting non-inferior to planned neck dissection in CRT-treated patients)
  - PET-positive neck → salvage neck dissection
- **No routine CT/MRI surveillance** after TORS alone (unless clinical concern)
- Annual chest imaging (CXR or CT chest) for late distant metastasis detection (NCCN recommendation)

### ctHPV DNA Surveillance
- Plasma **ctHPV DNA** (circulating tumour HPV DNA by ddPCR): rising levels after treatment = recurrence, often weeks to months before clinical/radiological detection
- Sensitivity ~90% for recurrence detection (Chera 2018)
- Not yet standard of care outside clinical trials; see [[Answers/HPV/HPV Testing and Detection|HPV Testing and Detection]]

## Management of Recurrence by Site

### A. Local Recurrence (Oropharynx)

#### Staging of Recurrence
- Full history, examination, flexible laryngoscopy
- CT or MRI of primary site and neck
- PET-CT if distant metastasis suspected
- Biopsy to confirm recurrence (do not treat on imaging alone)
- MDT discussion mandatory

#### Re-TORS (Salvage Transoral Robotic Surgery)
- **Indications**: small, accessible local recurrence; no major vessel encasement; no prior full-dose RT at recurrence site; adequate functional reserve
- Technical considerations: tissue planes altered by prior surgery and RT; trismus (common post-TORS at BOT); haemostasis more challenging in irradiated field
- Outcomes: small case series only; 2-year OS approximately 50–60% in selected cases
- Decision: multidisciplinary team (MDT) assessment; patient factors (performance status, comorbidity, swallowing function)

#### Re-Irradiation (Reirradiation)
- **Indications**: unresectable local recurrence; prior TORS without RT (adjuvant RT virgin field); prior RT — cumulative dose considerations
- Techniques: **SBRT (stereotactic body radiotherapy)** 40–50 Gy in 5 fractions for small volume recurrences OR **IMRT** 45–60 Gy with daily fractions
- Concurrent chemotherapy: cisplatin 40 mg/m² weekly or cetuximab can be added; modest improvement in locoregional control
- Major risks of reirradiation: carotid blowout (especially in previously irradiated field with neck dissection), osteoradionecrosis, severe dysphagia/aspiration, catastrophic wound breakdown
- **Prior full-dose RT (>60 Gy)**: re-RT feasibility depends on time interval (>6 months minimum from prior RT), volume of re-RT, and cumulative dose to critical structures (spinal cord, brainstem, carotid)

#### Palliative Intent
- Extensive or unresectable local recurrence with poor functional status → palliative intent systemic therapy or best supportive care

---

### B. Regional Recurrence (Neck)

#### Salvage Neck Dissection
- **Indications**: isolated regional recurrence (no distant disease); resectable — no encasement of common or internal carotid artery by tumour
- Type of dissection:
  - **Modified radical neck dissection (MRND)** if structures (SCM, IJV) not involved by recurrence
  - **Radical neck dissection (RND)** if IJV/SCM encased — reconstruction required
  - **Re-dissection of previously dissected field**: higher complication rate (chyle leak, cranial nerve injury, wound breakdown)
- Adjuvant therapy after salvage neck dissection:
  - If no prior RT: adjuvant RT 60–66 Gy ± cisplatin
  - If prior RT: re-RT considerations as above

#### Isolated Neck Recurrence Without Prior Neck Dissection
- Rare in practice (most TORS for OPC includes concurrent neck dissection)
- If neck not previously dissected and no prior RT: salvage neck dissection followed by adjuvant RT — excellent salvage rate
- PET-NECK study data: watchful waiting after negative 12-week PET in CRT-treated patients; PET-positive patients who underwent planned neck dissection had similar outcomes to those salvaged at clinical recurrence

---

### C. Distant Metastasis Only

#### Systemic Therapy (First-Line Recurrent/Metastatic)
- **KEYNOTE-048** (Burtness 2019, Lancet): pembrolizumab + cisplatin/carboplatin + 5-FU vs cetuximab + platinum/5-FU (EXTREME regimen)
  - Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy: superior OS in CPS ≥1 (most HPV+ OPC is CPS high)
  - Pembrolizumab monotherapy (CPS ≥20): non-inferior to EXTREME with better tolerability
  - **Current standard**: pembrolizumab ± chemotherapy for RM-HNSCC (see [[Answers/Radiotherapy and Chemotherapy/Pembrolizumab in HNSCC|Pembrolizumab in HNSCC]])
- HPV+ OPC: generally high PD-L1 expression (TIL-rich, immunologically hot) → excellent response rates to pembrolizumab
- Nivolumab (CHECKMATE-141): second-line for platinum-refractory RM-HNSCC; OS benefit
- **Oligometastatic disease** (1–3 lung metastases): SBRT to metastases ± systemic therapy; case series suggest some long-term survivors in HPV+ OPC

---

### D. Combined Locoregional + Distant Recurrence

- Systemic therapy first to assess systemic disease response
- If systemic disease controlled and locoregional component is isolated and resectable — consider salvage surgery or re-RT for locoregional component
- Palliative approach in most cases of combined failure

---

## Prognosis After Recurrence

| Recurrence Type | Approximate 2-Year OS |
|---|---|
| Local only — salvageable (re-TORS or re-RT) | 50–60% |
| Regional only — salvage neck dissection | 40–55% |
| Distant only — oligometastatic (lung SBRT) | 30–50% |
| Distant only — systemic therapy (pembrolizumab) | 30–35% (mOS ~13 months) |
| Combined locoregional + distant | <20% |

> Important caveat: **HPV-positive status does NOT guarantee salvageability of recurrent disease**. Prognosis after recurrence is significantly worse than primary treatment, even in HPV+ OPC. Late distant metastasis (>3 years from primary) carries better prognosis than early recurrence.

---

> [!example] Examiner's Summary
> After primary TORS for HPV+ OPC, locoregional recurrence (10–15% at 2 years) is managed by salvage re-TORS for accessible local recurrence, re-irradiation (SBRT or IMRT 45–60 Gy) for unresectable local disease, and salvage neck dissection for isolated regional recurrence — all requiring MDT assessment and biopsy confirmation before treatment. Distant metastasis, the predominant failure pattern in HPV+ OPC, is managed with pembrolizumab ± chemotherapy (KEYNOTE-048 standard); despite HPV+ advantageous biology, 2-year OS after recurrence is only 30–60% depending on site, and HPV-positive status does not guarantee successful salvage.

---

> [!tip] Clinical Pearls
> 1. HPV+ OPC fails predominantly by DISTANT metastasis (lung, bone, liver) — in contrast to HPV- OPC which fails locoregionally.
> 2. Most recurrences occur within 2 years; late failure (>3 years) is more common in HPV+ than HPV- and carries better prognosis.
> 3. PET-CT at 12 weeks post-CRT completion guides neck management (PET-NECK protocol): PET-negative neck does not require planned neck dissection.
> 4. ctHPV DNA surveillance detects recurrence weeks before clinical/imaging evidence — rising levels in plasma are highly predictive of relapse (sensitivity ~90%).
> 5. Salvage re-TORS is feasible only for small, accessible, biopsy-confirmed local recurrence without major vessel encasement.
> 6. Re-irradiation risks in previously irradiated field include carotid blowout, osteoradionecrosis, severe dysphagia — cumulative dose and time interval (≥6 months) are critical factors.
> 7. Pembrolizumab is the current first-line standard for recurrent/metastatic HPV+ OPC (KEYNOTE-048); HPV+ OPC has high PD-L1/TIL density → excellent immunotherapy responses.
> 8. ORATOR trial (Palma 2020, Lancet): TORS vs RT had similar OS and locoregional control; RT had better swallowing at 1 year; TORS had better QoL at 2 years — both are valid primary treatment approaches.
