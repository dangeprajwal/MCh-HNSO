## [[Hamman-2022.pdf|Impact of Close Margins in Head and Neck Mucosal Squamous Cell Carcinoma: A Systematic Review]]

#SurgicalMargins  

> [!Cite]  


> [!important]
> For overall survival at 5 years, patients with clear surgical margins had survival rates ranging from about 53% up to 91%. Those with close margins had lower survival, between roughly 38% and 70%. Patients with involved margins showed the poorest outcomes overall, with survival ranging from about 30% to 85%.
> 
For local recurrence at 5 years, the lowest recurrence was seen in patients with clear margins, ranging between about 5% and 27%. Recurrence was higher in patients with close margins, ranging from about 11% to 36%. The highest recurrence rates were observed in patients with involved margins, which ranged widely from about 9% up to nearly 80%.

Research question  
In patients with head and neck mucosal squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) who undergo surgical excision, do close surgical margins lead to different oncologic outcomes compared with clear or involved margins?

Study design  
Systematic review and meta-analysis (26 included cohort studies, total ==8,435 patients==).

Population and distribution  
• 96% oral cavity, 2% oropharynx, 2% hypopharynx/larynx  
• T stage: 68% T1/T2, 32% T3/T4  
• HPV/p16 status not available in oropharyngeal studies

Margin definitions  
• Clear: >5 mm  
• Close: 1–5 mm (some variation, one study used 2–5 mm)  
• Involved: at ink/<1 mm (one study <2 mm)

Primary endpoints  
• Overall survival (OS)  
• Local recurrence (LR)  
• Locoregional recurrence (LRR)

Secondary endpoints  
• Impact of adjuvant therapy (reported variably across studies)  
• Stratification by subsite and tumor stage

Results

Patient characteristics and treatments  
• 89% of pooled patients underwent neck dissection  
• 35% received adjuvant radiation, 15% chemoradiation (data inconsistently reported across studies)  
• Minimum 6 months follow-up in all studies

Oncologic outcomes across included studies 
• 5-year OS: clear 53.3–91.2%, close 37.5–70.1%, involved 29.8–85.1%  
• 5-year LR: clear 5.2–27%, close 10.7–36%, involved 8.9–79.7%

Meta-analysis  
Clear vs close margins  
• OS at 5 years: significantly higher with clear margins (RR 1.22, 95% CI 1.11–1.35)  
• OS at last follow-up: higher with clear margins (RR 1.16, 95% CI 1.07–1.25)  
• LR at 5 years: lower with clear margins (RR 0.50, 95% CI 0.38–0.65)  
• LR at last follow-up: lower with clear margins (RR 0.59, 95% CI 0.47–0.74)  
• LRR at last follow-up: no significant difference (RR 0.90, 95% CI 0.64–1.26, heterogeneity high)

Involved vs close margins  
• 5-year OS: no difference (RR 0.82, 95% CI 0.60–1.11)  
• OS at last follow-up: marginally worse with involved margins (RR 0.78, 95% CI 0.63–0.97)  
• 5-year LR: higher with involved margins (RR 1.75, 95% CI 1.16–2.64)  
• LR at last follow-up: higher with involved margins (RR 1.80, 95% CI 1.36–2.40)  
• LRR at last follow-up: higher with involved margins (RR 1.66, 95% CI 1.37–2.00)

Clear vs involved margins  
• OS at 5 years: higher with clear margins (RR 1.52, 95% CI 1.11–2.09)  
• OS at last follow-up: higher with clear margins (RR 1.51, 95% CI 1.21–1.89)  
• LR at 5 years: lower with clear margins (RR 0.30, 95% CI 0.18–0.50)  
• LR at last follow-up: lower with clear margins (RR 0.34, 95% CI 0.22–0.50)  
• LRR at last follow-up: lower with clear margins (RR 0.54, 95% CI 0.39–0.73)

Statistically nonsignificant results  
• LRR at last follow-up between clear and close margins (RR 0.90, 95% CI 0.64–1.26)  
• 5-year OS between involved and close margins (RR 0.82, 95% CI 0.60–1.11)

Discussion summary  
• There is a stepwise improvement in outcomes: involved → close → clear margins.  
• Close margins are associated with worse OS and LR compared to clear margins, but better than involved margins.  
• No significant difference was observed in LRR between close and clear margins, suggesting that close margins primarily affect local control rather than regional spread.  
• Most studies involved oral cavity tumors; evidence is sparse for other head and neck subsites.  
• Functional compromise due to wider resection remains a concern.  
• Close margins may warrant adjuvant therapy, but few studies used close margins alone as a criterion.

Limitations  
• Predominantly retrospective studies  
• Heterogeneous definitions of “close” margin across studies  
• Limited representation of non-oral cavity sites  
• HPV/p16 status not reported for oropharyngeal cancers  
• Variable reporting of adjuvant treatment data
